## Card: What it is
image: macronutrientsHero
The bulk of a nutrition panel lists the **macronutrients** — carbohydrates, protein, and fat — plus their breakdowns. Knowing what these lines mean lets you understand what a food is mostly made of and judge whether it fits what you're looking for, without needing any expertise.

## Card: Why it matters
These lines tell the real story of a food's makeup — whether that "healthy" cereal is mostly sugar, or a snack is high in protein. Understanding them helps you compare products meaningfully and make choices that match your goals, instead of relying on vague front-of-pack impressions.

## Card: Key terms
- **Carbohydrates**: energy nutrient, includes starches and sugars.
- **"Of which sugars"**: the sugar portion within total carbs.
- **Protein**: nutrient for building and repair.
- **"Of which saturates"**: the saturated-fat portion within total fat.

## Card: Reading the breakdowns
image: macroBreakdown
Labels show totals *and* sub-lines. Under **carbohydrates**, "**of which sugars**" reveals how much is sugar versus starch — crucial, since a food can be high-carb but low-sugar, or sneakily sugary. Under **fat**, "**of which saturates**" shows the saturated portion, which health guidelines suggest limiting. These sub-lines often matter more than the totals.

## Card: What to look at
For a quick read: **protein** tends to be filling and useful; high "**of which sugars**" (especially per 100g) flags a sugary product; and the **saturates** line helps you spot fattier foods. There's no single "good" or "bad" number — it depends on the food and your goals — but these lines let you see what you're really getting.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: looking only at total carbs or fat while ignoring the more telling "of which" sub-lines; assuming high-carb always means unhealthy (it depends on the type); and judging by front-of-pack claims rather than these actual figures. The sub-lines are where the useful detail hides.

## Card: Quick recap
The macronutrient lines show a food's makeup: carbs (with "of which sugars"), protein, and fat (with "of which saturates"). The "of which" sub-lines often reveal more than the totals — flagging hidden sugar or saturated fat. There's no universal good number; these figures simply let you see what a food really is.
