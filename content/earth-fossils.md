## Card: What it is
image: fossilsHero
**Fossils** are the preserved remains or traces of ancient living things — bones, shells, imprints, even footprints — turned to stone over vast time. They're nature's record of life from the deep past, letting us glimpse creatures and worlds that vanished millions of years ago. Fossils are how we know about dinosaurs and ancient life.

## Card: Why it matters
Fossils are our main window into the history of life on Earth — how life began, evolved, and changed over billions of years. They're evidence for evolution, reveal extinct creatures and lost worlds, and help us understand Earth's deep past. Beyond their scientific value, they spark wonder about the immense story of life before humans.

## Card: Key terms
- **Fossil**: preserved remains or traces of ancient life.
- **Fossilization**: the rare process that creates a fossil.
- **Sedimentary rock**: where most fossils are found.
- **Fossil record**: the collective evidence fossils provide.

## Card: How fossils form
**Fossilization** is rare and usually slow. Typically, an organism is buried quickly (in mud or sand) before it fully decays. Over long ages, minerals gradually replace the original material, turning it to stone, while layers of **sedimentary rock** build up around it. This is why fossils are mostly found in sedimentary rock. Because the conditions are so specific, only a tiny fraction of living things ever become fossils.

## Card: What fossils tell us
image: fossilCluesHero
Together, fossils form the **fossil record** — the accumulated evidence of past life. By studying which fossils appear in which rock layers (older deeper, younger higher), scientists trace how life changed over time, providing key evidence for evolution. Fossils reveal extinct creatures (like dinosaurs), ancient environments, and mass extinctions. They're the closest thing we have to a history book of life itself.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking fossils are the actual original bones (often the material has been replaced by minerals, turned to stone); imagining fossilization is common (it's rare, so the record is incomplete); thinking only dinosaurs fossilize (all kinds of life can); and not realizing how fossils provide evidence for evolution. Fossils are precious, partial glimpses of life's deep history.

## Card: Quick recap
Fossils are preserved remains or traces of ancient life, usually formed when an organism is buried and minerals slowly replace it, turning it to stone in sedimentary rock. Fossilization is rare, so the fossil record is incomplete but invaluable — it traces how life evolved over billions of years, revealing extinct creatures and lost worlds. Fossils are our window into life's deep past.
