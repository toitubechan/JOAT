## Card: What it is
image: talkStructureHero
**Structure** is the backbone of any good talk — how you organize your ideas so the audience can follow and remember them. A well-structured talk feels clear and effortless to listen to; a poorly structured one loses people, no matter how good the individual points. Structure does much of the work for you.

## Card: Why it matters
Audiences can't rewind a live talk, so if they get lost, they stay lost. Clear structure keeps them with you and makes your message stick. It also makes the talk far easier to *deliver* — you know where you're going. Most "boring" or confusing talks are really just badly structured ones.

## Card: Key terms
- **Structure**: the organized flow of a talk.
- **The rule of three**: people remember points grouped in threes.
- **Signposting**: telling the audience where you are in the talk.
- **Opening and closing**: the start and end, which matter most.

## Card: The classic structure
A reliable structure: **tell them what you'll say** (a clear opening with your main message), **say it** (your key points, ideally a memorable few — the **rule of three** works well), and **tell them what you said** (a closing that recaps). This simple beginning-middle-end shape is clear, easy to follow, and hard to get wrong.

## Card: Openings, closings, and signposting
image: signpostTalkHero
The **opening** and **closing** matter most — people remember the start and end best, so grab attention early and end with a clear, memorable point or call to action. Throughout, use **signposting** ("first... second... finally...") so the audience always knows where they are. These small cues keep listeners oriented and stop them getting lost.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: no clear structure, just a wandering stream of points; too many points (people remember a few, not fifteen); a weak opening that loses the audience early; trailing off with no real conclusion; and no signposting, so listeners can't follow the flow. A simple, clear structure fixes nearly all of these.

## Card: Quick recap
Structure is the backbone of a good talk: tell them what you'll say, say it (a memorable few points, not many), then recap. Make the opening and closing strong, since they're remembered most, and use signposting so the audience always knows where they are. Clear structure does much of the work for you.
