## Card: What it is
image: routineHero
A **cleaning routine** is a simple, repeating schedule that spreads chores across the week instead of letting them pile into one exhausting marathon. By breaking cleaning into small regular habits, you keep a home consistently tidy with far less total effort and dread.

## Card: Why it matters
The "let it build up, then blitz it" approach makes cleaning a huge, hated chore. A routine flips that: small, frequent tasks keep mess from accumulating, so deep grime never forms. It's the difference between a home that's always *almost* clean and one that swings between spotless and overwhelming.

## Card: Key terms
- **Daily tasks**: tiny upkeep done every day.
- **Weekly tasks**: bigger jobs done once a week.
- **Routine**: a regular, repeating schedule.
- **Reset**: a quick tidy that returns a room to order.

## Card: Split tasks by frequency
image: taskFrequency
Sort chores by how often they're needed. **Daily tasks** are quick: dishes, wiping counters, a tidy **reset** before bed. **Weekly tasks** are bigger: vacuuming, bathrooms, changing sheets. Occasional tasks (windows, deep cleans) happen monthly or seasonally. Assigning each a frequency stops everything landing at once.

## Card: Make it stick
Routines survive when they're easy to start. Attach cleaning to existing habits — wipe the counter while the kettle boils, do a two-minute tidy before bed. Tackle small messes immediately rather than "later." The goal isn't perfection; it's consistency. A modest routine you actually follow beats an ambitious one you abandon.

## Card: Common mistakes
The classic mistake is no routine at all, leading to overwhelming blitz cleans. Another is an over-ambitious schedule that's abandoned within a week. People also "leave it for later," letting small messes pile up, and try to deep-clean everything in one go and burn out instead of spreading it sensibly.

## Card: Quick recap
A cleaning routine spreads chores into daily, weekly, and occasional tasks so mess never piles up. Keep daily tasks tiny, attach them to existing habits, and tackle small messes right away. Aim for consistency, not perfection. Little and often makes a home that's always nearly clean, with far less effort.
