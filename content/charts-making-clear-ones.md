## Card: What it is
image: dataChartsMakingClearOnesHero
Making your own **clear charts** is about presenting data honestly and understandably — choosing the right chart type, labeling it well, and avoiding the tricks that mislead. Whether for work, study, or sharing information, knowing how to make a good chart is increasingly useful. It's the creative flip side of reading charts.

## Card: Why it matters
We increasingly need to present data ourselves — in reports, presentations, or online. A clear, honest chart communicates effectively and builds trust; a confusing or misleading one undermines your message and credibility. Knowing the principles helps you share information well and ethically, and reinforces your ability to spot bad charts.

## Card: Key terms
- **Chart type**: the form that best fits your data.
- **Clear labeling**: titles, axis labels, and legends that explain the chart.
- **Honest scale**: a scale (usually starting at zero) that doesn't distort.
- **Simplicity**: avoiding clutter that obscures the message.

## Card: Choose the right type and label it
image: dataChartsMakingClearOnesChooseTheRightTypeAndLabelItHero
Start by matching the **chart type** to your data: bar for comparisons, line for trends over time, pie for parts of a whole. Then ensure **clear labeling** — a descriptive title, labeled axes with units, and a legend if needed. The reader should understand what the chart shows without guessing. The right type, clearly labeled, does most of the work of a good chart.

## Card: Be honest and keep it simple
Use an **honest scale** — generally start axes at zero (especially for bar charts) so you don't exaggerate differences, and don't cherry-pick ranges. Aim for **simplicity**: include only what's needed, avoid clutter, decoration, and distracting 3D effects that obscure or distort the data. The best charts present the real message clearly, letting the data speak without manipulation or noise.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: choosing a chart type that doesn't fit the data; poor or missing labels, leaving readers guessing; dishonest scales (truncated axes) that exaggerate; cherry-picked ranges; and cluttered, over-decorated charts (excessive colors, 3D effects) that obscure the point. Match the type, label clearly, use an honest scale, and keep it simple.

## Card: Quick recap
Make clear, honest charts by matching the chart type to your data, labeling it well (title, axes, units, legend), using an honest scale (usually starting at zero), and keeping it simple and uncluttered. Avoid the misleading tricks you've learned to spot. A good chart lets the real message come through clearly, building understanding and trust.
