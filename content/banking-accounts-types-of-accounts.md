## Card: What it is
image: bankAccountTypesHero
Banks offer different **account types**, each built for a job. The two you'll meet first are a **checking account** (also called a current account) for everyday spending, and a **savings account** for money you want to set aside and grow a little. Picking the right one for each purpose keeps your money organized.

## Card: Why it matters
Using one account for everything makes it hard to see what you can actually spend versus what you're saving. Separating them gives you clarity at a glance and stops you from accidentally dipping into savings. The right account also means earning more interest or paying fewer fees.

## Card: Key terms
- **Checking / current account**: for daily spending and bills.
- **Savings account**: for money set aside, usually earning interest.
- **Interest rate**: what the bank pays you for keeping money there.
- **Withdrawal limit**: a cap on how often you can take money out.

## Card: The checking account
image: checkingAccountFlowHero
A **checking account** is your money's front door. Your salary lands here, and your card payments, bills, and transfers go out from it. It's built for frequent movement, so it usually pays little or no interest. Think of it as a wallet you keep just enough in to cover day-to-day life.

## Card: The savings account
A **savings account** is for money you don't need right now. It usually pays more **interest** than checking, rewarding you for leaving it alone — but may limit how often you can withdraw. It's ideal for your emergency fund or a specific goal, kept separate so it grows quietly without tempting you.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common slip is keeping all your money in checking, earning nothing, when a chunk could sit in savings earning interest. The opposite mistake is locking your everyday money in a savings account with withdrawal limits, then getting caught short. Match the account to how soon you'll need the money.

## Card: Quick recap
Checking is for spending and moves often with little interest; savings is for setting money aside and earning more. Use checking as your day-to-day wallet and savings for goals and emergencies. Splitting them gives clarity and helps your set-aside money actually grow.
