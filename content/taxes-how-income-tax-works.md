## Card: What it is
image: financeTaxesHowIncomeTaxWorksHero
**Income tax** usually isn't a single flat rate on everything you earn. Most systems use **brackets**: your income is sliced into bands, and each band is taxed at its own rate, with higher bands taxed more. Understanding this clears up one of the most common money myths.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people fear a pay rise will "push them into a higher bracket" and leave them worse off. That's a myth, and believing it can lead to turning down raises. Understanding how brackets actually work means you'll never fall for it — and you'll read your tax situation more accurately.

## Card: Key terms
- **Tax bracket**: an income band taxed at a specific rate.
- **Marginal rate**: the rate on your *next* euro earned.
- **Deduction**: an amount subtracted from taxable income.
- **Tax-free allowance**: income you can earn before any tax.

## Card: How brackets really work
image: financeTaxesHowIncomeTaxWorksHowBracketsReallyWorkHero
Only the income *within* each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. Say the first band is taxed at 10% and the next at 20%. If you move up a band, only the portion above the threshold is taxed at 20% — not your whole income. Your earlier income keeps its lower rates. So a raise always leaves you with more, never less.

## Card: Deductions and allowances
A **tax-free allowance** lets you earn a certain amount before any tax applies. **Deductions** reduce your *taxable* income — things like certain work expenses or retirement contributions, depending on your country. Lowering taxable income lowers your bill. Knowing which deductions you qualify for can legitimately reduce what you owe.

## Card: The marginal rate
Your **marginal rate** is the tax on your *next* euro earned — the rate of your top bracket. It's useful for decisions: it tells you how much of a raise or extra work you'll actually keep. It's different from your *average* rate, which is the overall share of your income that goes to tax (usually lower).

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest myth is fearing a higher bracket makes you worse off — it never does, because only the top slice is taxed more. Another mistake is missing deductions and allowances you're entitled to, overpaying as a result. And many confuse their marginal rate with their average rate, overestimating their true tax burden.

## Card: Quick recap
Income tax uses brackets: each band of income is taxed at its own rate, so a raise always leaves you better off. Allowances and deductions reduce your taxable income and your bill. Your marginal rate is the tax on your next euro; your average rate is lower. Rules vary — check yours or ask a professional.
