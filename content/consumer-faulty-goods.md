## Card: What it is
image: faultyGoodsHero
When a product is **faulty** or a service is done badly, you have rights and a process to get it put right. Knowing what counts as faulty, and the steps to take, helps you get a refund, repair, or replacement instead of being stuck with something broken. (General guidance; specifics vary by country.)

## Card: Why it matters
Faulty goods and poor services are common, and many people either give up or don't know what they're owed. Understanding your position helps you confidently pursue a fair outcome. It saves money and frustration, and stops sellers from wrongly dodging their responsibilities.

## Card: Key terms
- **Faulty / defective**: not working as it should.
- **Remedy**: the fix you're owed (refund, repair, replacement).
- **Reasonable timeframe**: how soon issues should be raised.
- **Seller responsibility**: the seller, not the maker, is usually who you claim from.

## Card: What counts and who's responsible
A product is **faulty** if it doesn't work, breaks too soon, isn't as described, or isn't fit for purpose. Crucially, your claim is usually with the **seller** you bought from — not the manufacturer. Sellers sometimes try to redirect you to the maker, but it's typically the seller's responsibility to provide a **remedy**. Knowing this stops you being passed around.

## Card: The steps to take
image: sellerRemedySteps
A practical process: act within a **reasonable timeframe** (don't sit on it); contact the seller, clearly state the fault and what remedy you want; have your proof of purchase ready; be polite but firm, referencing your rights. If they refuse, escalate — to a manager, then to consumer protection bodies or dispute schemes where they exist. Persistence and knowing your rights usually win fair outcomes.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: assuming you must claim from the manufacturer (it's usually the seller); waiting too long to raise the issue; giving up at the first refusal instead of escalating; not having proof of purchase; and being unclear about the remedy you want. Act promptly, claim from the seller, stay firm, and escalate if needed.

## Card: Quick recap
For faulty goods or bad services, you're owed a remedy (refund, repair, or replacement) — and your claim is usually with the *seller*, not the maker. Act within a reasonable time, contact them clearly with proof of purchase, be polite but firm about the remedy you want, and escalate if refused. Persistence and knowing your rights win fair outcomes.
