## Card: What it is
image: dataStatisticsCorrelationCausationHero
**Correlation** is when two things tend to occur together; **causation** is when one actually *causes* the other. A crucial principle of data literacy is that **correlation does not imply causation** — just because two things are linked doesn't mean one caused the other. Confusing the two is one of the most common reasoning errors.

## Card: Why it matters
This single principle protects you from a huge range of bad reasoning, false health claims, misleading headlines, and faulty arguments. Understanding it sharpens your thinking about cause and effect in everything from news to personal decisions. It's perhaps the single most valuable idea in everyday data literacy.

## Card: Key terms
- **Correlation**: two things tending to occur together.
- **Causation**: one thing actually causing another.
- **Confounding factor**: a hidden third cause behind both.
- **Coincidence**: a chance link with no real connection.

## Card: Why linked isn't caused
image: dataStatisticsCorrelationCausationWhyLinkedIsnTCausedHero
Two things can be **correlated** for reasons other than one causing the other. There might be a **confounding factor** — a hidden third thing causing both (ice cream sales and drowning both rise in summer; the heat causes both, not each other). It could be **coincidence** (random chance links). Or the causation could even run the *opposite* way. So a correlation alone never proves what causes what.

## Card: Spotting the trap
The classic error is seeing two things move together and assuming one causes the other. "People who do X are healthier, so X causes health!" — but maybe healthy people tend to do X (reverse), or wealth causes both (confounder). When you hear a causal claim from a correlation, ask: could something else explain this link? This question alone defuses countless misleading claims.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming correlation proves causation (it doesn't); ignoring confounding factors; not considering that causation might run the other way; and being swayed by health or news claims built on mere correlations. The fix is a single reflex: when two things are linked, ask whether one truly *causes* the other, or if something else explains it.

## Card: Quick recap
Correlation (things occurring together) does not imply causation (one causing the other). Links can arise from confounding factors (a hidden third cause), coincidence, or reverse causation. The classic error is assuming a correlation proves cause and effect. The defense is one reflex question: "could something else explain this link?" It's the most valuable idea in everyday data literacy.
