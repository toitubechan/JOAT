## Card: What it is
image: bigBangDeepDiveHero
The **Big Bang** is the leading scientific explanation for how the universe began — about 13.8 billion years ago, from an incredibly hot, dense state, expanding and cooling ever since. It's not an explosion *in* space, but the expansion *of* space itself. It's the best-supported account of the origin of everything we can observe.

## Card: Why it matters
The Big Bang addresses one of humanity's biggest questions: where did everything come from? It's the foundation of modern cosmology, supported by extensive evidence. Understanding it gives you a grasp of the universe's origin and scale, and helps you engage with science news about the cosmos. It's a profound, mind-stretching idea grounded in real evidence.

## Card: Key terms
- **Big Bang**: the theory of the universe's origin and expansion.
- **Expansion**: space itself stretching and growing.
- **13.8 billion years**: the universe's approximate age.
- **Evidence**: observations supporting the theory.

## Card: Not an explosion in space
image: expandingSpaceHero
A crucial point: the Big Bang wasn't an explosion that happened *somewhere* in pre-existing space. Rather, it was the beginning of space (and time) itself, which has been **expanding** ever since — like the surface of a balloon stretching as it inflates, with everything moving apart. The universe started incredibly hot and dense, then expanded and cooled over billions of years into what we see today.

## Card: The evidence
The Big Bang isn't a guess — it rests on strong **evidence**. Galaxies are observed moving away from each other, meaning the universe is expanding (rewind that, and everything was once together). There's also a faint "afterglow" of radiation filling all space — the leftover heat from the early hot universe. And the proportions of the lightest elements match predictions. Together, these observations make the Big Bang the well-supported scientific consensus.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: imagining the Big Bang as an explosion in existing space (it was the expansion of space itself); asking "what's outside" or "where did it happen" (it happened everywhere, as all of space began); thinking it's mere speculation (it's backed by strong evidence); and confusing it with a theory of *why* rather than *how*. It explains the universe's observed origin and expansion.

## Card: Quick recap
The Big Bang is the leading, well-evidenced explanation for the universe's origin: about 13.8 billion years ago, space and time began in an incredibly hot, dense state and have been expanding and cooling ever since. It's not an explosion *in* space but the expansion *of* space. Evidence includes galaxies moving apart, the leftover radiation afterglow, and element proportions.
