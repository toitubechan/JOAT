## Card: What it is
image: insuranceHero
**Insurance** is a deal where you pay a small, regular amount, and in return a company promises to cover a large cost if something bad happens — a crash, an illness, a house fire. You're trading a predictable little expense for protection against a rare but potentially ruinous one.

## Card: Why it matters
Some events are too expensive to absorb alone: a major medical bill or a destroyed home could wipe out years of savings. Insurance spreads that risk across many people, so no single person is financially destroyed by bad luck. It's about protecting against the disasters you can't afford, not the small stuff.

## Card: Key terms
- **Premium**: the regular amount you pay for cover.
- **Claim**: a request for the insurer to pay for a covered loss.
- **Coverage**: what the policy actually protects against.
- **Insurer**: the company providing the insurance.

## Card: How the pool works
image: riskPool
Imagine a thousand people each paying a small **premium** into a shared pot. Most won't suffer a disaster this year, but a few will — and the pot pays their large bills. You're essentially chipping in so that whoever gets unlucky is covered. Next year it might be you who needs it.

## Card: What insurance is (and isn't) for
Insurance is for **large, unaffordable losses** — not minor, expected costs. Insuring a cheap phone may cost more in premiums than just replacing it. But not insuring your health or home, where a single event could cost a fortune, is a genuine risk. Use it where the loss would be too big to handle yourself.

## Card: Common mistakes
The first mistake is over-insuring trivial things, paying premiums that exceed the item's worth. The opposite — and more dangerous — is skipping insurance on big risks to save money, then facing a catastrophic bill. A third is assuming you're covered without checking what the policy actually includes.

## Card: Quick recap
Insurance trades a small regular premium for protection against rare, expensive disasters, spreading risk across many people. Use it for losses too big to absorb alone, not for cheap, expected costs. The key question is always: could I afford this loss myself? If not, insurance earns its place.
