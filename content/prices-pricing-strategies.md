## Card: What it is
image: econPricesPricingStrategiesHero
Businesses use deliberate **pricing strategies** and psychological tricks to influence what you buy and how much you pay. Prices aren't just set by costs — they're carefully crafted to nudge your behavior. Understanding these tactics helps you see through them and make clearer-headed decisions.

## Card: Why it matters
You're exposed to pricing tactics every day, often without realizing. Recognizing them — the charm of "9.99," the lure of "anchor" prices, the pull of fake urgency — helps you resist manipulation and judge real value. It's practical knowledge that directly protects your wallet from clever marketing.

## Card: Key terms
- **Charm pricing**: ending prices in .99 to seem cheaper.
- **Anchoring**: showing a high price to make another seem cheap.
- **Decoy pricing**: an option designed to steer you to another.
- **Artificial urgency**: fake time pressure to rush you.

## Card: Psychological pricing tricks
image: econPricesPricingStrategiesPsychologicalPricingTricksHero
Common tactics: **charm pricing** (9.99 feels much cheaper than 10, though it's nearly the same); **anchoring** (showing a high "original" price so a lower one feels like a steal); and **decoy pricing** (adding an unattractive option to make a pricier one look reasonable). These exploit how our brains judge prices by comparison and first impressions rather than pure logic.

## Card: Urgency and bundling
More tactics: **artificial urgency** ("only 2 left!", "sale ends today!") pressures you to buy before thinking — often the scarcity is manufactured. **Bundling** combines items so the deal seems good but may include things you don't need. Subscriptions and "free trials" rely on inertia (you forget to cancel). These tactics aren't always dishonest, but they're designed to influence you.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: falling for charm pricing as a real difference; trusting "original price" anchors without checking; rushing at artificial urgency; assuming bundles always save money; and forgetting to cancel trials. The fix: pause, compare actual prices, ignore manufactured pressure, and ask "do I actually need this at this price?" rather than reacting to the tactic.

## Card: Quick recap
Businesses use pricing tactics — charm pricing (.99), anchoring (high "original" prices), decoys, artificial urgency, and bundling — to nudge your spending. They exploit how brains judge prices by comparison and impulse. Recognizing them lets you pause, compare real prices, ignore fake pressure, and decide on genuine value rather than reacting to the trick.
