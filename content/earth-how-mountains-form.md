## Card: What it is
image: mountainsFormHero
**Mountains** form through powerful Earth processes — mostly the slow collision and movement of the giant plates that make up Earth's surface. Over millions of years, these forces push, fold, and lift rock into towering peaks. Mountains aren't permanent fixtures; they rise and erode over vast spans of time.

## Card: Why it matters
Mountains shape weather, water, ecosystems, and human life, and they're among Earth's most striking features. Understanding how they form reveals that our seemingly solid planet is dynamic and ever-changing. It's a window into the powerful geological forces shaping the world beneath our feet, over timescales far beyond a human life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Tectonic plates**: giant slabs of Earth's surface that slowly move.
- **Collision**: plates pushing into each other.
- **Uplift**: the raising of land.
- **Erosion**: the wearing down of rock over time.

## Card: Plates colliding
Earth's surface is broken into giant **tectonic plates** that slowly move. The main way mountains form is **collision**: when two plates push into each other, the immense force crumples and folds the rock upward, like a rug bunching when pushed. This **uplift** builds great mountain ranges over millions of years. The world's biggest ranges sit where plates have collided.

## Card: Building up and wearing down
image: upliftErosionHero
Mountains involve a constant tug-of-war between **uplift** (building them up) and **erosion** (wearing them down). Wind, water, and ice slowly grind mountains away. Young mountains are tall and jagged; old ones are lower and rounded, worn down over time. Some mountains also form from volcanoes building up, or blocks of crust being pushed up along faults. All of it unfolds over immense timescales.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking mountains are permanent and unchanging (they rise and erode over millions of years); imagining they form quickly (it's incredibly slow); not realizing Earth's surface moves at all; and thinking all mountains form the same way (collision is the main one, but volcanoes and faults build mountains too). The Earth is dynamic, just very slow.

## Card: Quick recap
Mountains form mainly when tectonic plates collide, crumpling and lifting rock upward over millions of years. They're shaped by a tug-of-war between uplift (building up) and erosion (wearing down) — young mountains are jagged, old ones rounded. Volcanoes and faults also build mountains. It all reveals that our "solid" planet is dynamic, just on a vast, slow timescale.
