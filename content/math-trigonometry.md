## Card: What it is
image: mathTrigonometryHero
**Trigonometry** is the math of triangles — specifically, the relationships between their angles and side lengths. It sounds intimidating, but its core idea is simple and surprisingly useful: if you know some parts of a triangle, trigonometry lets you figure out the others. It's a powerful tool for measuring things you can't directly reach.

## Card: Why it matters
Trigonometry powers navigation, construction, engineering, physics, computer graphics, and more — anywhere distances, heights, and angles matter. Understanding its basic purpose (relating angles and sides of triangles) demystifies a subject many find scary, and reveals why it's one of math's most practically applied tools.

## Card: Key terms
- **Trigonometry**: the math of triangles' angles and sides.
- **Angle**: the measure of turn between two lines.
- **Right triangle**: a triangle with a 90-degree angle.
- **Indirect measurement**: finding distances you can't measure directly.

## Card: Relating angles and sides
image: mathTrigonometryRelatingAnglesAndSidesHero
The heart of trigonometry is the fixed relationship between a triangle's **angles** and its side lengths. In a triangle (especially a **right triangle**, with a 90-degree corner), if you know certain angles and sides, the rest are determined — you can calculate them. Trigonometry provides the tools (often using ratios of sides) to do this. The key insight: angles and side lengths in a triangle are reliably linked, so knowing some lets you find the others.

## Card: Measuring the unreachable
Trigonometry's most striking power is **indirect measurement** — finding distances and heights you can't measure directly. How tall is a mountain or building? How far away is a ship? By measuring an angle and a known distance, trigonometry lets you calculate the unknown height or distance without physically reaching it. This is why it's essential to surveying, navigation, astronomy, and engineering — it measures the unmeasurable.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking trigonometry is just abstract triangle puzzles (it's a practical tool for real measurement); being intimidated by the terminology while missing the simple core idea (angles and sides are linked); not knowing its huge real-world uses; and assuming it's only for advanced math. At heart, trigonometry uses the link between angles and sides to measure things you can't reach directly.

## Card: Quick recap
Trigonometry is the math of triangles — the reliable relationships between their angles and side lengths. If you know some parts, you can calculate the rest. Its standout power is indirect measurement: finding heights and distances you can't reach directly (a mountain's height, a ship's distance) using an angle and a known length. It's behind navigation, construction, and engineering — a practical tool, not just abstract puzzles.
