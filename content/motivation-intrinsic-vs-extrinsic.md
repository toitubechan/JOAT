## Card: What it is
image: psychMotivationIntrinsicVsExtrinsicHero
Motivation comes in two broad types: **intrinsic** (doing something for its own sake — enjoyment, interest, meaning) and **extrinsic** (doing it for an external reward or to avoid punishment — money, grades, praise). Understanding the difference reveals why some motivation lasts and some fizzles.

## Card: Why it matters
The type of motivation behind a goal strongly affects how long it lasts and how it feels. Relying on the wrong type can lead to burnout or quitting. Understanding intrinsic and extrinsic motivation helps you set up goals and pursuits in ways that stay sustainable and rewarding, rather than draining.

## Card: Key terms
- **Intrinsic motivation**: doing something for its own sake.
- **Extrinsic motivation**: doing something for an external reward.
- **Sustainability**: how well motivation lasts over time.
- **Overjustification**: rewards undermining intrinsic interest.

## Card: The two types
image: psychMotivationIntrinsicVsExtrinsicTheTwoTypesHero
**Intrinsic motivation** comes from within — you do something because it's enjoyable, interesting, or meaningful to *you*. **Extrinsic motivation** comes from outside — rewards (money, praise, grades) or avoiding punishment. Both are real and useful, but they behave differently: intrinsic motivation tends to be more sustainable and satisfying, while extrinsic can be powerful but often fades when the reward stops.

## Card: When each works (and backfires)
Extrinsic rewards are great for getting started or for tasks that aren't inherently fun. But relying on them alone risks fading motivation and even **overjustification** — where adding rewards to something you already enjoyed can *reduce* your intrinsic interest. For lasting motivation, connecting a goal to intrinsic drivers (genuine interest, meaning, growth) tends to sustain you far longer than external rewards alone.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: relying purely on extrinsic rewards for long-term goals (they fade); not finding any intrinsic meaning in important pursuits; ignoring that rewards can sometimes undermine genuine enjoyment; and assuming one type is simply "better" (both have uses). The wise approach: use extrinsic rewards thoughtfully, but root lasting motivation in intrinsic drivers.

## Card: Quick recap
Intrinsic motivation (doing something for its own sake) tends to be more sustainable and satisfying; extrinsic motivation (external rewards) is powerful but can fade when rewards stop, and may even undermine intrinsic interest. Both have uses, but for lasting motivation, connect goals to genuine interest, meaning, and growth rather than relying on rewards alone.
