## Card: What it is
image: carBudgetHero
Setting a car **budget** means accounting for far more than the purchase price. A car has ongoing **running costs** — fuel, insurance, maintenance, taxes — that add up significantly. Understanding the *total cost of ownership* prevents buying a car you can't actually afford to run. (General guidance; not financial advice.)

## Card: Why it matters
Many people budget only for the purchase price, then struggle with the ongoing costs. A car you can afford to *buy* may be one you can't afford to *run*. Understanding total cost of ownership helps you set a realistic budget and avoid the financial strain that catches out so many car buyers.

## Card: Key terms
- **Total cost of ownership**: all costs over the time you own the car.
- **Running costs**: ongoing expenses like fuel, insurance, and maintenance.
- **Insurance**: a major recurring cost, varying by car and driver.
- **Depreciation**: value lost over time, a hidden ownership cost.

## Card: Beyond the sticker price
image: beyondStickerPrice
The purchase price is just the start. **Running costs** include fuel (more for thirsty cars), **insurance** (which varies hugely by car, driver, and location), maintenance and repairs, taxes/registration, and depreciation. Together, these can rival or exceed the purchase price over the years you own a car. Budgeting only for the sticker price badly underestimates the real cost.

## Card: Match the car to the full budget
When setting your budget, consider the **total cost of ownership** — what the car costs to *own and run*, not just buy. A cheaper car that's expensive to insure, fuel, or repair might cost more overall than a pricier but efficient one. Check insurance quotes *before* buying, and favor cars known for reliability and low running costs if budget is tight. Match the whole picture to what you can afford.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: budgeting only for the purchase price; not checking insurance costs before buying (they can be a shock); ignoring fuel efficiency and likely repair costs; and choosing a car cheap to buy but expensive to run. The fix: estimate the total cost of ownership and check insurance quotes upfront, so the car fits your real budget.

## Card: Quick recap
A car's true cost is its total cost of ownership — purchase price *plus* running costs (fuel, insurance, maintenance, taxes, depreciation), which can rival the purchase price over time. Budget for the whole picture, check insurance quotes before buying, and favor reliable, efficient cars if money is tight. A car you can buy isn't always one you can afford to run.
