## Card: What it is
image: financeSavingCommonMistakesHero
Most people don't fail at budgeting because they're bad with money — they fail because of a handful of predictable traps. This subtopic collects the common reasons budgets collapse, so you can design yours to survive contact with real life from day one.

## Card: Why it matters
A budget only helps if you keep using it. Knowing why budgets typically die — too strict, too vague, no buffer — lets you build in the fixes upfront. Avoiding these traps is the difference between a budget that lasts years and one abandoned by week three.

## Card: Key terms
- **Buffer**: a small margin for things that go slightly over.
- **Irregular expense**: a cost that doesn't occur every month.
- **Lifestyle inflation**: spending rising to match higher income.
- **All-or-nothing**: quitting entirely after one slip.

## Card: The "too strict" trap
image: financeSavingCommonMistakesTheTooStrictTrapHero
The most common killer is an over-tight budget with zero room for fun or error. The first unexpected dinner or impulse buy makes you feel like you've "failed," so you quit — the **all-or-nothing** trap. The fix: build in a **buffer** and a fun category, and treat overspending as data to adjust, not a reason to abandon ship.

## Card: The "forgot the irregulars" trap
Budgets that only count monthly bills break the moment an **irregular expense** appears — an annual subscription, a birthday, a car repair. Suddenly the plan's blown. The fix is to list yearly and occasional costs too, then save for them in advance with sinking funds, so they never ambush the budget.

## Card: Common mistakes
Beyond those: not tracking actual spending, so you never know if the plan worked; letting **lifestyle inflation** eat every raise instead of boosting savings; and budgeting in your head instead of writing it down, where it's vague and easy to fudge. Each has a simple fix — track, automate savings of raises, and put the plan on paper or an app.

## Card: Quick recap
Budgets usually fail from being too strict, ignoring irregular costs, not tracking reality, or quitting after one slip. Build in a buffer and fun, plan for irregular expenses, track what you actually spend, and treat a bad week as data — not a reason to give up. Forgiving budgets are the ones that last.
