## Card: What it is
image: econTradeBarriersAndAgreementsHero
Countries don't always trade freely — they use **trade barriers** (like tariffs) to restrict trade, and **trade agreements** to encourage it. These policy choices shape prices, industries, and international relations. Understanding them explains a lot of economic and political news about trade.

## Card: Why it matters
Trade barriers and agreements are constant subjects of political debate and directly affect prices and jobs. Understanding the basics — what a tariff is, why countries restrict or open trade — helps you follow these debates and grasp the real trade-offs involved. It's central to understanding trade policy in the news.

## Card: Key terms
- **Tariff**: a tax on imported goods.
- **Trade barrier**: anything restricting imports or exports.
- **Free trade**: trade with few or no barriers.
- **Trade agreement**: a deal between countries to ease trade.

## Card: Barriers like tariffs
image: econTradeBarriersAndAgreementsBarriersLikeTariffsHero
A **tariff** is a tax on imported goods, the most common **trade barrier**. Governments use tariffs to make foreign goods more expensive, often to protect domestic industries from cheaper imports. But there's a trade-off: tariffs protect some local producers and jobs, yet they also raise prices for consumers and can invite retaliation. Barriers help some groups while costing others.

## Card: Agreements and free trade
**Trade agreements** are deals where countries agree to reduce barriers between them, moving toward **free trade** (few or no restrictions). The aim is to boost trade, lower prices, and increase efficiency by letting goods flow more freely. The debate is real: free trade generally lowers prices and grows economies overall, but can disrupt specific local industries and workers exposed to foreign competition.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking tariffs are simply "good" (they protect some but raise prices for everyone and can trigger retaliation); assuming free trade has only benefits (it disrupts some industries); seeing trade policy as simply right or wrong rather than full of trade-offs; and not knowing a tariff is essentially a tax that consumers often end up paying.

## Card: Quick recap
Countries use trade barriers (like tariffs — taxes on imports) to restrict trade, often to protect domestic industries, and trade agreements to encourage it toward free trade. Each involves trade-offs: barriers protect some jobs but raise prices and invite retaliation; free trade lowers prices overall but disrupts some local industries. Trade policy is about balancing these.
