## Card: What it is
image: financeCreditCreditScoresHero
A **credit score** is a number that sums up how reliably you handle borrowed money. Lenders use it as a quick read on how risky it is to lend to you. A higher score signals "this person repays on time"; a lower one signals risk. It's like a financial reputation, boiled down to a single figure.

## Card: Why it matters
Your score quietly decides a lot: whether you're approved for a loan or card, the interest rate you're offered, and sometimes even renting a flat or getting a phone contract. A good score can save you thousands in interest over a lifetime, because lenders reward low risk with lower rates.

## Card: Key terms
- **Credit score**: a number rating your borrowing reliability.
- **Credit report**: the detailed record behind the score.
- **Payment history**: your record of paying on time.
- **Credit utilization**: how much of your available credit you're using.

## Card: What moves it up
image: financeCreditCreditScoresWhatMovesItUpHero
The biggest booster is **payment history** — paying every bill on time, every time. Keeping **credit utilization** low (using only a small slice of your available credit) helps too. A longer history of responsible borrowing and a mix of credit types also nudge it up. In short: borrow modestly and repay reliably.

## Card: What drags it down
Missed or late payments hurt the most and linger for years. Maxing out cards (high utilization) signals stress. Applying for lots of credit at once can ding it, as can defaulting on a loan. The score rewards steady, boring reliability and penalizes anything that looks like financial strain.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common myth is that checking your own score harms it — it doesn't. Another mistake is closing your oldest credit card, which can shorten your history and lower your score. And many don't realize a single missed payment can outweigh months of good behavior, so consistency beats occasional big gestures.

## Card: Quick recap
A credit score rates how reliably you repay, and it shapes your loan approvals and interest rates. Pay on time, keep balances low, and let your history grow. Avoid late payments and maxed cards. Checking your own score is free and harmless — so know your number.
