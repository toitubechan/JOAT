## Card: What it is
image: kitchenOrganizationHero
**Kitchen organization** is arranging your space, tools, and ingredients so cooking flows smoothly. A well-organized kitchen makes cooking faster, calmer, and more enjoyable; a chaotic one makes even simple meals stressful. It's less about a big tidy and more about a few smart, lasting habits.

## Card: Why it matters
Much cooking stress comes from a disorganized kitchen — hunting for tools, cluttered counters, ingredients you forgot you had. Good organization saves time, reduces waste, and makes you far more likely to actually cook. It turns the kitchen from a place of friction into one that works with you.

## Card: Key terms
- **Mise en place**: prepping and arranging everything before cooking.
- **Zones**: grouping related items where you use them.
- **Clean as you go**: tidying during cooking, not only after.
- **FIFO**: "first in, first out" — using older food first.

## Card: Set up smart zones
Organize by **zones**: keep things where you use them — cooking utensils near the stove, knives and boards near prep space, everyday plates within easy reach. Group similar items together so you're not hunting. This simple principle, borrowed from professional kitchens, removes a surprising amount of daily friction.

## Card: Habits that keep it working
image: miseEnPlaceCleanFlow
Two habits transform cooking. **Mise en place**: prep and lay out all ingredients before you start, so you cook calmly without scrambling. And **clean as you go**: wash and tidy during the gaps while cooking, so you're not left with a mountain afterward. For food, use **FIFO** — older items to the front, so nothing rots forgotten.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: storing tools far from where they're used; cluttered counters leaving no room to work; starting to cook before prepping (mise en place), then panicking; leaving all cleanup to the end; and burying older food behind new, so it spoils. Small organizational habits prevent all of these.

## Card: Quick recap
An organized kitchen makes cooking easy: set up zones so tools are where you use them, practice mise en place by prepping before you start, clean as you go to avoid a pile-up, and rotate food first-in-first-out to cut waste. A few habits turn kitchen chaos into smooth, enjoyable cooking.
