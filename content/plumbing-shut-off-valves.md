## Card: What it is
image: shutOffValveHero
A **shut-off valve** is a tap that stops water flowing to a fixture or to your whole home. There are small ones under sinks and behind toilets, and one **main shut-off** that cuts water to the entire house. Knowing where they are is the single most useful piece of plumbing knowledge you can have.

## Card: Why it matters
When a pipe bursts or a fixture floods, every second counts — water can do enormous damage fast. The difference between a minor mess and a ruined room is often whether you can shut the water off quickly. Finding your valves *before* an emergency, not during one, is what saves the day.

## Card: Key terms
- **Main shut-off valve**: stops water to the whole house.
- **Fixture valve**: stops water to one sink, toilet, or appliance.
- **Stopcock**: another name for a main shut-off valve.
- **Quarter-turn valve**: a lever type that's off at 90 degrees.

## Card: Find them now
image: mainValveLocalValve
Locate your **main shut-off** today — it's often where the water pipe enters the home, near the meter, in a basement, utility area, or outside box. Then find the smaller **fixture valves** under sinks and behind the toilet. Make sure they actually turn; valves left untouched for years can seize up.

## Card: How to use them
Most round valves close by turning **clockwise** ("righty-tighty"). Lever-style **quarter-turn valves** are off when the handle sits across (90 degrees to) the pipe. In an emergency, shut the nearest fixture valve first; if that's not enough or you can't reach it, use the main shut-off to kill water to the whole house.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is not knowing where any valve is until water is already pouring out. Another is discovering, mid-emergency, that a valve is seized from never being turned — so test them occasionally. And some force a stuck valve so hard it breaks; if it won't budge gently, the main shut-off is the backup.

## Card: Quick recap
Shut-off valves stop water — fixture valves for one sink or toilet, the main shut-off for the whole house. Find and test them before you ever need them. Round valves close clockwise; lever valves are off when crosswise. In a flood, shut the nearest valve fast — it's your best damage control.
