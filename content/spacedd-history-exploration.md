## Card: What it is
image: spaceddHistoryHero
The **history of space exploration** is the story of how humanity went from looking up at the sky to launching satellites, landing on the Moon, and sending probes across the solar system — all within a single century. It's a remarkable tale of science, competition, and ambition that shaped the modern world.

## Card: Why it matters
Space exploration produced technologies we use daily (satellites, GPS, countless spin-offs), expanded human knowledge enormously, and stands as one of our greatest achievements. Understanding its history reveals how fast we progressed, the forces that drove it, and why space still captures imagination and investment today.

## Card: Key terms
- **Space Age**: the era beginning with the first satellites.
- **Space Race**: the Cold War competition to reach space.
- **Satellite**: an object placed in orbit around Earth.
- **Probe**: an uncrewed spacecraft sent to explore.

## Card: From first satellites to the Moon
image: satelliteMoonMilestoneHero
The **Space Age** began in the mid-20th century when the first artificial **satellites** were launched into orbit — a stunning leap. This sparked the **Space Race**, a Cold War competition between superpowers to achieve space milestones, which accelerated progress dramatically. Its peak was landing humans on the Moon — one of humanity's most extraordinary achievements, accomplished astonishingly soon after the first satellites.

## Card: Probes, stations, and a new era
After the Moon, exploration broadened. Uncrewed **probes** were sent to study other planets, moons, and beyond — some even leaving the solar system. Space stations let humans live and work in orbit for long periods. Reusable rockets and private companies have recently lowered costs and opened a new era of space activity. From first satellite to interplanetary probes, the pace of space exploration has been breathtaking.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking space exploration is ancient (it's barely older than a human lifetime); assuming it was purely scientific (Cold War competition was a huge driver); not realizing how many everyday technologies came from it; and imagining humans have traveled far (we've only reached the Moon in person — probes do the distant exploring). The progress has been remarkably fast and recent.

## Card: Quick recap
Space exploration went from the first satellites, through the Cold War Space Race and the Moon landings, to probes exploring the whole solar system and humans living on space stations — all within about a century. Driven by science and superpower competition, it produced everyday technologies and vast knowledge. Recently, reusable rockets and private firms have opened a new era. The pace has been breathtaking.
