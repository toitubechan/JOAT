## Card: What it is
image: influencePrinciplesHero
Researchers have identified recurring **principles of influence** — psychological tendencies that make people more likely to say yes. Understanding them helps you persuade more effectively *and* recognize when they're being used on you. They're tools: powerful when used honestly, manipulative when used to deceive.

## Card: Why it matters
These principles work on all of us, often below conscious awareness. Knowing them lets you make your honest case more compelling, and just as importantly, spot when marketers or manipulators are pulling these levers on you. Awareness is power in both directions — to persuade ethically and to resist manipulation.

## Card: Key terms
- **Reciprocity**: we feel obliged to return favors.
- **Social proof**: we follow what others do.
- **Authority**: we're swayed by credible experts.
- **Scarcity**: we value things more when they're limited.

## Card: The classic principles
Several well-known levers: **reciprocity** (we want to return favors, so giving first makes people more receptive); **social proof** (we follow what others, especially similar people, do); **authority** (we trust credible experts); **liking** (we say yes to people we like); **scarcity** (we value what's rare or running out); and **consistency** (we like to act in line with our prior commitments).

## Card: Use them honestly
image: honestInfluenceHero
These principles are most powerful when used *honestly* — pointing to genuine social proof, real expertise, true scarcity. Used deceptively (fake reviews, false "only 2 left!", manufactured authority), they become manipulation. The ethical use: deploy them to highlight real reasons to agree, not to fabricate pressure. And recognizing them protects you when others use them dishonestly on you.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: not knowing these principles, so you persuade weakly and get manipulated easily; using them dishonestly (fake scarcity or social proof), which is manipulation and backfires when discovered; and over-relying on tricks instead of a genuine case. The principles should support honest persuasion, not replace it.

## Card: Quick recap
Principles of influence — reciprocity, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, consistency — are psychological levers that make people more likely to agree. Used honestly, they make a genuine case more compelling; used to fabricate pressure, they're manipulation. Know them to persuade ethically *and* to recognize when they're being used on you.
