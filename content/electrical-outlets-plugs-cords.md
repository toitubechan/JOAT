## Card: What it is
image: outletSafetyHero
Outlets, plugs, and cords are the parts of your electrical system you touch every day — and the ones most likely to cause trouble if neglected. Knowing how to use and inspect them safely prevents most household electrical hazards, without ever needing to open anything up.

## Card: Why it matters
Damaged cords and overloaded outlets are leading causes of electrical fires and shocks. Because we handle these parts constantly, small problems are easy to spot early — if you know what to look for. This is everyday, hands-on safety that anyone can practice.

## Card: Key terms
- **Outlet / socket**: the wall point you plug into.
- **Frayed cord**: a cord with damaged or exposed wiring.
- **Extension lead**: a portable strip of extra sockets.
- **Daisy-chaining**: plugging extension leads into each other (unsafe).

## Card: Cords and plugs
image: cordHazards
Inspect cords for damage: a **frayed cord** with exposed wires, cracked insulation, or a hot plug is a real hazard — stop using it. Pull plugs out by the body, never by yanking the cord, which damages the wires inside. A plug that's loose in the socket or warm to the touch needs attention.

## Card: Using extension leads safely
**Extension leads** are handy but misused often. Don't **daisy-chain** them (plug one into another) — it overloads the wiring. Don't run them under rugs or carpets, where heat can't escape and a fault goes unseen. And check the lead's rating: high-power appliances like heaters can overload a cheap strip.

## Card: Common mistakes
The top mistakes: using visibly damaged or frayed cords; overloading a single outlet with stacked adapters; daisy-chaining extension leads; and running cords under rugs or pinched behind furniture where they overheat. Each is a common, avoidable cause of fires — and each is easy to spot once you're looking.

## Card: Quick recap
Treat outlets, plugs, and cords with everyday care: replace frayed cords, unplug by the body not the cord, and don't overload sockets. Never daisy-chain extension leads or hide cords under rugs. These small habits prevent most household electrical fires and shocks — and cost nothing.
