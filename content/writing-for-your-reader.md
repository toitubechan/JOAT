## Card: What it is
image: readerFocusedWritingHero
**Writing for your reader** means tailoring what and how you write to the person who'll read it — their knowledge, needs, and situation. Good writing isn't about *you* expressing yourself; it's about the *reader* understanding. Keeping them in mind shapes every choice, from vocabulary to length to tone.

## Card: Why it matters
The same information needs to be written differently for an expert versus a beginner, a boss versus a friend. Writing that ignores the reader confuses or bores them. Considering your reader is what makes writing genuinely effective — it's the difference between writing that connects and writing that misses.

## Card: Key terms
- **Audience**: the person or people who'll read it.
- **Reader's perspective**: their knowledge, needs, and viewpoint.
- **Tone**: the writing's attitude and formality.
- **Purpose**: what you want the reader to know, feel, or do.

## Card: Know your audience
image: knowAudienceHero
Before writing, ask: who's my **audience**, and what do they already know? An expert needs no basics explained; a beginner needs jargon defined. A busy executive wants the bottom line; a curious learner wants detail. Adjusting to your reader's knowledge and needs is what stops writing being too simple, too complex, or off-target.

## Card: Match tone and purpose
**Tone** should fit the reader and context — formal for a job application, warm for a colleague, simple for the public. And be clear on your **purpose**: what do you want the reader to know, feel, or *do* by the end? Writing with a clear purpose and the right tone for your audience makes it land. Picture an actual reader as you write.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: writing for yourself rather than the reader; using jargon a beginner won't follow, or over-explaining to an expert; the wrong tone (too casual or too stiff for the context); and no clear purpose, so the reader isn't sure what to take away. The fix: picture your actual reader and write for them.

## Card: Quick recap
Write for your reader, not yourself: consider what they already know, what they need, and what you want them to do. Match your vocabulary and detail to their knowledge, your tone to the context, and keep your purpose clear. Picturing an actual reader as you write is what makes writing connect.
