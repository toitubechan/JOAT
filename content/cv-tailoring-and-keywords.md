## Card: What it is
image: cvTailoringHero
**Tailoring** means adjusting your CV for each specific job, so it clearly matches what that employer is looking for. Combined with using the right **keywords**, it dramatically raises your chances. A generic CV sent everywhere is far weaker than one targeted to the role in front of you.

## Card: Why it matters
Employers want someone who fits *their* role, and a tailored CV shows you do. Many CVs are also first screened by software scanning for keywords, so the right terms help you get past that gate to a human. Tailoring is often the difference between being shortlisted and being skipped.

## Card: Key terms
- **Tailoring**: adjusting your CV for a specific job.
- **Job description**: the employer's list of what they want.
- **Keywords**: important terms from the job description.
- **Applicant tracking system (ATS)**: software that screens CVs.

## Card: Read the job description closely
image: jobDescriptionKeywordsHero
The **job description** is your blueprint. It tells you exactly what skills and experience the employer values — so mirror them. Tailoring means highlighting the parts of your experience most relevant to *this* role, and using the same language they use. You're showing, point by point, that you're what they asked for.

## Card: Keywords and the ATS
Many employers use an **applicant tracking system (ATS)** — software that scans CVs for relevant **keywords** before a human ever sees them. If the job description emphasizes certain skills or tools, make sure those exact terms appear in your CV (where true). Missing keywords can get a qualified candidate filtered out before review.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: sending the same generic CV to every job; ignoring the job description's language; stuffing in keywords dishonestly or nonsensically (which reads badly and backfires); and not highlighting the most relevant experience for each role. Tailor genuinely — match real experience to the role, in the employer's own words.

## Card: Quick recap
Tailor your CV to each job by mirroring the job description: highlight your most relevant experience and use the employer's own keywords (which also helps pass ATS screening software). Avoid generic CVs and dishonest keyword-stuffing. Show, point by point, that you're the candidate they described.
