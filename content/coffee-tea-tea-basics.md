## Card: What it is
image: teaBasicsHero
Tea comes in a few main types — **green**, **black**, **white**, **oolong**, and the **herbal** infusions — each with its own flavor and ideal brewing. Knowing the differences and how to steep each properly is the key to a good cup, and to avoiding the bitter, stewed tea many people accidentally make.

## Card: Why it matters
A surprising amount of "I don't like tea" comes from badly brewed tea — water too hot or steeped too long, turning it bitter. Understanding the types and their brewing brings out their real character. It's a small bit of knowledge that can transform a daily drink and open up a whole range of flavors.

## Card: Key terms
- **Oxidation**: the reaction that turns green tea leaves dark, creating black tea.
- **Steeping time**: how long leaves sit in hot water.
- **Water temperature**: hotter for black tea, cooler for green.
- **Herbal infusion**: a "tea" from other plants, naturally caffeine-free.

## Card: The main types
The true teas differ by how much the leaves **oxidize**: **green** (little oxidation, light and fresh), **black** (fully oxidized, strong and bold), **white** (minimally processed, delicate), and **oolong** (in between). **Herbal infusions** (mint, chamomile, fruit) aren't true tea and are usually naturally caffeine-free — good for evenings.

## Card: Brewing each well
image: teaSteepControl
The two levers are **water temperature** and **steeping time**. Delicate teas like green and white want *cooler* water (boiling water makes them bitter) and a shorter steep. Black tea handles hotter water and a longer steep. Over-steeping is the classic mistake — leaving the bag in too long makes most teas bitter and astringent.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: pouring boiling water on green tea (use cooler water to avoid bitterness); over-steeping until tea turns bitter; reusing water that's been boiled repeatedly (it tastes flat); and assuming all tea is brewed the same way. Matching temperature and time to the tea type makes a dramatic difference.

## Card: Quick recap
Tea types differ by oxidation: green (light), black (bold), white (delicate), oolong (between), plus caffeine-free herbal infusions. Brew well by matching water temperature and steeping time — cooler and shorter for green and white, hotter and longer for black. Avoid over-steeping, the main cause of bitter tea.
