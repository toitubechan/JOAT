## Card: What it is
image: futuretech3dPrintingHero
**3D printing** builds physical objects layer by layer from a digital design, instead of carving them from a block or molding them. A 3D printer adds material gradually — building up an object from the ground up. It lets people make custom physical objects from a computer file, transforming manufacturing, prototyping, and even hobbies.

## Card: Why it matters
3D printing is reshaping how things are made — enabling rapid prototyping, custom and on-demand manufacturing, and putting some production into people's hands. Understanding it reveals a shift in manufacturing and creativity, and helps you grasp its real (and sometimes overhyped) potential across fields from medicine to engineering to home use.

## Card: Key terms
- **3D printing**: building objects layer by layer from a digital design.
- **Additive manufacturing**: the technical term (adding material).
- **Prototype**: an early model used to test a design.
- **Digital design**: the computer file the printer builds from.

## Card: How it works
image: futuretech3dPrintingHowItWorksHero
3D printing is also called **additive manufacturing** because it *adds* material to build an object, rather than cutting it away. Starting from a **digital design** (a 3D computer file), the printer lays down material — often melted plastic, but also metal, resin, and more — in thin layers, one on top of another, gradually building up the full object. This layer-by-layer approach can create complex shapes that traditional methods struggle with.

## Card: What it's used for
3D printing shines for **prototypes** (quickly making and testing a design before mass production), custom one-off items (tailored medical implants, replacement parts, personalized products), and small-scale or on-demand production. It's used in engineering, medicine, aerospace, art, and increasingly homes and small businesses. It's especially valuable when you need custom, complex, or low-volume objects — though it's not (yet) ideal for cheap mass production of simple items.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking 3D printers can instantly make anything (they're limited by materials, size, speed, and design); imagining it will replace all manufacturing (it excels at custom, complex, low-volume items, not cheap mass production); not knowing it builds layer by layer; and over-hyping it. 3D printing is a transformative but specialized tool with real strengths and limits.

## Card: Quick recap
3D printing (additive manufacturing) builds objects layer by layer from a digital design, adding material rather than cutting it away. It excels at prototypes, custom one-off items, and complex shapes that traditional methods struggle with — used across engineering, medicine, and homes. It's transformative but specialized, best for custom, complex, low-volume objects rather than cheap mass production of simple items.
