## Card: What it is
image: energyFossilFuelsHero
**Fossil fuels** — coal, oil, and natural gas — are energy sources formed from the remains of ancient living things buried for millions of years. Burning them releases stored energy, which has powered the modern world. They remain the dominant global energy source, though their downsides are driving a shift away from them.

## Card: Why it matters
Fossil fuels still provide most of the world's energy — for electricity, transport, heating, and industry. They also drive major issues like climate change and air pollution. Understanding what they are and why they're both so useful and so problematic is key to understanding energy, the economy, and climate debates.

## Card: Key terms
- **Fossil fuels**: coal, oil, gas formed from ancient organisms.
- **Non-renewable**: finite; they run out and don't replenish.
- **Combustion**: burning fuel to release energy.
- **Emissions**: gases released by burning, including CO₂.

## Card: Where they came from and why we use them
image: energyFossilFuelsWhereTheyCameFromAndHero
Fossil fuels formed over millions of years from buried ancient plants and organisms, concentrating energy from the sun. We use them because they pack a lot of energy, are (or were) cheap and abundant, and are easy to transport and burn. **Combustion** releases their stored energy as heat, which we use directly or to generate electricity. This made industrialization and modern life possible.

## Card: Why they're problematic
Fossil fuels have two big problems. They're **non-renewable** — finite resources that took millions of years to form and will eventually run out. And burning them produces **emissions**, especially carbon dioxide, the main driver of climate change, plus air pollution that harms health. These downsides are why the world is working to transition toward cleaner energy sources.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking fossil fuels are unlimited (they're finite); not realizing how dominant they still are in the global energy mix; conflating the three (coal, oil, and gas differ in uses and emissions); and assuming a switch away from them is simple (it's a massive, complex transition). They're powerful but finite and polluting.

## Card: Quick recap
Fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas — formed from ancient organisms over millions of years and store lots of energy, which we release by burning. They powered the modern world and still dominate global energy. But they're non-renewable (finite) and their emissions, especially CO₂, drive climate change — which is why the world is transitioning toward cleaner energy.
