## Card: What it is
image: naturalWondersHero
**Natural wonders** are Earth's most spectacular geological features — vast canyons, towering waterfalls, immense reefs, dramatic rock formations, and more. Each is the result of natural processes (erosion, plate movement, water, time) working on a grand scale. They showcase the planet's power and beauty, and the science behind them makes them even more awe-inspiring.

## Card: Why it matters
Natural wonders inspire awe and travel, but they're also dramatic illustrations of the geological processes covered in this topic — erosion, tectonics, water, and time made visible at grand scale. Understanding how they formed turns sightseeing into understanding, and reveals that even the most jaw-dropping features arise from the same processes shaping the whole planet.

## Card: Key terms
- **Natural wonder**: a spectacular natural feature.
- **Canyon**: a deep valley carved by a river.
- **Erosion at scale**: wearing-away processes over vast time.
- **Geological time**: the immense timescales of Earth processes.

## Card: Wonders carved by water and time
Many natural wonders show **erosion at scale**. Great **canyons** are carved by rivers slowly cutting down through rock over millions of years, exposing layers of Earth's history. Spectacular waterfalls form where rivers meet a sudden drop in resistant rock. Striking rock arches, pillars, and formations are sculpted by wind and water patiently wearing away softer rock. Water plus time is one of Earth's greatest sculptors.

## Card: Wonders built by Earth's forces
image: earthForcesWondersHero
Other wonders are *built up* rather than carved. Towering mountain ranges rise from plate collisions; volcanic islands and dramatic peaks emerge from eruptions; vast coral reefs are built by countless tiny organisms over ages. Each wonder is a visible record of the forces — tectonic, volcanic, biological, erosional — covered in earth science, operating over **geological time** to produce something breathtaking.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking natural wonders are static and unchanging (they're still slowly forming and eroding); imagining they appeared quickly (most took millions of years); seeing them as mere scenery rather than science made visible; and not connecting them to the everyday geological processes that built them. Each wonder is dynamic geology, frozen at a moment in vast time.

## Card: Quick recap
Earth's natural wonders — canyons, waterfalls, rock formations, reefs, mountain ranges — are spectacular results of geological processes at grand scale. Some are carved by water and erosion over millions of years; others are built up by plate movement, volcanoes, or living organisms. Each is dynamic geology made visible, still slowly changing. Understanding them turns awe into deeper wonder.
