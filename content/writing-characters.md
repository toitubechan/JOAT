## Card: What it is
image: charactersHero
**Building characters** is creating the people who populate your story — making them feel real, distinct, and worth caring about. Compelling characters are often what readers remember most and what makes a story come alive. Understanding how to build them helps you create people, not cardboard cutouts. It's one of the most important creative writing skills.

## Card: Why it matters
Readers connect with stories through characters — we care about *people*, not just plots. Flat, lifeless characters sink even a clever plot, while vivid, relatable ones carry a story. Understanding how to build characters who feel real and matter is central to engaging writing. It's often what readers remember long after they forget the plot.

## Card: Key terms
- **Character**: a person in your story.
- **Motivation**: what a character wants and why.
- **Flaws and depth**: imperfections that make characters real.
- **Showing character**: revealing who someone is through action.

## Card: Give them wants and flaws
image: wantsFlawsHero
Compelling characters need two things especially. **Motivation** — they must *want* something (a goal, desire, or need), which drives their actions and engages readers (we follow characters pursuing something). And **flaws and depth** — perfect characters are boring and unrelatable; real people have weaknesses, contradictions, and complexity. Giving characters genuine desires *and* imperfections makes them feel human and interesting, rather than flat or idealized. Wants plus flaws bring characters to life.

## Card: Show, don't just describe
A key technique: reveal character through **showing** — their actions, choices, words, and reactions — rather than just telling the reader "she was brave" or "he was kind." We learn who real people are by what they *do*, and the same is true in fiction. Let characters reveal themselves through how they behave, especially under pressure or when making choices. This makes characters feel alive and lets readers discover them, which is far more powerful than flat description.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: creating flat characters with no clear wants or motivation; making characters perfect (boring and unrelatable — real people have flaws); telling readers what a character is like instead of showing it through action; and making everyone sound and act the same (characters should be distinct). Build characters with genuine motivations and flaws, revealed through what they do — that's what makes them feel real and worth caring about.

## Card: Quick recap
Building characters means creating people who feel real and worth caring about — often what readers remember most. Give them clear motivations (they must *want* something, driving the story) and flaws and depth (perfect characters are boring; imperfections make them human). Reveal who they are by *showing* their actions and choices, not just describing them. Distinct, motivated, flawed characters revealed through action bring a story to life.
