## Card: What it is
image: govtSeparationOfPowersHero
The **separation of powers** is the principle of dividing government into separate branches — typically those that *make* laws, *enforce* laws, and *interpret* laws — so no single part holds all the power. It's a cornerstone of many democracies, designed to prevent abuse of power. Understanding it reveals a key safeguard built into how governments are structured. This is a neutral, educational overview.

## Card: Why it matters
The separation of powers is one of the most important ideas in how free societies are governed — it's a core defense against tyranny and abuse. Understanding it helps you grasp how governments are structured, why power is divided, and how systems try to keep any one part from becoming too powerful. It's foundational to understanding modern government.

## Card: Key terms
- **Separation of powers**: dividing government into separate branches.
- **Legislative**: the branch that makes laws.
- **Executive**: the branch that enforces laws and runs government.
- **Judicial**: the branch that interprets laws (courts).

## Card: The three branches
image: govtSeparationOfPowersTheThreeBranchesHero
Many governments divide power into three branches: the **legislative** (makes the laws — like a parliament or congress), the **executive** (enforces the laws and runs day-to-day government — like a president or prime minister and their administration), and the **judicial** (interprets the laws and settles disputes — the courts). Each has its own role and powers, so that law-making, enforcing, and judging are handled by different parts of government rather than one.

## Card: Checks and balances
The reason for separating powers is to create **checks and balances** — each branch can limit and check the others, preventing any one from becoming too powerful or abusing its authority. For example, courts can rule actions unlawful, and law-makers and the executive constrain each other. This division and mutual checking is a deliberate safeguard: it makes government slower and more constrained, but far harder for power to be concentrated and abused. It's a defense against tyranny.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking dividing power makes government inefficient with no benefit (the trade-off is a crucial safeguard against abuse); not knowing the three branches' distinct roles; imagining the branches are fully independent (they're separate but deliberately check each other); and assuming all governments separate powers (it varies). The separation of powers divides government to prevent any one part holding all the power.

## Card: Quick recap
The separation of powers divides government into branches with distinct roles — legislative (makes laws), executive (enforces them), and judicial (interprets them) — so no single part holds all power. Through checks and balances, each branch limits the others, preventing concentration and abuse of power. It's a deliberate safeguard against tyranny, and a cornerstone of how many free societies are governed.
