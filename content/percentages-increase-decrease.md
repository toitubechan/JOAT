## Card: What it is
image: dataPercentagesIncreaseDecreaseHero
**Percentage increase and decrease** describe how much something has gone up or down, in percentage terms — like a 20% price rise or a 30% discount. These appear constantly in prices, statistics, and news. Understanding them, and a few of their tricky quirks, helps you read changes accurately.

## Card: Why it matters
Percentage changes are everywhere — sales, pay rises, inflation, statistics. They're also often misunderstood or used misleadingly. Knowing how they work helps you judge discounts, understand the news, and avoid being fooled by some genuinely counterintuitive percentage quirks that trip up many people.

## Card: Key terms
- **Percentage increase**: how much something went up, in percent.
- **Percentage decrease**: how much something went down, in percent.
- **Original value**: the starting amount a change is measured from.
- **Percentage points**: a different measure from percentage change.

## Card: How changes work
image: dataPercentagesIncreaseDecreaseHowChangesWorkHero
A **percentage increase** is the rise as a percent of the **original value**: if something goes from 100 to 120, that's a 20% increase. A **decrease** works the same downward: 100 to 80 is a 20% decrease. The key is that the percentage is always *of the original* — a discount of 25% off 80 is 20 off, giving 60. Always anchor the percentage to the starting amount.

## Card: The tricky quirks
Percentage changes have counterintuitive quirks. A 50% *decrease* followed by a 50% *increase* does *not* return you to the start (100 → 50 → 75), because each percentage applies to a different base. Also beware "**percentage points**": if interest rises from 2% to 4%, that's a *2 percentage point* rise but a *100% increase* — very different statements. These distinctions are often used misleadingly.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a percentage rise then an equal percentage fall cancels out (it doesn't); confusing "percentage points" with "percent change" (a common way statistics mislead); forgetting the percentage is always of the *original* value; and being fooled by changes stated to sound bigger or smaller than they are. Knowing these quirks protects you from being misled.

## Card: Quick recap
Percentage increase/decrease measures change as a percent of the original value (100 to 120 is +20%). Watch the quirks: an equal percentage rise and fall don't cancel out (different bases), and "percentage points" differ from "percent change." These distinctions are often used misleadingly, so understanding them helps you read changes accurately and avoid being fooled.
