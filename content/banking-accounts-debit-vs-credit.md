## Card: What it is
image: debitCreditHero
A **debit card** spends money you already have — it pulls straight from your bank account. A **credit card** spends the bank's money up to a limit, which you pay back later. They look identical and tap the same way, but behind the scenes they do opposite things: one uses your cash, the other borrows.

## Card: Why it matters
Mixing them up is easy and costly. A debit card can't put you in debt but offers fewer protections. A credit card offers strong protections and perks but can quietly pile up debt if you don't pay the full balance. Knowing which is which helps you pick the right one for each purchase.

## Card: Key terms
- **Debit card**: spends money from your own account instantly.
- **Credit card**: borrows from the bank, repaid later.
- **Credit limit**: the most you can borrow on the card.
- **Statement balance**: what you owe the card at the end of a cycle.

## Card: How a debit card works
When you tap a **debit card**, the money leaves your account almost immediately. You can only spend what you have, which makes overspending hard. The trade-off: if your card details are stolen, thieves are spending *your* real cash, and getting it back can take longer than with credit.

## Card: How a credit card works
image: creditCardCycleHero
A **credit card** lets you spend the bank's money up to your **credit limit**. Each month you get a bill. Pay the full **statement balance** and you owe no interest — it's basically a free short-term loan. Pay only part, and interest starts on the rest, often at a high rate. The card is only "free" if you clear it fully.

## Card: Common mistakes
The classic mistake is treating a credit card like free money and paying only the minimum, letting interest snowball. Another is avoiding credit cards entirely and missing out on fraud protection and building a credit history. The sweet spot: use credit for protection and convenience, then pay it off in full every month.

## Card: Quick recap
Debit spends your own money instantly; credit borrows the bank's to repay later. Credit offers better protection and perks but charges interest if you don't pay in full. Use whichever fits — just clear any credit balance every month so borrowing stays free.
