## Card: What it is
image: philosophyFreeWillHero
**Free will** is the question of whether we genuinely make free choices, or whether our actions are determined by prior causes (our biology, environment, and the chain of events leading up to them). It's one of philosophy's oldest and most fascinating debates, touching on responsibility, identity, and what it means to be human. There's no settled answer — only deep, ongoing discussion.

## Card: Why it matters
Free will underlies how we think about responsibility, blame, praise, justice, and our sense of self. If we don't truly choose freely, what does that mean for holding people accountable? Engaging with the question deepens your thinking about human nature and morality. It's a genuinely mind-bending topic that connects philosophy to everyday ideas of responsibility.

## Card: Key terms
- **Free will**: the ability to make genuinely free choices.
- **Determinism**: the view that all events are caused by prior events.
- **Responsibility**: being accountable for one's actions.
- **The debate**: the unresolved tension between free will and determinism.

## Card: The core tension
image: philosophyFreeWillTheCoreTensionHero
The puzzle: everything seems to have causes. Your choices come from your brain, shaped by genetics, upbringing, and circumstances — all caused by prior events. This is **determinism** — the view that every event, including your decisions, is determined by what came before. But it *feels* like we choose freely. So which is it? Are our choices genuinely free, or just the inevitable result of prior causes we didn't choose? This tension is the heart of **the debate**.

## Card: Different positions
Philosophers take various views. Some argue free will is an illusion (everything is determined). Others insist we genuinely have free will. And many take a middle path — arguing free will and determinism can be *compatible* (perhaps "free" means acting according to your own desires and reasoning, even if those have causes). The question also ties to **responsibility**: most people feel we *should* hold others accountable, which seems to assume some kind of free will. There's no consensus — it remains genuinely open.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the question has an obvious or settled answer (it's genuinely unresolved); assuming it's purely abstract (it bears on responsibility and justice); treating it as either-or without knowing the compatibilist middle ground; and dismissing it as unanswerable nonsense (it's a serious, deep question). Free will is a profound, open debate worth pondering, not a solved problem.

## Card: Quick recap
Free will asks whether we truly make free choices or whether our actions are determined by prior causes (biology, upbringing, circumstances) — the tension between free will and determinism. Positions range from "free will is an illusion" to "we genuinely choose freely" to compatibilist middle grounds. It bears deeply on responsibility and justice. There's no settled answer — it's a profound, ongoing philosophical debate.
