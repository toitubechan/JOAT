## Card: What it is
image: howworksShipsFloatHero
**Floating** seems puzzling: a small steel bolt sinks, yet a giant steel ship floats. The answer is **buoyancy** and how the ship's shape displaces water. Floating isn't about being light — it's about displacing enough water to support the object's weight. Understanding this resolves one of the most common everyday physics puzzles.

## Card: Why it matters
The question "why do heavy ships float?" puzzles almost everyone. Understanding buoyancy explains it elegantly, and the same principle explains why some things float and others sink, how submarines work, and more. It's a satisfying example of physics solving an everyday mystery.

## Card: Key terms
- **Buoyancy**: the upward force water exerts on an object.
- **Displacement**: the water pushed aside by an object.
- **Density**: how much mass is packed into a volume.
- **Average density**: the overall density of an object including hollow spaces.

## Card: Buoyancy and displacement
image: howworksShipsFloatBuoyancyAndDisplacementHero
When an object enters water, it pushes water aside (**displacement**), and the water pushes back up with a force called **buoyancy**. If the buoyancy (upward force) equals the object's weight, it floats; if the object is too dense, it sinks. The key: buoyancy depends on how much water is *displaced* — so an object's *shape*, not just its weight, determines whether it floats.

## Card: Why a steel ship floats
A solid steel bolt sinks because it's dense and displaces little water. But a steel ship is *hollow* — shaped to enclose a huge volume of air. This makes its **average density** (steel plus the air inside) less than water. Spread over its large shape, the ship displaces a huge amount of water — enough to generate buoyancy equal to its enormous weight. So the shape, enclosing air and displacing lots of water, is what keeps it afloat.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking floating is about being lightweight (it's about displacing enough water relative to weight); not understanding shape matters (a steel ball sinks, but the same steel shaped as a hull floats); confusing density of the material with the average density of the whole object including air; and finding it mysterious when buoyancy explains it. Shape and displacement, not lightness, decide floating.

## Card: Quick recap
Ships float due to buoyancy: water pushes up on submerged objects with a force equal to the weight of water displaced. A solid steel bolt sinks (dense, displaces little), but a hollow steel ship encloses air, lowering its average density and displacing a huge amount of water — generating enough buoyancy to support its weight. Floating is about shape and displacement, not being lightweight.
