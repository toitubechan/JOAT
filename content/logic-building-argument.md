## Card: What it is
image: logicBuildingArgumentHero
A **solid argument** is one where a clear conclusion is genuinely supported by good reasons and evidence. Building one is the flip side of spotting fallacies — constructing reasoning that actually holds up. It's a core skill for thinking clearly, persuading honestly, and making well-founded decisions.

## Card: Why it matters
Whether you're making a case, deciding something, or just thinking through a problem, the ability to build sound arguments is invaluable. It helps you reason rigorously, persuade with integrity, and recognize when your own thinking is weak. Strong argumentation underpins clear thinking and honest, effective communication.

## Card: Key terms
- **Argument**: a conclusion supported by reasons.
- **Premise**: a reason or piece of evidence supporting a conclusion.
- **Conclusion**: the claim the argument aims to establish.
- **Valid reasoning**: the conclusion genuinely follows from the premises.

## Card: The structure of an argument
image: logicBuildingArgumentTheStructureOfAnArgumentHero
An **argument** has two parts: **premises** (the reasons or evidence) and a **conclusion** (the claim they support). A solid argument needs both *true premises* (the reasons are actually correct) and **valid reasoning** (the conclusion genuinely follows from them). Both matter: true reasons with broken logic fail, and perfect logic from false reasons also fails. A strong argument has good reasons *and* sound logic connecting them to the conclusion.

## Card: Building one well
To build a solid argument: state your **conclusion** clearly; support it with relevant, true, well-evidenced **premises**; ensure the logic actually connects them (no fallacies); and address obvious counterarguments rather than ignoring them. Honesty matters too — a genuinely solid argument can withstand scrutiny. The aim isn't to "win" by any means, but to reach and support a well-founded conclusion through sound reasoning.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: stating conclusions without supporting reasons (just asserting); using irrelevant or false premises; assuming logic is fine without checking it follows; ignoring counterarguments; and trying to "win" rather than reason soundly. A solid argument needs true reasons *and* valid logic connecting them to a clear conclusion — and the willingness to face objections honestly.

## Card: Quick recap
A solid argument supports a clear conclusion with good reasons (premises) through valid reasoning. It needs both true premises *and* logic that genuinely connects them to the conclusion — either alone isn't enough. Build one by stating your conclusion clearly, supporting it with true, relevant evidence, ensuring the logic holds, and honestly addressing counterarguments. It's the foundation of clear thinking and honest persuasion.
