## Card: What it is
image: commonBiasesHero
There are many cognitive biases, but a handful show up constantly in everyday thinking. This card set introduces the most common ones — **confirmation bias**, **anchoring**, and a few others — so you can start recognizing them in yourself and others. Knowing their names makes them easier to spot.

## Card: Why it matters
A few biases account for a huge share of everyday misjudgments. Learning to recognize these common ones is the most practical step in thinking more clearly. Once you can name "confirmation bias" or "anchoring," you'll start noticing them everywhere — in arguments, shopping, news, and your own decisions.

## Card: Key terms
- **Confirmation bias**: favoring information that fits your beliefs.
- **Anchoring**: over-relying on the first piece of information.
- **Availability bias**: judging by what comes to mind easily.
- **Bandwagon effect**: believing something because others do.

## Card: Confirmation bias and anchoring
image: confirmationAnchoringHero
**Confirmation bias** is our tendency to seek, notice, and favor information that *confirms* what we already believe, while ignoring what contradicts it. **Anchoring** is over-relying on the first number or fact we hear — an initial price "anchors" our sense of value. These two are everywhere: in how we form opinions, judge prices, and interpret evidence.

## Card: Availability and bandwagon
**Availability bias** is judging how likely or important something is by how easily examples come to mind — so vivid, recent, or scary events (like plane crashes) feel more common than they are. The **bandwagon effect** is believing or doing something because many others do, regardless of evidence. Both lead us to misjudge reality based on what's salient or popular rather than what's true.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes driven by these: only consuming news that confirms your views (confirmation bias); being swayed by an inflated "original price" (anchoring); fearing rare vivid risks while ignoring common ones (availability); and adopting beliefs just because they're popular (bandwagon). Naming the bias as it happens is the key to catching it.

## Card: Quick recap
Common biases include confirmation bias (favoring info that fits your beliefs), anchoring (over-relying on the first information), availability bias (judging by what easily comes to mind), and the bandwagon effect (believing what others do). These account for much everyday misjudgment. Learning their names helps you spot them in yourself and others.
