## Card: What it is
image: chocolateWorkHero
**Chocolate work** is using chocolate in baking and desserts — melting it, and sometimes "tempering" it for a glossy, snappy finish. Chocolate is beloved but surprisingly finicky: it can seize, burn, or turn dull if mishandled. Understanding how chocolate behaves helps you work with it successfully and avoid common frustrations. It's a rewarding ingredient once you understand its quirks.

## Card: Why it matters
Chocolate is one of the most loved ingredients, but working with it trips up many people (melted chocolate that seizes into a grainy mess, or sets dull and streaky). Understanding how chocolate behaves — and the basic techniques — lets you use it confidently for everything from simple melting to coatings. It demystifies a finicky but delightful ingredient.

## Card: Key terms
- **Melting**: gently liquefying chocolate.
- **Seizing**: chocolate turning grainy and stiff (a common failure).
- **Tempering**: a technique for glossy, snappy chocolate.
- **Gentle heat**: the key to handling chocolate.

## Card: Melting without disaster
The most common chocolate task is **melting**, and the key is **gentle heat**. Chocolate is sensitive: too much heat burns it (ruining the flavor), and — surprisingly — a small amount of water can cause it to **seize**, turning suddenly from smooth to a grainy, stiff mess. So melt chocolate gently and slowly, and keep water away from it (unless a recipe deliberately includes liquid). Understanding these two pitfalls — overheating and water causing seizing — prevents most chocolate-melting disasters.

## Card: Tempering for a perfect finish
image: chocolateTemperingHero
For chocolate that's glossy, firm, and snaps cleanly (like good chocolate bars or coatings), there's a technique called **tempering** — carefully controlling the temperature as chocolate melts and cools so it sets correctly. Untempered melted chocolate can set dull, soft, or streaky. Tempering is more advanced and not needed for everything (it's unnecessary if you're just mixing chocolate into a batter), but it's why professionally finished chocolate looks and snaps so perfectly. Knowing it exists explains the difference.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: overheating chocolate (burning and ruining it — use gentle heat); letting water get into melting chocolate (causing it to seize into a grainy mess); expecting melted chocolate to set glossy without tempering (it may set dull or streaky); and being frustrated without understanding chocolate's quirks. Gentle heat, keeping water away, and knowing about tempering are the keys to successful chocolate work.

## Card: Quick recap
Chocolate is beloved but finicky. The most common task, melting, requires gentle heat — overheating burns it, and even a little water can make it "seize" into a grainy mess, so keep water away. For a glossy, snappy finish (like coatings or bars), there's a more advanced technique called tempering, controlling temperature so it sets correctly; it's not needed when mixing chocolate into batter. Understanding these quirks makes chocolate work successful.
