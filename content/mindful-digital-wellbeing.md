## Card: What it is
image: digitalWellbeingHero
**Digital wellbeing** is having a healthy, intentional relationship with technology — phones, social media, and screens — so they serve you rather than control you. As digital devices increasingly dominate daily life, managing their effect on our attention, mood, and time matters. Understanding digital wellbeing helps you use technology more mindfully. This is general wellbeing knowledge, not medical advice.

## Card: Why it matters
Technology, especially smartphones and social media, increasingly shapes our attention, time, mood, and relationships — often in ways we don't fully control or even notice. Many feel overwhelmed, distracted, or worse from their digital habits. Understanding digital wellbeing helps you take back control, use technology intentionally, and protect your attention and mental wellbeing in an increasingly digital world. It's an increasingly essential modern skill.

## Card: Key terms
- **Digital wellbeing**: a healthy, intentional relationship with technology.
- **Attention economy**: tech designed to capture and hold your attention.
- **Intentional use**: using technology purposefully, not compulsively.
- **Boundaries**: limits on technology use that protect wellbeing.

## Card: Technology is designed to grab attention
image: attentionHook
A crucial insight: much technology, especially social media and apps, is *deliberately designed* to capture and hold your attention as long as possible (the **attention economy**) — using features engineered to keep you engaged. This means your distraction or compulsive checking isn't simply a personal failing; it's partly by design. Understanding this is empowering: it shifts the question from "why can't I control myself?" to "how do I use this technology intentionally, given it's built to grab my attention?" Awareness is the first step to a healthier relationship.

## Card: Using technology intentionally
**Digital wellbeing** comes from **intentional use** and **boundaries** — using technology purposefully rather than compulsively. Practical approaches: setting boundaries (like limiting certain apps, or tech-free times and spaces); being intentional (asking "why am I picking this up?" rather than reflexively); reducing distractions (notifications, easy access to time-sink apps); and ensuring technology doesn't crowd out sleep, relationships, activity, and presence. The goal isn't necessarily *less* technology for its own sake, but technology that *serves* you rather than controls you.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: blaming yourself entirely for distraction or compulsive use (technology is partly designed to grab attention); using technology reflexively without intention; not setting any boundaries; and letting screens crowd out sleep, relationships, and presence. Digital wellbeing comes from awareness (tech is designed to capture attention), intentional use, and boundaries — making technology serve you rather than control you.

## Card: Quick recap
Digital wellbeing is a healthy, intentional relationship with technology so it serves you rather than controls you. A key insight: much tech (especially social media) is deliberately designed to capture your attention (the attention economy), so distraction isn't purely a personal failing. The path is intentional use and boundaries — using tech purposefully, setting limits and tech-free times, reducing distractions, and not letting screens crowd out sleep, relationships, and presence.
