## Card: What it is
image: cakesSpongesHero
**Cakes and sponges** are sweet baked goods prized for being light, tender, and moist. Achieving that lightness depends on understanding how cakes work — how air and leavening create a soft texture, and how ingredients balance. Understanding the basics helps you bake better cakes and know why they sometimes go wrong (dense, dry, or collapsed).

## Card: Why it matters
Cakes are beloved treats, but baking a good one — light, moist, and tender — can be frustratingly hit-or-miss without understanding the principles. Knowing how cakes achieve their texture, and what causes common failures, makes you a more reliable baker. It demystifies why a cake turns out perfectly or disappointingly, turning luck into understanding.

## Card: Key terms
- **Sponge**: a light cake leavened largely by air.
- **Aeration**: incorporating air for lightness.
- **Leavening**: agents and air that make cakes rise.
- **Balance of ingredients**: the ratios that give good texture.

## Card: Lightness from air and leavening
image: cakeAirLeaveningHero
The key to a good cake's light texture is getting air and gas into the batter. **Aeration** — beating air into the mixture (often by creaming butter and sugar, or whisking eggs) — creates tiny air pockets. **Leavening** agents (like baking powder) produce gas that, with the trapped air, expands in the oven to make the cake rise and become light. A **sponge** especially relies on whisked-in air for its lightness. So the airy texture comes from incorporating and then expanding air and gas — get this right, and the cake is light.

## Card: Balance and why cakes fail
A good cake depends on the **balance of ingredients** — flour (structure), fat (tenderness and moisture), sugar (sweetness, moisture, browning), eggs (binding, richness, aeration), and leavening (rise) — in the right ratios. This is why cake recipes need precision: imbalance causes failures. Common problems: too dense (not enough aeration/leavening, or overmixing knocking out air), dry (overbaked or wrong ratios), or collapsed (too much leavening, or disturbed while baking). Understanding the balance explains and prevents these.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: not aerating enough (dense cake); overmixing (knocking out the air, causing toughness or density); imbalanced ingredients or imprecise measuring (since ratios matter); overbaking (dry cake); and opening the oven or moving the cake mid-bake (can cause collapse). A good cake comes from proper aeration, balanced ingredients, and careful baking — understanding these prevents the common failures.

## Card: Quick recap
Cakes and sponges get their light, tender texture from air and leavening — aeration (beating air in, e.g. creaming butter and sugar or whisking eggs) plus leavening gas, which expand in the oven. A good cake also depends on balanced ingredients (flour, fat, sugar, eggs, leavening) in the right ratios, which is why precision matters. Common failures — dense, dry, collapsed — come from poor aeration, imbalance, overmixing, or overbaking.
