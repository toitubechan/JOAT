## Card: What it is
image: dataProbabilityRiskInRealLifeHero
**Risk** is probability applied to things that matter — the chance of something good or bad happening. We constantly judge risk: health, money, safety, decisions. But human intuition about risk is systematically flawed. Understanding how helps you assess real risks more sensibly and worry about the right things.

## Card: Why it matters
We often fear the wrong things — dreading rare dramatic risks while ignoring common mundane ones. This leads to poor decisions about health, safety, and money. Understanding how we misjudge risk helps you allocate your worry and caution where they actually matter, making you both safer and calmer.

## Card: Key terms
- **Risk**: the probability and impact of something happening.
- **Relative vs. absolute risk**: change in risk vs. the actual chance.
- **Perceived risk**: how risky something feels.
- **Availability bias**: judging risk by how easily examples come to mind.

## Card: Feeling vs. reality
image: dataProbabilityRiskInRealLifeFeelingVsRealityHero
Our **perceived risk** often doesn't match the actual probability. Thanks to **availability bias**, vivid, dramatic, or recent events (plane crashes, shark attacks) feel far more likely than they are, while common quiet risks (car accidents, poor diet) feel less threatening despite being statistically far more dangerous. We fear the memorable, not the probable. Recognizing this mismatch is the first step to judging risk sensibly.

## Card: Relative vs. absolute risk
A key distinction often used to mislead: **relative** versus **absolute** risk. "This doubles your risk!" (relative) sounds alarming — but if the original risk was 1 in a million, doubling it to 2 in a million is still tiny (absolute). Headlines love relative risk because it sounds dramatic. Always ask for the *absolute* numbers: doubled from what, to what? This defuses many scary-sounding risk claims.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: fearing rare dramatic risks while ignoring common ones (availability bias); being alarmed by "relative risk" without the absolute numbers; treating any risk as either zero or certain (most are in between); and trusting gut feelings over actual probabilities. The fix: ask for absolute numbers, and check whether your fear matches the real likelihood.

## Card: Quick recap
Risk is probability applied to what matters, and our intuition about it is systematically flawed — we fear vivid, rare events (availability bias) over common ones. Watch the relative-versus-absolute trap: "doubles your risk" means little without the absolute numbers. Ask "from what, to what?" and check whether your fear matches the real likelihood. Judge risk by probability, not drama.
