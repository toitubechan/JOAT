## Card: What it is
image: financeCreditInterestAndAprHero
When you borrow, you pay back more than you took — that extra is **interest**, the price of the loan. **APR** (Annual Percentage Rate) bundles the interest *plus* certain fees into one yearly percentage, so you can compare loans fairly. Think of APR as the true sticker price of borrowing money for a year.

## Card: Why it matters
Two loans can look similar but cost wildly different amounts once you see the APR. Lenders often advertise a low monthly payment to seem cheap, while the real cost hides in the rate and the loan length. Knowing how to read APR lets you compare offers like for like and avoid overpaying.

## Card: Key terms
- **Interest**: the fee charged for borrowing.
- **APR**: yearly cost of a loan including interest and certain fees.
- **Principal**: the original amount borrowed.
- **Term**: how long you take to repay the loan.

## Card: APR vs. interest rate
image: financeCreditInterestAndAprAprVsInterestRateHero
The plain **interest rate** is just the cost of the borrowed money. **APR** is broader — it folds in mandatory fees, so it's usually a truer picture of cost. When comparing two loans, compare APRs, not just interest rates or monthly payments. A lower APR almost always means a cheaper loan overall.

## Card: How the term changes cost
A longer **term** lowers your monthly payment but raises the total interest you pay, because you're borrowing for longer. A €10,000 loan over 5 years costs far more in interest than over 2 years, even at the same rate. Cheaper monthly, pricier overall — that trade-off is the heart of every loan decision.

## Card: Common mistakes
The classic trap is shopping by monthly payment alone — a low payment can hide a long term and huge total interest. Another is ignoring fees and looking only at the headline interest rate; APR exists precisely to catch those. And many forget that a longer loan means more total cost, even when each payment feels easy.

## Card: Quick recap
Interest is the price of borrowing; APR rolls in fees to show the true yearly cost, so compare loans by APR. A longer term shrinks the monthly payment but grows the total you pay. Look past the monthly number to the rate and the term — that's where the real cost lives.
