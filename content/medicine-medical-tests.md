## Card: What it is
image: medicineMedicalTestsHero
**Medical tests** — blood tests, scans, screenings, and more — help doctors detect, diagnose, and monitor health conditions. Understanding the basics of what they do, and their limits, helps you make sense of your own healthcare. (General educational overview; interpreting your specific results is a job for your doctor.)

## Card: Why it matters
Tests are central to modern medicine, yet patients often misunderstand them — over-trusting or over-worrying about results. Understanding what tests can and can't tell you, including that no test is perfect, helps you engage with your healthcare more calmly and sensibly. It demystifies a routine but often anxiety-inducing part of medicine.

## Card: Key terms
- **Diagnostic test**: a test to identify a condition.
- **Screening**: testing for disease before symptoms appear.
- **False positive / negative**: a wrong test result.
- **Reference range**: the "normal" range for a result.

## Card: What tests do
image: medicineMedicalTestsWhatTestsDoHero
Medical tests serve different purposes: **diagnostic tests** help identify what's causing symptoms; **screening** tests check for disease *before* symptoms appear (to catch things early); and monitoring tests track a known condition over time. Results are often compared to a **reference range** (the typical "normal" range), but what's normal varies by person and context — which is why a doctor interprets results, not just the numbers alone.

## Card: No test is perfect
A crucial point: no test is 100% accurate. Tests can give a **false positive** (saying you have a condition when you don't) or a **false negative** (missing a condition you do have). This is why doctors interpret tests in context — alongside symptoms, history, and sometimes further tests — rather than relying on a single result. A test result is information to be interpreted, not an absolute verdict.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: treating any test result as a definitive verdict (tests can be wrong, and need interpretation); panicking over an out-of-range number (context matters, and ranges are guides); self-diagnosing from results without a doctor; and assuming a normal result guarantees perfect health. Tests are valuable information, interpreted by a doctor in context — not infallible answers.

## Card: Quick recap
Medical tests (blood tests, scans, screenings) help detect, diagnose, and monitor conditions, often compared to a "normal" reference range. But no test is perfect — false positives and negatives happen — so doctors interpret results in context alongside symptoms and history, not as absolute verdicts. Understanding this helps you engage with healthcare calmly. For your specific results, rely on your doctor.
