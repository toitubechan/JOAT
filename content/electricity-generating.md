## Card: What it is
image: electricityGenerationHero
The electricity in your home is **generated** at power stations and delivered through a vast network. Most generation works on one surprising principle: moving a magnet near a coil of wire produces electricity. Understanding where your power comes from connects the science to the wider world of energy.

## Card: Why it matters
Electricity feels like it just appears from the socket, but it's produced somewhere and travels to you. Understanding generation explains energy debates, why different sources matter (coal, gas, wind, solar, nuclear), and how your daily power connects to bigger questions about climate and resources. It links a science basic to real-world choices.

## Card: Key terms
- **Generator**: a device that turns motion into electricity.
- **Turbine**: a spinning wheel that drives a generator.
- **Renewable**: a source that doesn't run out, like wind or solar.
- **The grid**: the network delivering electricity to homes.

## Card: How most electricity is made
image: generatorMagnetCoil
Most electricity comes from a **generator** — and the core trick is that spinning a magnet near a coil of wire (or vice versa) generates an electric current. Usually a **turbine** is spun to drive the generator. What spins the turbine varies: steam from burning coal or gas, or from nuclear heat, or directly by wind or flowing water. The spinning motion becomes electricity.

## Card: Sources and the grid
Generation sources differ. **Renewables** like wind, solar, and hydro don't run out and produce little carbon; fossil fuels (coal, gas) are finite and emit carbon; nuclear is low-carbon but has other concerns. Solar panels work differently, converting light directly to electricity. From the source, **the grid** — a network of cables and substations — delivers power to homes, often across great distances.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking electricity is "stored" in the wires waiting (it's mostly generated as needed); not knowing most generation comes from spinning turbines; and assuming all sources are equivalent, when they differ greatly in cost, carbon, and sustainability. Knowing the basics clarifies energy and climate discussions.

## Card: Quick recap
Most electricity is generated by spinning a turbine to drive a generator — spinning a magnet near a coil produces current. What spins the turbine (coal, gas, nuclear heat, wind, water) defines the source, while solar converts light directly. The grid then delivers power to homes. Sources differ greatly in carbon and sustainability.
