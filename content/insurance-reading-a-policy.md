## Card: What it is
image: financeInsuranceReadingAPolicyHero
An insurance **policy** is the contract spelling out exactly what you're covered for, what you're not, and the rules for claiming. It's often long and dry — but the few pages that matter decide whether you actually get paid when something goes wrong. Reading them is worth the small effort.

## Card: Why it matters
The worst time to discover what your policy *doesn't* cover is right after a disaster. Insurers pay according to the contract, not your assumptions. A few minutes understanding the key sections upfront can save you a shock — and a denied claim — when you can least afford it.

## Card: Key terms
- **Exclusions**: things the policy specifically does *not* cover.
- **Conditions**: rules you must follow to stay covered.
- **Coverage limit**: the maximum the insurer will pay.
- **Excess / deductible**: your share before the insurer pays.

## Card: Hunt for the exclusions
image: financeInsuranceReadingAPolicyHuntForTheExclusionsHero
The most important section is often the **exclusions** — the list of what's *not* covered. Travel insurance might exclude extreme sports; home insurance might exclude floods. Exclusions are where "I thought I was covered" disasters live. Read them first, because they define the real edges of your protection.

## Card: Check conditions and limits
**Conditions** are rules you must follow or risk losing cover — like fitting smoke alarms or reporting a theft within a set time. **Coverage limits** cap the payout, sometimes per item. Knowing both means you keep your cover valid and aren't shocked by a cap when a big claim arrives.

## Card: Common mistakes
The first mistake is never reading the policy, then assuming everything's covered. The second is ignoring conditions, accidentally voiding cover by breaking a rule you didn't know existed. And many overlook per-item limits, expecting a full payout on a valuable item that's actually capped at a fraction of its worth.

## Card: Quick recap
A policy is the contract that decides if you get paid — so read the parts that matter. Check the exclusions (what's not covered), the conditions (rules you must follow), and the limits (the most they'll pay). Understand these before disaster strikes, not after, when assumptions cost you.
