## Card: What it is
image: financeTaxesTypesOfTaxesHero
You don't pay one big tax — you pay several smaller ones, each on a different activity. The main ones touch your **income**, the things you **buy**, and sometimes what you **own** or **earn from investments**. Knowing the categories makes the whole system far less mysterious.

## Card: Why it matters
Different taxes hit at different moments, so the "real" cost of earning and spending is more than the headline numbers suggest. Recognizing each type helps you understand your payslip, the prices you pay, and why your take-home is less than your gross salary. It demystifies where your money actually goes.

## Card: Key terms
- **Income tax**: tax on money you earn.
- **Sales tax / VAT**: tax added to things you buy.
- **Property tax**: tax on property you own.
- **Capital gains tax**: tax on profit from selling investments.

## Card: Taxes on earning and buying
image: financeTaxesTypesOfTaxesTaxesOnEarningAndBuyingHero
**Income tax** is taken on the money you earn from work, often straight from your paycheck. **Sales tax** or **VAT** (Value Added Tax) is added to the price of goods and services — you pay it at the till, often without noticing. These two are the taxes most people meet most often.

## Card: Taxes on owning and gains
**Property tax** is charged on property you own, like a home, usually yearly. **Capital gains tax** applies to the profit when you sell an investment for more than you paid. There may also be taxes on inheritance or specific goods. Which apply to you depends heavily on your country and circumstances.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common confusion is forgetting that the advertised salary isn't the take-home amount — income tax and other deductions reduce it. Another is not realizing prices may or may not already include sales tax, depending on where you are. And many overlook capital gains tax when selling investments, getting surprised by a bill.

## Card: Quick recap
The main taxes hit earning (income tax), buying (sales tax/VAT), owning (property tax), and investment profit (capital gains). They strike at different moments, which is why take-home and shelf prices differ from headline figures. Exact rules vary by place — check your local system or a professional.
