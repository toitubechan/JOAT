## Card: What it is
image: sleepCyclesHero
Sleep isn't one uniform state — you cycle through different **stages** all night, from light sleep to deep sleep to dreaming. Each cycle lasts roughly 90 minutes and repeats several times. Understanding these stages explains a lot about why and when you feel rested or groggy.

## Card: Why it matters
Each stage does a different job, so you need *complete* cycles, not just hours in bed. Knowing how cycles work explains why waking mid-deep-sleep feels awful, why naps can refresh or wreck you, and why broken sleep leaves you tired even after "enough" hours. It makes sleep less of a mystery.

## Card: Key terms
- **Sleep cycle**: one full run through the stages, about 90 minutes.
- **Light sleep**: the easy-to-wake early stage.
- **Deep sleep**: the most physically restorative stage.
- **REM sleep**: the dreaming, brain-active stage.

## Card: The stages
A cycle moves through **light sleep** (drifting off, easily woken), into **deep sleep** (slow brain waves, hard to wake, the most physically restorative), and then **REM sleep** (Rapid Eye Movement), when most vivid dreaming happens and the brain processes memories and emotions. Then the cycle begins again.

## Card: Why cycles matter
image: sleepCycleTiming
You repeat these cycles four to six times a night. Early cycles have more **deep sleep** (body repair); later ones have more **REM** (mental processing) — so cutting sleep short robs you of REM. Waking at the end of a cycle (in light sleep) feels natural; waking mid-deep-sleep causes that heavy, groggy feeling.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common mistake is judging sleep only by total hours, ignoring whether cycles completed. Another is long late naps that push you into deep sleep, leaving you groggy and harming night sleep — short naps avoid this. And many disrupt their cycles with broken, interrupted sleep yet wonder why they feel unrested.

## Card: Quick recap
Sleep runs in ~90-minute cycles through light, deep, and REM stages, each with a job — deep sleep repairs the body, REM processes the mind. You need complete cycles, not just hours. Cutting sleep short steals REM, and waking mid-deep-sleep feels groggy. Quality and continuity matter as much as quantity.
