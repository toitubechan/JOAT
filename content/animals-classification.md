## Card: What it is
image: animalClassificationHero
**Classification** is how scientists organize the millions of animal species into groups based on their characteristics and evolutionary relationships. It's like a vast filing system for life — sorting animals into nested groups from broad to specific. This system brings order to the staggering diversity of the animal kingdom.

## Card: Why it matters
With millions of species, we need a way to organize and understand them. Classification reveals how animals are related, helps scientists communicate clearly, and shows the structure of life. Understanding the basics helps you make sense of terms like "mammal," "reptile," or "species," and see the connections between creatures.

## Card: Key terms
- **Classification**: organizing living things into groups.
- **Species**: a specific type of organism that can breed together.
- **Vertebrate / invertebrate**: animals with or without a backbone.
- **Nested groups**: broad categories containing narrower ones.

## Card: Nested groups, broad to specific
image: nestedAnimalGroupsHero
Animals are sorted into **nested groups**, from very broad to very specific. Broad groups (like "animals with backbones") contain narrower ones (like "mammals"), which contain narrower still, down to the **species** — a specific type of animal (like a lion). It's like a series of boxes within boxes. The more closely two animals share groups, the more closely related they are.

## Card: A key early split
One of the most basic divisions is **vertebrates** (animals with a backbone — mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, fish) versus **invertebrates** (animals without a backbone — insects, spiders, worms, jellyfish, and far more). Surprisingly, the vast majority of animal species are *invertebrates* (especially insects). Within vertebrates, the familiar groups — mammals, birds, and so on — each share defining features.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking most animals are big vertebrates like mammals (most species are invertebrates, especially insects); confusing classification groups (a whale is a mammal, not a fish, despite living in water); imagining classification is arbitrary (it reflects real evolutionary relationships); and not grasping the nested structure. Classification organizes life by shared features and ancestry.

## Card: Quick recap
Classification organizes the millions of animal species into nested groups, from broad (like vertebrates vs. invertebrates) down to the specific species. Closely related animals share more groups. Most species are actually invertebrates, especially insects, despite our focus on big animals. Classification reflects real evolutionary relationships, bringing order to the animal kingdom's staggering diversity.
