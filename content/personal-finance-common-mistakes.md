## Card: What it is
image: financePersonalCommonMistakesHero
Most money trouble doesn't come from rare disasters — it comes from small, repeated habits. This subtopic gathers the most common traps beginners fall into. The good news: once you can name them, they're surprisingly easy to avoid.

## Card: Why it matters
You learn faster by sidestepping mistakes than by making every one yourself. A few of these habits, left unchecked, can quietly cost you years of progress. Spotting them early means your money grows instead of leaking away through small, invisible cracks.

## Card: Key terms
- **Impulse buying**: unplanned purchases driven by emotion.
- **Minimum payment**: the smallest amount due, which keeps debt alive.
- **No budget**: spending without any plan or tracking.
- **Living paycheck to paycheck**: spending all you earn each month.

## Card: The spending traps
image: financePersonalCommonMistakesTheSpendingTrapsHero
Two big ones: **impulse buying**, where emotion drives unplanned purchases, and **lifestyle inflation**, where every raise gets fully spent. Both feel harmless in the moment. The fix is a small pause before buying — sleep on bigger purchases — and deciding in advance to save part of any income increase.

## Card: The debt and planning traps
Paying only the **minimum payment** on a credit card keeps you in debt for years while interest piles up. Having **no budget** means money vanishes with no record of where. And skipping an emergency fund means every surprise becomes debt. Each trap has a simple counter: pay more than the minimum, track your spending, and keep a cash cushion.

## Card: The mindset traps
Two quiet ones: thinking you'll "start managing money once you earn more" (habits matter more than amounts), and comparing your spending to others' visible lifestyles (which are often funded by debt). Manage the money you have now, and measure progress against your own goals — not someone else's highlight reel.

## Card: Quick recap
Money trouble usually grows from small habits: impulse buys, lifestyle creep, minimum payments, no budget, and waiting for "more money" before starting. The fixes are simple — pause before buying, track your spending, pay down high-interest debt, and start now, whatever your income.
