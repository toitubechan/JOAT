## Card: What it is
image: energyNuclearPowerHero
**Nuclear power** generates electricity by splitting atoms (**nuclear fission**), which releases enormous amounts of heat used to make steam and drive generators. It's a powerful, low-carbon energy source — but also one of the most debated, due to concerns about safety and waste. Understanding the basics cuts through the controversy.

## Card: Why it matters
Nuclear is a major low-carbon energy source that produces large, steady amounts of electricity without the carbon emissions of fossil fuels. It's central to energy and climate debates — praised by some as essential for cutting emissions, opposed by others over safety and waste. Understanding it helps you engage with these debates thoughtfully rather than emotionally.

## Card: Key terms
- **Nuclear fission**: splitting atoms to release energy.
- **Reactor**: where the controlled nuclear reaction happens.
- **Low-carbon**: producing little CO₂ while running.
- **Nuclear waste**: radioactive material left after use.

## Card: How nuclear power works
image: energyNuclearPowerHowNuclearPowerWorksHero
Inside a **reactor**, atoms (usually uranium) are split through **nuclear fission**, releasing huge amounts of heat. That heat boils water into steam, which spins turbines connected to generators — the same final step as most power plants. The difference is the heat source: a controlled nuclear reaction instead of burning fuel. A tiny amount of nuclear fuel produces an enormous amount of energy.

## Card: The benefits and concerns
Nuclear's big benefit: it's **low-carbon** (almost no CO₂ while running) and provides large, reliable, steady power regardless of weather — unlike intermittent solar and wind. Its concerns: the risk of accidents (rare but serious), long-lived **nuclear waste** that must be safely stored for a long time, high build costs, and public fear. The debate weighs these reliable low-carbon benefits against the risks and costs.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking nuclear plants emit lots of CO₂ (they're low-carbon while running); imagining a reactor can explode like a bomb (it can't — it's a different process); and treating the debate as simple (it genuinely weighs real benefits against real concerns). Nuclear is a powerful, low-carbon but controversial part of the energy picture.

## Card: Quick recap
Nuclear power splits atoms (fission) to release heat, making steam to drive generators — a low-carbon source that, unlike solar and wind, provides large, steady power regardless of weather. Its concerns are accident risk, long-lived radioactive waste, and high costs. The debate weighs reliable, low-carbon power against these risks — genuinely complex, not simple.
