## Card: What it is
image: langSignLanguageHero
**Sign languages** are complete, natural languages that use handshapes, movements, facial expressions, and body language instead of sound. Used primarily by Deaf communities, they're not simply "spelling out" spoken words with hands — they're full languages with their own vocabulary and grammar. Understanding this corrects common misconceptions and reveals the richness of these visual languages.

## Card: Why it matters
Sign languages are vital to millions of Deaf people and are genuine, rich languages often misunderstood by those who don't use them. Understanding what they really are fosters respect, corrects myths, and broadens your view of what language can be. It's also a reminder that language isn't limited to sound — it can be fully visual.

## Card: Key terms
- **Sign language**: a full language using visual-gestural means.
- **Deaf community**: people who use sign language as a primary language.
- **Grammar**: sign languages have their own grammatical rules.
- **Not universal**: different countries have different sign languages.

## Card: Real, complete languages
image: langSignLanguageRealCompleteLanguagesHero
A crucial point: **sign languages** are *full, natural languages* — not gestures or mime, and not just spelling out spoken words on the hands. Each has its own **vocabulary** and its own **grammar** (its own grammatical rules, often quite different from the surrounding spoken language). They can express anything spoken languages can — abstract ideas, humor, nuance, poetry. Sign languages develop naturally within **Deaf communities**, just as spoken languages develop within speaking communities.

## Card: Common misconceptions cleared up
Several myths persist. Sign language is **not universal** — different countries and communities have different sign languages (they're not one global system). It's not a simplified or lesser form of communication — it's linguistically full and complex. And it's not just spoken language translated to the hands — it has its own structure. Facial expressions and body movement are grammatically important, not just emotional. Sign languages are rich languages in their own right.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking sign language is universal (different countries have different ones); believing it's just spelling out or miming spoken words (it's a full language with its own grammar); seeing it as simpler or lesser than spoken language (it's linguistically complete); and not realizing facial expressions are grammatically meaningful. Sign languages are genuine, rich, full languages, vital to Deaf communities.

## Card: Quick recap
Sign languages are complete, natural languages using handshapes, movement, and facial expressions instead of sound — with their own vocabulary and grammar, not just spelling out spoken words. They can express anything spoken languages can. They're not universal (different communities have different ones) and not simpler or lesser. Vital to Deaf communities, they reveal that full language can be entirely visual.
