## Card: What it is
image: econPricesWhyTheyRiseHero
Prices rise for identifiable reasons — rising demand, falling supply, higher production costs, or broad inflation. When something gets more expensive, there's usually a real cause behind it. Understanding these reasons replaces the vague feeling that prices "just go up" or that someone's simply being greedy.

## Card: Why it matters
Rising prices affect everyone, and they're a constant source of frustration and confusion. Understanding *why* prices rise helps you make sense of news, judge whether a price rise is justified, and anticipate future increases. It turns a frustrating mystery into something comprehensible — and sometimes predictable.

## Card: Key terms
- **Rising demand**: more people wanting something pushes prices up.
- **Falling supply**: less availability pushes prices up.
- **Rising costs**: pricier inputs passed on to buyers.
- **Inflation**: a general, economy-wide rise in prices.

## Card: Demand up or supply down
image: econPricesWhyTheyRiseDemandUpOrSupplyDownHero
Two supply-and-demand causes. **Rising demand**: when more people want something (a trend, more buyers, higher incomes) and supply can't keep up, prices rise. **Falling supply**: when less is available (a bad harvest, a disruption, a factory closure) while demand holds, prices rise. Both create the classic "too many buyers for too few goods" pressure that lifts prices.

## Card: Costs and inflation
Two more drivers. **Rising costs**: when the inputs to make something get pricier (energy, materials, wages, transport), businesses often pass those costs on as higher prices. And broad **inflation**: when prices rise across the whole economy (from too much money or other factors), individual prices climb with it. So a price rise might be specific (that item) or part of a general inflationary trend.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming every price rise is just "greed" (there are usually real cost, supply, or demand causes); confusing a specific item's rise with economy-wide inflation (they're different); not distinguishing supply-driven from demand-driven rises; and feeling prices rise randomly, when there's typically an identifiable driver. Look for the cause behind the rise.

## Card: Quick recap
Prices rise for real reasons: rising demand, falling supply, higher production costs passed on to buyers, or broad inflation. A rise might be specific to one item or part of an economy-wide trend. It's rarely just "greed" or random — there's usually an identifiable driver, so looking for it makes price rises far more comprehensible.
