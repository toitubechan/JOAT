## Card: What it is
image: moistHeatHero
**Moist-heat** methods cook food using water or steam rather than dry heat. They include **boiling**, **simmering**, **steaming**, and **braising**. They're gentler than dry heat, great for cooking through evenly, tenderizing tough ingredients, and cooking without added fat.

## Card: Why it matters
Moist heat is behind everyday staples — pasta, rice, steamed vegetables, soups, and slow-cooked stews. Understanding it helps you cook these reliably and know which method suits which food. It's also the key to making tough, cheap cuts of meat meltingly tender, saving money and effort.

## Card: Key terms
- **Boiling**: cooking in bubbling water at its hottest.
- **Simmering**: gentle cooking just below boiling.
- **Steaming**: cooking in steam above water.
- **Braising**: slow-cooking in a little liquid, often after browning.

## Card: Boiling vs. simmering
image: boilSimmer
**Boiling** is full, rolling bubbles — good for pasta and getting things hot fast. But many foods do better with a **simmer**: gentle bubbles just below boiling. A hard boil can toughen delicate foods, break them apart, or make them rubbery. Knowing when to turn it down to a simmer is a small skill with big results.

## Card: Steaming and braising
**Steaming** cooks food in the steam above boiling water, keeping nutrients and color in vegetables especially well, with no fat needed. **Braising** is the magic one for tough meat: brown it first for flavor, then cook it slowly in a little liquid for a long time. The slow, moist heat breaks down toughness into tenderness.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: boiling hard when a gentle simmer is needed, toughening or shredding food; not salting pasta or vegetable water (a key flavor step); over-boiling vegetables until grey and mushy (steaming or brief cooking keeps them vibrant); and rushing a braise, which needs low, slow heat to tenderize.

## Card: Quick recap
Moist-heat methods cook with water or steam: boiling for pasta, gentle simmering for delicate foods, steaming to preserve vegetables, and slow braising to make tough meat tender. Match the method to the food, simmer rather than hard-boil when in doubt, and don't forget to salt the water.
