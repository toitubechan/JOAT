## Card: What it is
image: depositRightsHero
Your **deposit** is a significant sum held by the landlord against damage or unpaid rent, and getting it back is a common source of disputes. As a tenant you also have **rights** — to a safe home, privacy, and fair treatment. Knowing both helps you protect your money and stand up for yourself. (Rules vary widely by location.)

## Card: Why it matters
Deposit disputes are extremely common, and many tenants lose money they shouldn't. Tenants also often don't know their rights and accept poor treatment. Understanding deposits and rights protects a big chunk of money and ensures you're treated fairly. It's practical knowledge that pays off directly.

## Card: Key terms
- **Deposit**: money held against damage or unpaid rent.
- **Inventory / condition report**: a record of the property's state at move-in.
- **Fair wear and tear**: normal aging the landlord can't charge you for.
- **Tenant rights**: legal protections for renters.

## Card: Protecting your deposit
image: moveInInventory
The key to getting your deposit back: document the property's condition at move-in with a detailed **inventory** and photos (timestamped), and get the landlord to agree it. This proves what was already there, so you're not charged for pre-existing damage. Crucially, landlords can't charge you for **fair wear and tear** — normal aging from living there — only for actual damage. In many places, deposits must also be protected in an official scheme.

## Card: Knowing your tenant rights
Tenants generally have **rights** to: a safe, habitable home (working heating, no serious hazards); reasonable privacy (the landlord usually must give notice before entering, not just show up); protection from unfair eviction (proper process and notice); and to have repairs done. These vary by location, but knowing you *have* rights helps you push back against landlords who overstep.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: not documenting the property's condition at move-in (then being blamed for pre-existing damage); not knowing about fair wear and tear (accepting unfair deductions); assuming a landlord can enter or evict at will (usually they can't, without notice/process); and not knowing local deposit protection rules. Document everything and know your rights.

## Card: Quick recap
Protect your deposit by documenting the property's condition at move-in (detailed inventory plus timestamped photos) — landlords can't charge for fair wear and tear, only real damage, and deposits are often protected by law. Know your tenant rights too: a safe home, reasonable privacy with notice before entry, and protection from unfair eviction. Rules vary, so check locally.
