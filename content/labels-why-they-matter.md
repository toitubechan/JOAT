## Card: What it is
image: nutritionLabelHero
A **nutrition label** is the panel on food packaging listing what's inside — calories, nutrients, and ingredients. Learning to read it lets you see past marketing on the front of the pack and judge food for yourself. It's a practical skill for making informed choices, not for obsessing.

## Card: Why it matters
The front of a package is marketing; the label is the facts. Brands highlight flattering claims ("low fat!") while the real story is on the back. Reading labels helps you compare products honestly, spot hidden sugar or salt, and choose what genuinely fits your needs. This is general knowledge, not dietary advice for specific conditions.

## Card: Key terms
- **Nutrition label**: the panel listing nutrients and amounts.
- **Serving size**: the amount the figures are based on.
- **Per 100g**: figures standardized to 100 grams for comparison.
- **Ingredients list**: what's actually in the product, by amount.

## Card: The two key parts
A label has two main parts: the **nutrition panel** (numbers for calories, fat, sugar, salt, etc.) and the **ingredients list** (what's actually in it). The panel tells you *how much* of each nutrient; the ingredients list tells you *what* the food is made of. Together they give you the full, honest picture.

## Card: Per serving vs. per 100g
image: servingCompare
Labels often show figures **per serving** *and* **per 100g**. Per-serving numbers can mislead if the "serving" is unrealistically small. The **per 100g** figure is the fair way to *compare* two products, since it's standardized. When judging "is this high in sugar?", per 100g is usually the more honest number to look at.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: trusting front-of-pack claims instead of the label; reading per-serving figures without checking the serving size is realistic; comparing two products using different serving sizes; and ignoring the ingredients list entirely. The label exists to inform you — the trick is knowing which numbers actually matter.

## Card: Quick recap
A nutrition label has the nutrition panel (the numbers) and the ingredients list (what's in it) — the honest counterpart to front-of-pack marketing. Use per-100g figures to compare products fairly, and always check the serving size. Reading labels lets you judge food for yourself, calmly and informed.
