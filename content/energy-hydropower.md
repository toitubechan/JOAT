## Card: What it is
image: energyHydropowerHero
**Hydropower** generates electricity from moving water — usually by using dams to channel flowing or falling water through turbines. It's the oldest and one of the largest renewable energy sources, providing reliable, low-carbon power. Water's energy has been harnessed for centuries, now to produce electricity at scale.

## Card: Why it matters
Hydropower is a major source of renewable electricity worldwide and, unlike solar and wind, can provide steady, controllable power. Understanding it rounds out your picture of clean energy and shows that renewables aren't all intermittent. It also illustrates the trade-offs even clean energy can involve.

## Card: Key terms
- **Hydropower**: electricity generated from moving water.
- **Dam**: a barrier that stores water and controls its flow.
- **Turbine**: spun by water to drive a generator.
- **Reservoir**: the stored body of water behind a dam.

## Card: How hydropower works
image: energyHydropowerHowHydropowerWorksHero
The classic setup uses a **dam** to hold back a river, creating a **reservoir**. Water is released through **turbines**, and the force of the flowing or falling water spins them, driving generators to produce electricity (again, the spinning-generator principle). The higher the water falls and the more that flows, the more power generated. It's a clean way to turn water's energy into electricity.

## Card: Strengths and trade-offs
Hydropower's big strength: it's renewable, low-carbon, *and* controllable — operators can release water on demand, providing steady or adjustable power, unlike intermittent solar and wind. Dams can also store energy (pumping water uphill to release later). The trade-offs: dams can disrupt rivers, ecosystems, and communities, and good locations are limited. So even this clean source involves environmental and social considerations.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: lumping hydropower in with intermittent renewables (it's controllable and steady); thinking it's impact-free (dams affect rivers, wildlife, and people); and not realizing it's one of the largest and oldest renewable sources. Hydropower is clean and reliable but limited by geography and not without environmental trade-offs.

## Card: Quick recap
Hydropower generates electricity from moving water, typically by releasing dammed water through turbines. It's renewable, low-carbon, and — unlike solar and wind — controllable and steady, even able to store energy. But dams have environmental and social trade-offs and depend on suitable geography. It's one of the oldest and largest renewable energy sources.
