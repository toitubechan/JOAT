## Card: What it is
image: growthLearningStudyTechniquesHero
Beyond the principles, there are specific **study techniques** proven to work — and many popular ones that don't. This card set gathers the most effective methods, so you can spend your study time on what genuinely builds knowledge rather than what merely feels productive.

## Card: Why it matters
Students and learners often pour hours into weak techniques (highlighting, rereading) and wonder why little sticks. Knowing the few high-impact methods means your effort pays off. These techniques apply far beyond school — to any skill, subject, or professional learning you take on.

## Card: Key terms
- **Self-testing**: quizzing yourself to practice retrieval.
- **Elaboration**: explaining ideas and connecting them to what you know.
- **Interleaving**: mixing different topics in one study session.
- **The Feynman technique**: explaining a concept simply, as if teaching.

## Card: The high-impact techniques
image: growthLearningStudyTechniquesTheHighImpactTechniquesHero
The proven winners: **self-testing** (quiz yourself, use flashcards — retrieval practice in action); **spaced repetition** (review over time); **elaboration** (explain *why* and connect new ideas to what you already know); and **interleaving** (mix related topics rather than blocking one at a time, which improves your ability to tell them apart and apply them).

## Card: The Feynman technique
A favorite method: explain a concept in **plain language, as if teaching a child**. When you try, you quickly find the gaps where your understanding is fuzzy — exactly the spots to revisit. If you can't explain it simply, you don't fully understand it yet. Teaching, even to an imaginary student, is one of the best tests of real knowledge.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: relying on highlighting and rereading (low impact); studying one topic in a long block instead of interleaving; passively consuming without elaborating or self-testing; and skipping the "explain it simply" test that reveals gaps. Effective study is active and a bit uncomfortable — that discomfort is the learning happening.

## Card: Quick recap
The proven study techniques are active: self-testing, spaced repetition, elaboration (explaining and connecting ideas), and interleaving (mixing topics). The Feynman technique — explaining simply as if teaching — exposes the gaps in your understanding. Skip passive highlighting and rereading; effective study feels effortful, and that effort is the point.
