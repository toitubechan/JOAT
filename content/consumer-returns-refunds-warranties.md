## Card: What it is
image: returnsRefundsHero
**Returns**, **refunds**, and **warranties** are the main ways you get redress when a purchase isn't right. Understanding how they differ — and what you're entitled to versus what's a bonus — helps you successfully get money back, replacements, or repairs. (Specifics vary by country and seller; this is general knowledge.)

## Card: Why it matters
People often miss out on refunds or repairs they're entitled to, or misunderstand warranties. Knowing how these work means you can confidently claim what's yours, avoid being fobbed off, and make smarter purchases. It directly protects your money when things go wrong with what you buy.

## Card: Key terms
- **Return**: sending an item back to the seller.
- **Refund**: getting your money back.
- **Warranty / guarantee**: a promise to repair or replace for a period.
- **Change of mind**: returning simply because you no longer want it.

## Card: Returns and refunds
A key distinction: returning a **faulty** item usually triggers your *legal right* to a remedy (refund, repair, or replacement). Returning for a **change of mind** (you just don't want it) is often *not* a legal right in physical shops — it depends on the seller's goodwill policy. (Online purchases frequently *do* have a cancellation/return window by law in many places.) Knowing which situation you're in sets your expectations.

## Card: Warranties and guarantees
image: warrantyAddsRights
A **warranty** (or guarantee) is a promise — usually from the maker or seller — to repair or replace a product if it fails within a set period. It's useful, but remember: a warranty is *in addition to* your legal consumer rights, not a replacement for them. So even if a warranty expires, your underlying legal rights for faulty goods may still apply. Don't let "the warranty's expired" be the final word.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: assuming you can always return for a change of mind (often not a legal right in shops); thinking a warranty replaces your legal rights (it adds to them); not keeping receipts/proof of purchase (usually needed to claim); and accepting "warranty expired" when legal rights might still apply. Know the type of issue and keep your proof of purchase.

## Card: Quick recap
Faulty goods usually invoke your legal right to a refund, repair, or replacement; change-of-mind returns in shops often rely on seller goodwill (though online buys frequently have a legal return window). A warranty adds to your legal rights rather than replacing them. Keep proof of purchase, and don't accept "warranty expired" as automatically final.
