## Card: What it is
image: firstLanguageHero
With many **programming languages** out there, beginners often agonize over which to learn first. The reassuring truth: the *specific* language matters far less than starting and sticking with one. Most beginner-friendly languages teach the same core concepts. Choosing a first language is about fit and motivation, not finding the one "perfect" choice.

## Card: Why it matters
"Which language should I learn?" is the first hurdle for aspiring coders, and overthinking it causes paralysis. Understanding that the choice matters less than starting frees you to begin. It also helps you pick sensibly based on your goals, rather than chasing a mythical perfect language. Getting started is what counts.

## Card: Key terms
- **Programming language**: a language for writing code.
- **Beginner-friendly**: easy to learn and forgiving.
- **Transferable skills**: core concepts that carry across languages.
- **Goal-based choice**: picking a language for what you want to build.

## Card: The concepts transfer
image: conceptsTransferHero
Here's the key insight: the core concepts of programming — variables, loops, logic, functions — are **transferable** across nearly all languages. Once you learn them in one language, learning another is far easier. So your first language is really a vehicle for learning these fundamentals, not a lifelong commitment. This is why the *specific* first language matters much less than people fear.

## Card: How to choose sensibly
Pick based on simple, practical factors. Choose a **beginner-friendly** language (some are designed to be easier and more forgiving for newcomers). Or make a **goal-based choice** — pick a language suited to what you want to build (e.g., a language commonly used for websites if that's your interest). Either way, choose one with good learning resources and a supportive community, then *commit* to it long enough to learn the fundamentals.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: agonizing over the "perfect" first language instead of starting; jumping between languages without learning any deeply; choosing an advanced or unfriendly language as a beginner; and thinking your first language locks you in forever (skills transfer). The biggest mistake is not starting at all. Pick a sensible, beginner-friendly option and commit to learning the fundamentals.

## Card: Quick recap
