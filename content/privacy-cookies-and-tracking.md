## Card: What it is
image: cookiesTrackingHero
**Cookies** are small files websites store on your device to remember things about you. Some are helpful (keeping you logged in); others **track** your activity across many sites to build an advertising profile. Understanding the difference demystifies those endless "accept cookies" pop-ups.

## Card: Why it matters
Tracking is how that product you glanced at follows you across the web in ads, and how detailed profiles of your interests get built. Knowing how it works lets you make sensible choices about what to allow, and reduces how much you're silently followed and profiled as you browse.

## Card: Key terms
- **Cookie**: a small file a site stores on your device.
- **First-party cookie**: set by the site you're actually visiting.
- **Third-party cookie**: set by others (often advertisers) to track you across sites.
- **Tracking**: following your activity to build a profile.

## Card: Helpful vs. tracking cookies
image: helpfulTrackingCookies
Not all cookies are bad. **First-party cookies** from the site you're on do useful jobs — remembering your login, language, or cart. The privacy concern is mainly **third-party cookies**, set by advertisers and other companies to follow you *across* different sites, stitching together a picture of your browsing for targeting.

## Card: How tracking follows you
Beyond cookies, sites use other tricks to recognize your device and link your activity. This is why searching for shoes leads to shoe ads everywhere: trackers shared across sites report your behavior to advertising networks. The "accept cookies" prompts exist because laws now require sites to ask before doing much of this.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: clicking "accept all" on every cookie prompt out of habit, allowing maximal tracking; not knowing you can usually choose "reject" or "essential only"; and assuming nothing can be done about tracking. In reality, browser settings, rejecting non-essential cookies, and private modes all reduce how much you're followed.

## Card: Quick recap
Cookies are small files sites store on you — first-party ones are often helpful, while third-party tracking cookies follow you across sites to build ad profiles. The pop-ups exist because laws require consent. You're not powerless: reject non-essential cookies, adjust browser settings, and you'll cut down the tracking.
