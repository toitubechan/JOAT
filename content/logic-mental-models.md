## Card: What it is
image: logicMentalModelsHero
**Mental models** are simple frameworks or ways of thinking that help you understand situations and make better decisions. They're like thinking tools — reusable lenses for viewing problems clearly. Collecting a few good mental models gives you flexible, powerful ways to reason about the world, beyond just gut instinct.

## Card: Why it matters
Good thinkers often have a toolkit of mental models they apply to different situations. These frameworks help you see problems more clearly, avoid common errors, and decide more wisely. Understanding the idea — and a few examples — gives you practical thinking tools you can use across work, decisions, and life. It's like upgrading your reasoning toolkit.

## Card: Key terms
- **Mental model**: a framework or lens for understanding situations.
- **First principles**: reasoning up from basic truths.
- **Opportunity cost**: what you give up by choosing one option.
- **Second-order thinking**: considering the consequences of consequences.

## Card: A few powerful models
image: logicMentalModelsAFewPowerfulModelsHero
Some widely useful mental models: **First principles** thinking — breaking a problem down to its basic truths and reasoning up from there, rather than copying assumptions. **Opportunity cost** — recognizing that choosing one thing means giving up alternatives, so the real cost of a choice is what you forgo. **Second-order thinking** — asking "and then what?" to consider the *consequences of consequences*, not just immediate effects. Each is a simple lens that improves decisions.

## Card: Using models well
A mental model is a *tool*, not a rigid rule — useful in some situations, less so in others. The skill is having several models and applying the right one to the situation (as the saying goes, "if all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail"). Collecting a varied toolkit of models, and knowing when each applies, lets you approach problems flexibly and see angles you'd otherwise miss. Models complement, not replace, judgment.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking one model or framework explains everything (each is a tool for certain situations); applying a model rigidly where it doesn't fit; collecting models as trivia without actually using them; and relying on gut instinct alone instead of useful frameworks. Mental models are flexible thinking tools — most valuable when you have several and apply the right one thoughtfully.

## Card: Quick recap
Mental models are simple frameworks — thinking tools — that help you understand situations and decide better. Examples: first principles (reason up from basic truths), opportunity cost (the real cost is what you give up), and second-order thinking ("and then what?"). Each is a lens useful in certain situations. The skill is collecting several and applying the right one thoughtfully, complementing your judgment.
