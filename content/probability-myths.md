## Card: What it is
image: dataProbabilityMythsHero
Probability is surrounded by **myths** — about luck, streaks, "being due," and patterns in randomness. These false beliefs feel intuitive but lead to poor decisions, especially around gambling. This card set debunks the common ones, replacing gut-feel myths with how chance actually works.

## Card: Why it matters
Probability myths cost people money (especially in gambling) and lead to faulty reasoning about everyday chance. Busting them protects your wallet and sharpens your thinking. Because these beliefs are so intuitive and widespread, knowing they're false is genuinely valuable — it's a defense against both casinos and your own misleading instincts.

## Card: Key terms
- **Gambler's fallacy**: thinking a result is "due" after a streak.
- **Hot hand**: wrongly expecting a streak to continue.
- **Luck**: a belief in personal influence over random outcomes.
- **Pattern-seeking**: seeing patterns in true randomness.

## Card: Common probability myths busted
image: dataProbabilityMythsCommonProbabilityMythsBustedHero
- "**I'm due for a win** after losing" — the **gambler's fallacy**; independent odds don't change.
- "**I'm on a hot streak**" — the **hot hand** belief; past wins don't make future ones more likely in games of chance.
- "**Lucky numbers/charms** improve my odds" — they don't affect random outcomes.
- "**This pattern means something**" — humans see **patterns** even in pure randomness (random often looks streaky).

## Card: Why these myths persist
These myths persist because the human brain is a relentless **pattern-seeker** — we instinctively look for meaning, control, and patterns, even in randomness. Random sequences often *look* non-random (clusters and streaks happen by chance), fooling us. And we crave a sense of control over uncertain outcomes, so beliefs in luck and "being due" feel comforting. But chance doesn't work the way our instincts insist.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: believing in "due" outcomes (gambler's fallacy) or "hot" streaks in games of chance; thinking luck or charms affect random events; seeing meaningful patterns in randomness; and trusting these intuitions, especially while gambling. The reality: in games of pure chance, the odds are fixed, past results don't matter, and no system or luck beats them.

## Card: Quick recap
Probability myths — being "due" for a win (gambler's fallacy), "hot streaks," lucky charms, and patterns in randomness — feel intuitive but are false. They persist because our brains seek patterns and control even in randomness. In games of pure chance, odds are fixed and past results don't matter. Knowing this defends your wallet and sharpens your thinking.
