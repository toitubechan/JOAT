## Card: What it is
image: oversharingHero
Social media invites us to share — but **oversharing** can expose more than we intend, sometimes with real consequences. Sharing wisely means knowing what's risky to post, who can really see it, and how seemingly harmless details can add up. The goal is enjoying social media without leaving yourself exposed.

## Card: Why it matters
Posts can reveal your location, routines, and personal details to a far wider audience than you imagine — including strangers, employers, and criminals. Small overshares can enable scams, theft, or identity fraud. A little awareness lets you share the fun parts of life while keeping the risky details to yourself.

## Card: Key terms
- **Oversharing**: posting more personal information than is wise.
- **Audience**: who can actually see your posts.
- **Geotagging**: attaching your location to a post.
- **Identity theft**: using your details to impersonate you.

## Card: What's risky to share
Some details are riskier than they seem: your home address or **geotagged** location; real-time posts that announce you're away (an invitation to burglars); your full birth date, which aids **identity theft**; and answers to common security questions (pet's name, first school). Photos can also leak details in the background you didn't notice.

## Card: Know your real audience
image: realAudiencePrivacy
People often misjudge their **audience** — assuming "friends only" when posts are public, or forgetting that anything shared can be re-shared and screenshotted. Check your privacy settings to see who can really view your posts, and remember that even a private post lives on others' devices once seen. Share as if it could become public.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: posting holiday photos in real time (signaling an empty home); sharing details that double as security-question answers; geotagging your home; and accepting friend/follower requests from strangers, widening your audience invisibly. Each individually seems minor, but combined they hand over a detailed, exploitable picture of you.

## Card: Quick recap
Share on social media wisely: avoid posting your address, real-time travel, full birth date, or security-question answers. Know your true audience — assume posts can become public and be re-shared. Watch background details in photos and stranger requests. Enjoy sharing the good parts while keeping risky specifics private.
