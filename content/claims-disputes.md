## Card: What it is
image: claimDisputeHero
Sometimes a claim is **denied** or paid less than you expected, and you disagree. Handling a **dispute** properly — understanding why, challenging it, and escalating if needed — can overturn unfair decisions. A denial isn't always the final word. (General guidance; specifics vary by insurer and country.)

## Card: Why it matters
Insurers sometimes deny or underpay claims wrongly, and many people accept it without challenge, losing money they're owed. Knowing how to dispute a decision means you can stand up for a fair outcome. Disputes are often winnable when you understand the process — so it's worth knowing rather than giving up.

## Card: Key terms
- **Denial**: the insurer refusing to pay a claim.
- **Reason for decision**: the insurer's stated basis for denying or reducing.
- **Appeal**: formally challenging the insurer's decision.
- **Ombudsman / regulator**: an independent body for unresolved disputes.

## Card: Understand the reason, then appeal
image: appealEvidence
First, get the **reason for the decision** in writing — *why* was it denied or reduced? Sometimes it's a fixable misunderstanding or missing evidence; sometimes it's a genuine policy exclusion. If you believe it's wrong, **appeal**: formally challenge it, providing additional evidence or pointing to where your policy actually covers the loss. Be clear, factual, and reference your policy wording. Many disputes are resolved at this stage.

## Card: Escalate if needed
If the insurer's own appeal process doesn't resolve it and you still believe you're right, you can escalate to an independent body — often a financial **ombudsman** or **regulator** — that reviews disputes between consumers and insurers, frequently for free. Keep all your documentation throughout. Knowing this external option exists is powerful: insurers know it too, which can encourage fair treatment.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: accepting a denial without understanding why; giving up when you have a valid case; not getting the decision and reasons in writing; appealing emotionally rather than with facts and policy references; and not knowing about the independent ombudsman/regulator option. Understand the reason, challenge with evidence, escalate if needed, and keep records.

## Card: Quick recap
If a claim is denied or underpaid, don't automatically accept it. Get the reason in writing, then appeal with facts, evidence, and your policy wording — many disputes resolve here. If unresolved, escalate to an independent ombudsman or regulator, often free. Keep all documentation. A denial isn't always final, and valid disputes are often winnable.
