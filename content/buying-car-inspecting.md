## Card: What it is
image: carInspectionHero
Before buying a used car, **inspecting** it and **test driving** it are essential to avoid expensive surprises. A car can look fine but hide costly problems. Knowing the basic checks — and that a professional inspection is worth it — protects you from buying someone else's troubles. (General guidance.)

## Card: Why it matters
Used cars vary enormously in condition, and hidden faults can cost a fortune after purchase. A proper inspection and test drive catch many problems *before* you pay, when you can walk away or negotiate. Skipping this step is how people end up with unreliable, money-draining cars. It's a critical protective step.

## Card: Key terms
- **Test drive**: driving the car to check how it performs.
- **Inspection**: checking the car's condition thoroughly.
- **Service history**: records of past maintenance.
- **Independent inspection**: a check by a neutral mechanic.

## Card: Inspect and test drive
Always **test drive** a used car — listen for odd noises, check it brakes, steers, and accelerates smoothly, and that everything (lights, electronics, air con) works. **Inspect** the body for rust, damage, or mismatched paint (signs of past repairs), check the tires, and look for fluid leaks. Review the **service history** — a well-documented record suggests good care. Never buy a used car unseen or undriven.

## Card: Get an independent inspection
image: independentMechanic
For an unfamiliar used car, the smartest move is an **independent inspection** — paying a trusted, neutral mechanic to check it before you buy. They'll spot problems an untrained eye (and certainly an eager buyer) misses. The modest cost can save you from a hugely expensive mistake. Don't rely solely on the seller's word or your own quick look, especially for a major purchase.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: buying without a test drive or proper inspection; trusting the seller's word about condition and history; not checking service records; ignoring warning signs (noises, leaks, rust) out of eagerness; and skipping an independent mechanic's check on a costly car. Inspect thoroughly, test drive, verify history, and get a professional check.

## Card: Quick recap
Before buying a used car, always test drive it (listen and check everything works) and inspect it (body, tires, leaks, service history). For an unfamiliar car, pay for an independent mechanic's inspection — it catches problems you'd miss and can save a fortune. Never buy unseen, and don't rely solely on the seller's word.
