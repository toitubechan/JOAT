## Card: What it is
image: cognitiveBiasHero
A **cognitive bias** is a systematic error in how we think — a mental shortcut that often helps but sometimes leads us astray. Our brains use these shortcuts to make quick decisions, but they can distort our judgment in predictable ways. Everyone has them; they're a feature of how human minds work.

## Card: Why it matters
Biases affect every decision you make, often without you realizing. They lead to poor choices, false beliefs, and misjudgments — and others (advertisers, manipulators) can exploit them. Understanding biases helps you think more clearly, decide better, and recognize when your judgment might be skewed. It's foundational self-knowledge.

## Card: Key terms
- **Cognitive bias**: a systematic thinking error.
- **Mental shortcut (heuristic)**: a quick rule of thumb for deciding.
- **Systematic**: happening in predictable, consistent ways.
- **Blind spot**: failing to notice your own biases.

## Card: Helpful shortcuts gone wrong
image: shortcutGoneWrongHero
Our brains face too much information to analyze everything fully, so they use **mental shortcuts (heuristics)** — fast rules of thumb. Usually these serve us well. But they misfire in predictable situations, producing **cognitive biases**. So biases aren't a sign of stupidity; they're the downside of an otherwise efficient system. The same shortcuts that let us decide quickly can also mislead us.

## Card: Everyone has them
A crucial point: biases are universal — *everyone* has them, including the smart and well-educated. In fact, a famous bias is the **blind spot**: we readily see biases in others but miss them in ourselves. Accepting that you, too, are biased is the first step to thinking more clearly. Believing you're immune is itself a bias.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking biases only affect "other people" (the bias blind spot); believing intelligence makes you immune (it doesn't); treating biases as rare glitches rather than constant influences; and assuming you can simply "decide" not to be biased. Biases are built into how we think, so awareness and strategies, not willpower, are the answer.

## Card: Quick recap
A cognitive bias is a systematic thinking error from a mental shortcut that usually helps but sometimes misleads. These shortcuts are efficient but misfire predictably. Everyone has biases — and we tend to spot them in others but not ourselves (the blind spot). Accepting your own biases is the first step to thinking more clearly.
