## Card: What it is
image: spaceddMoonTidesHero
The **Moon** is Earth's only natural satellite — a world in its own right that orbits us, shines by reflected sunlight, drives the ocean **tides**, and has shaped life and culture for ages. This is a closer look at the Moon: its phases, its pull on our seas, its hidden far side, and how it likely formed.

## Card: Why it matters
The Moon is our closest celestial neighbor and the only other world humans have visited. It governs the tides, stabilizes Earth, and lights our nights. Understanding it in depth explains familiar phenomena (phases, tides, eclipses) and reveals a surprisingly dynamic relationship between the Moon and Earth that affects life itself.

## Card: Key terms
- **Phases**: the Moon's changing apparent shape.
- **Tides**: the rise and fall of seas, driven by the Moon's gravity.
- **Far side**: the side of the Moon always facing away from Earth.
- **Tidal locking**: why we always see the same face.

## Card: Phases and the far side
The Moon's **phases** come from how much of its sunlit half we see as it orbits Earth — not from Earth's shadow. Intriguingly, the Moon is **tidally locked**: it rotates once per orbit, so the *same face* always points toward us. The **far side** (often wrongly called the "dark side") isn't permanently dark — it gets sunlight too — we just never see it from Earth without a spacecraft.

## Card: Tides and origins
image: moonTidesGravityHero
The Moon's gravity tugs on Earth's oceans, pulling them into bulges that create the **tides** — roughly two high and two low each day as Earth rotates through them (the Sun contributes too). As for origins, the leading theory is that the Moon formed when a Mars-sized body struck the early Earth, flinging out debris that clumped together into the Moon. The Moon is also very slowly drifting away from Earth.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking phases come from Earth's shadow (that's an eclipse); calling the far side the permanently "dark side" (it gets sunlight, we just never see it); not knowing the Moon causes tides; and imagining the Moon is fixed (it's tidally locked and slowly drifting away). The Moon is a dynamic world with a deep connection to Earth.

## Card: Quick recap
The Moon, Earth's only natural satellite, shines by reflected sunlight. Its phases come from how much of its sunlit side we see, and it's tidally locked, always showing the same face (the far side isn't permanently dark). Its gravity drives the ocean tides. It likely formed from a giant impact on early Earth and is slowly drifting away. A dynamic neighbor deeply tied to Earth.
