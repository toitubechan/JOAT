## Card: What it is
image: mathFamousNumbersHero
Some numbers are so special they appear throughout math and nature, earning fame and even their own symbols. **Pi (π)** relates to circles; **e** relates to growth; and there are others. These "famous numbers" turn up in surprising places, revealing deep patterns. Understanding what they represent is more interesting (and accessible) than their endless digits.

## Card: Why it matters
These numbers reveal the hidden mathematical order in the world — circles, growth, nature — and they're cultural touchstones (people celebrate "Pi Day"). Understanding what they *mean*, rather than memorizing digits, offers a glimpse of math's surprising beauty and the deep patterns connecting seemingly unrelated things. It makes abstract math feel wondrous.

## Card: Key terms
- **Pi (π)**: the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter.
- **e**: a number central to growth and change.
- **Irrational number**: a number with infinite, non-repeating decimals.
- **Constant**: a fixed, special number that appears repeatedly.

## Card: Pi — the circle number
image: mathFamousNumbersPiTheCircleNumberHero
**Pi (π)**, roughly 3.14, is the ratio of any circle's circumference (distance around) to its diameter (distance across). Remarkably, this ratio is the *same for every circle*, no matter the size — a beautiful universal constant. Pi appears far beyond circles too, throughout math and physics. It's an **irrational number**: its decimals go on forever without repeating, which is part of its mystique (people have computed trillions of digits).

## Card: e and other famous numbers
**e** (roughly 2.72) is central to *growth and change* — it naturally appears whenever something grows continuously (like compound interest or populations), which is why it's fundamental to calculus and many natural processes. Like pi, it's irrational. There are other famous numbers too (like the golden ratio, linked to patterns in nature and art). These **constants** keep appearing across unrelated areas, hinting at deep underlying mathematical structure.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking pi is just "about circles" (it appears far more widely); focusing on memorizing digits rather than understanding what the numbers *mean*; not knowing e relates to growth; and seeing these as mere trivia rather than glimpses of deep patterns. The wonder of famous numbers is in what they represent and where they surprisingly appear, not their endless decimals.

## Card: Quick recap
Famous numbers like pi (π ≈ 3.14, the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, the same for every circle) and e (≈ 2.72, central to growth and change) appear throughout math and nature. Both are irrational (infinite, non-repeating decimals). These constants turn up in surprising, unrelated places, revealing deep mathematical patterns. Their meaning, not their digits, is what makes them wondrous.
