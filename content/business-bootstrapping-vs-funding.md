## Card: What it is
image: bootstrapFundingHero
There are two broad ways to fund a business: **bootstrapping** (self-funding — growing using your own money and the revenue the business generates) or **raising money** (getting funds from outside investors in exchange for a share of the business). Each has major trade-offs. Understanding them helps you choose the right path for your situation — there's no single right answer.

## Card: Why it matters
How you fund a business profoundly shapes it — your control, your growth speed, your risk, and your pressures. Many founders assume they *must* raise money (or that they can't start without it), missing that bootstrapping is often viable and sometimes better. Understanding both helps you make this big decision wisely, rather than by default or assumption.

## Card: Key terms
- **Bootstrapping**: funding a business yourself, from savings and revenue.
- **Raising money**: getting funds from outside investors.
- **Equity**: ownership of the business.
- **Trade-offs**: the pros and cons each path involves.

## Card: Bootstrapping
image: bootstrappingHero
**Bootstrapping** means funding the business yourself — using your savings and reinvesting the revenue it generates — without outside investors. Pros: you keep full ownership and control, face no investor pressure, and stay disciplined (limited money forces focus). Cons: growth is usually slower (limited by your own funds), and you bear the financial risk personally. It suits businesses that can generate revenue early and grow steadily. Many successful businesses are bootstrapped.

## Card: Raising money
**Raising money** means getting funds from outside investors, usually giving up some **equity** (ownership) in return. Pros: access to more money lets you grow faster and pursue bigger ambitions, plus investors may bring expertise and connections. Cons: you give up some ownership and control, take on investor expectations and pressure (often for fast growth), and it can be hard to obtain. It suits businesses needing significant upfront capital or aiming to scale quickly.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming you *must* raise money to start (many businesses bootstrap successfully); thinking raising money is "free" (you give up ownership and control, and take on pressure); chasing investment when bootstrapping would serve better; and not weighing the **trade-offs** for your specific situation. There's no universally right path — it depends on your business, goals, and circumstances.

## Card: Quick recap
Two ways to fund a business: bootstrapping (self-funding from savings and revenue — keeping full control but growing slower) or raising money (outside investors fund you in exchange for equity — enabling faster growth but giving up ownership and control, with investor pressure). Each suits different situations. Don't assume you must raise money; weigh the trade-offs for your business, goals, and circumstances. There's no single right answer.
