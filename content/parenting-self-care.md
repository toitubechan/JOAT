## Card: What it is
image: parentingSelfCareHero
**Looking after yourself as a parent** is the often-neglected practice of caring for your own wellbeing while caring for children. Far from selfish, it's essential — a depleted, exhausted caregiver can't parent at their best. Understanding why caregiver self-care matters helps parents sustain themselves and, in turn, care better for their children. This is general educational knowledge, not professional advice.

## Card: Why it matters
Parents often pour everything into their children while neglecting themselves, leading to burnout, exhaustion, and stress — which ultimately harms both parent and child. Understanding that caregiver wellbeing is essential (not selfish) helps parents sustain themselves for the long, demanding job of parenting. It benefits the whole family, since children do better with caregivers who aren't depleted and overwhelmed.

## Card: Key terms
- **Self-care**: caring for your own wellbeing.
- **Burnout**: exhaustion from sustained, unrelieved stress.
- **Not selfish**: reframing self-care as necessary, not indulgent.
- **Sustainability**: keeping yourself able to parent over the long term.

## Card: You can't pour from an empty cup
image: parentingSelfCareYouCanTPourFromAnEmptyCupHero
A key reframe: caring for yourself is **not selfish** — it's essential for caring for others. The analogy of putting on your own oxygen mask first applies: a depleted, exhausted, burnt-out caregiver can't parent at their best. **Burnout** — exhaustion from relentless, unrelieved demands — harms both parent and child. So **self-care** (rest, support, attending to your own needs and wellbeing) isn't an indulgence; it's what keeps you *able* to care for your children well. You can't pour from an empty cup.

## Card: Sustaining yourself
Parenting is a long, demanding job, so **sustainability** matters. Looking after yourself means attending to your basic needs (sleep, rest, health), maintaining some support and connection (you don't have to do it all alone — accepting and seeking help is wise, not weak), and preserving some space for yourself where possible. Even small amounts of self-care help. The goal isn't perfection but staying replenished enough to parent patiently and well over the long haul. Caring for the caregiver benefits the whole family.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking self-care is selfish (it's essential — you can't pour from an empty cup); neglecting your own needs until burnout; trying to do everything alone rather than accepting help; and not realizing caregiver wellbeing directly affects children. Looking after yourself is necessary, not indulgent — it sustains you to parent well, and benefits the whole family.

## Card: Quick recap
Looking after yourself as a parent is essential, not selfish — a depleted, burnt-out caregiver can't parent at their best ("you can't pour from an empty cup"). Self-care means attending to your basic needs (sleep, rest, health), seeking and accepting support rather than doing everything alone, and preserving some space for yourself. Parenting is a long job, so sustaining yourself matters. Caring for the caregiver benefits the whole family.
