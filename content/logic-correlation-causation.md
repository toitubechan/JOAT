## Card: What it is
image: logicCorrelationCausationHero
**Correlation** is when two things tend to occur together; **causation** is when one actually *causes* the other. A crucial principle of clear thinking: **correlation does not imply causation**. Just because two things are linked doesn't mean one caused the other — confusing the two is one of the most common and consequential reasoning errors.

## Card: Why it matters
This single principle guards against a huge range of bad reasoning — false health claims, misleading headlines, faulty conclusions, and superstitions. It's perhaps the most valuable critical-thinking idea for everyday life, helping you avoid being fooled by claims that mistake a mere link for a cause. It sharpens your judgment about cause and effect everywhere.

## Card: Key terms
- **Correlation**: two things tending to occur together.
- **Causation**: one thing actually causing another.
- **Confounding factor**: a hidden third cause behind both.
- **Coincidence**: a chance link with no real connection.

## Card: Why linked isn't caused
image: logicCorrelationCausationWhyLinkedIsnTCausedHero
Two things can be **correlated** without one causing the other, for several reasons. There may be a **confounding factor** — a hidden third thing causing both (ice cream sales and drownings both rise in summer; heat causes both, not each other). It might be pure **coincidence** (random chance). Or the causation could run the *opposite* way from what's assumed. So a correlation alone never proves what causes what — you need more to establish causation.

## Card: Spotting the trap
The classic error: seeing two things move together and assuming one causes the other. "People who do X are healthier, so X causes health!" — but maybe healthy people tend to do X (reverse causation), or wealth causes both (confounder). When you hear a causal claim based on a correlation, ask: *could something else explain this link?* This single question defuses countless misleading claims in news, ads, and arguments.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: assuming correlation proves causation (it doesn't); ignoring confounding factors; not considering reverse causation; falling for health, diet, or product claims built on mere correlations; and seeing meaning in coincidental links. The fix is one reflex: when two things are linked, ask whether one truly *causes* the other, or whether something else explains it.

## Card: Quick recap
Correlation (things occurring together) does not imply causation (one causing the other). A link can arise from a confounding factor (a hidden third cause), coincidence, or reverse causation. The classic error is assuming a correlation proves cause and effect. The defense is one reflex question: "could something else explain this link?" It's among the most valuable critical-thinking ideas for everyday life.
