## Card: What it is
image: econInflationWhatItIsHero
**Inflation** is the gradual rise in the prices of goods and services over time, which means your money buys less than it used to. A coffee that cost less a decade ago costs more today — that's inflation. It's a normal feature of most economies, and understanding it explains a lot about money.

## Card: Why it matters
Inflation quietly affects everyone — your shopping, savings, wages, and the cost of living. Understanding it helps you make sense of the news, your finances, and why prices keep creeping up. It's one of the most important everyday economic ideas, and it directly impacts the real value of the money in your pocket.

## Card: Key terms
- **Inflation**: a general rise in prices over time.
- **Purchasing power**: how much your money can buy.
- **Inflation rate**: how fast prices are rising, as a percentage.
- **Cost of living**: the overall expense of everyday life.

## Card: Money loses purchasing power
image: econInflationWhatItIsMoneyLosesPurchasingPowerHero
The core effect of inflation is falling **purchasing power**: the same amount of money buys less over time. If prices rise 3% in a year, something that cost 100 now costs 103 — so your 100 buys less than before. This is why money kept "under the mattress" slowly loses real value, even though the number of notes stays the same.

## Card: A normal feature, in moderation
Mild, steady inflation (often a few percent a year) is normal and even considered healthy for an economy. It's measured as the **inflation rate** — the percentage prices rise over a period. Problems arise when inflation is *too high* (prices rising fast, eroding savings and wages) or, more rarely, negative. A little is expected; a lot is destabilizing.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking rising prices mean someone is simply "greedy" (inflation is a broad economic phenomenon with real causes); assuming money holds its value if you just save it (inflation erodes it); confusing one item's price rise with general inflation; and panicking at mild inflation, which is normal. Understanding it brings perspective.

## Card: Quick recap
Inflation is the general rise in prices over time, reducing your money's purchasing power — the same amount buys less. Mild, steady inflation is normal and even healthy; high inflation is destabilizing. It affects savings, wages, and the cost of living, which is why money simply stored loses real value over the years.
