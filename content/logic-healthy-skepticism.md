## Card: What it is
image: logicHealthySkepticismHero
**Healthy skepticism** is the habit of questioning claims and asking for good evidence before believing them — while staying open to being convinced. It's a balance: not gullibly accepting everything, but not cynically rejecting everything either. It's the mindset of a careful, fair thinker who follows the evidence.

## Card: Why it matters
In a world full of claims, misinformation, and persuasion, healthy skepticism is essential protection — it stops you being fooled. But done wrong, skepticism curdles into cynicism, rejecting everything (which is just as foolish). Understanding the balance helps you think clearly: skeptical enough to question, open enough to learn. It's a cornerstone of good thinking.

## Card: Key terms
- **Skepticism**: questioning claims and seeking good evidence.
- **Gullibility**: believing things too easily.
- **Cynicism**: rejecting everything, trusting nothing.
- **Open-mindedness**: willingness to be convinced by good evidence.

## Card: The balance to strike
image: logicHealthySkepticismTheBalanceToStrikeHero
Healthy skepticism sits between two errors. **Gullibility** is believing things too readily, without evidence — easily fooled. **Cynicism** is the opposite extreme — rejecting everything and trusting nothing, which is equally lazy and closes you off from truth. Healthy skepticism threads the middle: question claims and demand good evidence, *but* remain genuinely **open-minded** — willing to believe when the evidence is good. Skeptical, not cynical.

## Card: Skepticism in practice
Practically, healthy skepticism means: when you hear a claim, asking "what's the evidence?" rather than accepting or dismissing it on impulse. It means proportioning your belief to the evidence — believing strongly-supported claims, doubting weakly-supported ones, and updating when evidence changes. Crucially, it applies to your *own* beliefs too: questioning what you already think, not just others' claims. Good skepticism is fair and even-handed, following the evidence wherever it leads.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: confusing skepticism with cynicism (rejecting everything isn't smart, it's closed-minded); the opposite, being gullible and believing too easily; applying skepticism only to views you dislike while accepting agreeable claims uncritically; and refusing to update beliefs even with good evidence. Healthy skepticism is balanced, fair, and open — questioning all claims, including your own, and following the evidence.

## Card: Quick recap
Healthy skepticism means questioning claims and seeking good evidence, while staying open to being convinced — the balance between gullibility (believing too easily) and cynicism (rejecting everything). In practice, ask "what's the evidence?", proportion belief to evidence, apply it fairly (including to your own views), and update when evidence changes. Skeptical but open is the mark of a careful, clear thinker.
