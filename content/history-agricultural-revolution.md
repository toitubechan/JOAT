## Card: What it is
image: histTurningpointsAgriculturalHero
The **Agricultural Revolution** — when humans shifted from hunting and gathering to farming, around 12,000 years ago — is arguably the most important turning point in human history. It transformed how people lived, leading to settlements, cities, and eventually civilization itself. Everything since rests on this shift.

## Card: Why it matters
For most of human history, people were nomadic hunter-gatherers. Farming changed everything — it's the foundation on which all later civilization was built. Understanding it explains how humanity went from small wandering bands to cities, nations, and the modern world. It's a quiet revolution with the biggest consequences of all.

## Card: Key terms
- **Hunter-gatherers**: people who forage and hunt for food.
- **Agriculture**: farming crops and raising animals.
- **Settlement**: a permanent place people live.
- **Surplus**: extra food beyond immediate needs.

## Card: From wandering to settling
image: histTurningpointsAgriculturalFromWanderingToSettlingHero
Before farming, **hunter-gatherers** roamed in search of food, in small groups. **Agriculture** — growing crops and raising animals — let people stay in one place, creating permanent **settlements**. This was a profound shift: instead of following food, people produced it where they lived. Settling down made everything that followed possible, from villages to cities.

## Card: Why surplus changed everything
The key consequence was **surplus** — farming could produce *more* food than immediately needed. Surplus food meant not everyone had to farm, so some people could become craftspeople, traders, priests, soldiers, and rulers. This division of labor enabled specialization, trade, social hierarchy, writing, and ultimately civilization. The humble idea of growing extra food set the stage for all of organized human society.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming farming was obviously "better" and instantly adopted (it was gradual, and brought new problems like disease and harder labor); thinking civilization came first (farming and surplus enabled it); and underrating this shift because it wasn't a dramatic event. Quietly, agriculture reshaped human life more than almost anything.

## Card: Quick recap
The Agricultural Revolution, around 12,000 years ago, shifted humans from nomadic hunter-gatherers to settled farmers. Producing food where they lived created permanent settlements, and crucially, food *surplus* freed some people from farming — enabling specialization, trade, hierarchy, and ultimately civilization. It's the quiet foundation of everything that followed.
