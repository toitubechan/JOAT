## Card: What it is
image: econMarketsCompetitionAndMonopoliesHero
**Competition** is when multiple sellers vie for customers, while a **monopoly** is when one seller dominates with no real competition. The amount of competition in a market hugely affects prices, quality, and innovation. Understanding this explains why competition is valued and monopolies are often regulated.

## Card: Why it matters
Competition affects what you pay and the quality you get for nearly everything. Understanding it explains why some markets serve customers well and others don't, why governments regulate monopolies, and why "more competition" is often seen as good for consumers. It's central to how well markets actually work for people.

## Card: Key terms
- **Competition**: multiple sellers vying for customers.
- **Monopoly**: a single dominant seller with no real rivals.
- **Innovation**: new and better products or methods.
- **Regulation**: government rules to keep markets fair.

## Card: Why competition helps consumers
image: econMarketsCompetitionAndMonopoliesWhyCompetitionHelpsConsumersHero
**Competition** is generally good for buyers. When sellers compete for your business, they're pushed to lower prices, improve quality, and innovate — because if they don't, you'll go elsewhere. Competition keeps sellers honest and customer-focused. This pressure is a big part of why markets can deliver better, cheaper goods over time.

## Card: Why monopolies worry us
A **monopoly** (or near-monopoly) removes that pressure. With no real rivals, a dominant seller can charge higher prices, let quality slip, and innovate less — customers have nowhere else to go. This is why monopolies often face **regulation**: governments may step in to prevent or break them up, or to oversee them, protecting consumers from the downsides of no competition.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming bigger companies are always better for consumers (dominance can reduce the competition that benefits buyers); thinking competition is wasteful rather than beneficial; not seeing why monopolies are regulated; and confusing a popular company with a harmful monopoly (success isn't the issue; lack of competition and abuse of it is).

## Card: Quick recap
Competition — multiple sellers vying for customers — pushes prices down, quality up, and drives innovation, benefiting consumers. A monopoly (one dominant seller) removes that pressure, risking higher prices and worse quality, which is why monopolies are often regulated. The level of competition strongly shapes how well a market serves people.
