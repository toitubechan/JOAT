## Card: What it is
image: showDontTellHero
"**Show, don't tell**" is one of the most famous pieces of writing advice. It means letting readers *experience* a story through vivid details, actions, and senses — rather than just being *told* facts or conclusions. Instead of "she was sad," you show her tears or silence. Understanding this technique transforms flat, distant writing into something vivid and immersive.

## Card: Why it matters
"Show, don't tell" is widely repeated because it genuinely makes writing more engaging and immersive. Telling keeps readers at a distance; showing draws them in to experience the story themselves. Understanding (and applying) it is one of the biggest improvements a developing writer can make. It's the difference between reading *about* a story and feeling like you're *in* it.

## Card: Key terms
- **Showing**: conveying through details, actions, and senses.
- **Telling**: stating facts or conclusions directly.
- **Vivid detail**: specific, sensory details that bring scenes alive.
- **Reader experience**: letting readers feel and infer for themselves.

## Card: Show versus tell
image: sceneSensoryHero
**Telling** states a conclusion: "He was nervous." **Showing** conveys it through **vivid detail** and action: "His hands trembled as he fumbled with the keys." Showing lets readers *experience* and *infer* — they feel the nervousness rather than just being informed of it. Showing uses specific, sensory details (what's seen, heard, done) to bring scenes alive, drawing readers into the story. It's more engaging because readers participate in making meaning, rather than passively receiving facts.

## Card: When to use each
Important nuance: "show, don't tell" doesn't mean *never* tell. Telling is efficient and useful for quickly conveying unimportant information or skipping over minor moments (you don't need to vividly dramatize every detail). The skill is *balance*: **show** the important, emotional, or vivid moments (where you want readers immersed), and **tell** to move efficiently through the less important parts. Overusing showing everywhere makes writing bloated; the art is knowing which moments deserve to be shown.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: telling everything (stating emotions and conclusions, keeping readers at a distance); the overcorrection of trying to show *everything* (making writing bloated and slow); not using specific sensory detail when showing; and not knowing when each is appropriate. Master the balance: show the important, vivid, emotional moments to immerse readers, and tell to move efficiently through the rest.

## Card: Quick recap
"Show, don't tell" means conveying through vivid details, actions, and senses so readers *experience* the story, rather than just stating facts and conclusions ("she was sad" vs. showing her tears). Showing immerses readers and lets them infer for themselves. But it's about balance — show the important, emotional moments, and tell to move efficiently through minor parts. Mastering this transforms flat writing into something vivid and engaging.
