## Card: What it is
image: gravityHero
**Gravity** is the force that attracts objects with mass toward each other. It's what keeps you on the ground, makes things fall, holds the Moon in orbit, and keeps planets circling the Sun. The same force that drops a dropped cup also shapes the entire cosmos — a beautifully universal idea.

## Card: Why it matters
Gravity is one of the most fundamental forces, governing everything from a falling apple to the structure of galaxies. Understanding it explains everyday experiences and cosmic phenomena alike with one idea. It's a striking example of how a single principle of physics can unite the very small and everyday with the vast and astronomical.

## Card: Key terms
- **Gravity**: the attractive force between objects with mass.
- **Mass**: the amount of matter in an object.
- **Weight**: the force of gravity on an object's mass.
- **Orbit**: a stable path resulting from gravity and motion.

## Card: Mass attracts mass
Every object with **mass** attracts every other — that's **gravity**. The more mass, the stronger the pull, which is why we're drawn to the enormous Earth, not to small objects around us. Earth's gravity pulls everything toward its center, which is why things fall "down." Gravity is always attractive and reaches across any distance, though it weakens with distance.

## Card: Weight, falling, and orbits
image: fallingOrbitGravity
Your **weight** is just the force of Earth's gravity on your **mass** (so you'd weigh less on the smaller Moon, though your mass is unchanged). Gravity makes objects fall — and interestingly, ignoring air resistance, all objects fall at the same rate regardless of weight. Gravity also creates **orbits**: the Moon and planets are essentially "falling" around a larger body, but moving sideways fast enough to keep circling.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: confusing mass (amount of matter) with weight (gravity's pull on it); thinking heavier objects fall faster (they don't, without air resistance); believing there's "no gravity" in space (it's there — astronauts float because they're in free fall, orbiting); and not realizing the same gravity governs both falling cups and planetary orbits.

## Card: Quick recap
Gravity is the attractive force between objects with mass — more mass means more pull, so everything is drawn to massive Earth and falls "down." Weight is gravity's pull on your mass. All objects fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance), and orbits are objects "falling" sideways around a larger body. One force, from cups to cosmos.
