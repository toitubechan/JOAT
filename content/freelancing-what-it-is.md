## Card: What it is
image: freelancingHero
**Freelancing** means working for yourself, offering services to clients on a project or contract basis, rather than being an employee of one company. You're effectively running a one-person business — finding your own work, setting your rates, and managing everything yourself. It offers real freedom, with real responsibilities attached.

## Card: Why it matters
Freelancing is increasingly common, and many people consider it for flexibility or extra income. But it's romanticized — the freedom comes with running a business, unstable income, and wearing every hat. Understanding the realistic picture helps you decide if it suits you and succeed if you try it. (General overview, not financial or legal advice.)

## Card: Key terms
- **Freelancer**: a self-employed person offering services to clients.
- **Client**: someone who hires you for a project.
- **Income variability**: the ups and downs of irregular earnings.
- **Self-employment**: working for yourself, not an employer.

## Card: The freedom and the reality
image: freedomRealityHero
Freelancing offers genuine perks: choosing your work, clients, hours, and place. But the flip side is real: you're responsible for *everything* — finding work, doing it, invoicing, taxes, and admin. There's no steady paycheck, paid leave, or employer benefits. The freedom is real, but so is the responsibility; it's a trade, not a free lunch.

## Card: You're running a business
A key mindset shift: as a freelancer, you're not just doing your craft — you're running a **business**. Much of your time goes to non-craft work: finding clients, negotiating, invoicing, bookkeeping, and managing **income variability** (irregular earnings). People who succeed embrace the business side; those who only want to do their craft often struggle with the rest.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: romanticizing freelancing as pure freedom while ignoring the business burden; underestimating income instability and not saving for lean months; only focusing on the craft and neglecting finding clients and admin; and not setting aside money for taxes. Going in with realistic expectations prevents most early freelancing failures.

## Card: Quick recap
Freelancing is working for yourself, offering services to clients — effectively running a one-person business. It offers genuine freedom (your work, hours, clients) but real trade-offs: unstable income, no employer benefits, and doing all the admin and business tasks yourself. Embrace the business side, plan for income ups and downs, and go in with realistic expectations.
