## Card: What it is
image: philosophyMeaningOfLifeHero
"**What is the meaning of life?**" is perhaps the ultimate philosophical question. Thinkers across history have offered very different answers — from religious purposes to creating your own meaning to questioning whether the question even makes sense. There's no single agreed answer, but exploring the debate is genuinely enriching and clarifying.

## Card: Why it matters
This question touches everyone at some point — why are we here, what's the point, how should we live? Engaging with the different perspectives doesn't hand you a final answer, but it can clarify your own values, deepen your thinking, and offer perspective and comfort. It's the question philosophy was perhaps made for, and pondering it is part of a reflective life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Meaning of life**: the purpose or significance of existence.
- **Given meaning**: purpose from an external source (like religion).
- **Created meaning**: meaning you make for yourself.
- **Nihilism**: the view that life has no inherent meaning.

## Card: Different answers
image: philosophyMeaningOfLifeDifferentAnswersHero
Major perspectives differ sharply. Some find **given meaning** — purpose handed by a religious or external source, a reason we exist beyond ourselves. Others argue for **created meaning** — that there's no pre-set purpose, so we *make* our own meaning through what we choose to value, love, and pursue (a view common in existentialism). **Nihilism** holds that life has no inherent meaning at all. And some question whether "the meaning of life" is even a well-formed question. Each view has thoughtful defenders.

## Card: Meaning in practice
Beyond grand theories, many thinkers note that meaning is often found in *concrete* things: relationships, contributing to others, pursuing what you care about, growth, and experiences. Whether or not life has *given* meaning, people across views find that engaging deeply with life — connection, purpose, creativity — *feels* meaningful. So the question isn't only abstract; it connects to how you actually live and what you find worthwhile day to day.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: expecting one definitive answer (there isn't a settled one); assuming the only options are "given meaning" or "no meaning" (created meaning is a major view); thinking the question is pointless (exploring it clarifies values and enriches life); and treating it as purely abstract (it connects to how you live). The value lies in thoughtfully engaging the question, not in a final answer.

## Card: Quick recap
"What is the meaning of life?" has no single agreed answer. Major views: meaning given by an external source (religion), meaning we create for ourselves (existentialism), nihilism (no inherent meaning), and questioning the question itself. Many find meaning concretely — in relationships, purpose, and growth. Exploring the debate clarifies your values and enriches life, even without a final answer. The engagement is the point.
