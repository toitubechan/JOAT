## Card: What it is
image: mathAlgebraHero
**Algebra** is the branch of math that uses letters (like x) to stand for unknown or changing numbers, letting you solve problems and describe general relationships. Many people find it abstract, but its purpose is practical: algebra is a tool for finding unknowns and expressing patterns that work for *any* number, not just specific ones.

## Card: Why it matters
Algebra often feels pointless in school ("when will I use x?"), but it underpins science, technology, finance, and everyday problem-solving. Understanding *what it's for* — finding unknowns and describing general rules — makes it click as a useful tool rather than abstract symbol-shuffling. The purpose is the key to appreciating it.

## Card: Key terms
- **Algebra**: math using symbols for unknown or changing values.
- **Variable**: a letter standing for an unknown or changing number.
- **Equation**: a statement that two things are equal.
- **Generalization**: a rule that works for any number.

## Card: Finding the unknown
image: mathAlgebraFindingTheUnknownHero
One core use of algebra is finding an **unknown**. When you don't know a number but know how it relates to others, you can represent it with a letter (a **variable**) and use an **equation** to solve for it. Everyday example: "I have a budget of 100, spent 60, how much is left?" — algebra formalizes solving for the unknown. It's a systematic way to figure out missing values from what you do know.

## Card: Describing general rules
Algebra's other power is **generalization** — expressing a rule that works for *any* number, not just specific ones. For example, "the area of a rectangle is length times width" works for every rectangle, written with letters. This lets us capture patterns and relationships compactly and apply them universally. Formulas in science, finance, and engineering are algebra doing exactly this — describing how things relate, in general.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking algebra is pointless symbol-shuffling (it's a practical tool for finding unknowns and expressing general rules); fearing the letters (they just stand for numbers); not seeing its real-world uses; and missing that formulas everywhere are algebra. Once you see algebra as "solve for the unknown" and "express a rule for any number," its purpose becomes clear.

## Card: Quick recap
Algebra uses letters to stand for unknown or changing numbers, for two main purposes: *finding unknowns* (solving for a missing value from what you know) and *describing general rules* (a formula that works for any number, like a rectangle's area). It underpins science, finance, and problem-solving. Seeing its purpose — not just the symbols — is what makes algebra click as a genuinely useful tool.
