## Card: What it is
image: mealPlanningHero
**Meal planning** is deciding in advance what you'll eat over a period — usually a week — before you shop or cook. It can be as simple as a rough list of dinners. The point isn't rigid control; it's removing the daily "what's for dinner?" scramble that drains time, money, and willpower.

## Card: Why it matters
Without a plan, meals are decided when you're tired and hungry — the worst time, leading to takeaways, waste, and stress. A little planning means you shop smarter, waste less food, eat better, and reclaim mental energy. It's one of the highest-return habits in everyday life, for surprisingly little effort.

## Card: Key terms
- **Meal plan**: a rough schedule of what you'll eat.
- **Decision fatigue**: the drain of making endless small choices.
- **Food waste**: food bought but thrown away unused.
- **Batch cooking**: making several meals' worth at once.

## Card: Beating decision fatigue
image: decisionFatiguePlan
Deciding what to eat every single day, repeatedly, causes **decision fatigue** — and tired decisions tend to be poor ones (ordering in, grabbing junk). A plan makes the decisions *once*, in advance, when you're thinking clearly. Then each day you simply follow it, freeing energy for everything else. The plan does the deciding for you.

## Card: Saving money and food
Planning means you buy what you'll actually use, so less rots in the fridge — directly cutting **food waste** and grocery bills. You also make fewer impulse purchases and emergency takeaways. A rough plan plus a matching shopping list is one of the simplest ways to spend less on food while eating better.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: making an overly rigid, ambitious plan that collapses; planning meals with no overlap in ingredients, so nothing gets used up; not checking what you already have first; and abandoning the plan after one off day. A loose, realistic plan you actually follow beats a perfect one you don't.

## Card: Quick recap
Meal planning means roughly deciding meals in advance, which beats deciding when tired and hungry. It fights decision fatigue, cuts food waste and cost, and reduces stress. Keep it loose and realistic, plan around ingredients that overlap, and check what you already have. Small effort, big everyday payoff.
