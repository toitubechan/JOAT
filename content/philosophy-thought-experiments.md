## Card: What it is
image: philosophyThoughtExperimentsHero
A **thought experiment** is an imaginary scenario used to explore a philosophical idea or test our intuitions. Philosophers can't always run real experiments on questions about ethics, reality, or the mind — so they use vivid hypotheticals instead. Famous thought experiments make abstract ideas concrete and reveal surprising things about how we think.

## Card: Why it matters
Thought experiments are a powerful, accessible way into deep ideas — they're memorable, engaging, and reveal hidden assumptions in our thinking. They're how many philosophical insights are explored and debated. Understanding them gives you intriguing mental tools and a window into some of the most famous ideas in philosophy. They make hard questions vivid and fun to ponder.

## Card: Key terms
- **Thought experiment**: an imaginary scenario probing an idea.
- **Intuition**: our gut sense of what's right or true.
- **Hypothetical**: an imagined "what if" situation.
- **Testing ideas**: using scenarios to examine our reasoning.

## Card: How they work
image: philosophyThoughtExperimentsHowTheyWorkHero
A **thought experiment** poses a vivid **hypothetical** — "imagine if..." — designed to **test ideas** or **intuitions**. By imagining the scenario and noticing our reactions, we uncover assumptions, contradictions, or insights we'd otherwise miss. For example, scenarios about identity ("if you replaced every part of a ship, is it the same ship?") probe what makes something stay "itself" over time. They turn abstract questions into something we can vividly reason about.

## Card: Famous examples
Some celebrated thought experiments: the *trolley problem* (would you sacrifice one to save five? — probing ethics), the *brain in a vat* (how do you know reality is real and not a simulation? — probing knowledge), the *ship of Theseus* (replace every part — is it still the same ship? — probing identity), and *Mary's room* (probing consciousness and experience). Each uses a memorable scenario to crack open a deep question and reveal how slippery our intuitions can be.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: dismissing thought experiments as silly because they're imaginary (the *point* is to test ideas, not be realistic); expecting them to have one "right" answer (they often reveal that our intuitions conflict); treating them as mere riddles rather than tools for insight; and missing what they're actually probing. Thought experiments are serious, illuminating tools dressed up as intriguing scenarios.

## Card: Quick recap
Thought experiments are imaginary scenarios used to explore philosophical ideas and test our intuitions, making abstract questions vivid and concrete. Famous ones — the trolley problem (ethics), brain in a vat (knowledge/reality), ship of Theseus (identity) — crack open deep questions and reveal how our intuitions can conflict. They're accessible, memorable tools for thinking, not mere riddles.
