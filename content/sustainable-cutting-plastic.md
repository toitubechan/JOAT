## Card: What it is
image: sustainCuttingPlasticHero
**Cutting plastic use** means reducing how much plastic — especially single-use plastic — you consume and discard. Plastic is useful but persistent: it lasts for centuries and much of it isn't recycled. Reducing it at the source is far more effective than trying to recycle your way out.

## Card: Why it matters
Plastic pollution is a major environmental problem — it litters land and oceans, breaks into microplastics, and most is never recycled. Cutting your plastic use reduces this at the source. Since plastic is so woven into daily life, even modest reductions add up, and the habit shifts you toward more durable, reusable alternatives.

## Card: Key terms
- **Single-use plastic**: plastic used once then discarded.
- **Microplastics**: tiny plastic fragments that spread everywhere.
- **Reusable alternative**: a durable item replacing a disposable one.
- **Source reduction**: cutting use rather than just recycling.

## Card: Target single-use plastic
image: sustainCuttingPlasticTargetSingleUsePlasticHero
The biggest, easiest target is **single-use plastic** — bags, bottles, cups, straws, cutlery, packaging used once and binned. Swapping these for **reusable alternatives** (a refillable bottle, cloth bags, a reusable cup) eliminates a stream of waste with one-time changes. Most everyday plastic waste is single-use, so this is where cutting back delivers the most.

## Card: Why recycling isn't enough
A sobering fact: most plastic is *not* recycled, and plastic can only be recycled a limited number of times before degrading. Recycling also produces **microplastics** issues and doesn't solve the root problem. This is why **source reduction** — using less plastic in the first place — matters more than relying on recycling to handle it after the fact.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: assuming recycling solves plastic (most isn't recycled); buying expensive "eco" replacements you don't need (reuse what you have first); trying to go zero-plastic overnight and giving up; and overlooking hidden single-use plastic in packaging. Start with easy single-use swaps, use what you already own, and build the habit gradually.

## Card: Quick recap
Cutting plastic use means reducing it at the source, especially single-use plastic — bags, bottles, cups, packaging — by switching to reusable alternatives. Recycling isn't enough, since most plastic isn't recycled. Start with easy swaps, reuse what you have, and build gradually. Source reduction beats recycling for tackling plastic.
