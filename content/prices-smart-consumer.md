## Card: What it is
image: econPricesSmartConsumerHero
Being a **smart consumer** means using your understanding of prices to get genuine value and avoid being manipulated. It pulls together everything about why prices change into practical habits for spending wisely. The goal isn't to be cheap — it's to pay fairly for what you actually value.

## Card: Why it matters
You make countless buying decisions, and small improvements in how you judge prices add up to real money over time. Being a savvy consumer protects you from marketing tricks, helps you time purchases, and ensures your money goes toward genuine value. It's the practical payoff of understanding prices.

## Card: Key terms
- **Value**: worth relative to price, not just low price.
- **Comparison shopping**: checking prices across sellers.
- **Timing**: buying when prices are favorable.
- **Need vs. want**: distinguishing essential from optional.

## Card: Judge value, not just price
image: econPricesSmartConsumerJudgeValueNotJustPriceHero
Smart shopping isn't about always buying the cheapest — it's about **value**: getting genuine worth for what you pay. Sometimes paying more for quality or durability is the better value; sometimes the cheap option is fine. Ask "is this worth this price *to me*?" rather than chasing low prices or being dazzled by expensive brands. Value is the real measure, not price alone.

## Card: Practical smart-shopping habits
A few habits pay off: **comparison shopping** (checking prices across sellers, since the same item varies); good **timing** (buying off-season, after newer models launch, or during genuine sales); resisting impulse buys and pricing tricks by pausing; and separating **need from want** before buying. Together these ensure you pay fairly and buy what genuinely serves you.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: chasing the lowest price regardless of value or quality; impulse-buying without comparing or pausing; falling for pricing tricks (charm prices, fake urgency); buying at peak prices when waiting would save money; and confusing wants with needs. Smart consuming is about deliberate, value-focused decisions, not reflexive bargain-hunting or impulse.

## Card: Quick recap
A smart consumer judges value (worth for the price), not just low price — sometimes quality is worth more, sometimes cheap is fine. Practical habits: comparison shop, time purchases well, pause to resist tricks and impulse, and separate needs from wants. The goal is paying fairly for genuine value, which adds up to real money over time.
