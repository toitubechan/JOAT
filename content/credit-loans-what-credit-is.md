## Card: What it is
image: creditHero
**Credit** is the ability to use money now and pay it back later. When a lender gives you credit, they're trusting you to repay — usually with **interest**, a fee for the privilege. A loan, a credit card, even a phone plan paid monthly: all are forms of credit. At its heart, credit is borrowed trust.

## Card: Why it matters
Credit lets you handle big costs before you've saved the full amount — a home, a car, an education. Used well, it's a powerful tool. Used carelessly, it's a trap. And because lenders track how reliably you repay, your past behavior shapes what credit you can get in the future.

## Card: Key terms
- **Credit**: using money now to repay later.
- **Lender**: the bank or company providing the money.
- **Borrower**: you, the one receiving and repaying it.
- **Interest**: the fee charged for borrowing.

## Card: How credit works
image: borrowRepayFlow
A **lender** gives you money or lets you delay payment, trusting you'll repay on agreed terms. You repay the original amount plus **interest** over time. The lender profits from the interest; you get to use the money early. The deal only works if both sides keep their end — which is why reliability matters so much.

## Card: Building a track record
Every time you borrow and repay on time, you prove you're trustworthy — and lenders notice. This track record, your **credit history**, follows you. A good one unlocks bigger loans at lower rates; a poor one means higher costs or refusals. Credit, in this sense, is a reputation you build through consistent repayment.

## Card: Common mistakes
A frequent mistake is treating credit as "extra money" rather than borrowed money you must repay with interest. Another is taking on credit with no plan for repayment, assuming future-you will manage. And some avoid all credit forever, which feels safe but leaves them with no track record when they genuinely need to borrow.

## Card: Quick recap
Credit is using money now and repaying later, usually with interest — essentially borrowed trust. It lets you afford big things early, but it's borrowed, not free. Repaying reliably builds a reputation that unlocks better terms later. Use it as a planned tool, never as bonus cash.
