## Card: What it is
image: understandingAudienceHero
The foundation of persuasion is **understanding your audience** — what they care about, believe, fear, and want. You can't convince someone effectively without first understanding their perspective. Persuasion starts not with your argument, but with genuine insight into the person you're trying to persuade.

## Card: Why it matters
The same argument that convinces one person leaves another cold, because people are moved by different things. Tailoring your case to what *this* person values is what makes persuasion work. Skipping this step — and just pushing your own reasons — is why so many well-intentioned attempts to convince people fall flat.

## Card: Key terms
- **Audience understanding**: knowing what your listener cares about.
- **Their motivations**: what drives the person you're persuading.
- **Common ground**: shared values or goals to build from.
- **Objections**: the concerns or resistance they may have.

## Card: Start with their motivations
image: audienceMotivationsHero
Before making your case, understand **their motivations** — what does this person actually care about? People act for *their* reasons, not yours. If you want to persuade someone to do something, frame it in terms of what *they* value (their goals, fears, interests), not just why *you* want it. The most persuasive case is the one that speaks to the listener's own motivations.

## Card: Find common ground and anticipate objections
Look for **common ground** — shared values or goals — to build from; people are more open when they feel you're on the same side. And anticipate their **objections** — the concerns or resistance they'll have — so you can address them. Understanding and acknowledging someone's reservations, rather than ignoring them, makes them far more open to your view.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: launching into your argument without understanding the other person; framing everything in terms of what *you* want; ignoring their concerns and objections rather than addressing them; and assuming everyone is persuaded by the same things you are. Understand them first, and your persuasion becomes targeted instead of generic.

## Card: Quick recap
Persuasion starts with understanding your audience — their motivations, values, and concerns — not with your argument. Frame your case in terms of what *they* care about, find common ground to build from, and anticipate and address their objections. You can't persuade someone you don't first understand.
