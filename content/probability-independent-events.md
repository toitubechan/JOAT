## Card: What it is
image: dataProbabilityIndependentEventsHero
**Independent events** are those where one outcome doesn't affect the next — like coin flips or dice rolls. A key truth that trips up almost everyone: independent events have no "memory." The coin doesn't know it just landed heads five times. Understanding independence corrects one of the most common reasoning errors about chance.

## Card: Why it matters
Misunderstanding independence leads to costly errors — especially in gambling, but also in everyday reasoning about luck and chance. Grasping that independent events don't influence each other protects you from a whole family of false beliefs (like "I'm due for a win"). It's one of the most practically important ideas in probability.

## Card: Key terms
- **Independent events**: outcomes that don't affect each other.
- **Dependent events**: outcomes that do affect each other.
- **No memory**: independent events aren't influenced by past results.
- **Gambler's fallacy**: wrongly thinking past results change future odds.

## Card: Independent events have no memory
image: dataProbabilityIndependentEventsIndependentEventsHaveNoMemoryHero
With **independent events**, each outcome is unaffected by previous ones. A fair coin has a 50% chance of heads on *every* flip — even after five heads in a row, the next flip is still 50/50. The coin has **no memory** of past flips. This feels wrong to our intuition, which expects things to "balance out" soon, but the odds genuinely reset each time.

## Card: The gambler's fallacy
The classic error is the **gambler's fallacy**: believing that after a run of one outcome, the other is "due." "Red has come up five times, so black must be next!" — but on independent spins, black is no more likely than before. Casinos profit from this fallacy. The opposite error also exists: thinking a streak will continue ("hot hand"). With independent events, neither past streaks nor "being due" affect the next outcome.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: the gambler's fallacy (thinking past results make an outcome "due"); the reverse, expecting streaks to continue; confusing independent events with dependent ones (where outcomes *do* affect each other, like drawing cards without replacing them); and trusting the gut feeling that things must "even out" soon. Independent events simply have no memory.

## Card: Quick recap
Independent events (coin flips, dice rolls) don't affect each other — they have no memory, so each outcome's odds reset regardless of the past. After five heads, the next flip is still 50/50. The gambler's fallacy — thinking a result is "due" after a streak — is wrong and costly. Past results never change the odds of independent future events.
