## Card: What it is
image: filmStorytellingOnScreenHero
**Storytelling on screen** is how films tell stories using the unique tools of the medium — images, sound, performance, editing, and time — rather than the words of a novel. Film tells stories *differently* from books, primarily by *showing*. Understanding screen storytelling reveals how movies move us and why "show, don't tell" is even more central to film than to writing.

## Card: Why it matters
Film is one of the most powerful storytelling mediums, and it tells stories in its own distinctive way. Understanding how screen storytelling works deepens appreciation of films and is essential for anyone making video. It reveals why some films grip us and others don't, and how the medium's unique tools create emotion and meaning beyond what words alone can do.

## Card: Key terms
- **Screen storytelling**: telling stories through film's unique tools.
- **Visual storytelling**: conveying story through images, not words.
- **Show, don't tell**: revealing through action and image rather than exposition.
- **Combined tools**: image, sound, performance, and editing working together.

## Card: Show, don't tell — on screen
image: filmStorytellingOnScreenShowDonTTellOnHero
The principle "**show, don't tell**" is even more central to film than to writing, because film is fundamentally a *visual* medium. Rather than narrating "she was heartbroken," a film *shows* it — through the actor's face, the lighting, the music, a lingering shot. Great screen storytelling conveys emotion and meaning through what we *see and hear*, not through characters explaining things. Over-relying on dialogue to explain (rather than showing) is a common weakness; the best films trust the visuals.

## Card: All the tools at once
Film tells stories by **combining tools** that work together: images (what's shown and how it's framed), sound and music (shaping emotion), performance (actors conveying inner life), editing (how shots are arranged and paced), and the use of time. These layers combine to create an experience more immediate and emotional than words alone. This is film's unique power — and its storytelling craft lies in orchestrating all these elements together to move the audience.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking film tells stories the same way books do (film is primarily visual — showing, not narrating); over-relying on dialogue to explain everything (weak screen storytelling); ignoring how image, sound, and editing combine to tell the story; and underrating performance and the visual. Screen storytelling shows rather than tells, orchestrating image, sound, performance, and editing together.

## Card: Quick recap
Storytelling on screen uses film's unique tools — images, sound, performance, editing, and time — to tell stories visually rather than through a novel's words. "Show, don't tell" is even more central here, since film is fundamentally visual: great films convey emotion through what we see and hear, not characters explaining. These tools combine to create an immediate, emotional experience. Screen storytelling's craft is orchestrating them together.
