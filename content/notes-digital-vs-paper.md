## Card: What it is
image: growthNotesDigitalVsPaperHero
Notes can be taken on **paper** or **digitally**, and each has real strengths. There's no universally "best" option — the right choice depends on your purpose, situation, and preference. Understanding the trade-offs helps you choose well, or sensibly use both.

## Card: Why it matters
People often default to one without thinking, missing the benefits of the other. Knowing the trade-offs lets you pick the tool that genuinely fits — better notes, less friction. For some tasks paper wins; for others digital does. The goal is matching the tool to the job, not loyalty to one.

## Card: Key terms
- **Digital notes**: notes on a device or app.
- **Paper notes**: handwritten notes.
- **Searchability**: how easily you can find a note later.
- **Retention**: how well you remember what you noted.

## Card: The case for paper
image: growthNotesDigitalVsPaperTheCaseForPaperHero
**Paper** has surprising strengths: handwriting is slower, which forces you to summarize and process (often improving **retention** and understanding). It has no distractions or notifications, is flexible for diagrams and free-form thinking, and never runs out of battery. For learning and focused thinking, paper often has the edge.

## Card: The case for digital
**Digital notes** win on practicality: they're **searchable** (find anything instantly), easy to organize, edit, and back up, syncable across devices, and can hold images, links, and audio. For large volumes of notes, reference material, and anything you'll search later, digital is hard to beat. The trade-off is distraction risk from the device.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: using digital for everything but getting distracted by the device; using paper for everything, then being unable to find old notes; typing notes verbatim (fast but shallow processing); and not considering that you can use both — paper for focused learning, digital for reference and search. Match the tool to the task.

## Card: Quick recap
Paper notes aid retention through slower, processed handwriting and avoid distractions — great for learning and focused thinking. Digital notes win on searchability, organization, backup, and capacity — great for reference and volume. Neither is universally best; match the tool to the task, and feel free to use both.
