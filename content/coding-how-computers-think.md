## Card: What it is
image: computerThinkingHero
Computers don't "think" like humans at all — they follow simple instructions extremely fast, using basic logic and switches that are either on or off. Understanding this reveals that a computer's apparent intelligence comes from doing simple things at incredible speed and scale, not from genuine understanding. It's the foundation for understanding all computing.

## Card: Why it matters
We often imagine computers as smart, thinking machines, which leads to confusion. Understanding how they *actually* work — fast, literal, logical instruction-following — demystifies technology and is the foundation for learning to code. It also clarifies what computers are good at and where they fail, which is genuinely useful.

## Card: Key terms
- **Binary**: the on/off (1/0) system computers use.
- **Logic**: simple true/false reasoning.
- **Instructions**: the steps a computer executes.
- **Speed and scale**: doing simple things incredibly fast and often.

## Card: On and off, true and false
At the most basic level, computers work with **binary** — everything reduces to switches that are either on or off (represented as 1 or 0). From this simple on/off foundation, computers build up **logic**: simple true/false operations (like "if this is true, do that"). Astonishingly, all the complex things computers do are built from billions of these tiny, simple on/off logical operations.

## Card: Simple steps, incredible speed
image: simpleStepsSpeedHero
A computer follows **instructions** one step at a time, and each step is simple. Its power comes from **speed and scale** — it performs billions of these simple operations per second, flawlessly and tirelessly. So a computer isn't "smart"; it's a fast, literal, logical follower of instructions. What looks like intelligence is really vast numbers of simple steps executed at incredible speed.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking computers "think" or understand like humans (they follow simple logic fast, without understanding); imagining they're creative or intuitive (they're literal and logical); not realizing everything reduces to on/off binary; and attributing intelligence to what's really speed and scale. Knowing computers are fast, literal logic-followers clarifies both their power and their limits.

## Card: Quick recap
Computers don't think like humans — they follow simple instructions extremely fast, built on binary (on/off switches) and basic true/false logic. Their apparent intelligence comes from doing billions of simple operations per second flawlessly — speed and scale, not understanding. They're fast, literal, logical instruction-followers. Grasping this is the foundation for understanding computing and learning to code.
