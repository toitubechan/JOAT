## Card: What it is
image: langWritingSystemsHero
**Writing systems** are the methods used to represent language in visible form. They're not all alike: some use letters for sounds (like the alphabet you're reading), others use symbols for whole words or syllables. Writing was a revolutionary human invention, and understanding its different systems reveals the variety of ways humans have captured language in symbols.

## Card: Why it matters
Writing transformed human civilization — letting knowledge be recorded, stored, and passed across time and distance. Understanding that writing systems differ (and that ours is just one approach) broadens your view of language and culture. It also reveals what a remarkable invention writing was, underpinning history, science, and culture itself.

## Card: Key terms
- **Writing system**: a method of representing language visibly.
- **Alphabet**: symbols representing individual sounds.
- **Logographic**: symbols representing whole words or meanings.
- **Syllabary**: symbols representing syllables.

## Card: Different approaches
image: langWritingSystemsDifferentApproachesHero
Writing systems represent language in different ways. An **alphabet** uses symbols (letters) for individual *sounds*, combined to spell words. A **logographic** system uses symbols that represent whole *words or meanings* (so there are many symbols to learn). A **syllabary** uses symbols for *syllables*. Each approach is a different solution to the same challenge — capturing spoken language in visible symbols. The system you grew up with is just one of several human inventions.

## Card: Writing as a revolution
Writing was one of humanity's most transformative inventions. Before it, knowledge depended on memory and speech, vanishing with each generation. Writing let people *record* information — laws, stories, accounts, discoveries — to be preserved and shared across time and distance. This enabled history, accumulated knowledge, complex societies, and eventually science. Different cultures invented writing independently, in different systems, but all unlocked this same revolutionary power to store and transmit knowledge.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking all writing works like an alphabet (some systems use symbols for whole words or syllables); assuming one writing system is "advanced" and others "primitive" (each is a valid solution suited to its language); not realizing how revolutionary writing was; and confusing language with writing (a language can exist spoken without writing). Writing systems are diverse human inventions for capturing language visibly.

## Card: Quick recap
Writing systems represent language in visible form, in different ways: alphabets (symbols for sounds), logographic systems (symbols for whole words/meanings), and syllabaries (symbols for syllables). Each is a valid solution to capturing spoken language. Writing was a revolutionary invention — letting knowledge be recorded and passed across time, enabling history and accumulated knowledge. The system you know is just one of several.
