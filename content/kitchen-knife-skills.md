## Card: What it is
image: knifeSkillsHero
**Knife skills** are the basics of holding, using, and caring for a kitchen knife safely and efficiently. Good knife skills make cooking faster, safer, and far less frustrating. The biggest surprise for beginners: a *sharp* knife is safer than a dull one, not more dangerous.

## Card: Why it matters
Chopping is a huge part of cooking, so doing it well speeds everything up and reduces injuries. Most kitchen cuts come from poor technique or dull knives, not sharp ones. A little knowledge of grip, board setup, and the "claw" hand position prevents most accidents and builds real confidence.

## Card: Key terms
- **Chef's knife**: the main all-purpose kitchen knife.
- **The claw**: curling fingertips under to protect them while cutting.
- **Sharp vs. dull**: a sharp knife cuts predictably; a dull one slips.
- **Pinch grip**: holding the blade, not just the handle, for control.

## Card: Sharp is safe
Counterintuitive but true: a **sharp** knife is safer. A dull one needs force and slips off food unpredictably — toward your fingers. A sharp one glides through cleanly and predictably, so you stay in control. Keeping knives sharp (and using the right one) is a genuine safety measure, not just about performance.

## Card: Safe technique
image: safeKnifeTechnique
Two key habits. Use the **claw**: curl the fingertips of your guiding hand under, knuckles facing the blade, so the flat of the knife rests against your knuckles — fingertips stay tucked safely away. And steady your **cutting board** (a damp cloth underneath stops it sliding). A pinch grip on the blade gives more control than gripping only the handle.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: using a dull knife and forcing it; flat fingertips in the path of the blade instead of the claw; a board sliding on the counter; cutting toward yourself; and trying to rush before the technique is comfortable. Slow, correct cuts build the muscle memory that later becomes fast and safe.

## Card: Quick recap
Good knife skills mean cutting safely and efficiently: keep knives sharp (safer than dull), use the claw grip to protect fingertips, steady the board with a damp cloth, and never cut toward yourself. Start slow to build technique. Sharp knife, claw hand, stable board — that's the safe foundation.
