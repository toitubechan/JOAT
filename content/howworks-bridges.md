## Card: What it is
image: howworksBridgesHero
**Bridges** stay up by cleverly managing forces — directing the weight of the bridge and its traffic safely down to the ground. Different bridge designs handle these forces in different ways. Understanding the basic idea reveals the elegant engineering behind structures we cross without a second thought.

## Card: Why it matters
Bridges are everyday engineering marvels we rarely think about. Understanding how they stay up reveals fundamental engineering principles — managing forces — that apply to all structures. It turns a routine crossing into an appreciation of clever design, and explains why bridges come in such different shapes.

## Card: Key terms
- **Force**: a push or pull acting on the structure.
- **Compression**: a squeezing/pushing force.
- **Tension**: a stretching/pulling force.
- **Load**: the weight a bridge must support.

## Card: Managing forces
image: howworksBridgesManagingForcesHero
A bridge must support its own weight plus the **load** of traffic, without collapsing. The key is managing two main **forces**: **compression** (squeezing/pushing) and **tension** (stretching/pulling). A good bridge design channels the loads into materials and shapes that handle these forces well, ultimately directing all the weight safely down into the ground (the supports and foundations). Stay within what the materials can bear, and the bridge holds.

## Card: Why bridges have different shapes
Different bridge types manage forces differently. A simple **beam bridge** rests on supports but can only span short distances. An **arch bridge** channels weight outward and down through the curve, using compression. A **suspension bridge** hangs the roadway from cables under tension, letting it span huge distances. Each design is a different clever solution to handling forces over a given gap — which is why bridges look so varied.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking bridges stay up just by being strong/heavy (it's about cleverly directing forces, not brute strength); not realizing different shapes handle forces in different ways; imagining all bridges work the same; and overlooking that the load must ultimately reach the ground. Bridges are about elegantly managing compression and tension, not just raw material strength.

## Card: Quick recap
Bridges stay up by managing forces — directing their own weight plus traffic loads safely down to the ground, handling compression (pushing) and tension (pulling) within what materials can bear. Different designs solve this differently: beam bridges for short spans, arches channeling force through a curve, suspension bridges hanging the road from tension cables for huge spans. It's elegant force management, not brute strength.
