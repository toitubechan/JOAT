## Card: What it is
image: validatingIdeaHero
**Validating** a business idea means testing whether people actually want it — *before* you invest lots of time and money building it. Instead of assuming your idea is great, you gather real evidence of demand. It's one of the most important (and most skipped) steps in starting a business, and it can save you from building something nobody wants.

## Card: Why it matters
A huge proportion of businesses fail because they build something there's no real demand for. Validation is the cure: it tests demand cheaply and early, before you've sunk everything in. Understanding how to validate dramatically improves your chances of success and saves enormous wasted effort and money. It's the difference between hoping and knowing.

## Card: Key terms
- **Validation**: testing whether real demand exists before building.
- **Assumptions**: things you believe but haven't confirmed.
- **Real evidence**: actual proof of demand, not just opinions.
- **Minimum viable product (MVP)**: the simplest version to test the idea.

## Card: Test before you build
image: testBeforeBuildHero
The core principle: don't assume people want your idea — *test* it. Founders often fall in love with an idea, build it fully, then discover no one wants it. Instead, identify your key **assumptions** (especially "people will want and pay for this") and test them cheaply *first*. The goal is **real evidence** of demand before committing major time and money. It's far cheaper to discover a flaw through a quick test than after building the whole thing.

## Card: How to validate cheaply
Ways to gather **real evidence** cheaply: talk to potential customers about their problem (and listen, rather than pitching); see if people will actually commit (pre-orders, sign-ups, or payment are far stronger signals than "yes, I'd buy that"); and build a **minimum viable product (MVP)** — the simplest possible version — to test real interest. The strongest validation is people actually paying or committing, not just saying they like the idea. Actions beat opinions.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: skipping validation and building the full thing on assumption; trusting polite encouragement ("great idea!") as proof of demand (people are nice; real commitment is the test); asking leading questions instead of genuinely listening; and only seeking confirmation rather than honestly testing. Validate with real evidence — especially actual commitment or payment — before investing heavily.

## Card: Quick recap
Validating an idea means testing whether people genuinely want it *before* building it fully — gathering real evidence of demand rather than assuming. Founders often build first and discover too late that no one wants it. Test cheaply: talk to customers, seek real commitment (pre-orders, payment, sign-ups), and build a minimal version (MVP). Actions beat opinions; polite encouragement isn't proof. Validation saves enormous wasted effort.
