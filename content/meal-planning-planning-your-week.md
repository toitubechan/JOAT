## Card: What it is
image: weeklyMealPlanHero
Planning your week is the practical "how" of meal planning — a simple, repeatable method to map out meals without it becoming a chore. The aim is a quick, flexible plan that fits your real life, not a rigid timetable you'll resent and abandon.

## Card: Why it matters
A method makes planning fast and sustainable. Many people like the *idea* of meal planning but find it fiddly; a simple routine removes that friction. With a repeatable approach, the whole week's food becomes a 10-minute task that saves hours and stress later.

## Card: Key terms
- **Inventory check**: seeing what you already have.
- **Anchor meals**: a few reliable go-to dishes.
- **Theme nights**: assigning a loose theme to each day.
- **Flexibility**: leaving room for changes and eating out.

## Card: A simple weekly method
image: inventoryMealList
A quick routine: first, do an **inventory check** — look at what's already in the fridge, freezer, and cupboards to use up. Then pick meals for the week, leaning on a handful of reliable **anchor meals** you know how to make. Finally, build a shopping list from the gaps. That's the whole loop, in about ten minutes.

## Card: Make it easy and flexible
Lower the effort: reuse a rotating set of favorite meals rather than planning every meal from scratch. Some people use **theme nights** (pasta night, stir-fry night) to cut decisions further. And keep **flexibility** — plan most days, not every one, and leave space for leftovers, eating out, or a change of mood. A loose plan survives real life.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: planning every single meal with no slack, so one change derails it; not checking what you already have, leading to waste and duplicate buys; trying brand-new complex recipes every night; and over-planning to perfection until it feels like a burden. Simple and flexible is what lasts.

## Card: Quick recap
Plan your week with a simple loop: check what you already have, choose meals leaning on reliable favorites, then list what to buy. Use theme nights or a rotating set to cut decisions, and keep it flexible — plan most days, not all. A 10-minute routine, kept loose, is what makes meal planning stick.
