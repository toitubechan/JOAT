## Card: What it is
image: reptilesAmphibiansHero
**Reptiles** (like snakes, lizards, turtles, crocodiles) and **amphibians** (like frogs, toads, salamanders) are two groups of cold-blooded vertebrates. Though often grouped together, they're distinct: amphibians typically live partly in water and partly on land, while reptiles are more fully adapted to dry land. Understanding their differences clarifies a commonly confused part of the animal kingdom.

## Card: Why it matters
Reptiles and amphibians are fascinating, ancient, and ecologically important groups, yet they're widely confused. Understanding what distinguishes them — and what "cold-blooded" really means — clears up misconceptions and deepens your understanding of animal diversity and how creatures adapt to land and water.

## Card: Key terms
- **Cold-blooded**: body temperature depends on surroundings.
- **Reptile**: scaly, dry-land vertebrate (snakes, lizards, etc.).
- **Amphibian**: vertebrate living partly in water (frogs, etc.).
- **Metamorphosis**: amphibians transforming as they grow.

## Card: What cold-blooded means
Both groups are **cold-blooded** — but this doesn't mean their blood is literally cold. It means they can't generate their own body heat like mammals and birds; instead, their body temperature depends on their surroundings. This is why you see lizards basking in the sun (to warm up) and why reptiles and amphibians are more common in warm climates and less active in the cold.

## Card: Reptiles vs. amphibians
image: reptileAmphibianCompareHero
The key differences: **Amphibians** (frogs, toads, salamanders) usually start life in water (as tadpoles) and undergo **metamorphosis** to a land-capable adult; they have moist, permeable skin and typically stay near water. **Reptiles** (snakes, lizards, turtles, crocodiles) are more fully adapted to dry land, with dry, scaly skin, and lay eggs on land (or give live birth). Reptiles don't need water the way amphibians do.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking "cold-blooded" means literally cold blood (it means body heat depends on surroundings); lumping reptiles and amphibians as the same (they're distinct groups); confusing the two (a frog is an amphibian, a lizard a reptile); and assuming all lay eggs identically. The water-dependence of amphibians versus the dry-land adaptation of reptiles is the key distinction.

## Card: Quick recap
Reptiles (snakes, lizards, turtles, crocodiles) and amphibians (frogs, toads, salamanders) are both cold-blooded vertebrates — meaning their body temperature depends on their surroundings, not literally cold blood. Amphibians live partly in water, often starting as tadpoles and undergoing metamorphosis, with moist skin. Reptiles are dry-land adapted, with scaly skin. They're distinct, commonly confused groups.
