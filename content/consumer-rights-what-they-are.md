## Card: What it is
image: consumerRightsHero
**Consumer rights** are legal protections you have when buying goods and services. They mean sellers must meet certain standards — products should work, match their description, and be as advertised. Knowing your rights helps you stand up for yourself when something goes wrong. (Exact rights vary by country; this is general knowledge.)

## Card: Why it matters
Many people don't know their consumer rights, so they accept poor treatment, faulty goods, or unfair refusals they could challenge. Understanding the basics empowers you to get refunds, repairs, or replacements you're entitled to. It's practical knowledge that saves money and prevents you from being taken advantage of.

## Card: Key terms
- **Consumer rights**: legal protections for buyers.
- **Fit for purpose**: a product must do what it's meant to.
- **As described**: it must match how it was advertised.
- **Trader/seller**: the business you bought from.

## Card: What you're generally entitled to
In most places, consumer law requires that goods are of satisfactory quality, **fit for purpose** (they do what they're supposed to), and **as described** (matching the advert or label). Services should be carried out with reasonable care and skill. If these aren't met, you typically have the right to a remedy — a repair, replacement, or refund. These protections exist by law, not just seller goodwill.

## Card: Rights vs. goodwill
image: rightsVsGoodwill
A key distinction: some things a seller offers (like a "no-questions-asked" return policy for a change of mind) are **goodwill**, beyond your legal rights. But other things — faulty goods, items not as described — are your *legal rights*, which a seller can't simply refuse. Knowing the difference helps you push firmly when you're legally entitled, and understand when you're asking for a favor.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: not knowing your rights, so accepting "no refunds" when the item is genuinely faulty; confusing goodwill policies with legal rights; assuming "sale" or "no refund" signs override the law (legal rights usually stand regardless); and giving up when first refused. Knowing your rights lets you stand firm when you're entitled.

## Card: Quick recap
Consumer rights are legal protections requiring goods to be satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described, with services done with reasonable care. If not met, you're typically entitled to a repair, replacement, or refund — by law, not just goodwill. Knowing your rights (versus goodwill policies) lets you stand firm. Exact rights vary by country.
