## Card: What it is
image: marketingStorytellingHero
**Storytelling in marketing** is using narrative — characters, struggles, and resolutions — to connect with an audience emotionally, rather than just listing facts and features. Humans are wired for stories, and a good story is far more memorable and persuasive than dry information. Understanding this reveals why the most effective marketing so often tells a story.

## Card: Why it matters
People rarely make decisions on facts alone — emotions and stories drive much of how we connect and choose. Storytelling makes marketing memorable, relatable, and persuasive in a way feature lists never are. Understanding it helps you communicate far more effectively, whether marketing a product, an idea, or yourself. It's one of the most powerful tools in communication.

## Card: Key terms
- **Storytelling**: using narrative to connect and persuade.
- **Emotional connection**: engaging feelings, not just logic.
- **Relatability**: an audience seeing themselves in the story.
- **Features vs. story**: dry facts versus an engaging narrative.

## Card: Why stories work
image: marketingStorytellingWhyStoriesWorkHero
Humans are wired for stories — we remember, relate to, and are moved by narratives far more than by facts or **features**. A good story creates an **emotional connection**, and emotions drive much of human decision-making. Stories are also memorable (we recall narratives long after we forget bullet points) and **relatable** (we see ourselves in them). This is why "facts tell, stories sell" — a compelling story engages people in a way that a list of features simply can't.

## Card: Telling a good marketing story
Effective marketing stories often center the *customer* as the hero (not the product), showing a relatable struggle the audience recognizes, and how things improve — with your product as the helpful guide, not the star. The audience should see *themselves* in the story. A good narrative is authentic, emotionally resonant, and clear. Rather than listing what your product *does*, a story shows what it *means* for someone's life — which is what truly connects and persuades.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: relying only on features and facts (which don't connect emotionally); making the *product* the hero instead of the customer; telling stories that aren't relatable or authentic; and underrating how much emotion and narrative drive decisions. Effective marketing storytelling creates emotional connection by making the audience the hero and showing what your offering means for their life.

## Card: Quick recap
Storytelling in marketing uses narrative to connect emotionally, rather than just listing facts and features — because humans are wired for stories, which are more memorable, relatable, and persuasive ("facts tell, stories sell"). The best marketing stories make the *customer* the hero, show a relatable struggle, and cast your product as the helpful guide. Showing what your offering *means* for someone's life connects far more than feature lists.
