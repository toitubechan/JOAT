## Card: What it is
image: emergencyCallHero
Calling **emergency services** is often the single most important first aid action — it gets trained professionals on the way and gives you expert guidance immediately. Knowing how to call effectively, and what to say, means help arrives faster and you're supported through whatever you're facing.

## Card: Why it matters
In a serious emergency, professional care is what the casualty truly needs, and the sooner it's dispatched, the better the outcome. A clear, calm call saves precious time. The dispatcher can also talk you through what to do — so calling early means you're never facing the situation completely alone.

## Card: Key terms
- **Emergency number**: the local number for urgent help (varies by country).
- **Dispatcher**: the trained person who answers the call.
- **Location**: where help needs to come — the most vital detail.
- **Triage**: how dispatchers prioritize by severity.

## Card: Know your number
**Emergency numbers vary by country** — for example 112 across much of Europe, 911 in North America, 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia. Learn the number(s) for where you live and travel. Many phones can call local emergency services even when locked. Knowing the right number in advance saves fumbling in a crisis.

## Card: What to say
image: locationDetails
Stay as calm as you can and give the key facts clearly: **where** you are (location is the most important detail), **what** happened, **how many** people are hurt, and their condition. Then **don't hang up** — let the **dispatcher** guide you, follow their instructions, and answer their questions. They're trained to help you help.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: not knowing the local emergency number; panicking and giving an unclear location, delaying help; hanging up before the dispatcher has what they need; and hesitating to call at all for fear of "overreacting." It's better to call and be reassured than to delay genuine help. When in doubt, call.

## Card: Quick recap
Calling emergency services fast is often the most important first aid step. Know your local number, give your location first and clearly, describe what happened and the casualty's condition, then stay on the line and follow the dispatcher's instructions. When unsure whether it's serious enough — call anyway.
