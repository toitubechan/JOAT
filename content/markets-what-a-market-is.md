## Card: What it is
image: econMarketsWhatAMarketIsHero
A **market** is anywhere buyers and sellers come together to exchange goods, services, or money. It doesn't have to be a physical place — it can be a shop, a website, a stock exchange, or the whole economy. Markets are the basic mechanism through which most economic activity happens.

## Card: Why it matters
Markets coordinate the vast, complex activity of an economy — getting goods made, priced, and delivered to those who want them, mostly without central direction. Understanding what a market *is*, and how it self-organizes, is foundational to making sense of economics, business, and much of modern life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Market**: where buyers and sellers exchange.
- **Buyer and seller**: the two sides of any exchange.
- **Voluntary exchange**: trade both sides agree to.
- **Invisible hand**: the idea that self-interest can benefit all.

## Card: More than a physical place
image: econMarketsWhatAMarketIsMoreThanAPhysicalPlaceHero
A **market** is any arrangement where **buyers and sellers** exchange — a farmers' market, an online store, a job market, a stock market. The common thread is **voluntary exchange**: both sides trade because each expects to benefit. You give money for bread because you'd rather have the bread; the baker would rather have the money. Both come out ahead, which is why trade happens.

## Card: Self-organizing coordination
Markets achieve something remarkable: they coordinate enormous, complex activity *without* anyone being in charge. Countless individual decisions — what to make, buy, and at what price — self-organize through prices and exchange. This idea, often called the **invisible hand**, describes how people pursuing their own interests can, through markets, end up coordinating to meet society's needs.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a market must be a physical place (it's any buyer-seller arrangement); assuming exchange means one side loses (voluntary trade usually benefits both); imagining the economy is centrally directed (markets largely self-organize); and missing that prices are how this coordination happens. Markets are a decentralized coordination system.

## Card: Quick recap
A market is any arrangement where buyers and sellers exchange — physical or not — through voluntary trade that benefits both sides. Markets coordinate vast, complex economic activity without anyone in charge, through prices and individual decisions (the "invisible hand"). Understanding this self-organizing exchange is foundational to economics.
