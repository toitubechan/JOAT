## Card: What it is
image: mentalSupportNetworkHero
Getting **support** for mental health means reaching out — to people you trust, or to professionals — when you're struggling. Support comes in many forms, from a conversation with a friend to therapy or medical help. Knowing the options, and that help exists, makes reaching out far less daunting.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people suffer longer than they need to because they don't know help is available or feel they should cope alone. As with physical health, earlier support usually makes recovery easier. Knowing when and how to reach out is one of the most valuable things you can learn about mental health.

## Card: Key terms
- **Support network**: trusted people you can turn to.
- **Therapy / counseling**: talking with a trained professional.
- **GP / doctor**: often the first medical point of contact.
- **Crisis line**: urgent help for severe distress.

## Card: When to reach out
Consider seeking support if difficult feelings are persistent, intense, or interfering with daily life — work, relationships, sleep, or enjoyment. You don't need to be in crisis to deserve help; struggling is reason enough. The rule of thumb: if it's affecting your life and not lifting, it's worth talking to someone.

## Card: Where help comes from
image: helpSourcesHero
Support exists at every level. Your **support network** — trusted friends or family — is a powerful first step. A **doctor (GP)** can assess and point you to options. **Therapy or counseling** offers professional, confidential help. For severe distress or thoughts of self-harm, **crisis lines** and emergency services provide urgent support — please use them.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is waiting until things become a crisis before seeking help. Another is believing you should handle it alone or that others have "bigger problems." People also stop at one option that didn't fit (one unhelpful therapist) and give up, when other people and approaches may suit better. Keep reaching.

## Card: Quick recap
Reaching out is a strength. Seek support if difficult feelings are persistent, intense, or affecting your life — you needn't be in crisis to deserve help. Options range from trusted people to doctors, therapy, and crisis lines for emergencies. Don't wait for a crisis, and don't give up if the first option doesn't fit.
