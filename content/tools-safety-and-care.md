## Card: What it is
image: toolSafetyHero
Tool safety is the set of simple habits that prevent injuries, and tool care is what keeps your tools working well for years. Both are easy to overlook in the excitement of a project — but a few basic practices protect both your body and your investment.

## Card: Why it matters
Most tool injuries come from rushing, distraction, or a dull or wrong tool — all avoidable. And neglected tools rust, blunt, and fail, costing money to replace. A little attention to safety and upkeep means fewer accidents and tools that stay reliable, sharp, and ready whenever you need them.

## Card: Key terms
- **PPE**: personal protective equipment, like goggles and gloves.
- **Dull blade**: a blunt edge that's actually more dangerous.
- **Maintenance**: cleaning and caring for tools.
- **Workspace**: a clear, well-lit area to work in.

## Card: Core safety habits
The essentials: wear eye protection (**PPE**) when anything could fly up; keep a clear, well-lit **workspace**; never rush; and cut or push *away* from your body. Keep fingers clear of blades and bits. Unplug or remove batteries from power tools before changing parts. Most accidents trace back to ignoring one of these.

## Card: Why dull is dangerous
image: sharpBladeControl
It sounds backwards, but a **dull blade** is more dangerous than a sharp one. A blunt edge needs more force and slips unpredictably, while a sharp one cuts cleanly and predictably. So keeping cutting tools sharp isn't just about performance — it's a genuine safety measure. The same logic applies to using the *right* tool for the job.

## Card: Common mistakes
The big safety mistakes: skipping eye protection, working in clutter or poor light, rushing, and using a tool for the wrong job (like a knife as a screwdriver). For care: leaving tools dirty or damp so they rust, storing them loose so edges dull, and not unplugging power tools before adjusting them.

## Card: Quick recap
Work safely by wearing eye protection, keeping a clear workspace, never rushing, and cutting away from yourself. Keep blades sharp — dull ones are more dangerous — and use the right tool for each job. Clean and store tools dry to prevent rust. Good habits protect both you and your tools.
