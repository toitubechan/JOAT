## Card: What it is
image: businessIdeaHero
**Finding a business idea** is the starting point of entrepreneurship — but good ideas aren't usually flashes of genius. They typically come from spotting a *problem* people have and a way to solve it. Understanding where real business ideas come from helps you generate ones with genuine potential, rather than waiting for a magical brainwave.

## Card: Why it matters
Many aspiring entrepreneurs get stuck waiting for the "perfect" original idea, or chase ideas with no real demand. Understanding how good ideas actually arise helps you find viable ones grounded in real needs. Since the idea is the foundation of any business, starting from the right place — real problems — dramatically improves your odds.

## Card: Key terms
- **Problem-solving**: building a business around solving a real problem.
- **Market need**: a genuine demand people will pay to satisfy.
- **Opportunity**: a gap where a need isn't well met.
- **Originality myth**: the false idea that businesses need to be totally new.

## Card: Solve a real problem
image: realProblemHero
The best business ideas usually start with a **problem** people genuinely have — something frustrating, missing, or inefficient that they'd pay to solve. Rather than asking "what cool product could I make?", ask "what problem can I solve for people, that they actually care about?" A business exists to meet a **market need**. Ideas grounded in real problems people will pay to fix have far more potential than clever products no one needs.

## Card: You don't need to be original
A common myth (the **originality myth**): that a business idea must be totally new and never-done-before. In reality, most successful businesses *aren't* wholly original — they solve a known problem *better*, cheaper, for a specific audience, or in a new context. Competition can even be a good sign (it shows a real market exists). So look for **opportunities** to do something *better* or *differently*, not just something nobody's ever done.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: waiting for a flash of original genius (good ideas come from spotting problems); building a product no one actually needs (no market need); thinking you must invent something totally new (solving a known problem better works); and falling in love with an idea without checking demand. Start from real problems people will pay to solve, and don't fear that it's "been done."

## Card: Quick recap
Good business ideas come from spotting real problems people will pay to solve, not from flashes of original genius. Ask "what problem can I solve that people care about?" rather than chasing a clever product. You don't need a totally new idea — solving a known problem better, cheaper, or for a specific audience works, and competition shows a real market exists. Ground ideas in genuine market needs.
