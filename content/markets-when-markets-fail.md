## Card: What it is
image: econMarketsWhenMarketsFailHero
Markets are powerful, but they don't always produce good outcomes for society — situations called **market failures**. Understanding where and why markets fall short explains why governments sometimes intervene, and gives a balanced view: markets are excellent tools, but not perfect for everything.

## Card: Why it matters
A balanced understanding of markets includes their limits. Knowing about market failures explains things like pollution, why some goods are publicly provided, and the rationale for regulation. It helps you think critically about economic debates rather than assuming markets are either flawless or worthless. Both extremes miss the nuance.

## Card: Key terms
- **Market failure**: when markets produce poor outcomes for society.
- **Externality**: a cost or benefit affecting others, not in the price.
- **Public good**: something hard for markets to provide (like clean air).
- **Information gap**: when buyers or sellers lack key knowledge.

## Card: Externalities
image: econMarketsWhenMarketsFailExternalitiesHero
A key market failure is the **externality** — a cost or benefit that falls on others but isn't reflected in the price. Classic example: pollution. A factory's product price may not include the cost of the pollution harming the community — so the market over-produces it. Externalities are a major reason for regulation (like pollution rules), to make prices reflect the true costs.

## Card: Public goods and information gaps
Some things are **public goods** — hard for markets to provide because people can benefit without paying (clean air, national defense, street lighting). Markets under-supply these, so governments often provide them. **Information gaps** are another failure: if buyers can't judge quality or safety (medicines, for example), markets need rules and oversight to protect people. These are areas where pure markets fall short.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming markets always produce ideal outcomes (they can fail); the opposite, dismissing markets as always bad (they work very well for most things); not recognizing externalities like pollution as a market failure; and not seeing why some goods are publicly provided. The balanced view: markets are powerful but imperfect, and failures explain interventions.

## Card: Quick recap
Markets are powerful but can fail to serve society well: externalities (costs like pollution not in the price), public goods (under-supplied because people benefit without paying), and information gaps (buyers can't judge quality). These failures explain why governments regulate and provide some things. Markets are excellent tools — but not perfect for everything.
