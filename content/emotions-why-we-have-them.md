## Card: What it is
image: psychEmotionsWhyWeHaveThemHero
Emotions aren't pointless or a design flaw — they evolved because they serve **purposes**. Each emotion helped our ancestors survive and navigate life, and they still guide us today. Understanding *why* we have emotions reveals that even uncomfortable ones have value, which changes how we relate to them.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people wish they could be free of difficult emotions, seeing them as obstacles. Understanding that emotions evolved for good reasons helps you accept and work with them rather than fighting them. It reframes feelings from nuisances into a useful (if sometimes uncomfortable) guidance system you can learn to read.

## Card: Key terms
- **Function**: the useful purpose an emotion serves.
- **Survival value**: how emotions helped our ancestors survive.
- **Social function**: how emotions help us connect and communicate.
- **Guidance**: emotions steering our attention and behavior.

## Card: Emotions helped us survive
image: psychEmotionsWhyWeHaveThemEmotionsHelpedUsSurviveHero
Emotions have deep **survival value**. Fear made our ancestors flee danger; disgust kept them away from things that could make them sick; anger helped them defend themselves and their resources; love and attachment bonded families and groups for protection. These responses were fast and automatic — useful when there's no time to reason. We've inherited this ancient, life-preserving system.

## Card: Emotions guide and connect us
Beyond survival, emotions serve a **social function** and provide **guidance**. They help us connect — empathy, love, and guilt bond us and smooth cooperation. They guide attention and decisions, flagging what matters (you can't decide well with *no* emotional input). And expressed emotions communicate to others (a smile, a frown) faster than words. Far from useless, emotions are a core part of how we think, relate, and live.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: wishing to eliminate emotions as obstacles (they serve real purposes); thinking emotions are the opposite of reason (good decisions need both); seeing uncomfortable emotions as malfunctions (they're often doing their job, flagging something); and underrating how emotions help us connect with others. Emotions are functional, not flaws.

## Card: Quick recap
Emotions evolved because they serve purposes: survival (fear flees danger, disgust avoids sickness), guidance (flagging what matters for decisions), and social connection (empathy and expression bond us). Even uncomfortable emotions are often doing their job. They're not flaws or the enemy of reason — they're a core, useful part of being human.
