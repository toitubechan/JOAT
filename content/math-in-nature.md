## Card: What it is
image: mathInNatureHero
**Math in nature** is the surprising appearance of mathematical patterns throughout the natural world — in flowers, shells, trees, snowflakes, and more. Nature seems to "use" math: recurring shapes, sequences, and proportions appear again and again. Recognizing these patterns reveals a hidden order beneath the natural beauty all around us.

## Card: Why it matters
Seeing math in nature transforms how you view the world — patterns you never noticed become visible everywhere. It bridges abstract math and the tangible natural world, making math feel alive and beautiful rather than dry. It's a delightful reminder that mathematical order underlies the apparent randomness of nature.

## Card: Key terms
- **Pattern**: a regularity that repeats.
- **Symmetry**: balanced, matching arrangement.
- **Fibonacci sequence**: a famous number pattern found in nature.
- **Spiral**: a curve that appears widely in nature.

## Card: Patterns everywhere
image: mathInNaturePatternsEverywhereHero
Nature is full of mathematical **patterns**. **Symmetry** appears in snowflakes, butterfly wings, and flowers. **Spirals** show up in shells, hurricanes, sunflowers, and galaxies. Branching patterns recur in trees, rivers, and even your lungs' airways. The same shapes and structures repeat across wildly different things, hinting that nature follows underlying mathematical rules — efficient forms that arise again and again.

## Card: Famous natural patterns
A celebrated example is the **Fibonacci sequence** — a number pattern (each number is the sum of the two before: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8...) that appears remarkably often in nature, like the arrangement of seeds in a sunflower, petals on flowers, and spirals in pinecones. Related is a special proportion (the golden ratio) linked to many natural forms. These patterns recur because they're often efficient or stable solutions that nature "settles into."

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking math is purely human-made and separate from nature (mathematical patterns pervade the natural world); seeing nature as random rather than patterned; over-claiming (not *everything* in nature is a neat formula — patterns are common but not universal); and not noticing the patterns at all. Math in nature reveals genuine, widespread order beneath natural beauty, without forcing patterns where there are none.

## Card: Quick recap
Math in nature is the recurring appearance of patterns — symmetry (snowflakes, flowers), spirals (shells, galaxies), branching (trees, lungs), and famous sequences like Fibonacci (sunflower seeds, pinecones) — across the natural world. These patterns recur because they're often efficient, stable forms. Recognizing them reveals a hidden mathematical order beneath natural beauty, making math feel alive and wondrous.
