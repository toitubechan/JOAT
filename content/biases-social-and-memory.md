## Card: What it is
image: socialMemoryBiasHero
Biases don't just affect decisions — they shape how we judge *people* and how we remember the *past*. Social biases distort our view of others, and memory biases distort our recollections. Understanding these reveals that even our impressions of people and our memories aren't as objective as they feel.

## Card: Why it matters
Social biases lead to unfair judgments, stereotyping, and conflict. Memory biases mean we misremember and rewrite the past, often without knowing. Understanding both makes you fairer to others and more humble about your own memories and impressions — which improves relationships and decisions alike. They affect how you see everyone and everything you recall.

## Card: Key terms
- **Halo effect**: letting one good trait color our whole view.
- **Fundamental attribution error**: blaming others' character, excusing our own situation.
- **Hindsight bias**: feeling you "knew it all along" after the fact.
- **Misremembering**: memory being reconstructed, not replayed.

## Card: How we judge people
Two social biases. The **halo effect**: one positive trait (attractiveness, confidence) makes us assume someone is good in *other* ways too — we judge a whole person by one feature. The **fundamental attribution error**: we blame others' *character* for their behavior ("they're rude") but excuse our own with *circumstances* ("I was stressed"). Both distort how fairly we judge people.

## Card: How we remember
image: memoryRewriteHero
Memory feels like replaying a recording, but it's actually *reconstructed* each time — and biased. **Hindsight bias** makes us feel we "knew it all along" once we know the outcome, distorting how we judge past decisions. We also unconsciously edit memories to fit our current beliefs and feelings. Your confident memory of an event may differ significantly from what actually happened.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: judging a whole person by one trait (halo effect); harshly blaming others' character while excusing your own circumstances (attribution error); believing outcomes were "obvious" in hindsight; and trusting your memories as perfect recordings. Humility about your impressions of people and the reliability of your memory is the wise correction.

## Card: Quick recap
Biases shape how we see people (the halo effect judges a whole person by one trait; the attribution error blames others' character but excuses our own circumstances) and how we remember (hindsight bias makes outcomes seem obvious; memory is reconstructed, not replayed). Being humble about your impressions and memories makes you fairer and wiser.
