## Card: What it is
image: econMarketsEconomicSystemsHero
Societies organize their economies in different ways, along a spectrum from **market economies** (decisions made by private buyers and sellers) to **planned economies** (decisions made centrally by government). Most real economies are a **mix**. Understanding these systems explains big differences between countries and political debates.

## Card: Why it matters
Economic systems shape how a society produces and distributes everything. Understanding the basic types — and that most economies blend them — helps you make sense of political and economic debates, news about different countries, and arguments about the role of government. It's foundational for being an informed citizen.

## Card: Key terms
- **Market economy**: decisions driven by private supply and demand.
- **Planned economy**: decisions made centrally by government.
- **Mixed economy**: a blend of market and government.
- **Trade-offs**: the strengths and weaknesses of each system.

## Card: The spectrum of systems
image: econMarketsEconomicSystemsTheSpectrumOfSystemsHero
At one end, a pure **market economy** leaves decisions (what to make, prices, who gets what) to private buyers and sellers via supply and demand. At the other, a **planned economy** has the government decide centrally. Each has **trade-offs**: markets are efficient and innovative but can produce inequality and neglect some needs; planning aims for control and equality but often struggles with efficiency and shortages.

## Card: Most economies are mixed
In reality, almost every modern economy is a **mixed economy** — mostly market-based, but with government involvement in some areas (regulation, public services like healthcare or roads, social support, taxes). Countries differ in *how much* government versus market, which is much of what political debate is about. Pure versions of either extreme are rare; the real question is the balance.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking economies are purely one type (almost all are mixed); assuming one system is simply "good" and another "bad" (each has trade-offs); confusing economic systems with specific political labels; and not realizing most debate is about the *balance* of market and government, not a pure choice between them. Reality is a spectrum.

## Card: Quick recap
Economies range from market (private supply and demand decide) to planned (government decides centrally), each with trade-offs — markets bring efficiency and innovation but inequality; planning brings control but often inefficiency. Almost all real economies are mixed, and most debate concerns the *balance* of market and government, not a pure either-or.
