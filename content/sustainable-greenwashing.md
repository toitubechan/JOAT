## Card: What it is
image: sustainGreenwashingHero
**Greenwashing** is when companies market products or themselves as more environmentally friendly than they really are. It uses green imagery, vague claims, and selective facts to *seem* sustainable, exploiting good intentions. Learning to spot it protects you from being misled and helps you support genuinely better choices.

## Card: Why it matters
As people care more about sustainability, companies have a strong incentive to *appear* green — whether or not they are. Greenwashing wastes your money and good intentions on products that aren't really better, and rewards misleading behavior. Spotting it lets you direct your support to genuine improvements rather than clever marketing.

## Card: Key terms
- **Greenwashing**: marketing something as greener than it is.
- **Vague claims**: meaningless eco-sounding words.
- **Selective focus**: highlighting one green feature, hiding the rest.
- **Genuine credentials**: real, verifiable environmental proof.

## Card: Common greenwashing tactics
image: sustainGreenwashingCommonGreenwashingTacticsHero
Watch for: **vague claims** — words like "natural," "eco-friendly," "green," or "sustainable" with no specifics or proof (they're often unregulated and meaningless). **Selective focus** — highlighting one small green feature while ignoring a product's larger impact. Green imagery (leaves, earth tones) on something not actually green. And impressive-sounding but empty pledges with no real action behind them.

## Card: How to see through it
To spot genuine green claims versus greenwashing, look for **specifics and proof**: real numbers, verifiable details, and credible third-party certifications rather than vague feel-good words. Ask: what does this claim actually *mean*, and is there evidence? Be especially skeptical when the green messaging seems disproportionate to the product. Genuine sustainability tends to be specific and verifiable; greenwashing stays vague.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: trusting green imagery and words like "natural" or "eco" at face value; being swayed by one highlighted green feature while ignoring the bigger picture; assuming a green-marketed product is genuinely better; and feeling you must buy "eco" products at all (buying less is often greener). Demand specifics and proof, and stay skeptical of vague claims.

## Card: Quick recap
Greenwashing is marketing that makes products *seem* greener than they are — through vague words ("natural," "eco"), green imagery, and selective focus on one feature. See through it by demanding specifics, proof, and credible certifications rather than feel-good language. Genuine sustainability is specific and verifiable; greenwashing stays vague. Spotting it directs your support where it counts.
