## Card: What it is
image: leakStopHero
A **leak** is water escaping where it shouldn't — a dripping tap, a damp patch under a sink, a seeping seal around a bath. Many small leaks are easy to spot and slow to start, giving you a chance to fix them early. This card set covers recognizing and handling the simple ones.

## Card: Why it matters
Water is patient and destructive. A slow leak can rot wood, ruin floors, grow mould, and spike your water bill — all while looking minor. Catching and stopping leaks early is one of the highest-value home habits, because water damage is among the most expensive problems to repair.

## Card: Key terms
- **Leak**: water escaping where it shouldn't.
- **Washer**: a small rubber ring that seals a tap.
- **Sealant / caulk**: waterproof filler around sinks and baths.
- **Shut-off valve**: the tap that stops water to a fixture.

## Card: Find the source first
image: findLeakSource
Before fixing anything, locate where the water actually comes from — it can travel along surfaces before dripping, so the wet spot isn't always the source. Dry the area, then watch to see where water reappears. Knowing the true source prevents "fixing" the wrong thing while the real leak continues.

## Card: Simple leak fixes
A dripping tap is often a worn **washer** that needs replacing — cheap and doable after turning off the **shut-off valve**. Gaps around a sink or bath where water seeps in usually need fresh **sealant**: scrape out the old, dry it, and apply a new bead. For anything behind walls or major, call a plumber.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is ignoring a "small" drip — it wastes water and quietly causes damage. Another is not turning off the water supply before working on a tap, leading to a soaking. And many apply new sealant over old, mouldy sealant, which won't bond — the old must be removed and the surface dried first.

## Card: Quick recap
Leaks are small but destructive, so fix them early. Find the true source first, since water travels. Replace worn washers for dripping taps (water off first), and re-seal gaps with fresh caulk on a clean, dry surface. For hidden or major leaks, call a professional plumber.
