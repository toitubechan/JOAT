## Card: What it is
image: logicEvaluatingEvidenceHero
**Evaluating evidence** is the skill of judging how much to trust a claim based on the quality of the evidence behind it. Not all evidence is equal — some is strong and reliable, some weak or misleading. Learning to assess evidence helps you separate what's well-supported from what's merely asserted, especially amid today's flood of information and misinformation.

## Card: Why it matters
We're bombarded with claims — in news, ads, social media, conversations — many poorly supported or false. Being able to evaluate evidence is a crucial defense against misinformation and a foundation of clear thinking. It helps you decide what to believe based on quality, not just confidence or repetition. It's essential in the information age.

## Card: Key terms
- **Evidence**: information offered to support a claim.
- **Source**: where the evidence comes from, and its reliability.
- **Anecdote**: a single personal story (weak general evidence).
- **Strong vs. weak evidence**: how reliable and relevant evidence is.

## Card: Not all evidence is equal
image: logicEvaluatingEvidenceNotAllEvidenceIsEqualHero
A key principle: **evidence varies in quality**. **Strong evidence** is reliable, relevant, and ideally verifiable and repeatable (like solid research or multiple credible sources). **Weak evidence** includes single **anecdotes** (one person's story doesn't prove a general claim), unreliable sources, or cherry-picked examples. A confident claim or a vivid story isn't the same as good evidence. Always ask: how strong is the evidence, really?

## Card: Questions to ask
To evaluate evidence, ask: What's the **source**, and is it reliable and unbiased? Is this solid evidence or just an anecdote? Is there enough of it, or one cherry-picked example? Can it be verified, and do other sources agree? Could the person have a motive to mislead? These questions help you weigh how much trust a claim deserves, rather than accepting (or rejecting) it on impulse or feeling.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: treating a single anecdote as proof ("it worked for my friend"); trusting confident assertions without evidence; not checking the source or its reliability; being swayed by vivid stories over solid data; and accepting evidence that merely confirms what you already believe. Evaluating evidence means judging its quality and source, not its persuasiveness or how well it fits your views.

## Card: Quick recap
Evaluating evidence means judging how much to trust a claim by the quality of evidence behind it — and not all evidence is equal. Strong evidence is reliable, relevant, and verifiable; weak evidence includes single anecdotes, biased sources, and cherry-picked examples. Ask about the source, reliability, amount, and motives. This skill is a crucial defense against misinformation and a foundation of clear thinking.
