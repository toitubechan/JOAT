## Card: What it is
image: personalFinanceHero
**Personal finance** is simply how you handle your own money — what you earn, what you spend, what you save, and what you owe. It's not about being rich or good at math. It's a set of everyday habits, like cooking or driving, that anyone can learn and get better at over time.

## Card: Why it matters
Money touches almost every part of life: where you live, what you eat, how you handle emergencies. When you understand your own finances, you feel less stressed and more in control. Small, steady choices add up — and they decide whether money works for you or against you.

## Card: Key terms
- **Income**: the money coming in, like a salary or wages.
- **Expenses**: the money going out, like rent and groceries.
- **Savings**: money you keep aside instead of spending.
- **Budget**: a simple plan for where your money should go.

## Card: The core idea
image: spendLessGap
Healthy personal finance comes down to one habit: **spend less than you earn**, and put the difference to work. That leftover gap is what lets you build savings, handle surprises, and eventually reach bigger goals. Everything else in personal finance is just a tool to grow or protect that gap.

## Card: It's a skill, not a talent
Nobody is born good with money. People who manage it well mostly follow simple routines: they track what they spend, plan ahead a little, and avoid obvious traps. You don't need a finance degree — you need a few good habits repeated consistently, just like brushing your teeth.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest beginner mistake is avoidance — never checking your accounts because it feels scary or boring. Ignoring money doesn't make problems go away; it lets small ones grow. The second mistake is thinking you need a lot of money before it's "worth" managing. Habits matter more than amounts.

## Card: Quick recap
Personal finance is just managing your own money: income in, expenses out, savings aside. The golden rule is to spend less than you earn. It's a learnable skill built on small habits — and starting early, even with little, beats waiting for the "right" time.
