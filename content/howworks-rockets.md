## Card: What it is
image: howworksRocketsHero
A **rocket** launches into space by burning fuel to blast gas out of its rear at high speed, which pushes the rocket forward (and up). It works on a simple principle of physics: for every push in one direction, there's an equal push the other way. Remarkably, rockets work even in the vacuum of space, where there's nothing to push against.

## Card: Why it matters
Rockets are how we reach space — for satellites, exploration, and travel. Understanding how they work demystifies spaceflight and illustrates a fundamental law of physics in action. It also clears up a common confusion: how a rocket can move in empty space with nothing to push off. It's physics made dramatic.

## Card: Key terms
- **Thrust**: the forward force pushing the rocket.
- **Action–reaction**: every force has an equal, opposite force.
- **Propellant**: the fuel and material expelled to create thrust.
- **Escape velocity**: the speed needed to leave Earth's gravity.

## Card: Action and reaction
image: howworksRocketsActionAndReactionHero
A rocket works on **action–reaction** (Newton's third law): every force has an equal and opposite force. The rocket burns **propellant** to blast hot gas downward at enormous speed (the action); in reaction, the rocket is pushed upward with equal force (the reaction). It's like the recoil of a gun, but continuous. The faster and more mass it expels, the more **thrust** it generates.

## Card: Why rockets work in space
A common puzzle: how does a rocket move in space with nothing to push against? The answer: it doesn't need anything to push against. By the action–reaction principle, expelling gas backward pushes the rocket forward regardless of whether there's air — it's the expulsion itself that creates the force. This is why rockets, unlike planes (which need air for lift), work perfectly in the vacuum of space. Reaching orbit also requires enormous speed (**escape velocity**), which is why rockets are so powerful and fuel-hungry.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a rocket pushes against the air or ground to move (it pushes against the gas it expels, via action–reaction); assuming rockets can't work in space (they work *better* without air resistance); confusing rockets with jet planes (jets need air; rockets carry everything they need); and underrating the speed needed to reach orbit. Rockets work by throwing mass backward to push themselves forward.

## Card: Quick recap
A rocket burns propellant to blast gas backward at high speed; by action–reaction (every force has an equal, opposite one), this pushes the rocket forward — no air or ground needed. That's why rockets work in the vacuum of space. Reaching orbit demands enormous speed, hence rockets' immense power and fuel. They move by throwing mass backward to push themselves forward.
