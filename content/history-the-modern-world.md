## Card: What it is
image: histTurningpointsModernWorldHero
The **modern world** was shaped by a cluster of turning points in recent centuries — industrialization, global connection, world wars, and technological revolutions. These relatively recent shifts created the world we live in today. Understanding them connects history directly to the present you experience.

## Card: Why it matters
The modern world didn't just appear — it was shaped by identifiable recent developments. Understanding them explains why the world looks as it does: why we have nations and global trade, advanced technology, and the social structures around us. It connects the sweep of history to your everyday life, making the present more comprehensible.

## Card: Key terms
- **Industrialization**: the shift to machine-based production.
- **Globalization**: growing worldwide interconnection.
- **World wars**: 20th-century conflicts that reshaped the globe.
- **Technological revolution**: rapid waves of new technology.

## Card: Industry and connection
image: histTurningpointsModernWorldIndustryAndConnectionHero
Two recent forces shaped modernity. **Industrialization** (from the 1700s onward) shifted humanity from farms and handcraft to factories and machines, transforming work, cities, and wealth on a massive scale. And growing **globalization** — through trade, travel, and communication — wove the world's once-separate regions into one deeply interconnected system. Together, these built the economic foundations of modern life.

## Card: Conflict and acceleration
The 20th century brought devastating **world wars** that reshaped borders, powers, and global order, followed by rapid **technological revolutions** — from electricity and cars to computers and the internet. The pace of change accelerated dramatically: more transformation happened in the last couple of centuries than in vast stretches before. The modern world is the product of this recent, rapid, cascading change.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming the modern world has "always" looked roughly like this (it's very recent); not realizing how fast change has accelerated lately; seeing modern features (nations, global trade, technology) as natural rather than historically shaped; and disconnecting history from the present. The world you know is a recent, still-changing product of history.

## Card: Quick recap
The modern world was shaped by recent turning points: industrialization (machines transforming work and cities), globalization (weaving regions into one interconnected system), world wars (reshaping global order), and accelerating technological revolutions. More change happened in the last couple of centuries than in vast stretches before. The present is a recent, still-evolving product of history.
