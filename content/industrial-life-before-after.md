## Card: What it is
image: histIndustrialLifeBeforeAfterHero
The Industrial Revolution didn't just change *how things were made* — it transformed *how people lived*. Where they worked, where they lived, and the rhythm of daily life all shifted dramatically. Comparing life before and after reveals just how profound this turning point was for ordinary people.

## Card: Why it matters
Statistics about factories and machines can feel abstract; seeing how *daily life* changed makes the revolution real and relatable. Understanding the before-and-after helps you grasp the human scale of this transformation — and recognize that the way we live now (urban, clock-driven, working for wages) is itself a recent invention.

## Card: Key terms
- **Rural**: countryside, farming-based life.
- **Urbanization**: people moving to and growing cities.
- **Wage labor**: working for an employer for pay.
- **Cottage industry**: making goods at home by hand.

## Card: Before: rural and home-based
image: histIndustrialLifeBeforeAfterBeforeRuralAndHomeBasedHero
Before industrialization, most people lived **rural** lives, working the land or in **cottage industries** — making goods by hand at home, often at their own pace, tied to the seasons and daylight. Communities were small, work and home were intertwined, and life had changed little for generations. It was a slower, more local, agriculture-centered world.

## Card: After: cities and the clock
The revolution drove mass **urbanization** — people flocked to cities for factory jobs, and cities exploded in size. Work moved from home to the factory, becoming **wage labor** under an employer, governed by the clock and the machine's pace rather than the seasons. Daily life became more urban, regimented, and separated into "work" and "home." This new rhythm — commuting to a workplace for set hours — is now normal but was then revolutionary.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: imagining pre-industrial life as either idyllic or simply miserable (it was different, with its own hardships and rhythms); not realizing how recently the "normal" pattern of city living and clock-based wage work emerged; and underrating how deeply the revolution changed everyday existence, not just industry. Life itself was remade.

## Card: Quick recap
Before the Industrial Revolution, most people lived rural lives, working at home or on the land at a slower, seasonal pace. After, mass urbanization drew people to cities and factories, where work became clock-governed wage labor separated from home. The familiar modern pattern — commuting to a workplace for set hours — is a recent invention born of this transformation.
