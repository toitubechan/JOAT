## Card: What it is
image: econGdpWhatItIsHero
**GDP** (Gross Domestic Product) is the total value of all goods and services a country produces in a period, usually a year. It's the most common single measure of the size and health of an economy. When you hear "the economy grew 2%," that's GDP. Understanding it decodes a lot of economic news.

## Card: Why it matters
GDP is referenced constantly in news and politics as shorthand for how an economy is doing. Understanding what it measures — and what it leaves out — helps you interpret economic headlines critically. It's the single most useful number to understand for following the economy, while knowing its limits keeps you from over-relying on it.

## Card: Key terms
- **GDP**: the total value of goods and services produced.
- **Economic output**: everything an economy produces.
- **GDP growth**: the change in GDP over time.
- **Per capita**: per person, dividing by population.

## Card: A measure of total output
image: econGdpWhatItIsAMeasureOfTotalOutputHero
**GDP** adds up the value of everything an economy produces — all the goods made and services provided. A bigger GDP means more **economic output**. **GDP growth** (the percentage change over time) is watched closely: rising GDP generally signals a growing, healthier economy; falling GDP signals trouble. It's essentially the economy's overall "size" and a rough pulse-check on its health.

## Card: What GDP doesn't capture
GDP is useful but limited. It measures *output*, not wellbeing — it doesn't capture how wealth is *distributed* (a high GDP can hide great inequality), unpaid work (like caregiving), environmental damage, or quality of life. **Per capita** GDP (divided by population) gives a fairer sense of average prosperity than total GDP. Treat GDP as one important indicator, not the whole story.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: treating GDP as a measure of happiness or wellbeing (it's output, not welfare); ignoring distribution (high GDP can mask inequality); comparing total GDP between countries of very different sizes without using per capita; and assuming GDP captures everything that matters (it omits unpaid work, environment, and more).

## Card: Quick recap
GDP is the total value of goods and services a country produces, the most common measure of an economy's size and health. Rising GDP generally signals growth. But it measures output, not wellbeing — missing distribution, unpaid work, and the environment. Use per capita for fair comparisons, and treat GDP as one indicator, not the whole picture.
