## Card: What it is
image: bakingScienceHero
**Baking** is essentially edible chemistry — ingredients undergo precise reactions when combined and heated, transforming into bread, cakes, and pastries. Unlike free-form cooking, baking depends on these reactions working correctly, which is why it's more precise. Understanding the basic science explains *why* baking works as it does and why recipes must be followed carefully.

## Card: Why it matters
Baking can feel mysterious — why do small changes ruin a cake, and why must you measure so precisely? Understanding the science demystifies it: you learn *why* recipes work, why precision matters, and how to troubleshoot. This makes you a more confident, capable baker who understands rather than just follows. It turns baking from guesswork into something you can reason about.

## Card: Key terms
- **Baking science**: the chemistry and reactions behind baking.
- **Leavening**: what makes baked goods rise.
- **Reactions**: the chemical changes ingredients undergo when baked.
- **Precision**: why baking requires accurate measurement.

## Card: Baking is chemistry
image: bakingChemistryHero
When you bake, ingredients undergo chemical and physical **reactions**: flour's proteins and starches form structure, fats add tenderness, sugars sweeten and brown, eggs bind and enrich, and heat drives transformations (browning, setting, rising). A key one is **leavening** — gases (from agents like yeast or baking soda/powder, or trapped air) expand with heat to make baked goods rise and become light. Baking is essentially orchestrating these reactions; the ingredients have specific roles in the chemistry.

## Card: Why precision matters
Because baking depends on **reactions** working correctly, **precision** matters far more than in everyday cooking. The ratios and amounts of ingredients are balanced so the chemistry works — too much or too little of something (flour, leavening, liquid, fat) can throw off the reactions and ruin the result. This is why baking recipes should be followed and measured carefully, while free-form cooking allows more improvisation. Understanding the *why* behind the precision makes following recipes feel logical rather than fussy.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: treating baking like free-form cooking and improvising amounts (baking depends on balanced reactions, so precision matters); not understanding ingredients have specific chemical roles; thinking small changes won't matter (they can throw off the chemistry); and finding baking mysterious rather than understandable. Baking is edible chemistry — understanding the reactions explains why precision matters and how to bake successfully.
