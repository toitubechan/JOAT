## Card: What it is
image: econSupplydemandShortagesSurplusesHero
When supply and demand don't match at the current price, you get a **shortage** (not enough to go around) or a **surplus** (too much sitting unsold). These imbalances are common, and understanding them explains empty shelves, fire sales, queues, and many real-world economic situations.

## Card: Why it matters
Shortages and surpluses affect everyone — from sold-out products to discounted gluts. Understanding why they happen, and how markets tend to correct them, helps you interpret these everyday situations. It also reveals why price controls and panic-buying can make imbalances worse, a genuinely useful real-world insight.

## Card: Key terms
- **Shortage**: demand exceeds supply at the current price.
- **Surplus**: supply exceeds demand at the current price.
- **Price control**: a government-set price limit.
- **Self-correction**: how prices naturally resolve imbalances.

## Card: Shortages and surpluses
image: econSupplydemandShortagesSurplusesShortagesAndSurplusesHero
A **shortage** happens when more people want something than is available at the current price — empty shelves, sold-out tickets, long queues. A **surplus** is the opposite: more supply than demand, leaving unsold stock (leading to discounts and clearances). In a free market, prices tend to **self-correct** these: shortages push prices up (cooling demand, boosting supply), surpluses push them down.

## Card: When prices can't adjust
Imbalances persist when prices *can't* adjust freely. **Price controls** (a cap on price) can cause lasting shortages — if a price is held below equilibrium, demand keeps outstripping supply, and shelves stay empty (rationing or queues result). Panic-buying worsens shortages by spiking demand suddenly. Understanding this explains why well-meant price caps sometimes backfire into persistent scarcity.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking shortages mean something is simply "gone" rather than mispriced or disrupted; assuming price caps always help (they can entrench shortages); not seeing that prices normally self-correct imbalances; and panic-buying, which worsens the very shortage feared. Imbalances are about price and the supply-demand gap, not just availability.

## Card: Quick recap
Shortages (demand exceeds supply) and surpluses (supply exceeds demand) are imbalances at the current price. Free markets tend to self-correct them — shortages raise prices, surpluses lower them. But when prices can't adjust (like price controls), shortages can persist. Panic-buying worsens shortages. It's all about the supply-demand gap and price.
