## Card: What it is
image: budgetHero
A **budget** is just a plan for your money — a decision, made in advance, about where each euro should go. It's not about restriction or spreadsheets you'll never open. At its simplest, a budget answers one question: "Given what I earn, how do I want to split it between spending, saving, and bills?"

## Card: Why it matters
Without a plan, money tends to leak away on autopilot, and the month ends before the money does. A budget flips that: you direct your money on purpose instead of wondering where it went. It's the single most reliable tool for turning good intentions about saving into actual results.

## Card: Key terms
- **Budget**: a plan for how to use your income.
- **Income**: the money you have coming in.
- **Allocation**: deciding how much goes to each category.
- **Tracking**: recording what you actually spend.

## Card: How a budget works
image: allocateTrack
A budget has two halves. First, **allocation**: before the month starts, you divide your income across categories — rent, food, fun, savings. Second, **tracking**: through the month, you record what you actually spend and compare it to the plan. The gap between plan and reality is where you learn and adjust.

## Card: It's a guide, not a cage
A common fear is that budgeting means saying no to everything. The opposite is true: a good budget *includes* room for fun and treats, planned on purpose. It frees you from guilt, because once something is in the plan, you can enjoy it knowing it fits. A budget gives permission as much as it sets limits.

## Card: Common mistakes
The first mistake is making a budget so strict it's impossible to follow, then abandoning it entirely. The second is allocating money but never **tracking** the actual spending, so you never know if the plan worked. And many forget irregular costs — annual fees, gifts, repairs — which then blow up the budget when they arrive.

## Card: Quick recap
A budget is a plan that tells your money where to go before you spend it: allocate your income across categories, then track reality against the plan. Build in room for fun so it's sustainable, and remember irregular costs. It's a guide that gives control, not a cage.
