## Card: What it is
image: poorSleepHero
When sleep goes wrong — trouble falling asleep, waking in the night, or lying awake — there are practical, evidence-based steps that often help. This card set covers what to do in the moment and over time. These are general self-help basics, not treatment for medical sleep disorders.

## Card: Why it matters
Poor sleep feeds on itself: a bad night causes anxiety about sleep, which causes more bad nights. Knowing simple, proven responses breaks that cycle. Most occasional sleep trouble improves with behavior changes — and knowing when it's beyond self-help is itself valuable.

## Card: Key terms
- **Sleep anxiety**: stress about not sleeping, which worsens it.
- **Stimulus control**: linking the bed only with sleep.
- **Consistency**: keeping steady sleep and wake times.
- **Sleep disorder**: a medical condition needing professional help.

## Card: Don't fight the bed
image: leaveBedReset
A key trick is **stimulus control**: if you can't sleep after about 20 minutes, get up and do something calm and dim-lit until sleepy, then return to bed. Lying there frustrated trains your brain to associate the bed with stress. Keep the bed for sleep, so your body links it with rest, not struggle.

## Card: Address the cycle over time
For ongoing issues, focus on **consistency** (steady wake time, even after a bad night), good sleep habits, and reducing **sleep anxiety** by not catastrophizing one rough night. Daytime daylight and exercise help. Avoid clock-watching, which fuels stress. Small, steady changes usually beat any quick fix.

## Card: When to seek help
Self-help handles most occasional trouble, but see a doctor if poor sleep is persistent, severe, or affecting daily life, or if you have signs of a **sleep disorder** — like loud snoring with pauses in breathing, or overwhelming daytime sleepiness. These need professional assessment, not just better habits.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is lying in bed for hours trying to force sleep, building stress. Another is panicking after one bad night, creating sleep anxiety. People also sleep in erratically to "recover," which disrupts their rhythm further. And some ignore persistent serious symptoms that genuinely need a doctor.

## Card: Quick recap
For poor sleep: don't lie there fighting it — get up, stay calm, return when sleepy. Keep a consistent wake time, ease sleep anxiety, and improve daytime habits. Avoid clock-watching. Most occasional trouble responds to these steps, but persistent or severe problems deserve a doctor's input.
