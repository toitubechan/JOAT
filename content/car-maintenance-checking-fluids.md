## Card: What it is
image: fluidCheckHero
Your car relies on several **fluids** to run, cool, stop, and steer. The main ones are engine **oil**, **coolant**, brake fluid, and washer fluid. Checking their levels is one of the simplest, most valuable maintenance habits — most take under a minute and need no tools.

## Card: Why it matters
Running low on the wrong fluid can destroy an engine, cause brake failure, or leave you stranded. Engine oil especially: run out, and the engine can seize, a repair costing more than the car. Regular quick checks catch leaks and low levels before they become catastrophes.

## Card: Key terms
- **Engine oil**: lubricates and protects the engine's moving parts.
- **Coolant / antifreeze**: stops the engine overheating or freezing.
- **Dipstick**: a marked rod for checking oil level.
- **Reservoir**: a clear tank showing a fluid's level.

## Card: Checking your oil
image: dipstickOil
With the engine **off and cool**, on level ground: pull out the **dipstick**, wipe it, reinsert it fully, then pull it out again to read the level against the marks. It should sit between the minimum and maximum. The oil should look amber to brown — very black, gritty oil suggests it's due for a change.

## Card: Coolant and other fluids
Most fluids sit in clear **reservoirs** marked with min/max lines, so you just check the level against the marks — no dipstick needed. **Check coolant only when the engine is cold**: opening a hot coolant cap can release scalding steam. Top up with the correct fluid type from your manual, never the wrong one.

## Card: Common mistakes
A dangerous mistake is opening the coolant cap on a hot engine — it can spray boiling liquid. Another is checking oil with the engine running or on a slope, giving a false reading. People also top up with the wrong fluid, or ignore a level that keeps dropping — a falling level means a leak that needs investigating.

## Card: Quick recap
Check your car's fluids regularly: oil via the dipstick (engine off, cool, level ground), and coolant and others against reservoir marks. Never open a hot coolant cap. Use the correct fluid type, and treat a constantly dropping level as a leak to investigate. Quick checks prevent ruinous damage.
