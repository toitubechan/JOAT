## Card: What it is
image: financeSavingBudgetingMethodsHero
There's no single "right" budget — just different **methods** for splitting your money, each suiting a different personality. Some are simple rules of thumb; others track every euro. The best method is the one you'll actually keep using, so it helps to know the main options before picking.

## Card: Why it matters
People often quit budgeting because they tried a method that didn't fit them — too fiddly, or too loose. Matching the method to how you think about money makes it stick. A budget you maintain for years beats a "perfect" one you drop in a week, so choosing the right style genuinely matters.

## Card: Key terms
- **50/30/20**: split income into needs, wants, and savings.
- **Zero-based budget**: assign every euro a specific job.
- **Envelope method**: set cash or digital limits per category.
- **Pay-yourself-first**: save before you spend.

## Card: The 50/30/20 rule
image: financeSavingBudgetingMethodsThe503020RuleHero
A simple starting point: put **50%** of after-tax income toward **needs** (rent, food, bills), **30%** toward **wants** (fun, dining, extras), and **20%** toward **savings** and debt repayment. The percentages are guidelines, not laws — adjust them to your life. It's popular because it's easy to remember and flexible.

## Card: Zero-based and envelopes
A **zero-based budget** gives every euro a job until income minus assignments equals zero — nothing is left "unplanned." It's precise and great for detail-lovers. The **envelope method** sets a fixed limit per category (once cash envelopes, now often app pots); when an envelope's empty, you stop spending there. Both create strong, visible boundaries.

## Card: Common mistakes
The first mistake is picking the most complex method to seem disciplined, then burning out on the admin. The second is treating the 50/30/20 percentages as rigid rules rather than a flexible starting frame. And switching methods constantly never lets any of them prove themselves — give one a few months before judging.

## Card: Quick recap
Budgeting methods range from the simple 50/30/20 rule to detailed zero-based budgets and envelope limits. None is universally best — pick the one matching your style and commit to it for a few months. The right method is simply the one you'll keep using.
