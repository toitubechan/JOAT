## Card: What it is
image: medicineHospitalsTriageHero
**Triage** is the system hospitals use to decide who gets treated first — based on how urgent each case is, not who arrived first. It's why someone with a life-threatening emergency is seen before someone with a minor issue, even if the minor case has waited longer. Understanding it explains how hospitals (especially emergency departments) actually work.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people are confused or frustrated by hospital waits, not understanding triage. Knowing how it works explains why waits happen, why urgency beats arrival order, and how emergency care is organized. It helps you have realistic expectations and understand a system designed to save the most lives with limited resources.

## Card: Key terms
- **Triage**: prioritizing patients by urgency of need.
- **Emergency department**: where urgent cases are assessed.
- **Urgency**: how time-critical a case is.
- **Prioritization**: treating the most serious cases first.

## Card: Why urgency, not order
image: medicineHospitalsTriageWhyUrgencyNotOrderHero
In an **emergency department**, **triage** sorts incoming patients by **urgency** — a quick assessment of how serious and time-critical each case is. The most life-threatening cases are treated first, regardless of arrival order. This can mean a long wait for a non-urgent problem while sicker patients are rushed through. It feels unfair if you don't know the system, but it's designed to save the most lives.

## Card: How hospitals are organized
Beyond triage, hospitals are organized into specialized areas and teams — emergency, surgery, intensive care, various departments — each handling different needs. Resources (staff, beds, equipment) are limited, so the system constantly **prioritizes** to use them where they're most needed. Waits often reflect this balancing of urgent demand against limited resources, not inefficiency or being forgotten.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: expecting first-come-first-served (triage prioritizes by urgency); feeling forgotten during a long wait (a long wait often means your case isn't life-threatening — actually reassuring); not understanding that resources are limited and balanced; and frustration from not knowing the system. Triage and prioritization are designed to save the most lives, even when waits feel frustrating.

## Card: Quick recap
Triage is how hospitals prioritize patients by urgency rather than arrival order, so the most life-threatening cases are treated first. This can mean long waits for minor issues — which actually means your case isn't critical. Hospitals organize specialized teams and limited resources to handle the most urgent needs. Understanding triage explains waits and the system designed to save the most lives.
