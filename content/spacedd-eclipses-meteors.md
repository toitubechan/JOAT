## Card: What it is
image: eclipsesMeteorsHero
**Eclipses** and **meteor showers** are among the most spectacular sky events anyone can witness. An eclipse happens when one celestial body blocks or shadows another; a meteor shower is when many "shooting stars" streak across the sky. Both are predictable, natural events with understandable causes — and they're free to enjoy.

## Card: Why it matters
These events connect people to astronomy directly — you can see them with your own eyes, no equipment needed. Understanding what causes them turns a beautiful spectacle into something you genuinely comprehend, and helps you know when and how to watch. They're accessible doorways into appreciating the cosmos.

## Card: Key terms
- **Solar eclipse**: the Moon blocking the Sun.
- **Lunar eclipse**: Earth's shadow falling on the Moon.
- **Meteor**: a "shooting star" — debris burning up in the atmosphere.
- **Meteor shower**: many meteors as Earth passes through debris.

## Card: How eclipses work
image: eclipseAlignmentHero
Eclipses happen when the Sun, Earth, and Moon line up. A **solar eclipse** occurs when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, blocking the Sun's light and casting a shadow — by remarkable coincidence, the Moon can appear just big enough to cover the Sun entirely. A **lunar eclipse** occurs when Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, casting Earth's shadow on the Moon (often turning it reddish). Both are predictable from the orbits.

## Card: How meteor showers work
A **meteor** ("shooting star") is a small piece of space debris burning up as it streaks through Earth's atmosphere — not a real star at all. A **meteor shower** happens when Earth, in its orbit, passes through a trail of debris (often left by a comet), so many meteors appear over a few nights. Because Earth crosses these trails at the same time each year, major meteor showers are predictable annual events.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: confusing solar and lunar eclipses; thinking a "shooting star" is an actual star (it's tiny debris burning up); imagining these events are rare or random (they're predictable); and looking directly at a solar eclipse (never look at the Sun directly — it can damage your eyes; use proper protection). These spectacles are understandable, predictable, and safely enjoyable with care.

## Card: Quick recap
Eclipses occur when the Sun, Earth, and Moon align — solar (Moon blocks the Sun) or lunar (Earth's shadow falls on the Moon). Meteor showers happen when Earth passes through comet debris, so many "shooting stars" (tiny debris burning up) appear, predictably each year. Both are spectacular, understandable, and free to watch — but never look directly at a solar eclipse without protection.
