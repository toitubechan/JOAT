## Card: What it is
image: econTradeWhyCountriesTradeHero
**Global trade** is countries buying and selling goods and services across borders. Nations trade because no country produces everything best or cheapest on its own. By specializing in what they do well and trading for the rest, countries can all end up better off — the core logic behind global trade.

## Card: Why it matters
Trade shapes the prices and variety of nearly everything you buy — your phone, clothes, food, and fuel likely crossed borders. Understanding why countries trade explains globalization, supply chains, and a lot of economic and political debate. It connects your daily purchases to a worldwide web of exchange.

## Card: Key terms
- **Trade**: exchanging goods and services between countries.
- **Specialization**: focusing on what you produce best.
- **Comparative advantage**: producing something relatively more efficiently.
- **Mutual benefit**: both sides gaining from trade.

## Card: Specialization and advantage
image: econTradeWhyCountriesTradeSpecializationAndAdvantageHero
Countries differ in resources, climate, skills, and costs. Through **specialization**, each focuses on what it produces relatively best (its **comparative advantage**) and trades for the rest. A country good at growing coffee and another good at making machinery both gain by trading, rather than each trying to do everything. Specializing and trading produces more total wealth than self-sufficiency.

## Card: Trade benefits both sides
Like any voluntary exchange, trade is meant for **mutual benefit** — both countries trade because each expects to gain. One gets goods it can't produce well or cheaply; the other earns from selling what it's good at. This is why trade has expanded globally: it generally makes more goods available, often more cheaply, than countries acting alone. (Though benefits aren't always evenly shared within a country.)

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking trade means one country "wins" and the other "loses" (voluntary trade usually benefits both); assuming a country should make everything itself (specialization produces more total wealth); confusing trade deficits with simply "losing"; and ignoring that while trade benefits economies overall, the gains and costs aren't always evenly distributed within a country.

## Card: Quick recap
Countries trade because none produces everything best alone. By specializing in their comparative advantage and trading for the rest, nations create more total wealth than self-sufficiency. Trade is voluntary and generally benefits both sides, making more goods available, often cheaper. But the gains aren't always evenly shared within each country.
