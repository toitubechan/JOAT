## Card: What it is
image: predatorsPreyHero
The **predator–prey** relationship is the dynamic between animals that hunt (predators) and those that are hunted (prey). It's one of nature's most fundamental relationships, driving evolution, shaping behavior, and keeping ecosystems in balance. This eternal chase has profoundly influenced how animals look and behave.

## Card: Why it matters
Predator–prey dynamics are central to how ecosystems work and stay balanced. Understanding them explains animal adaptations (claws, speed, camouflage), why removing a predator can throw nature out of balance, and the constant evolutionary "arms race" between hunters and hunted. It's key to understanding nature as a connected system.

## Card: Key terms
- **Predator**: an animal that hunts others for food.
- **Prey**: an animal that is hunted.
- **Adaptation**: a trait helping hunt or avoid being hunted.
- **Balance**: the equilibrium predators and prey maintain.

## Card: An evolutionary arms race
image: armsRaceHero
The predator–prey relationship drives a constant evolutionary "arms race." **Predators** evolve **adaptations** to catch prey — speed, sharp senses, claws, teeth, stealth. **Prey** evolve countermeasures — speed, camouflage, armor, alertness, group living. As one side improves, the other is pushed to improve too, over generations. Many of the most striking animal features (a cheetah's speed, a chameleon's camouflage) arose from this endless chase.

## Card: Keeping ecosystems in balance
Predators and prey keep each other (and the wider ecosystem) in **balance**. Predators control prey numbers, preventing prey from overpopulating and exhausting their food. Prey numbers, in turn, limit how many predators an area can support. Remove a key predator, and prey can explode in number, overgrazing and disrupting the whole system. This is why predators, often feared, are actually vital to healthy ecosystems.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: seeing predators as simply "bad" or villains (they're vital to ecosystem balance); thinking removing predators helps prey (it often causes overpopulation and ecosystem damage); not connecting predator–prey dynamics to animal adaptations; and missing that it's a balanced relationship, not one-sided destruction. Predators and prey shape each other and sustain nature's balance.

## Card: Quick recap
The predator–prey relationship — hunters and hunted — drives an evolutionary arms race, producing adaptations like speed, claws, and camouflage on both sides. It also keeps ecosystems in balance: predators control prey numbers, and removing a key predator can cause prey to overpopulate and disrupt the whole system. Far from mere villains, predators are vital to healthy, balanced nature.
