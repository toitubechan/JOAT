## Card: What it is
image: growthFocusMythOfMultitaskingHero
**Multitasking** — doing several things at once — feels efficient, but for tasks needing thought, it's largely a myth. What actually happens is your brain rapidly *switches* between tasks, and each switch has a cost. Understanding this reframes multitasking from a skill to a trap.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people pride themselves on multitasking, yet it makes them slower, more error-prone, and more drained. Realizing this frees you to focus on one thing at a time — which actually gets more done, better. It's a counterintuitive but liberating productivity insight.

## Card: Key terms
- **Multitasking**: attempting several tasks at once.
- **Task switching**: rapidly shifting attention between tasks.
- **Switching cost**: the time and energy lost each time you switch.
- **Single-tasking**: focusing on one task at a time.

## Card: Switching, not multitasking
image: growthFocusMythOfMultitaskingSwitchingNotMultitaskingHero
For anything requiring thought, your brain can't truly do two things at once — it **task switches** rapidly between them. Each switch carries a **switching cost**: a moment to refocus, plus errors from the disruption. So "multitasking" is really fast switching, and the costs pile up, making you slower and sloppier than focusing would.

## Card: The hidden costs
Beyond being slower, multitasking drains mental energy, increases mistakes, and leaves you feeling frazzled. Even brief interruptions (a glance at a notification) can cost minutes to fully refocus. **Single-tasking** — giving one task your full attention before moving on — consistently produces faster, better, less stressful work, even though doing less at once feels counterintuitive.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: believing you're a great multitasker (almost no one is, for thinking tasks); keeping many tabs, chats, and notifications active while working; switching tasks the moment one gets hard; and underestimating how long it takes to refocus after an interruption. Focus on one thing, finish or pause it deliberately, then switch.

## Card: Quick recap
Multitasking is mostly a myth for tasks needing thought — the brain rapidly switches instead, paying a switching cost in time and errors each time. It feels efficient but makes you slower, sloppier, and more drained. Single-tasking gets more done, better. Give one thing your full attention before moving to the next.
