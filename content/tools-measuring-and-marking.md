## Card: What it is
image: measuringHero
**Measuring and marking** is the planning step before any cut, drill, or fixing. With a **tape measure**, a **pencil**, and a **spirit level**, you work out exactly where things go and mark them, so the actual doing is accurate. It's the least glamorous step and the one that most determines the result.

## Card: Why it matters
Most DIY disasters come from skipping or rushing this stage — a shelf that slopes, a hole in the wrong spot, a cut too short. Material you've cut wrong is often wasted for good. A few careful minutes measuring saves time, money, and the frustration of redoing a botched job.

## Card: Key terms
- **Tape measure**: a flexible ruler for distances.
- **Spirit level**: a tool showing if something is truly horizontal or vertical.
- **Level / plumb**: perfectly horizontal / perfectly vertical.
- **Measure twice, cut once**: check before any irreversible step.

## Card: Measure twice, cut once
image: measureTwice
This old rule is the heart of it: cutting or drilling is usually irreversible, so verify your measurement *before* you commit. Take each measurement twice, ideally noting the number down. Thirty seconds of double-checking prevents wasting material and time on a mistake you can't undo.

## Card: Getting things level
A **spirit level** has a bubble that sits between two lines when a surface is truly **level** (horizontal) or **plumb** (vertical). Eyes are easily fooled — what looks straight often isn't, especially since floors and ceilings are rarely true. For shelves, frames, and anything that should look straight, trust the bubble, not your eye.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is measuring once and cutting hopefully. Another is trusting your eye instead of a level, ending with sloping shelves. People also forget to account for the width of a saw blade or the thickness of materials, and mark with something too thick to be precise — a sharp pencil beats a fat marker.

## Card: Quick recap
Measure and mark carefully before cutting or drilling: use a tape measure, a sharp pencil, and a spirit level. Measure twice, cut once, because mistakes are often unfixable. Trust the bubble for level and plumb, not your eye. This unglamorous step decides whether the finished job looks right.
