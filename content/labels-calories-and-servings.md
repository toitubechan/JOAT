## Card: What it is
image: caloriesServingsHero
**Calories** measure the energy in food, and the **serving size** is the amount the label's figures refer to. These two together are often the most misunderstood part of a label — and the place where packaging can be most misleading. Understanding them is key to reading labels accurately.

## Card: Why it matters
Calories relate to energy balance and weight, so people watch them — but a calorie figure is meaningless without knowing the serving it's based on. Brands sometimes use tiny "servings" to make calorie counts look small. Grasping this protects you from a very common labeling trick.

## Card: Key terms
- **Calorie (kcal)**: a unit of food energy.
- **Serving size**: the portion the figures describe.
- **Servings per pack**: how many portions the package contains.
- **Energy balance**: calories taken in versus used.

## Card: What calories are
A **calorie** (properly kcal) is just a measure of energy. Your body needs a certain amount daily to function; consistently taking in much more than you use leads to weight gain, and much less to loss — the rough idea of **energy balance**. Calories aren't "good" or "bad"; they're simply energy, and needs vary by person.

## Card: The serving-size trap
image: servingSizeTrap
Here's the catch: a label's calorie figure is *per serving*, and the **serving size** is set by the manufacturer. A snack might show "100 calories" — but if the pack contains 3 servings and you eat the whole thing, that's 300. Always check the **servings per pack** and how big a serving actually is before trusting the headline number.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: reading the calorie figure without checking the serving size; assuming one package equals one serving (it often doesn't); obsessing over calories while ignoring nutrient quality; and treating calories as inherently bad rather than as energy. The number only means something once you know the portion behind it.

## Card: Quick recap
Calories measure food energy, and what matters is roughly your overall balance of energy in versus out. But a calorie figure is per serving, and manufacturers set serving sizes — sometimes tiny ones. Always check servings per pack and the real portion size, or the headline calorie number can badly mislead you.
