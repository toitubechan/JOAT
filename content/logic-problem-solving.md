## Card: What it is
image: logicProblemSolvingHero
**Problem-solving frameworks** are systematic approaches to working through problems, rather than tackling them haphazardly. While problems vary, a clear general process — understand, break down, generate options, choose, act, and review — applies broadly. Having a framework turns daunting problems into manageable steps and leads to better solutions.

## Card: Why it matters
We face problems constantly — at work, home, and in decisions. A systematic approach helps you avoid jumping to conclusions, solve problems more effectively, and feel less overwhelmed. Knowing a general framework gives you a reliable method for any problem, replacing panic or guesswork with structured thinking. It's a broadly useful life skill.

## Card: Key terms
- **Define the problem**: clearly understanding what you're solving.
- **Break it down**: splitting a big problem into smaller parts.
- **Generate options**: thinking of multiple possible solutions.
- **Review**: checking whether the solution worked.

## Card: A general framework
image: logicProblemSolvingAGeneralFrameworkHero
A reliable problem-solving process: First, **define the problem** clearly — what exactly are you solving? (Often people solve the wrong problem.) Then **break it down** into smaller, manageable parts. **Generate options** — brainstorm multiple possible solutions, not just the first idea. Evaluate and *choose* the best option. *Act* on it. Finally, **review** — did it work? If not, learn and adjust. This structured cycle handles most problems far better than diving in blindly.

## Card: Why structure helps
The biggest benefit of a framework is that it stops you skipping crucial steps. Common errors — solving the wrong problem, jumping to the first solution, or not checking if it worked — are avoided by following a process. Breaking a big problem into parts makes the overwhelming feel manageable, and generating multiple options leads to better choices than grabbing the first idea. Structure brings clarity and better outcomes to messy problems.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: jumping straight to solutions without first defining the actual problem; tackling a big problem whole instead of breaking it down; settling on the first idea without generating alternatives; and not reviewing whether the solution worked. A framework guards against all these. The key is a deliberate process — understand, break down, generate options, choose, act, review — rather than haphazard problem-solving.

## Card: Quick recap
Problem-solving frameworks give a systematic approach: define the problem clearly (don't solve the wrong one), break it into manageable parts, generate multiple options (not just the first), choose and act, then review whether it worked. This structure prevents common errors, makes daunting problems manageable, and leads to better solutions than diving in blindly. It's a reliable, broadly useful method for any problem.
