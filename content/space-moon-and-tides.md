## Card: What it is
image: moonTidesHero
The **Moon** is Earth's only natural satellite, orbiting us and lighting our night sky. It causes the ocean **tides**, shows changing **phases**, and is the only other world humans have visited. Understanding it explains several familiar everyday phenomena, from why the Moon changes shape to why the sea rises and falls.

## Card: Why it matters
The Moon's effects are visible to everyone — its phases, eclipses, and the tides. Understanding them turns familiar sights into comprehensible science. It also connects astronomy to everyday life, like coastal tides, and reveals how gravity links objects across space. The Moon is our most relatable piece of the cosmos.

## Card: Key terms
- **Moon**: Earth's natural satellite.
- **Phases**: the Moon's changing apparent shape.
- **Tides**: the rise and fall of the sea, caused by the Moon.
- **Gravity**: the force linking the Moon, Earth, and Sun.

## Card: Why the Moon has phases
image: moonPhasesHero
The Moon doesn't make its own light — it reflects sunlight. As it orbits Earth, we see different amounts of its sunlit side, creating the **phases** (new, crescent, half, full, and back). It's not Earth's shadow causing this (that's an eclipse, which is different) — it's simply how much of the lit half faces us at each point in the orbit.

## Card: How the Moon causes tides
The Moon's **gravity** pulls on Earth's oceans, causing them to bulge slightly toward (and away from) the Moon. As Earth rotates, coastlines pass through these bulges, giving roughly two high and two low **tides** a day. The Sun's gravity contributes too — when Sun and Moon align, tides are largest. Tides are gravity in action, visible at any coast.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking Moon phases are caused by Earth's shadow (that's an eclipse; phases are about the sunlit side we see); believing the Moon makes its own light (it reflects the Sun's); not knowing the Moon causes tides; and confusing the dark part of a crescent moon with a shadow rather than simply the unlit side.

## Card: Quick recap
The Moon is Earth's satellite, reflecting sunlight. Its phases come from how much of its sunlit side we see as it orbits — not from Earth's shadow. Its gravity pulls the oceans into bulges, causing roughly two high tides a day. The Moon makes astronomy relatable: phases and tides are visible everyday science.
