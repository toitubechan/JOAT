## Card: What it is
image: econGdpReadingEconomicNewsHero
Economic news is full of indicators — GDP, inflation, unemployment, interest rates — and headlines that can confuse or alarm. **Reading economic news** well means understanding what these indicators mean together, and approaching headlines with healthy skepticism. It ties together the whole topic into a practical skill.

## Card: Why it matters
Economic news affects decisions and is often reported sensationally or misleadingly. Being able to interpret it helps you stay informed without being panicked or fooled. It also empowers you as a citizen and in your own finances. This is where the abstract concepts become a genuinely useful everyday ability.

## Card: Key terms
- **Indicator**: a statistic signaling economic conditions.
- **Context**: the bigger picture around a single number.
- **Sensationalism**: dramatizing news for attention.
- **Trend vs. blip**: a lasting direction vs. a one-off change.

## Card: Reading indicators together
image: econGdpReadingEconomicNewsReadingIndicatorsTogetherHero
No single **indicator** tells the whole story; they make sense *together*. Strong GDP growth with high inflation means something different than growth with low inflation. Low unemployment with rising wages differs from low unemployment with falling real pay. Look at the **context** — how indicators combine — rather than reacting to one number. The economy is a system, and the indicators are connected pieces.

## Card: Skepticism toward headlines
Economic headlines often use **sensationalism** — dramatic framing to grab attention ("economy in freefall!"). Be skeptical: check whether a change is a real **trend** or a one-month **blip**, whether figures are adjusted for inflation, and whether the framing matches the actual data. Single scary headlines rarely capture the nuanced, often mixed reality of an economy.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: reacting to one indicator in isolation; taking sensational headlines at face value; confusing a one-off blip with a real trend; ignoring whether figures are inflation-adjusted (real vs. nominal); and assuming the economy is simply "good" or "bad" when it's usually a mixed, nuanced picture. Read with context and a critical eye.

## Card: Quick recap
Read economic news by interpreting indicators (GDP, inflation, unemployment, rates) *together* and in context — no single number tells the whole story. Be skeptical of sensational headlines, distinguish trends from blips, and check for inflation adjustment. The economy is usually a mixed, nuanced picture, so read it critically rather than reacting to one scary figure.
