## Card: What it is
image: growthNotesPopularMethodsHero
There are several proven **note-taking methods**, each structuring information differently to suit different needs — from linear outlines to visual maps. Knowing a few lets you pick the right tool for the situation, rather than defaulting to messy, hard-to-use notes.

## Card: Why it matters
The *way* you structure notes affects how useful they are later. A method that fits the material makes notes clearer, easier to review, and more effective for learning. Most people only know one (usually messy) approach; a few alternatives can dramatically improve how well your notes serve you.

## Card: Key terms
- **Outline method**: notes structured with headings and indented points.
- **Cornell method**: a page split into notes, cues, and a summary.
- **Mind mapping**: a visual web of connected ideas.
- **Bullet points**: short, scannable lines instead of paragraphs.

## Card: Linear methods
image: growthNotesPopularMethodsLinearMethodsHero
The **outline method** structures notes hierarchically — main points with indented sub-points — great for organized material like lectures. The **Cornell method** divides the page into a main notes area, a narrow "cues" column for keywords/questions, and a summary at the bottom — excellent for studying, since it builds in review.

## Card: Visual methods
**Mind mapping** puts a central idea in the middle with branches radiating out to related concepts — ideal for brainstorming, seeing connections, and visual thinkers. It captures how ideas *relate*, not just a list. Different material suits different methods: structured talks suit outlines; interconnected or creative thinking suits mind maps.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: only ever using one method regardless of the material; writing dense paragraphs instead of scannable **bullet points**; making notes so elaborate they take all your attention; and choosing a method for how it looks rather than how useful it is to review. Match the method to the material and to how you'll use the notes.

## Card: Quick recap
Note-taking methods include the outline (hierarchical, for structured material), Cornell (notes plus cues and summary, great for study), and mind mapping (visual web, great for connections and brainstorming). Match the method to the material and how you'll use it. Scannable beats dense; useful beats pretty.
