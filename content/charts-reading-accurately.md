## Card: What it is
image: dataChartsReadingAccuratelyHero
**Reading a chart accurately** means looking past the eye-catching shape to understand what the data actually says. It comes down to checking a few key elements — the axes, labels, scale, and source — rather than just absorbing the visual impression. This is the skill that turns chart-viewing into genuine understanding.

## Card: Why it matters
A chart's visual impression can be very different from what the data really shows. Reading the key details protects you from misreading or being misled, and lets you extract the true message. Since charts are designed to make an impression fast, deliberately checking the details is what keeps you accurate rather than fooled.

## Card: Key terms
- **Axes**: the reference lines, showing what's measured and the scale.
- **Scale**: the range and spacing of values on an axis.
- **Labels and legend**: text explaining what the chart shows.
- **Source**: who produced the data and chart.

## Card: Check the axes and scale
image: dataChartsReadingAccuratelyCheckTheAxesAndScaleHero
The most important habit: read the **axes** before the shape. What do they measure (the labels), and what's the **scale**? A dramatic-looking spike might vanish if you notice the axis doesn't start at zero, or covers a huge range. The scale and starting point dramatically affect how data *looks*, so always check them before trusting the visual impression. The shape lies if the scale is deceptive.

## Card: Read labels, legend, and source
Beyond the axes, check the **labels and legend** (what each line, bar, or color represents), the units, the time period covered, and the **source** of the data. A chart with vague labels, no source, or an unclear time frame deserves skepticism. Understanding exactly what's being measured, over what period, by whom, is essential to reading the real message rather than a vague impression.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: reacting to the chart's shape without checking the axes and scale; missing that an axis doesn't start at zero (exaggerating differences); ignoring labels, units, or time period; and not questioning the source. The fix is a habit: read the axes, scale, labels, and source *first*, then interpret the shape in that context.

## Card: Quick recap
Read charts accurately by checking the details before the shape: the axes (what's measured), the scale and starting point (which dramatically affect how data looks), the labels and legend, the units and time period, and the source. A striking visual can mislead if the scale is deceptive. Read the details first, then interpret the picture in context.
