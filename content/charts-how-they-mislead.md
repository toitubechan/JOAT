## Card: What it is
image: dataChartsHowTheyMisleadHero
Charts can **mislead** even with accurate data, through visual tricks in how they're drawn. Knowing the common deceptions — truncated axes, distorted scales, cherry-picked ranges — helps you spot when a chart is designed to mislead. This is one of the most practically useful data-literacy skills.

## Card: Why it matters
Misleading charts appear in ads, news, and arguments, shaping opinions with technically-true data presented deceptively. Learning the tricks protects you from manipulation and sharpens your judgment of the visuals you see daily. Since charts persuade so powerfully and quickly, recognizing their distortions is a genuine defense.

## Card: Key terms
- **Truncated axis**: an axis not starting at zero, exaggerating differences.
- **Cherry-picked range**: showing only a time span that supports a claim.
- **Distorted scale**: uneven or manipulated spacing.
- **Misleading visuals**: chart design that deceives.

## Card: The truncated axis trick
image: dataChartsHowTheyMisleadTheTruncatedAxisTrickHero
The most common chart deception: the **truncated axis**. By not starting the vertical axis at zero, small differences look dramatic — a tiny change can appear as a huge spike or cliff. A bar that's *slightly* taller can be drawn to look *many times* bigger. Always check whether the axis starts at zero; if it doesn't, the visual differences are exaggerated, sometimes wildly.

## Card: Cherry-picking and distortion
Other tricks: **cherry-picked ranges** — showing only the time period that supports a claim (a stock chart of just the months it rose), hiding the fuller picture. **Distorted scales** — uneven spacing or odd units that warp the shape. And misleading chart types (a pie chart whose slices don't add up, or 3D effects that distort sizes). Each makes accurate data tell a false visual story.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: trusting a dramatic-looking chart without checking the axis starts at zero; not noticing a cherry-picked time range; being fooled by distorted scales or 3D effects; and assuming accurate data can't be charted misleadingly (it can). The defense: check the axis, the range shown, and the scale before believing the visual.

## Card: Quick recap
Charts mislead through visual tricks even with accurate data: truncated axes (not starting at zero) exaggerate differences, cherry-picked ranges hide the fuller picture, and distorted scales or 3D effects warp shapes. The defense is to check whether the axis starts at zero, what time range is shown, and whether the scale is honest — before trusting the dramatic visual.
