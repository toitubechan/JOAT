## Card: What it is
image: dataChartsWhyTheyMatterHero
A **chart** or **graph** is a visual way of showing data — turning numbers into pictures so patterns become easy to see. A good chart can reveal a trend or comparison instantly that would be hard to spot in a table of numbers. Understanding charts is a core part of data literacy.

## Card: Why it matters
Charts are everywhere — news, reports, presentations, social media. They make data understandable at a glance, but they can also distort and mislead. Being able to read charts accurately helps you understand information quickly and spot when a chart is being used to deceive. It's an essential modern skill.

## Card: Key terms
- **Chart / graph**: a visual representation of data.
- **Axis**: a reference line (often labeled) on a chart.
- **Trend**: a pattern or direction in data over time.
- **Data visualization**: presenting data visually.

## Card: Why we use charts
image: dataChartsWhyTheyMatterWhyWeUseChartsHero
Our brains process visual patterns far faster than columns of numbers. A **chart** turns data into a shape — a rising line, a tall bar, a big slice — that we grasp instantly. This is the power of **data visualization**: it reveals **trends**, comparisons, and patterns at a glance. A good chart can make a complex dataset's main message obvious in seconds.

## Card: A double-edged tool
But this power cuts both ways. Because charts shape perception so strongly and quickly, they can be designed — accidentally or deliberately — to mislead. The same data can tell very different stories depending on how it's charted. So charts are immensely useful *and* a common tool of manipulation. Reading them well means appreciating both their clarity and their potential to deceive.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: trusting a chart's visual impression without checking the details (axes, labels, scale); assuming charts are neutral (they involve choices that shape the message); being persuaded by a striking visual more than the actual data; and not realizing the same data can be charted to tell opposite stories. Read the details, not just the picture.

## Card: Quick recap
Charts turn numbers into pictures, letting us grasp trends and comparisons instantly — because our brains read visual patterns far faster than tables. But this power is double-edged: charts strongly shape perception, so they can clarify *or* mislead, and the same data can tell different stories. Reading charts well means checking the details, not just the visual impression.
