## Card: What it is
image: econGdpInterestRatesHero
**Interest rates** — set heavily by a country's **central bank** — are one of the main levers used to steer the whole economy. Raising or lowering rates influences borrowing, spending, and inflation across the country. Understanding this connects your mortgage or savings rate to the bigger economic picture.

## Card: Why it matters
Central bank interest rate decisions make headlines and directly affect your loans, mortgage, and savings. Understanding why they raise or lower rates demystifies a major economic force and helps you anticipate effects on your own money. It's the link between abstract economic policy and your personal finances.

## Card: Key terms
- **Interest rate**: the cost of borrowing money.
- **Central bank**: the institution managing a country's money and rates.
- **Tightening**: raising rates to cool the economy.
- **Easing**: lowering rates to stimulate the economy.

## Card: The main lever
image: econGdpInterestRatesTheMainLeverHero
The **central bank** sets a key **interest rate** that influences borrowing costs throughout the economy. This is a powerful lever: when borrowing is cheap (low rates), people and businesses borrow and spend more, stimulating the economy. When borrowing is expensive (high rates), they borrow and spend less, cooling it. By adjusting rates, the central bank tries to keep the economy steady.

## Card: Fighting inflation and recession
Central banks use rates to balance growth and inflation. To fight high inflation, they often **tighten** (raise rates) — making borrowing costlier slows spending and cools rising prices. To fight a slump or recession, they **ease** (cut rates) — cheaper borrowing encourages spending and stimulates growth. This is why rates rise when inflation is high and fall when the economy needs a boost.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: not connecting central bank rates to your own mortgage and savings (they're linked); thinking rate changes are arbitrary (they target inflation and growth); assuming low rates are always good (they can fuel inflation); and not realizing the central bank is balancing competing goals. Rates are a deliberate tool to steer the economy.

## Card: Quick recap
Central banks set a key interest rate to steer the economy: low rates make borrowing cheap, boosting spending and growth; high rates make it costly, cooling spending and inflation. They raise rates to fight inflation and cut them to fight recession. These decisions directly affect your loans and savings, linking policy to your wallet.
