## Card: What it is
image: mathPrimeNumbersHero
A **prime number** is a whole number greater than 1 that can only be divided evenly by 1 and itself — like 2, 3, 5, 7, 11. Primes are the "building blocks" of all numbers, and despite their simple definition, they hold deep mysteries and have a surprising modern use: keeping your online data secure.

## Card: Why it matters
Primes are fundamental to mathematics and, remarkably, to everyday digital security — much online encryption relies on them. Understanding what primes are reveals an elegant idea at the heart of numbers, and how something so abstract underpins practical technology you use daily. They bridge pure math and real-world importance.

## Card: Key terms
- **Prime number**: a whole number divisible only by 1 and itself.
- **Composite number**: a number with more than two divisors.
- **Building blocks**: primes combine to form all other numbers.
- **Encryption**: securing data, often using primes.

## Card: The building blocks of numbers
image: mathPrimeNumbersTheBuildingBlocksOfNumbersHero
A **prime number** has exactly two divisors: 1 and itself (so 7 is prime — only 1 and 7 divide it evenly; 8 is not — 2 and 4 also divide it). Primes are the **building blocks** of all whole numbers: every whole number is either prime or can be built by multiplying primes together (8 = 2×2×2). This is why primes are so fundamental — they're like the "atoms" from which all numbers are constructed.

## Card: Mysterious and useful
Primes are full of mystery: they appear scattered among the numbers without an obvious pattern, and questions about them remain unsolved despite centuries of study. Surprisingly, this abstract topic has a vital modern use: **encryption**. Much of online security relies on the fact that multiplying two large primes is easy, but figuring out which primes were multiplied (for a huge result) is extremely hard — making primes the basis of keeping digital data secure.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking 1 is prime (it isn't — primes are greater than 1, with exactly two divisors); imagining primes follow a simple pattern (they're famously irregular); seeing them as useless abstraction (they secure your online data); and not realizing every number is built from primes. Primes are simple to define yet deeply fundamental, mysterious, and practically vital.

## Card: Quick recap
A prime number is a whole number greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and itself (2, 3, 5, 7, 11...). Primes are the building blocks of all numbers — every whole number is prime or a product of primes. They're scattered without an obvious pattern and full of unsolved mysteries, yet they underpin online encryption (multiplying large primes is easy; reversing it is hard). Simple to define, deeply important.
