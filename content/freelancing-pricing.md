## Card: What it is
image: freelancingPricingHero
**Pricing** is deciding what to charge for your freelance work — one of the trickiest and most important decisions you'll make. Price too high and you may lose clients; too low and you can't survive or you attract the wrong clients. Getting it roughly right is central to a sustainable freelance income.

## Card: Why it matters
Many freelancers, especially beginners, badly underprice themselves out of fear or inexperience, then struggle to make a living and burn out. Understanding how to price fairly — and that your rate must cover far more than just your time — is what makes freelancing financially viable rather than a path to exhaustion.

## Card: Key terms
- **Hourly vs. project rate**: charging per hour or per whole project.
- **Overheads**: business costs beyond your direct time.
- **Underpricing**: charging too little to be sustainable.
- **Value-based pricing**: pricing by the value delivered, not just time.

## Card: Your rate covers more than time
image: rateCoversMoreHero
A crucial realization: your freelance rate isn't just your "wage." It must also cover **overheads** — taxes, equipment, software, insurance, unpaid admin time, no paid holidays or sick days, and the gaps between projects. This is why a freelance rate looks higher than an equivalent salary's hourly pay: much of it goes to costs an employer would otherwise cover.

## Card: Ways to price
You can charge an **hourly rate** (simple, but caps your income and penalizes efficiency) or a **project rate** (a fixed price for the whole job, often better as you improve). More advanced is **value-based pricing** — charging based on the value you deliver to the client, not just hours spent. Research what others in your field charge to anchor your numbers.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: **underpricing** out of fear or to win work, then being unable to sustain it; forgetting that the rate must cover overheads and gaps, not just time; competing only on being cheapest (a race to the bottom); never raising rates as you gain experience; and not researching market rates. Charge sustainably, not desperately.

## Card: Quick recap
Pricing makes or breaks freelance viability. Your rate must cover far more than your time — taxes, overheads, admin, gaps between work, and no paid leave — which is why it looks higher than a salary's hourly pay. Charge by project or value, not just hours, research market rates, and avoid the common trap of underpricing.
