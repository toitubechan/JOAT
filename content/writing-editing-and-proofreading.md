## Card: What it is
image: editingProofreadingHero
**Editing** is improving your writing's content and clarity, and **proofreading** is catching errors like typos and grammar. Together they're how rough first drafts become polished, professional writing. Good writing is mostly rewriting — the magic happens in the revision, not the first attempt.

## Card: Why it matters
First drafts are always rough, and sending unedited writing means errors, unclear points, and a poor impression. Editing and proofreading are what make writing actually good. Skipping them is the most common reason otherwise capable people produce sloppy, confusing, or error-ridden writing that undermines their credibility.

## Card: Key terms
- **Editing**: improving content, structure, and clarity.
- **Proofreading**: catching typos, spelling, and grammar errors.
- **Self-distancing**: reviewing with fresh eyes.
- **Read aloud**: a powerful proofreading technique.

## Card: Edit then proofread
image: editThenProofreadHero
These are two distinct steps. First **edit** for the big stuff: is it clear, well-structured, concise, and right for the reader? Cut clutter, fix confusing parts, reorder if needed. *Then* **proofread** for surface errors: typos, spelling, grammar, punctuation. Doing them separately works better than trying to fix everything at once — first the substance, then the polish.

## Card: Techniques that work
A few powerful tricks. **Self-distancing**: take a break before reviewing, so you see it with fresh eyes — you'll catch what you missed when too close to it. **Read it aloud**: your ear catches clunky sentences and errors your eye skips. And review on a different device or in print. For anything important, having someone else read it is invaluable.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: skipping editing and sending the first draft; proofreading immediately while too close to spot errors; relying only on spellcheck (it misses many things); trying to edit and proofread in one pass; and not reading aloud or getting a second pair of eyes. Build in time to revise — good writing is rewriting.

## Card: Quick recap
Editing improves content and clarity; proofreading catches surface errors — do them as separate steps, substance first then polish. Use fresh eyes (take a break), read aloud to catch what your eye skips, and get a second reader for important pieces. First drafts are rough; the quality comes from revising them.
