## Card: What it is
image: electricityEverydayHero
Electricity does work in our homes by being converted into other useful forms — light, heat, motion, and the tiny signals that run electronics. Understanding these everyday conversions connects the abstract science to the devices you use constantly, and even helps you understand your energy bill.

## Card: Why it matters
Seeing how electricity becomes light, heat, and motion makes everyday technology comprehensible rather than magical. It also helps you grasp energy use: why a heater or kettle costs more to run than a phone charger, and how to use electricity efficiently. The science directly touches your daily life and your bills.

## Card: Key terms
- **Energy conversion**: changing electricity into another form.
- **Power (watts)**: the rate at which energy is used.
- **AC vs. DC**: alternating vs. direct current.
- **Efficiency**: how much energy does useful work vs. is wasted.

## Card: Electricity becomes useful forms
image: energyConversionHero
Devices work by **energy conversion** — turning electricity into what we need. A bulb converts it to **light**, a heater or kettle to **heat**, a motor (in fans, washing machines) to **motion**, and electronics into tiny controlled signals. All your appliances are essentially different ways of converting flowing electricity into a useful form of energy.

## Card: Power, watts, and your bill
**Power**, measured in **watts**, is how fast a device uses energy. High-power devices that make *heat or motion* (heaters, kettles, ovens, washing machines) use lots of energy and cost more to run; low-power electronics (phones, LED bulbs) use little. This is why heating-related appliances dominate energy bills. Knowing watts helps you understand and manage usage. (Homes use **AC**, while batteries supply **DC** — devices convert as needed.)

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming all plugged-in devices use similar energy (heat-making ones use vastly more); not realizing leaving heating appliances on is what drives bills; confusing the small charge cost of electronics with the large cost of heating; and not understanding why chargers convert AC to DC. Heat and motion are the energy-hungry conversions.

## Card: Quick recap
Electricity powers devices through energy conversion — into light, heat, motion, or electronic signals. Power (watts) measures how fast energy is used; heat- and motion-making appliances (heaters, kettles, washers) use the most and cost the most to run. Homes use AC, batteries DC. Understanding this demystifies devices and your energy bill.
