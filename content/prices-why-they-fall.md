## Card: What it is
image: econPricesWhyTheyFallHero
Prices don't only rise — they also fall, for understandable reasons: falling demand, rising supply, lower production costs, technology improvements, or competition. Understanding why prices drop completes the picture and helps you spot genuine bargains versus misleading "discounts."

## Card: Why it matters
Falling prices affect your buying decisions and timing. Understanding why prices drop helps you recognize real value, time purchases (like buying tech after newer models arrive), and see through fake "sales." It's the other half of understanding price changes, and a practically useful one for spending wisely.

## Card: Key terms
- **Falling demand**: fewer buyers pushes prices down.
- **Rising supply**: more availability pushes prices down.
- **Technology / efficiency**: cheaper ways to produce things.
- **Competition**: rivals undercutting each other on price.

## Card: Demand down or supply up
image: econPricesWhyTheyFallDemandDownOrSupplyUpHero
The supply-and-demand causes work in reverse too. **Falling demand**: when fewer people want something (a trend fades, a product ages), prices drop to attract buyers. **Rising supply**: when more becomes available (new producers, a bumper crop, overstock), prices fall as sellers compete to sell it. Both ease the "too many buyers for too few goods" pressure, pulling prices down.

## Card: Technology, efficiency, and competition
Over time, **technology** and **efficiency** make many things cheaper to produce — which is why electronics often fall in price as production improves and newer models arrive. **Competition** also drives prices down: rival sellers undercut each other to win customers. These forces explain long-term price drops (gadgets getting cheaper) and everyday discounts (shops competing for your business).

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming a "discount" is always a real saving (some are inflated "was" prices or marketing); not timing purchases around predictable price drops (older tech, end-of-season); thinking prices only ever rise; and missing how competition and technology steadily lower real prices over time. Understanding the causes helps you spot genuine value.

## Card: Quick recap
Prices fall for real reasons: falling demand, rising supply, cheaper production via technology and efficiency, and competition between sellers. This explains long-term drops (gadgets getting cheaper) and everyday discounts. Knowing the causes helps you time purchases, spot genuine bargains, and see through misleading "sales." Prices change both ways, for understandable reasons.
