## Card: What it is
image: cvStrongContentHero
Strong CV **content** is about *how* you describe your experience — turning flat job duties into compelling evidence of what you can do. The same role can sound dull or impressive depending on the wording. This is the skill of making your real experience shine without exaggerating.

## Card: Why it matters
Two people with identical experience can write wildly different CVs — one forgettable, one that lands interviews. Knowing how to phrase your achievements makes your genuine experience count for as much as it should. It's often the difference between a CV that gets skimmed past and one that stands out.

## Card: Key terms
- **Achievement**: a result you produced, not just a duty.
- **Action verb**: a strong verb that starts a bullet (led, built, improved).
- **Quantify**: backing claims with numbers.
- **Duties vs. results**: what you were responsible for vs. what you achieved.

## Card: Show results, not just duties
image: resultsNotDutiesHero
The biggest upgrade: describe **achievements**, not just **duties**. "Responsible for social media" is a duty; "Grew social media following by 40% in six months" is a result. Wherever possible, show the *impact* of what you did. Results prove your value far more powerfully than a list of responsibilities anyone in the role would have had.

## Card: Use action verbs and numbers
Start bullet points with strong **action verbs** — led, built, improved, launched, saved — which sound dynamic and confident. And **quantify** wherever you can: numbers (percentages, amounts, time saved) make achievements concrete and credible. "Improved efficiency" is vague; "cut processing time by 30%" is convincing. Specifics beat generalities every time.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: listing duties instead of achievements; weak, passive phrasing ("was involved in"); vague claims with no numbers; overused empty buzzwords ("hardworking team player") without evidence; and, at the other extreme, exaggerating or lying, which backfires in interviews. Be specific, results-focused, and honest.

## Card: Quick recap
Strong CV content shows achievements and results, not just duties — backed by strong action verbs and concrete numbers. "Grew sales by 25%" beats "responsible for sales." Avoid vague buzzwords and passive phrasing, but never exaggerate. Make your real experience shine through specific, quantified, results-focused writing.
