## Card: What it is
image: workEmailHero
Much workplace communication is **written** — emails, chat messages, documents. Written communication is trickier than it looks: there's no tone of voice or body language, so misunderstandings are easy. Knowing how to write clearly and appropriately is essential, because so much of work now happens in text.

## Card: Why it matters
Poorly written messages cause confusion, get ignored, or create a bad impression. Clear, well-structured writing gets your point across, gets faster responses, and makes you look competent. Since people judge you partly by your writing, this is a skill worth getting right — and most workplace writing problems are easily fixed.

## Card: Key terms
- **Subject line**: the email's title, summarizing its purpose.
- **Tone**: how a message comes across emotionally.
- **Concise**: short and to the point.
- **Call to action**: making clear what you want the reader to do.

## Card: Structure for the reader
image: readerStructureHero
Busy people skim. Use a clear **subject line** that states the purpose, lead with the main point or request, keep it **concise**, and make any **call to action** obvious (what you need, and by when). Bullet points help for multiple items. The reader should grasp what you want within seconds, not after a paragraph of preamble.

## Card: Mind the tone
Written text lacks vocal **tone** and body language, so it's easily misread — a brief reply can seem curt, a joke can fall flat. Err toward warmth and clarity, reread before sending, and match formality to the context and recipient. When something is sensitive or emotional, a conversation often beats a message, which can escalate misunderstandings.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: vague or missing subject lines; burying the request at the bottom; overly long, rambling messages; unclear next steps; tone that reads as cold or rude without meaning to; hitting "reply all" unnecessarily; and writing emotional messages in the heat of the moment. Reread before sending, every time.

## Card: Quick recap
Write workplace messages for a skimming reader: clear subject line, main point first, concise, with an obvious call to action. Mind the tone, since text lacks vocal cues and is easily misread — reread before sending and match the formality. For sensitive matters, a conversation often beats a message.
