## Card: What it is
image: blackHoleDeepDiveHero
A **black hole** is a region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape it. They form when massive stars collapse, packing enormous mass into a tiny space. Black holes are among the strangest and most extreme objects in the universe, where the normal rules seem to break down.

## Card: Why it matters
Black holes capture the imagination like few things in science, and understanding them reveals how extreme gravity can be and how stars end their lives. They're central to modern astronomy and physics. Grasping the basics demystifies a concept that sounds like science fiction but is very real.

## Card: Key terms
- **Black hole**: a region where gravity prevents anything escaping.
- **Event horizon**: the boundary beyond which nothing can return.
- **Gravity**: the attractive force, extreme in a black hole.
- **Singularity**: the incredibly dense center.

## Card: What makes them "black"
image: eventHorizonHero
A black hole's gravity is so intense that beyond a certain boundary — the **event horizon** — nothing can escape, not even light. That's why it's "black": no light can leave, so we can't see it directly. The event horizon is a point of no return: cross it, and you cannot get back out. The black hole's mass is crushed into an incredibly dense center called the **singularity**.

## Card: How they form and how we detect them
Most black holes form when a very **massive star** runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity, packing its mass into a tiny space. Since black holes emit no light, we detect them *indirectly* — by their gravitational effects on nearby stars and gas, and the radiation given off as material falls in. Giant black holes also sit at the centers of galaxies, including our own.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking black holes are "cosmic vacuum cleaners" sucking in everything (they only pull things that get close, like any gravity); imagining we can see them directly (we detect them indirectly, by their effects); thinking they're empty holes (they're extremely *dense* concentrations of mass); and seeing them as fiction (they're real and well-evidenced). They're extreme, but they obey physics.

## Card: Quick recap
A black hole is a region where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, escapes beyond its event horizon — the point of no return. They form when massive stars collapse, crushing mass into an incredibly dense center. Since they emit no light, we detect them indirectly by their effects. Real and well-evidenced, black holes are among the universe's most extreme objects.
