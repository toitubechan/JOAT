## Card: What it is
image: commonScamsHero
Scams come in recognizable flavors, repeated endlessly with small variations. Knowing the **common types** — from fake delivery texts to romance and investment scams — means you recognize them on sight. The disguises change, but the core cons are surprisingly few and stable.

## Card: Why it matters
Familiarity is protection. Once you've seen the shape of a romance scam or a "tech support" call, you'll spot the next one instantly, even with a new story. Knowing the common types also helps you warn others — especially those most targeted, like the elderly or the lonely.

## Card: Key terms
- **Impersonation scam**: pretending to be a bank, government, or company.
- **Romance scam**: faking a relationship to extract money.
- **Investment scam**: fake opportunities promising big returns.
- **Tech support scam**: pretending your device has a problem.

## Card: Everyday impersonation scams
image: impersonationScams
The most common: **impersonation scams**. Fake texts about a "missed delivery" needing a small fee; messages claiming to be your bank flagging "suspicious activity"; fake government or tax notices demanding payment; and the "hi mum/dad" scam, pretending to be a family member with a new number needing urgent money. All exploit trust and routine.

## Card: Higher-stakes scams
Some scams play a longer game. **Romance scams** build a fake relationship over weeks, then invent emergencies needing money. **Investment scams** (including crypto) promise guaranteed high returns. **Tech support scams** claim your computer is infected to gain access or payment. These cause the largest individual losses, precisely because they earn trust first.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: paying a small "fee" on a fake delivery text without checking; trusting an online romance that quickly asks for money; believing a "guaranteed return" investment; and giving remote access to a "tech support" caller you didn't contact. The rule: if you didn't initiate it, be deeply skeptical.

## Card: Quick recap
Common scams include fake deliveries, bank and government impersonation, "hi mum/dad" messages, romance scams, fake investments, and tech-support cons. The stories vary but the cons repeat. If a message or call you didn't start asks for money, details, or access, treat it as a likely scam until proven otherwise.
