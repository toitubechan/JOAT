## Card: What it is
image: creativeEditingHero
**Editing** is revising and improving your writing after the first draft. A common truth among writers: good writing is *rewriting*. First drafts are rarely good — they're raw material — and editing is where they become genuinely good. Understanding how to edit your own work is as important as writing it in the first place, yet often overlooked by beginners.

## Card: Why it matters
Beginners often think writing means getting it right the first time, then feel discouraged when their drafts are messy. Understanding that editing is where writing becomes good — and that everyone's first drafts are rough — is liberating and essential. Strong editing transforms mediocre drafts into polished work. It's where much of the real quality of writing actually comes from.

## Card: Key terms
- **Editing**: revising and improving a draft.
- **First draft**: the rough initial version (raw material).
- **Big-picture vs. line edits**: structure/content vs. word-level fixes.
- **Distance**: stepping back to see your work clearly.

## Card: First drafts are meant to be rough
image: roughDraftRefineHero
A liberating truth: **first drafts** are *supposed* to be rough. As the saying goes, the first draft is just you telling yourself the story — its job is to exist, not to be perfect. The real quality comes through **editing**. So don't judge your writing by the messy first draft; judge it after revision. This mindset frees you to write freely (getting it down) and then improve it separately — rather than expecting perfection first time.

## Card: How to edit well
Edit in layers. Start with **big-picture** issues — does the structure work? Is the story/argument clear? Are there problems with flow, pacing, or content? Fix these before polishing sentences (no point perfecting a paragraph you'll cut). Then do **line edits** — improving word choice, cutting unnecessary words, fixing clarity and errors. Crucially, get **distance** before editing — set the draft aside, then return with fresh eyes (or read it aloud), since you can't edit well what you're too close to. Editing is deliberate, layered improvement.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: expecting the first draft to be good (it's raw material); editing line-by-line before fixing big-picture problems (polishing what you'll cut); editing immediately without distance (you miss flaws when too close); not cutting unnecessary words (good editing often means cutting); and skipping editing entirely. Good writing is rewriting — edit in layers, with fresh eyes, big-picture first.

## Card: Quick recap
Editing — revising after the first draft — is where writing becomes good, since first drafts are meant to be rough raw material. Don't judge your writing by the messy draft; judge it after revision. Edit in layers: fix big-picture issues (structure, clarity, flow) before line-level polishing (word choice, cutting, errors). Get distance first, returning with fresh eyes. Good writing is rewriting — editing is where much of the quality comes from.
