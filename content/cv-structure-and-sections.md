## Card: What it is
image: cvStructureHero
A good CV follows a familiar **structure** so recruiters can find what they need fast. While details vary, most CVs share the same core **sections** in a logical order. Knowing this standard layout means yours looks professional and is easy to navigate.

## Card: Why it matters
Recruiters expect a certain format and get frustrated by CVs that hide information in unusual places. A clear, conventional structure signals professionalism and makes you easy to assess. It's not the place to be wildly creative — clarity and familiarity win, letting your actual content shine.

## Card: Key terms
- **Contact details**: how to reach you.
- **Personal statement / summary**: a short pitch at the top.
- **Work experience**: your job history, most recent first.
- **Skills and education**: your abilities and qualifications.

## Card: The core sections
image: coreCvSectionsHero
A standard CV includes: **contact details** (name, phone, email) at the top; a short **personal statement** summarizing who you are and what you offer; **work experience** (jobs listed most recent first); **education** and qualifications; and a **skills** section. Some add achievements or relevant projects. This order puts the most useful information where recruiters look.

## Card: Order and length
List experience in **reverse-chronological** order — most recent first — since recent roles matter most. Keep the CV concise: usually one to two pages. The strongest, most relevant content goes near the top, where skimming eyes land first. A focused two-page CV beats a rambling five-page one every time.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: missing or hard-to-find contact details; no summary, so the reader has to dig; listing jobs oldest-first; cramming in everything until it's many pages; and over-designing with unusual layouts that hide information. Stick to the familiar structure, reverse-chronological order, and a concise length.

## Card: Quick recap
A standard CV has contact details, a short summary, reverse-chronological work experience, education, and skills — in that familiar order, kept to one or two pages. This conventional structure lets recruiters find information fast and signals professionalism. Lead with your strongest, most relevant content near the top.
