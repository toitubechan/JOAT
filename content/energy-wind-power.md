## Card: What it is
image: energyWindPowerHero
**Wind power** generates electricity using **wind turbines** — large rotating blades driven by moving air. As the wind spins the blades, a generator inside turns that motion into electricity. It's a major renewable energy source, often working alongside solar in the clean-energy transition.

## Card: Why it matters
Wind is one of the fastest-growing renewable energy sources — clean, renewable, and increasingly cost-effective. Understanding it helps you grasp the clean-energy mix, why turbines dot landscapes and seas, and the challenges of running on renewables. Along with solar, it's central to reducing reliance on fossil fuels.

## Card: Key terms
- **Wind turbine**: a machine converting wind into electricity.
- **Renewable**: an energy source that doesn't run out.
- **Onshore vs. offshore**: turbines on land vs. at sea.
- **Intermittent**: producing power only when the wind blows.

## Card: How wind power works
image: energyWindPowerHowWindPowerWorksHero
A **wind turbine** works like a reverse fan: instead of using electricity to make wind, it uses wind to make electricity. Moving air pushes the large blades, which spin a shaft connected to a generator, producing electricity (the same spinning-magnet principle behind most generation). Turbines are built where wind is strong and steady — on hills, plains, and increasingly at sea (**offshore**), where winds are stronger.

## Card: Strengths and challenges
Wind's strengths: renewable, clean (no emissions while running), and cheap once built. **Offshore** wind especially benefits from strong, steady sea winds. Its main limitation, like solar, is being **intermittent** — it only generates when the wind blows. This variability is why a mix of energy sources, plus storage and a flexible grid, is needed to keep supply reliable.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking turbines work in any breeze (they need sufficient, steady wind); assuming wind alone can reliably power everything (its intermittency requires a balanced mix and storage); and not knowing the offshore-vs-onshore distinction. Wind is clean and increasingly cheap, but variable — best as part of a diverse energy mix.

## Card: Quick recap
Wind power uses turbines — wind spins the blades, driving a generator to make electricity, with no emissions while running. It's renewable, increasingly cheap, and strong offshore where sea winds are steady. Like solar, it's intermittent (only generating when the wind blows), so it works best in a diverse energy mix with storage and a flexible grid.
