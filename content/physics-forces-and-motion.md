## Card: What it is
image: forcesMotionHero
**Forces** are pushes and pulls, and they're what make things move, stop, speed up, slow down, or change direction. **Motion** is the result. This is the physics behind everyday actions — kicking a ball, braking a car, pushing a door. Understanding forces explains why things move the way they do.

## Card: Why it matters
Forces and motion govern everything physical around you. Understanding the basic rules — like why a moving object keeps going unless something stops it — demystifies everyday physics and makes the world predictable. These ideas, from a famous scientist named Newton, underpin much of how we understand the physical world.

## Card: Key terms
- **Force**: a push or a pull.
- **Inertia**: an object's resistance to changing its motion.
- **Friction**: a force that opposes motion between surfaces.
- **Newton's laws**: the basic rules of forces and motion.

## Card: Things keep doing what they're doing
A key idea (Newton's first law): an object stays still, or keeps moving steadily, unless a **force** acts on it. This is **inertia** — the tendency to resist changes in motion. A ball doesn't stop rolling on its own; something (like **friction**) stops it. Likewise, you lurch forward in a braking car because your body "wants" to keep moving. Motion persists until a force changes it.

## Card: Forces change motion
image: forcesChangeMotion
To change an object's motion — start it, stop it, speed it up, turn it — you need a **force**. Bigger forces cause bigger changes, and heavier objects need more force to move (Newton's second law). And forces come in pairs: push on something and it pushes back equally (Newton's third law) — which is how rockets work and why you feel recoil. Forces are the cause; motion is the effect.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking moving objects naturally slow down "by themselves" (it's friction, a force, that stops them); believing heavier things always fall faster (they don't, ignoring air resistance); not realizing forces come in pairs; and confusing the cause (force) with the effect (motion). Forces explain all changes in how things move.

## Card: Quick recap
Forces are pushes and pulls that cause all changes in motion. Objects keep doing what they're doing (inertia) until a force — like friction — acts on them. Bigger forces cause bigger changes, heavier objects need more force, and forces come in equal pairs. Forces are the cause; motion is the effect.
