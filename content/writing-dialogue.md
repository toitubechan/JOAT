## Card: What it is
image: dialogueHero
**Dialogue** is the conversation between characters in a story. Good dialogue sounds natural, reveals character, and moves the story forward — but it's a craft, not just transcribing how people actually talk. Understanding what makes dialogue work helps you write conversations that feel alive and serve your story, rather than flat or aimless exchanges.

## Card: Why it matters
Dialogue is often where stories come alive — it reveals character, creates drama, and engages readers directly. Poorly written dialogue feels stilted or pointless and can ruin otherwise good writing. Understanding the craft of dialogue helps you write conversations that feel real, reveal who characters are, and drive the story. It's a skill that dramatically affects how engaging your writing feels.

## Card: Key terms
- **Dialogue**: conversation between characters.
- **Subtext**: meaning beneath the literal words.
- **Purpose**: dialogue serving the story, not just filling space.
- **Distinct voices**: characters sounding like individuals.

## Card: Real but not realistic
A key paradox: good dialogue *sounds* natural but isn't a literal transcript of real speech. Real conversation is full of boring filler, repetition, and rambling — putting all that on the page would be tedious. Good dialogue is *crafted* to *feel* real while being more focused and purposeful than actual talk. It captures the rhythm and flavor of real speech, trimmed of the dull parts. Aim for the *impression* of natural conversation, not an exact recording of it.

## Card: Purpose, voice, and subtext
image: subtextVoiceHero
Strong dialogue does several things at once. It has **purpose** — it reveals character, advances the plot, or builds tension, rather than just filling space. Characters have **distinct voices** — they don't all sound the same; word choice and rhythm reflect who they are. And good dialogue often uses **subtext** — meaning beneath the literal words (people rarely say exactly what they mean; tension and depth come from what's left unsaid). Dialogue working on these levels feels alive and rich.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: writing dialogue as a literal transcript of rambling real speech (tedious); dialogue with no purpose (just filler that doesn't reveal character or move the story); every character sounding identical (no distinct voices); characters stating exactly what they feel with no subtext (flat and unrealistic); and over-explaining. Good dialogue is crafted to feel real, serve a purpose, give distinct voices, and carry subtext.

## Card: Quick recap
Dialogue is conversation between characters, and good dialogue is crafted to *feel* natural while being more focused than real speech (which is full of dull filler). Strong dialogue serves a purpose (revealing character, advancing plot, building tension), gives characters distinct voices, and uses subtext (meaning beneath the words). Avoid pointless filler, identical-sounding characters, and stating everything outright. Crafted dialogue makes a story come alive.
