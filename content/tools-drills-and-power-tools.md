## Card: What it is
image: drillHero
A **power drill** is usually the first power tool worth owning — it drills holes and, with the right bit, drives screws far faster than by hand. Modern **cordless** drills are easy and safe for beginners. Understanding the basics unlocks a huge range of home projects.

## Card: Why it matters
Many jobs that feel out of reach — hanging heavy shelves, assembling furniture, fixing into walls — become simple with a drill. It's the tool that most expands what a beginner can do. Learning to use it confidently and safely is a genuine step up in home capability.

## Card: Key terms
- **Drill bit**: the cutting piece that makes the hole.
- **Chuck**: the adjustable grip that holds the bit.
- **Torque**: the turning force, adjustable for driving screws.
- **Pilot hole**: a small guide hole drilled before a screw or larger hole.

## Card: The basic parts
The **chuck** is the jaws at the front that grip the **drill bit** — you open and close it to swap bits. A trigger controls speed. Most drills have a **torque** setting (a numbered collar) to limit force when driving screws, so you don't overtighten. A switch reverses direction for removing screws.

## Card: Drilling safely and well
image: safeDrillWall
Match the **drill bit** to the material (wood, metal, masonry each need their own type). Start a hole slowly and let the drill do the work — don't force it. Drill a small **pilot hole** first for accuracy and to prevent splitting. Crucially, check for hidden wires and pipes before drilling into any wall.

## Card: Common mistakes
A serious mistake is drilling into a wall without checking for hidden electrical wires or pipes — use a detector and avoid drilling directly above or below sockets and switches. Other errors: using the wrong bit for the material, forcing the drill, and wearing loose clothing or no eye protection near flying debris.

## Card: Quick recap
A power drill drills holes and drives screws, making many jobs easy. Learn the chuck and bits, match the bit to the material, start slow, and use a pilot hole. Above all, check walls for hidden wires and pipes before drilling, and wear eye protection. It's the beginner's most empowering tool.
