## Card: What it is
image: financeInvestingRiskAndReturnHero
In investing, **risk** is the chance your investment loses value or doesn't perform as hoped, and **return** is what you gain. The two are linked: investments offering higher potential returns almost always carry higher risk. There's no free lunch — bigger possible reward means bigger possible loss.

## Card: Why it matters
Understanding this trade-off protects you from two errors: chasing "amazing returns" without seeing the danger, and hiding entirely in ultra-safe options that barely grow. Knowing where an investment sits on the risk-return scale lets you match it to your goals and how much ups and downs you can stomach.

## Card: Key terms
- **Risk**: the chance of losing value or underperforming.
- **Return**: the gain or loss on an investment.
- **Volatility**: how much an investment's value swings up and down.
- **Risk tolerance**: how much risk you can handle, financially and emotionally.

## Card: The risk-return trade-off
image: financeInvestingRiskAndReturnTheRiskReturnTradeOffHero
Picture a ladder. At the bottom sit low-risk, low-return options like savings accounts — safe but slow. Higher up are bonds, then shares, offering more growth potential but more **volatility**. Anything promising high returns with "no risk" is a red flag, because that combination doesn't exist in honest investing.

## Card: Know your risk tolerance
**Risk tolerance** has two sides: how much loss your finances can absorb, and how much swinging you can emotionally handle without panic-selling. A young saver investing for decades can usually ride out dips; someone needing the money soon can't. Matching investments to your real tolerance keeps you from bailing at the worst moment.

## Card: Common mistakes
The classic mistake is being lured by high advertised returns while ignoring the matching risk — often a sign of a scam. The opposite mistake is so much caution that inflation quietly erodes your money. And many misjudge their own tolerance, taking big risks then panic-selling the first time prices drop, locking in the loss.

## Card: Quick recap
Risk and return rise together: higher potential gains mean higher potential losses, and "high return, no risk" is a warning sign. Know your risk tolerance — both financial and emotional — and match investments to it. Too much caution loses to inflation; too much risk invites panic. Balance is the goal.
