## Card: What it is
image: financePersonalEmergencyFundHero
An **emergency fund** is a stash of money set aside only for genuine surprises — a job loss, a medical bill, a broken-down car. It's not for holidays or shopping. It's a financial cushion that sits quietly until life throws something unexpected at you.

## Card: Why it matters
Without a cushion, a single surprise forces you into debt — credit cards, loans, or borrowing from friends. With one, the same surprise is just an inconvenience you pay for and move on. An emergency fund is what turns a crisis into a hassle.

## Card: Key terms
- **Emergency fund**: cash reserved for true, unexpected needs.
- **Liquid**: money you can access quickly, like a savings account.
- **Runway**: how many months you could cover with no income.
- **Buffer**: a smaller starter cushion for minor surprises.

## Card: How much to aim for
image: financePersonalEmergencyFundHowMuchToAimForHero
A common guideline is **three to six months** of essential expenses — enough runway to cover rent, food, and bills if income stops. That can feel huge, so start small: a **buffer** of one month's expenses, or even a fixed starter amount, already prevents most small emergencies from becoming debt.

## Card: Keep it separate and reachable
An emergency fund should be **liquid** — easy to access within a day or two — but kept apart from your everyday spending account so you're not tempted to dip in. A separate savings account works well. Avoid locking it in investments that can drop in value right when you need the cash.

## Card: Common mistakes
The first mistake is investing the emergency fund for higher returns — then a market dip leaves it shrunken exactly when you need it. The second is "borrowing" from it for non-emergencies and never refilling it. If you use it, treat refilling it as your next top priority.

## Card: Quick recap
An emergency fund is liquid cash for real surprises, ideally three to six months of essentials — but any amount beats none. Keep it separate, keep it safe, and refill it after use. It's the difference between a setback and a spiral.
