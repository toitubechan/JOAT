## Card: What it is
image: growthTimePlanningHero
**Planning** is mapping your tasks onto your time — deciding not just *what* to do, but *when*. A vague to-do list becomes far more powerful when its items have a place in your day. Good planning turns good intentions into action that actually happens.

## Card: Why it matters
A long to-do list with no plan is overwhelming and easy to ignore. Scheduling tasks makes them concrete and realistic, reveals when you're overcommitted, and removes the constant low-level stress of "what should I be doing?" A few minutes planning saves much more time and worry later.

## Card: Key terms
- **To-do list**: a list of tasks to complete.
- **Time blocking**: assigning tasks to specific time slots.
- **Single-tasking**: focusing on one thing at a time.
- **Buffer time**: spare slack for overruns and the unexpected.

## Card: From list to schedule
image: growthTimePlanningFromListToScheduleHero
A **to-do list** tells you *what*; a schedule tells you *when*. **Time blocking** — giving each important task a slot in your day or week — makes tasks concrete and stops them floating undone. It also exposes overcommitment: if your tasks don't fit your hours, planning shows it now, not at midnight when nothing's done.

## Card: Plan realistically
Two keys to plans that survive. **Single-task**: schedule focused blocks for one thing at a time, since multitasking is usually slower and worse. And leave **buffer time** — don't pack every minute, because things overrun and surprises happen. A plan with slack bends; a plan with none breaks at the first interruption.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: an endless to-do list with no schedule, so nothing has a time; over-packing the day with no buffer, then feeling like a failure when it slips; trying to multitask across blocks; and planning in unrealistic detail you'll never follow. A simple, realistic plan with breathing room beats an ambitious, brittle one.

## Card: Quick recap
Planning turns a to-do list into a schedule by deciding *when* tasks happen, often via time blocking. It makes tasks concrete and reveals overcommitment early. Keep it realistic: single-task in focused blocks and leave buffer time for overruns. A plan with slack survives real life; a packed one breaks.
