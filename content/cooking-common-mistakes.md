## Card: What it is
image: cookingMistakesHero
Most home-cooking disappointments come from a short list of repeated mistakes, not from lacking talent. This card set collects the big ones — so you can avoid them and instantly cook better. Fixing these few habits often does more than any new recipe.

## Card: Why it matters
You can follow a recipe perfectly and still get mediocre results if you're making these common errors. Recognizing them helps you diagnose *why* a dish didn't work and fix it next time. Cooking improves fastest not by learning more, but by stopping a few specific mistakes.

## Card: Key terms
- **Preheating**: getting the pan or oven hot before cooking.
- **Overcrowding**: cramming too much into the pan at once.
- **Resting meat**: letting cooked meat sit before cutting.
- **Mise en place**: prepping ingredients before you start cooking.

## Card: Heat and crowding errors
image: hotPanCrowding
Two big ones: not **preheating** the pan, so food sticks and never browns; and **overcrowding**, which drops the temperature and steams food grey instead of searing it. Cook in batches if needed. A hot, not-too-full pan is behind most successful browning — and most failures are one of these two errors.

## Card: Prep and patience errors
Other classics: not doing **mise en place** (prepping everything first), so you're scrambling and burning things mid-cook; not **resting meat** after cooking, so the juices run out the moment you cut it; and cutting against the natural approach. A little prep and patience prevents a lot of avoidable failure.

## Card: Common mistakes
To gather them: under-seasoning; not preheating; overcrowding the pan; constantly stirring so nothing browns; ignoring resting time for meat; not tasting as you go; and not reading the recipe fully before starting, then getting caught out by a step. Each is simple to fix once you know it.

## Card: Quick recap
Most cooking disappointments trace to a few fixable habits: under-seasoning, not preheating, overcrowding the pan, over-stirring, skipping meat's resting time, not tasting, and not prepping or reading the recipe first. Avoid these, and your cooking improves immediately — no new talent required, just better habits.
