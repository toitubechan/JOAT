## Card: What it is
image: wifiRouterHero
**Wi-Fi** is a way to connect to the internet wirelessly over short distances, usually through a **router** in your home or a public hotspot. It's how most phones and laptops get online without cables. Understanding it helps you connect reliably and, importantly, safely.

## Card: Why it matters
Wi-Fi is so routine we rarely think about it — until it's slow, won't connect, or turns out to be a security risk. Knowing the basics helps you fix common problems and avoid the genuine dangers of careless connecting, especially on public networks where others may be watching.

## Card: Key terms
- **Wi-Fi**: wireless internet over short distances.
- **Router**: the device that beams Wi-Fi and connects you to the internet.
- **Mobile data**: internet through the phone network, not Wi-Fi.
- **Public Wi-Fi**: open networks in cafés, airports, and hotels.

## Card: Wi-Fi vs. mobile data
image: wifiVsMobileData
Two main ways a phone gets online: **Wi-Fi** (wireless from a nearby router, usually free and faster at home) and **mobile data** (through the phone network, working almost anywhere but counted against your plan). Your phone switches between them. Knowing the difference helps you avoid surprise data charges and connect where it's best.

## Card: The router's role
At home, a **router** does two jobs: it connects to your internet provider's line, and it broadcasts Wi-Fi so your devices can join wirelessly. Many connection problems are fixed by restarting the router. Position matters too — thick walls and distance weaken the signal, which is why far rooms often have patchy Wi-Fi.

## Card: Common mistakes
The biggest mistake is treating **public Wi-Fi** as fully safe — on open networks, others may intercept what you do, so avoid logging into sensitive accounts unless protected. Another is never changing a router's default password, leaving it easy to misuse. And many forget that simply restarting the router fixes a surprising share of issues.

## Card: Quick recap
Wi-Fi connects devices wirelessly via a router; mobile data uses the phone network anywhere but counts against your plan. Restarting the router fixes many problems, and signal weakens with distance and walls. Treat public Wi-Fi with caution — avoid sensitive logins on open networks, and secure your home router.
