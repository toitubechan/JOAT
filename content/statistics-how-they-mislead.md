## Card: What it is
image: dataStatisticsHowTheyMisleadHero
Statistics can be technically true yet deeply **misleading**, depending on how they're presented. Knowing the common ways numbers deceive helps you read statistics critically and avoid being fooled by ads, headlines, and arguments that use real figures dishonestly. This is a vital data-literacy skill.

## Card: Why it matters
"There are lies, damned lies, and statistics." Misleading statistics shape opinions, sell products, and win arguments — often without anyone technically lying. Learning to spot the tricks protects you from manipulation and helps you think clearly about the figures behind important decisions. It's one of the most practically valuable things in data literacy.

## Card: Key terms
- **Cherry-picking**: showing only the data that supports a claim.
- **Misleading average**: choosing the average that paints the desired picture.
- **Small sample**: too few cases to draw reliable conclusions.
- **Missing context**: a figure presented without what it should be compared to.

## Card: Common misleading tricks
image: dataStatisticsHowTheyMisleadCommonMisleadingTricksHero
Watch for these: **cherry-picking** (showing only favorable data, hiding the rest); **misleading averages** (choosing mean vs. median to suit the message); **small samples** ("9 out of 10 people agree!" — out of how many?); and **missing context** ("crime up 40%!" — from what base, over what time, compared to what?). Each can make a technically true number tell a false story.

## Card: The base and the framing
Two big traps. The **base**: a "200% increase" sounds huge but could be from 1 case to 3. Always ask "out of what?" or "compared to what?". And **framing**: the same fact stated as "10% fail" versus "90% succeed" feels very different. Percentages without their base, and figures framed to alarm or reassure, are classic ways true numbers mislead. Asking for the full picture defuses them.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: accepting statistics at face value because they're "numbers"; not asking about the sample size or base; being swayed by framing; trusting cherry-picked figures; and assuming a true statistic can't be misleading (it absolutely can). The defense: always ask "out of what, compared to what, and what's not being shown?"

## Card: Quick recap
Statistics can be true yet misleading through cherry-picking, choosing a favorable average, tiny samples, missing context, and clever framing. A "200% increase" or "9 out of 10" can deceive without lying. The defense is to always ask: out of what? compared to what? what's not being shown? Critical questioning protects you from manipulation by numbers.
