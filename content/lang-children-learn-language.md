## Card: What it is
image: langChildrenLearnLanguageHero
**How children learn language** is one of the most remarkable feats in human development. Without formal lessons, young children absorb a complex language — its sounds, words, and grammar — astonishingly fast, just by being immersed in it. Understanding this reveals something profound about the human mind and how we're seemingly wired for language.

## Card: Why it matters
Every child accomplishes something adults find hard (learning a language fluently), yet we rarely marvel at it. Understanding how children learn language reveals insights into the mind, development, and language itself — and even informs how adults might learn languages. It's a window into a uniquely human, almost magical-seeming ability we all once had.

## Card: Key terms
- **Language acquisition**: how we naturally learn a first language.
- **Immersion**: learning through constant exposure.
- **Critical period**: the early window when learning language is easiest.
- **Innate ability**: a seemingly built-in human capacity for language.

## Card: Effortless absorption
image: langChildrenLearnLanguageEffortlessAbsorptionHero
Children learn their first language through **immersion** and **acquisition** — they're not formally taught grammar rules; they simply absorb language from hearing it constantly around them. Astonishingly, they figure out the complex patterns and rules on their own, making sense of the messy stream of speech, and within a few years become fluent. This effortless, rule-discovering process suggests humans have an **innate ability** — a built-in readiness — for learning language.

## Card: The critical period
A striking finding is the **critical period** — an early window in childhood when learning a language (especially a first one) comes easily and naturally. During these early years, the brain is especially primed to absorb language. After this window, learning languages becomes harder and tends to require more conscious effort (which is part of why adults find new languages tougher than children do). This early readiness is part of what makes children's language learning so remarkable.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking children learn language mainly by being formally taught (they mostly absorb it through immersion); assuming it's easy because language is simple (it's complex, and the feat is extraordinary); not knowing about the critical period; and overlooking how this reveals an innate human capacity. Children's effortless, rapid language learning is one of the mind's most astonishing achievements.

## Card: Quick recap
Children learn their first language not through formal lessons but through immersion — absorbing sounds, words, and grammar by hearing it constantly, and figuring out the complex rules on their own, becoming fluent within years. This suggests an innate human readiness for language. The "critical period" is an early window when this comes especially easily, which is partly why adults find new languages harder. It's a remarkable human feat.
