## Card: What it is
image: econInflationHowItAffectsYouHero
Inflation isn't just an abstract number — it directly affects your daily money: what you can afford, the real value of your wages, and whether your savings grow or shrink in real terms. Understanding these personal effects helps you see why inflation matters to you specifically, not just to economists.

## Card: Why it matters
Inflation shapes your real standard of living. If you don't account for it, you can feel mysteriously poorer even as your income stays the same or rises. Understanding its personal effects helps you make smarter decisions about saving, spending, and wages, and explains why "more money" doesn't always mean "better off."

## Card: Key terms
- **Real vs. nominal**: adjusted for inflation vs. the face value.
- **Real wage**: your pay adjusted for what it can actually buy.
- **Real return**: investment growth after subtracting inflation.
- **Cost of living**: the everyday expense of living.

## Card: Real vs. nominal value
image: econInflationHowItAffectsYouRealVsNominalValueHero
A key concept: **nominal** is the face number; **real** is adjusted for inflation. If your wage rises 2% but prices rise 3%, your **real wage** actually *fell* — you can buy less despite earning more "on paper." This is why a raise that doesn't beat inflation isn't really a raise, and why people can feel squeezed even as the numbers go up.

## Card: Savings and the real return
Inflation quietly erodes savings. If your savings earn 1% interest but inflation is 3%, your money's **real return** is *negative* — it's losing buying power, even as the balance grows. This is the inflation trap of cash savings: the number rises, but what it can buy falls. Beating inflation is the real benchmark for whether savings or investments are truly growing.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: judging wages and savings by the nominal number alone, ignoring inflation; feeling richer from a below-inflation raise (you're not, in real terms); assuming cash savings are "safe" (they're safe in number but lose real value to inflation); and not realizing why the cost of living can outpace income. Think in *real* terms.

## Card: Quick recap
Inflation affects you personally: a raise that doesn't beat inflation is a real-terms pay cut, and savings earning less than inflation lose buying power despite a growing balance. The lesson is to think in *real* (inflation-adjusted) terms, not just nominal numbers. Beating inflation is the true benchmark for whether you're getting ahead.
