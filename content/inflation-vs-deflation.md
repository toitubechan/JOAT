## Card: What it is
image: econInflationVsDeflationHero
**Deflation** is the opposite of inflation — a general *fall* in prices over time. It sounds appealing (cheaper things!), but it can actually be dangerous for an economy. Understanding deflation, and why economists often fear it more than mild inflation, completes the picture of how prices move.

## Card: Why it matters
Most people assume falling prices must be good, but the reality is more complex. Understanding why deflation can be harmful — and why a little inflation is preferred — deepens your grasp of how economies work and why central banks aim for steady, mild inflation rather than zero or falling prices.

## Card: Key terms
- **Deflation**: a general fall in prices over time.
- **Inflation**: a general rise in prices over time.
- **Deflationary spiral**: falling prices leading to economic decline.
- **Hyperinflation**: extremely rapid, destructive inflation.

## Card: Why falling prices can be bad
image: econInflationVsDeflationWhyFallingPricesCanBeHero
**Deflation** seems great — your money buys more. But it can trigger a **deflationary spiral**: if people expect prices to keep falling, they delay spending (why buy now if it'll be cheaper later?). Less spending means businesses earn less, cut jobs and wages, so people spend even less — a downward spiral. Falling prices can mean a shrinking, struggling economy.

## Card: The extremes to avoid
Both ends are dangerous. **Deflation** can spiral an economy downward. At the other extreme, **hyperinflation** — prices rising wildly and rapidly — destroys the value of money, wipes out savings, and causes chaos (history has stark examples). This is why economies aim for the "sweet spot": low, steady, predictable inflation, avoiding both falling prices and runaway price rises.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming falling prices are simply good (deflation can signal or cause economic trouble); thinking zero inflation is the ideal target (mild positive inflation is generally preferred); not knowing deflation exists at all; and underestimating how destructive hyperinflation is. The goal is steady, moderate inflation — not zero, and certainly not the extremes.

## Card: Quick recap
Deflation is a general fall in prices — appealing on the surface, but it can trigger a downward spiral as people delay spending and the economy shrinks. The opposite extreme, hyperinflation, destroys money's value. Economies aim for the sweet spot: low, steady, predictable inflation, avoiding both falling prices and runaway rises.
