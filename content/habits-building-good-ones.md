## Card: What it is
image: growthHabitsBuildingGoodOnesHero
Building a good habit is about making the desired behavior easy to start and rewarding to repeat, until it becomes automatic. There are proven, practical techniques — far more reliable than willpower — that dramatically raise your odds of a new habit actually sticking.

## Card: Why it matters
Most failed resolutions fail not from weak character but from poor method — going too big, too fast, with no system. Using the right techniques means new habits stick instead of fizzling out in a week. Knowing *how* to build habits is what turns good intentions into lasting change.

## Card: Key terms
- **Start small**: beginning with a tiny, easy version.
- **Habit stacking**: attaching a new habit to an existing one.
- **Make it easy**: reducing the effort to begin.
- **Tracking**: recording your habit to build a streak.

## Card: Start absurdly small
image: growthHabitsBuildingGoodOnesStartAbsurdlySmallHero
The most powerful technique is to **start small** — even smaller than feels worthwhile. Want to exercise? Begin with one push-up or a five-minute walk. A tiny habit is easy to do daily, and consistency is what wires it in. You can grow it later; the early goal is to *show up* every day, not to do a lot.

## Card: Stack, ease, and track
Three more tools. **Habit stacking**: attach the new habit to an existing one ("after I brush my teeth, I'll meditate for one minute"), borrowing an established cue. **Make it easy**: reduce friction (lay out gym clothes the night before). And **track** it — ticking off a streak is satisfying and motivating, turning progress visible.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: starting too big and burning out; relying on motivation instead of a system; having no clear cue for when the habit happens; trying to build several habits at once; and quitting after missing a day. The fix for most of these is the same: start smaller, attach to a cue, and don't break the chain over one slip.

## Card: Quick recap
Build good habits by starting absurdly small (consistency beats intensity early on), stacking them onto existing habits for a ready-made cue, reducing friction to make them easy, and tracking your streak. Don't rely on willpower or go too big. Show up daily, and let the habit grow once it's wired in.
