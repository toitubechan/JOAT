## Card: What it is
image: psychMemoryWhyWeForgetHero
**Forgetting** is the failure to recall something — and it happens for understandable reasons, not because your brain is broken. Memories fade without use, get crowded out, or were never stored well in the first place. Understanding why we forget is reassuring and helps you remember better.

## Card: Why it matters
Everyone forgets, and worrying about it causes needless stress. Understanding the normal reasons for forgetting reassures you that occasional lapses are natural, helps you distinguish normal forgetting from concerns, and reveals how to forget *less* by addressing the real causes. Forgetting isn't a flaw — it's part of how a healthy memory works.

## Card: Key terms
- **Forgetting curve**: how memories fade over time without review.
- **Interference**: memories crowding or confusing each other.
- **Retrieval failure**: it's stored but you can't access it right now.
- **Encoding failure**: it was never stored well to begin with.

## Card: Memories fade and interfere
image: psychMemoryWhyWeForgetMemoriesFadeAndInterfereHero
A big reason for forgetting is simply time: the **forgetting curve** shows memories fade steadily unless we revisit them. Another is **interference** — similar memories crowd or confuse each other (mixing up two people's names, for instance). These are normal features of memory, not malfunctions. Without reinforcement, even important memories naturally weaken over time.

## Card: Failures of retrieval and encoding
Sometimes forgetting is a **retrieval failure** — the memory *is* stored, but you can't access it right now (the frustrating "tip of the tongue" feeling, which often resolves later). Other times it's an **encoding failure** — you never properly formed the memory because you weren't paying attention (like forgetting a name you "heard" while distracted). Knowing which helps: pay attention to encode, use cues to retrieve.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking all forgetting means a failing memory (much is normal fading or distraction); blaming "bad memory" when you simply weren't paying attention (encoding failure); panicking over normal tip-of-the-tongue moments; and not reviewing things you want to keep. Most everyday forgetting is normal and addressable.

## Card: Quick recap
We forget for understandable reasons: memories fade over time (the forgetting curve), similar memories interfere, retrieval can temporarily fail (tip of the tongue), and poor attention means weak encoding. These are normal, not signs of a broken brain. To forget less, pay attention when encoding and review what you want to keep.
