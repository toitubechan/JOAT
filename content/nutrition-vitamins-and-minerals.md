## Card: What it is
image: vitaminsMineralsHero
**Vitamins** and **minerals** are nutrients your body needs in tiny amounts, yet they're essential for nearly everything it does — from energy and immunity to strong bones and healthy blood. They're called **micronutrients** because the quantities are small, but their importance is anything but.

## Card: Why it matters
A diet full of calories can still leave you short on micronutrients, causing tiredness, poor immunity, or worse over time. Understanding them — and that variety is the way to get them — helps you eat for genuine health, not just fullness. This is general knowledge; suspected deficiencies warrant a doctor's advice.

## Card: Key terms
- **Vitamin**: an organic micronutrient, like vitamin C or D.
- **Mineral**: an inorganic micronutrient, like iron or calcium.
- **Deficiency**: a harmful lack of a nutrient.
- **Varied diet**: eating a wide range of foods.

## Card: What they do
Different micronutrients run different jobs: vitamin C supports immunity and healing, vitamin D and calcium build bones, iron carries oxygen in your blood, and B vitamins help turn food into energy. There are many more. No single food contains them all — which is exactly why variety matters so much.

## Card: Eat the rainbow
image: eatRainbowHero
The simplest way to cover your micronutrients is to eat a wide range of foods, especially colorful fruits and vegetables. Different colors often signal different nutrients, so "eating the rainbow" is a handy rule of thumb. A **varied diet** of whole foods naturally supplies most of what your body needs.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common mistake is assuming supplements can replace a poor diet — whole foods provide nutrients in forms and combinations pills often can't match. Another is eating plenty of calories but little variety, risking **deficiency** despite being full. And some over-supplement, since a few vitamins can be harmful in large doses.

## Card: Quick recap
Vitamins and minerals are micronutrients — tiny in amount, huge in importance, running countless body processes. No one food has them all, so a varied, colorful diet of whole foods is the best way to get them. Supplements aren't a substitute for good eating; for suspected deficiencies, see a doctor.
