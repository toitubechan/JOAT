## Card: What it is
image: dataStatisticsAveragesHero
An **average** is a single number meant to represent the "typical" value in a set of data. But there are actually three common kinds — the **mean**, **median**, and **mode** — and they can give very different answers. Knowing the difference is one of the most useful statistical skills.

## Card: Why it matters
"Average" is used constantly, but which average is often hidden — and it matters hugely. Reporting different averages can paint very different pictures of the same data, sometimes misleadingly (especially with income or prices). Understanding the three types helps you interpret "average" claims accurately and spot when one is chosen to mislead.

## Card: Key terms
- **Mean**: the sum of values divided by how many there are.
- **Median**: the middle value when data is ordered.
- **Mode**: the most common value.
- **Outlier**: an extreme value that can distort the mean.

## Card: The three averages
image: dataStatisticsAveragesTheThreeAveragesHero
- **Mean**: add all values, divide by the count — the "average" most people think of.
- **Median**: line the values up in order; the **middle** one is the median.
- **Mode**: the value that appears **most often**.
For symmetric data they're similar, but for skewed data they can differ a lot — which is exactly when the choice matters.

## Card: Why the mean can mislead
The **mean** is easily distorted by **outliers** — a few extreme values. Classic example: if a room of people has average (mean) income skewed by one billionaire walking in, the mean income looks huge, even though almost everyone is ordinary. Here the **median** (middle person) better represents "typical." This is why "average income" figures can mislead, and why knowing *which* average is being used matters.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming "average" always means the mean (it might be median or mode); not realizing outliers can distort the mean badly; thinking the three averages always agree (they often don't for skewed data); and accepting an "average" figure without asking which kind it is. Always ask *which* average, especially for things like income or prices.

## Card: Quick recap
There are three averages: the mean (sum ÷ count), the median (middle value), and the mode (most common value). They can differ greatly for skewed data, and the mean is easily distorted by outliers — so for things like income, the median often better represents "typical." Always ask *which* average is being used, since the choice can mislead.
