## Card: What it is
image: musicHowInstrumentsSoundHero
**Instruments make sound** by creating vibrations that travel through the air to our ears. Different instruments produce these vibrations in different ways — strings, air columns, surfaces, or electronics — but all sound comes from vibration. Understanding how instruments make sound reveals the physics behind music and why instruments sound so different from each other.

## Card: Why it matters
Music is everywhere, but how instruments actually produce their sounds is rarely understood. Knowing the basics connects music to physics, explains why instruments sound different, and deepens appreciation of how musical sound is made. It's a satisfying link between the art of music and the science of sound, accessible to anyone curious.

## Card: Key terms
- **Vibration**: rapid back-and-forth motion that creates sound.
- **Pitch**: how high or low a sound is (from vibration speed).
- **Instrument families**: groups based on how they make sound.
- **Resonance**: amplifying and shaping vibrations.

## Card: Sound is vibration
image: musicHowInstrumentsSoundSoundIsVibrationHero
All sound — including from instruments — comes from **vibration**: something vibrating rapidly back and forth, which pushes the air, creating sound waves that reach your ears. The *speed* of vibration determines **pitch**: faster vibrations make higher notes, slower ones make lower notes. So an instrument makes a note by causing something to vibrate at a particular speed. This single principle — vibration creating sound waves — underlies every instrument, however different they look.

## Card: Different ways to vibrate
Instruments differ in *what* vibrates and *how*, giving us **instrument families**. *Strings* (guitar, violin, piano) make sound from vibrating strings. *Wind* instruments (flute, trumpet) vibrate a column of air. *Percussion* (drums) vibrate a surface when struck. *Electronic* instruments generate or manipulate sound electronically. Most instruments also use **resonance** (a body or chamber that amplifies and shapes the vibration — like a guitar's hollow body). This variety in how vibration is produced and shaped is why instruments have such distinct sounds.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: not realizing all sound comes from vibration; thinking pitch is unrelated to vibration (it's set by vibration speed — faster is higher); not knowing instruments are grouped by *how* they vibrate (strings, wind, percussion, electronic); and overlooking resonance's role in shaping an instrument's sound. Every instrument makes sound the same fundamental way — vibration — but differs in what vibrates and how it's shaped.

## Card: Quick recap
Instruments make sound through vibration — something vibrating rapidly pushes air into sound waves that reach our ears, with faster vibration producing higher pitch. Instruments differ in *what* vibrates: strings, columns of air (wind), surfaces (percussion), or electronics, and most use resonance (a body amplifying and shaping the sound). This single principle — vibration creating sound — underlies every instrument, while the variety explains their distinct sounds.
