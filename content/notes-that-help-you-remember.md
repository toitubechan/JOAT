## Card: What it is
image: growthNotesThatHelpYouRememberHero
The best notes don't just store information — they help you *remember and understand* it. This card set covers how to take notes that actively build knowledge, drawing on how learning really works. It's the difference between notes that gather dust and notes that make you smarter.

## Card: Why it matters
Many people take notes that look thorough but do nothing for their memory — verbatim, never revisited, passively copied. Taking notes that actually aid retention means your effort pays off in lasting knowledge. This connects note-taking to the science of learning, making it a genuine learning tool.

## Card: Key terms
- **Own words**: rephrasing instead of copying verbatim.
- **Summarizing**: condensing to the key points.
- **Connecting**: linking new notes to what you already know.
- **Active recall**: testing yourself from your notes.

## Card: Process, don't transcribe
image: growthNotesThatHelpYouRememberProcessDonTTranscribeHero
The single biggest tip: put things in your **own words** and **summarize** rather than copying verbatim. Rephrasing forces you to understand the material, which is what builds memory. Notes that are a word-for-word transcript prove you heard something; notes in your own words prove (and create) understanding. Process the information as you capture it.

## Card: Connect and recall
Two more boosters. **Connecting** new notes to things you already know — jotting how an idea links to prior knowledge — anchors it more firmly. And use your notes for **active recall**: instead of just rereading them, cover them and try to recall the content, or turn key points into questions. This turns notes into a self-testing tool, the most powerful way to remember.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: transcribing word-for-word instead of rephrasing; making notes a passive record you only reread; not connecting new information to what you know; and never using notes for self-testing. The fix is to treat note-taking and reviewing as active processing — understand, rephrase, connect, and recall.

## Card: Quick recap
Notes that help you remember are processed, not transcribed: rephrase in your own words, summarize, and connect ideas to what you already know. Then use them for active recall — test yourself rather than just rereading. This turns note-taking from passive storage into a powerful learning and memory tool.
