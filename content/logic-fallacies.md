## Card: What it is
image: logicFallaciesHero
**Logical fallacies** are common errors in reasoning — flawed arguments that *seem* convincing but don't actually hold up logically. They appear constantly in debates, ads, news, and arguments. Learning to spot them helps you think more clearly and avoid being persuaded by bad reasoning, whether others' or your own.

## Card: Why it matters
Fallacies are everywhere, used (often unintentionally) to win arguments and persuade unfairly. Recognizing them protects you from being misled, sharpens your own thinking, and helps you argue more honestly. It's one of the most practically useful critical-thinking skills — a defense against manipulation and muddled reasoning alike.

## Card: Key terms
- **Logical fallacy**: a flawed argument that seems convincing.
- **Ad hominem**: attacking the person instead of their argument.
- **Straw man**: misrepresenting an argument to attack it easily.
- **False dilemma**: presenting only two options when more exist.

## Card: Some common fallacies
image: logicFallaciesSomeCommonFallaciesHero
A few of the most common: **ad hominem** (attacking the *person* rather than their argument — "you're wrong because you're [insult]"); the **straw man** (misrepresenting someone's argument into a weaker version, then attacking that); the **false dilemma** (presenting only two options when more exist — "you're either with us or against us"); and the *slippery slope* (claiming one step inevitably leads to extreme consequences). Each *feels* persuasive but is logically flawed.

## Card: Why fallacies fool us
Fallacies work because they often *sound* reasonable, exploit emotions, or fit our existing beliefs. They can be persuasive even when logically empty. The defense is to focus on the actual *logic*: does the conclusion really follow from the reasons given? Spotting a fallacy means noticing when an argument's structure is broken, even if it feels convincing. Naming fallacies as they appear is a powerful clarity tool.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: being persuaded by fallacies because they sound convincing; using them yourself unintentionally; thinking only "other people" fall for them; and assuming a flawed argument's conclusion must be false (a bad argument doesn't prove the opposite — it just doesn't support its claim). The goal is to evaluate the *reasoning*, not just whether an argument feels persuasive.

## Card: Quick recap
Logical fallacies are flawed arguments that seem convincing but don't hold up — like ad hominem (attacking the person), straw man (misrepresenting an argument), and false dilemma (only two options offered). They fool us by sounding reasonable or fitting our beliefs. The defense: focus on whether the conclusion truly follows from the reasons. Spotting fallacies sharpens thinking and guards against being misled.
