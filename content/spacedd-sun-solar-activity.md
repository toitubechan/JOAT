## Card: What it is
image: spaceddSunHero
The **Sun** is the star at the center of our solar system — an immense ball of hot gas powered by nuclear fusion. It provides nearly all the energy for life on Earth. It's also dynamic, with **solar activity** like sunspots and solar storms that can even affect technology on Earth. The Sun is far more than a steady light in the sky.

## Card: Why it matters
The Sun makes life possible, drives weather and climate, and its activity can affect satellites, power grids, and communications. Understanding it deepens appreciation of our nearest star and explains phenomena from auroras to the risks of solar storms. It's the single most important object for life on Earth.

## Card: Key terms
- **Nuclear fusion**: merging atoms to release energy, powering the Sun.
- **Sunspots**: cooler, darker patches on the Sun's surface.
- **Solar flare / storm**: bursts of energy and particles from the Sun.
- **Aurora**: lights in the sky caused by solar particles.

## Card: How the Sun shines
The Sun shines through **nuclear fusion** in its core: under crushing pressure and heat, hydrogen atoms fuse together, releasing staggering amounts of energy as light and heat. This energy travels to Earth, powering nearly all life (via plants) and driving our weather and climate. The Sun is so massive it contains the vast majority of the solar system's matter, and its fusion will continue for billions of years.

## Card: A dynamic, active star
image: solarActivityHero
The Sun isn't static. **Sunspots** are cooler, darker patches that come and go in cycles. **Solar flares** and storms are bursts of energy and charged particles flung into space. When these particles reach Earth, they can create beautiful **auroras** (northern and southern lights) — but strong solar storms can also disrupt satellites, GPS, communications, and even power grids. The Sun's activity directly touches our technology.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the Sun "burns" like a fire (it's nuclear fusion, not combustion); imagining it's a static, unchanging light (it's dynamic, with cycles and storms); not realizing solar activity can affect Earth's technology; and underrating its scale (it dwarfs everything else in the solar system). The Sun is an active, powerful star, not just a lamp in the sky.

## Card: Quick recap
The Sun is our local star — a vast ball of hot gas powered by nuclear fusion, providing nearly all of Earth's energy. It's dynamic, with sunspot cycles and solar storms that fling charged particles into space, creating auroras but sometimes disrupting satellites and power grids. The Sun is far more than a steady light: it's an active, immensely powerful star.
