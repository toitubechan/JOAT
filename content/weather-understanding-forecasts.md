## Card: What it is
image: weatherForecastHero
A **weather forecast** is a prediction of future weather, made by measuring the current atmosphere and using computer models to project it forward. Understanding how forecasts work — and what terms like "70% chance of rain" actually mean — helps you use them wisely rather than being confused or frustrated by them.

## Card: Why it matters
We rely on forecasts daily, yet many misunderstand them and then complain they're "always wrong." Knowing how they're made and read helps you interpret them correctly, plan better, and have realistic expectations. It also reveals why forecasts are remarkably good in the short term but less certain further out.

## Card: Key terms
- **Forecast**: a prediction of future weather.
- **Weather model**: a computer simulation of the atmosphere.
- **Probability of precipitation**: the stated chance of rain.
- **Forecast horizon**: how far ahead a forecast reaches.

## Card: How forecasts are made
image: forecastModelHero
Forecasters gather huge amounts of data — temperature, pressure, wind, humidity — from satellites, stations, and balloons. They feed it into **weather models**: powerful computer simulations of the atmosphere's physics that project conditions forward. Because the atmosphere is so complex and chaotic, these are probabilities, not certainties — which is why forecasts are expressed in chances and degrade further out.

## Card: Reading the chance of rain
"**70% chance of rain**" confuses many. It broadly means rain is likely (in 7 of 10 similar situations, it would rain) — not that it'll rain 70% of the time or over 70% of the area. A low percentage means rain is unlikely but possible. Understanding it as a *probability* helps you decide whether to carry an umbrella, rather than expecting a guarantee.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: expecting forecasts to be perfect (the atmosphere is chaotic, so they're probabilities); misreading "chance of rain" as duration or area; trusting long-range forecasts as much as short-term ones (accuracy drops with distance); and calling forecasts "always wrong" when they're actually quite accurate over short horizons. Read them as informed probabilities.

## Card: Quick recap
Forecasts are made by feeding atmospheric data into computer models that project conditions forward — yielding probabilities, not certainties, because the atmosphere is chaotic. "70% chance of rain" means rain is likely, not a duration or area. Forecasts are accurate short-term and less so further out. Read them as informed probabilities.
