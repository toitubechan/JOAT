## Card: What it is
image: musicGenresHero
**Music genres** are categories that group music by shared characteristics — style, instruments, rhythm, origin, and feel. Genres like rock, jazz, classical, hip-hop, and electronic help us describe and find music, but their boundaries are fuzzier than they seem. Understanding how genres work clarifies a useful but imperfect way of organizing the vast world of music.

## Card: Why it matters
Genres shape how we discover, discuss, and understand music. Knowing what defines major genres enriches your listening and helps you explore music more deliberately. Understanding that genres are useful labels — not rigid boxes — also helps you appreciate how music blends and evolves, and why so much great music defies neat categorization.

## Card: Key terms
- **Genre**: a category of music with shared characteristics.
- **Characteristics**: the features (rhythm, instruments, style) that define a genre.
- **Fusion**: blending genres together.
- **Evolution**: how genres change and spawn new ones.

## Card: What defines a genre
image: musicGenresWhatDefinesAGenreHero
A **genre** groups music by shared **characteristics** — things like typical instruments, rhythms, structures, origins, and overall feel. For example, genres differ in their characteristic rhythms, instrumentation, and traditions. These shared features let us recognize and label music ("that sounds like jazz"). Genres also carry cultural and historical context — they often arose from particular times, places, and communities, which is part of what gives each its identity.

## Card: Fuzzy, evolving boundaries
A key insight: genre boundaries are *fuzzy and evolving*, not rigid. Music constantly blends genres (**fusion**) — combining elements of different styles — and genres themselves **evolve**, splitting into subgenres and spawning entirely new ones over time. Much great music doesn't fit neatly into one box, and labels can be debated. So genres are *useful* for describing and finding music, but they're approximate, overlapping, and ever-changing — not strict, permanent categories. Treat them as helpful guides, not rigid rules.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: treating genres as rigid, fixed boxes (they're fuzzy, overlapping, and evolving); thinking every piece fits neatly into one genre (much music blends or defies categories); seeing genre labels as objective facts rather than approximate, sometimes debated descriptions; and ignoring the cultural history behind genres. Genres are useful but imperfect labels for the vast, blending, evolving world of music.

## Card: Quick recap
Music genres group music by shared characteristics — instruments, rhythms, structures, origins, and feel — letting us describe and find music, often carrying cultural and historical context. But genre boundaries are fuzzy and evolving: music constantly blends styles (fusion), and genres split and spawn new ones over time. Treat genres as useful, approximate guides rather than rigid boxes — much great music defies neat categorization.
