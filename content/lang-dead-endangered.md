## Card: What it is
image: langDeadEndangeredHero
A **dead language** is one no longer spoken as anyone's native language, while an **endangered language** is one at risk of dying out as its speakers dwindle. Languages are vanishing at an alarming rate worldwide. Understanding why this happens, and what's lost, reveals an important and often-overlooked aspect of human culture and diversity.

## Card: Why it matters
Languages are disappearing rapidly — many of the world's languages are endangered. When a language dies, a unique way of seeing the world, plus culture, knowledge, and history embedded in it, is often lost forever. Understanding this raises awareness of a quiet crisis in human heritage and the efforts to preserve linguistic diversity. It's a significant cultural and human issue.

## Card: Key terms
- **Dead language**: one no longer spoken natively by anyone.
- **Endangered language**: one at risk of dying out.
- **Language extinction**: the disappearance of a language.
- **Revitalization**: efforts to preserve or revive a language.

## Card: How and why languages die
image: langDeadEndangeredHowAndWhyLanguagesDieHero
Languages become **endangered** and eventually die when speakers stop passing them to the next generation — often because a dominant language (used in schools, media, jobs, and wider society) takes over, and younger people shift to it. As the last fluent speakers age and pass away without new ones, the language faces **extinction**. This is happening rapidly worldwide; a large share of the world's languages are endangered, many with only a few speakers left.

## Card: What's lost, and what's being done
When a language dies, much is lost: a unique way of expressing and categorizing the world, oral traditions, cultural identity, and knowledge (some languages encode detailed knowledge of local plants, environment, or history). It's a loss to human heritage, like a species going extinct. In response, communities and linguists work on **revitalization** — documenting endangered languages, teaching them to new generations, and reviving them. Some efforts have successfully brought languages back from the brink.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking language loss doesn't matter ("everyone could just speak one language") — but each language carries unique culture and knowledge; assuming languages die naturally with no cause (it's usually pressure from dominant languages); not realizing how rapid and widespread the crisis is; and thinking nothing can be done (revitalization efforts exist and sometimes succeed). Losing a language is losing irreplaceable human heritage.

## Card: Quick recap
A dead language is no longer spoken natively; an endangered one is at risk as speakers dwindle, usually when younger generations shift to a dominant language. Languages are vanishing rapidly worldwide. Each loss means losing a unique worldview, culture, and knowledge — irreplaceable human heritage, like a species going extinct. Revitalization efforts work to document and revive endangered languages, sometimes successfully.
