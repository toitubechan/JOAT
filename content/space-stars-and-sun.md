## Card: What it is
image: starsSunHero
**Stars** are enormous balls of hot gas that produce their own light and heat through nuclear reactions. Our **Sun** is a star — an ordinary one, just very close. Every point of light in the night sky (except planets) is a distant sun, many far bigger than ours.

## Card: Why it matters
Stars are the most numerous and fundamental objects in the universe, and our Sun makes life on Earth possible. Understanding them reframes the night sky: those tiny points are colossal, distant suns. It also reveals our cosmic context — we orbit one fairly average star among countless billions.

## Card: Key terms
- **Star**: a giant ball of gas producing light by nuclear fusion.
- **Nuclear fusion**: merging atoms to release huge energy.
- **The Sun**: our local star.
- **Light-year**: the distance light travels in a year.

## Card: How stars shine
image: starFusionHero
Stars shine through **nuclear fusion** — in their cores, immense pressure and heat fuse hydrogen atoms together, releasing staggering amounts of energy as light and heat. This is fundamentally different from a planet, which produces no light of its own (we only see planets by reflected sunlight). A star is a self-powered furnace; the Sun is our nearest one.

## Card: Our Sun and distant stars
The **Sun** is a fairly average star that happens to be close, which is why it dominates our sky. The other stars are so far away we measure their distance in **light-years** (the distance light travels in a year — about 9 trillion km). Starlight you see left long ago; you're literally looking into the past. Some stars are vastly larger and brighter than the Sun.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the Sun is different from "real" stars (it *is* a star); confusing stars with planets (stars make light; planets reflect it); imagining stars are small (they're enormous; they only look tiny due to distance); and not grasping that starlight is old — you see stars as they were when their light departed.

## Card: Quick recap
Stars are giant balls of gas that shine via nuclear fusion, fusing atoms to release energy as light and heat. Our Sun is an ordinary, nearby star. Other stars are so distant we measure them in light-years, and their light is old — you see them as they were. Every star in the sky is a distant sun.
