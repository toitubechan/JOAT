## Card: What it is
image: cuttingClutterHero
**Cutting clutter** is removing the unnecessary words, phrases, and padding that bog writing down. Most first drafts are far longer than they need to be. Ruthless trimming makes writing punchier, clearer, and quicker to read. In writing, less is almost always more.

## Card: Why it matters
Wordy, padded writing is tiring and buries the message under filler. Concise writing respects the reader's time and makes your points land harder. Cutting clutter is one of the easiest, highest-impact edits you can make — and most writing improves dramatically just by removing what isn't needed.

## Card: Key terms
- **Conciseness**: saying it in as few words as needed.
- **Filler**: words that add length but no meaning.
- **Redundancy**: saying the same thing twice.
- **Wordiness**: using more words than necessary.

## Card: Cut the filler
image: cutFillerHero
Hunt for **filler** — words and phrases that add nothing. "In order to" → "to." "Due to the fact that" → "because." "Very," "really," "actually," "basically" often add nothing. **Redundancy** too: "free gift," "advance planning," "end result." Each cut tightens your writing. A good rule: if removing a word doesn't change the meaning, remove it.

## Card: Shorter is clearer
Favor **conciseness**: shorter words over long ones, shorter sentences over sprawling ones, and fewer words overall. Long, complex sentences make readers work harder and lose the thread. Breaking them up and trimming **wordiness** instantly improves readability. The famous advice "omit needless words" is the single most useful editing habit there is.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: padding to sound substantial or hit a length; long, tangled sentences that should be split; redundant phrases; hedging filler ("I just think that maybe..."); and not editing the first draft down. The fix is simple but disciplined: write your draft, then cut ruthlessly — most writing improves the moment you trim it.

## Card: Quick recap
Cutting clutter — removing filler, redundancy, and wordiness — is one of the highest-impact edits in writing. Favor shorter words and sentences; if removing a word doesn't change the meaning, cut it. Concise writing respects the reader and makes points land. Write your draft, then trim ruthlessly: less is almost always more.
