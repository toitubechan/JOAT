## Card: What it is
image: howworksSkyscrapersHero
**Skyscrapers** are very tall buildings that stay standing through clever engineering — a strong internal frame, deep foundations, and designs that resist wind and even earthquakes. Building tall is far harder than building wide; understanding how skyscrapers stand reveals the ingenuity behind the cities we live in.

## Card: Why it matters
Skyscrapers define modern cities, and how they stand safely is a feat of engineering most people never consider. Understanding the basics — the frame, foundations, and how they handle wind — reveals the cleverness behind these structures and the challenges of building tall. It deepens appreciation of the urban world around us.

## Card: Key terms
- **Frame**: the strong internal skeleton of a building.
- **Foundations**: the deep base anchoring it to the ground.
- **Load-bearing**: carrying the building's weight.
- **Wind load**: the force of wind on a tall building.

## Card: The frame and foundations
image: howworksSkyscrapersTheFrameAndFoundationsHero
A skyscraper's secret is its internal **frame** — a strong skeleton (usually steel and reinforced concrete) that carries the building's weight, rather than the walls themselves. The walls just hang on this frame as a "skin." Beneath, deep **foundations** anchor the building firmly into solid ground, spreading its enormous weight and stopping it sinking or toppling. The frame and foundations together are what let buildings rise so high.

## Card: Fighting wind and movement
A surprising challenge: the higher you build, the more the **wind load** matters — strong winds push hard on tall buildings, and the top can sway. Engineers design skyscrapers to be stiff enough to resist this, yet able to sway slightly and safely rather than break (a little flexibility is good). In earthquake zones, special designs help them absorb shaking. Managing these forces — gravity, wind, and sometimes earthquakes — is the core challenge of building tall.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the walls hold a skyscraper up (it's the internal frame; walls are just a skin); imagining foundations are shallow (they're deep, anchoring the huge weight); assuming tall buildings are perfectly rigid (they sway slightly and safely by design); and underrating wind as a challenge (it's a major force at height). Skyscrapers stand through frames, deep foundations, and clever force management.

## Card: Quick recap
Skyscrapers stand thanks to a strong internal frame (steel and concrete) that carries the weight, with the walls just a hanging "skin," anchored by deep foundations that spread the enormous load into solid ground. They're engineered to resist wind (a major force at height) and earthquakes — staying stiff yet able to sway slightly and safely. Building tall is all about managing these forces.
