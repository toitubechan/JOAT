## Card: What it is
image: financeInvestingDiversificationHero
**Diversification** means spreading your money across many different investments instead of betting it all on one. The idea is simple: if one investment falls, others may hold steady or rise, cushioning the blow. It's the investing version of the old saying, "don't put all your eggs in one basket."

## Card: Why it matters
Any single company, industry, or country can run into trouble — sometimes suddenly. If all your money is there, you're fully exposed. Diversification doesn't promise profits, but it dramatically reduces the risk that one bad event wipes out a big chunk of your savings. It smooths the ride.

## Card: Key terms
- **Diversification**: spreading money across many investments.
- **Concentration**: the opposite — most money in one place.
- **Asset class**: a category like stocks, bonds, or property.
- **Correlation**: how much investments move together.

## Card: How spreading works
image: financeInvestingDiversificationHowSpreadingWorksHero
Diversify on several levels: across many **companies** (not just one), across **industries** (not only tech), across **asset classes** (stocks and bonds), and even across countries. The goal is holdings that don't all rise and fall together — low **correlation** — so a slump in one area is offset by steadiness elsewhere.

## Card: The easy way to diversify
You don't have to hand-pick dozens of investments. A single broad **index fund** can hold hundreds or thousands of companies at once, instantly spreading your money widely at low cost. This is why such funds are popular with beginners: built-in diversification without needing to research individual stocks.

## Card: Common mistakes
A big mistake is **concentration** — putting most of your money in one stock, often your employer's, doubling your risk if it struggles. Another is "fake" diversification: owning five funds that all hold the same companies. And some over-diversify into things they don't understand, chasing spread for its own sake.

## Card: Quick recap
Diversification spreads money across many investments, industries, and asset classes so one failure doesn't sink you. It lowers risk without guaranteeing gains. Broad index funds make it easy. Avoid concentrating in a single stock, and make sure your holdings aren't secretly all the same thing.
