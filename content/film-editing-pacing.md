## Card: What it is
image: filmEditingPacingHero
**Editing** is assembling and arranging filmed shots into the final film — deciding what to use, in what order, and for how long. **Pacing** is the rhythm and speed this creates. Editing is often called the invisible art: it profoundly shapes the film, yet viewers rarely notice it. Understanding it reveals that a film is truly *made* in the edit, not just in the filming.

## Card: Why it matters
Editing shapes the entire experience of a film — its story, rhythm, tension, and emotion — yet it's invisible to most viewers. A film can be transformed (made or broken) in the editing. Understanding editing and pacing reveals how films control our attention and feelings, and it's a crucial skill for anyone making video. It's where much of a film's power is created.

## Card: Key terms
- **Editing**: assembling and arranging shots into the final film.
- **Cut**: the transition from one shot to the next.
- **Pacing**: the rhythm and speed created by editing.
- **The invisible art**: editing's powerful but unnoticed influence.

## Card: The film is made in the edit
image: filmEditingPacingTheFilmIsMadeInHero
**Editing** is where the filmed footage becomes the actual film. The editor chooses which shots to use, arranges their order, and decides how long each lasts, joining them with **cuts**. These choices shape the story, clarity, emotion, and meaning — the same footage can be assembled into very different films. This is why editing is called **the invisible art**: it powerfully shapes everything you experience, while a well-edited film makes the cuts feel seamless and unnoticed.

## Card: Pacing controls feeling
**Pacing** — the rhythm and speed created by editing — strongly controls how a film feels. Fast cutting (many short shots) creates energy, tension, or excitement (think action scenes); slow, lingering shots create calm, tension of a different kind, or reflection. By controlling the rhythm of cuts, editors guide the audience's emotional experience and attention moment to moment. Good pacing keeps you engaged; poor pacing makes a film feel rushed or dragging. It's a core part of editing's power.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a film is mostly made during filming (it's truly shaped in the edit); not noticing editing as a viewer (it's the invisible art); underrating pacing's effect on feeling; and assuming editing is just cutting out bad bits (it actively constructs the story, rhythm, and emotion). Editing and pacing are where much of a film's power is created — shaping story, emotion, and attention.

## Card: Quick recap
Editing is assembling filmed shots into the final film — choosing what to use, the order, and how long each lasts, joined by cuts. It's the "invisible art": it powerfully shapes story, emotion, and meaning while viewers rarely notice it. Pacing — the rhythm of the cutting — controls feeling (fast cuts create energy; slow shots create calm or reflection). A film is truly made in the edit, where much of its power is created.
