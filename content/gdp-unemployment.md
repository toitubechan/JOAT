## Card: What it is
image: econGdpUnemploymentHero
**Unemployment** measures the share of people who want a job and are looking but can't find one. The unemployment rate is a key economic indicator, closely watched alongside GDP. Understanding what it counts (and doesn't) helps you interpret a number that appears constantly in economic news.

## Card: Why it matters
Unemployment affects millions of lives and signals the health of an economy. It's one of the most reported economic figures and a major focus of government policy. Understanding it helps you read the news accurately and grasp why jobs are so central to economic and political debate.

## Card: Key terms
- **Unemployment rate**: the share of the workforce without a job but seeking one.
- **Workforce**: people working or actively looking for work.
- **Full employment**: when nearly everyone who wants work has it.
- **Underemployment**: working less or below one's skill level.

## Card: What the rate counts
image: econGdpUnemploymentWhatTheRateCountsHero
The **unemployment rate** is the percentage of the **workforce** (people working or actively job-seeking) who are without a job but looking. Importantly, it doesn't count people who aren't seeking work (students, retirees, those who've given up looking). So the rate can understate the full picture — someone who stopped looking isn't counted as "unemployed," even though they lack work.

## Card: Why some unemployment is normal
A zero unemployment rate isn't the goal or even realistic — some unemployment is normal, as people change jobs, enter the workforce, or industries shift. **Full employment** means roughly everyone who wants work can find it, not literally zero. Watch also for **underemployment** — people working part-time who want full-time, or in jobs below their skills — which the headline rate misses.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the unemployment rate counts everyone without a job (it only counts those actively seeking); expecting zero unemployment as ideal (some is normal and healthy); missing underemployment, which the headline rate hides; and not realizing people who give up looking drop out of the count, making the rate sometimes rosier than reality.

## Card: Quick recap
Unemployment measures the share of the workforce who want a job and are seeking but can't find one — a key indicator alongside GDP. It excludes those not looking, so it can understate the picture, and misses underemployment. Some unemployment is normal; "full employment" doesn't mean literally zero. Read the number with these nuances in mind.
