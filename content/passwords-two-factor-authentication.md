## Card: What it is
image: twoFactorHero
**Two-factor authentication (2FA)** adds a second step to logging in, on top of your password — usually a code from your phone or an app. So even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without that second factor. It's a powerful extra lock on your accounts.

## Card: Why it matters
Passwords can be stolen, guessed, or leaked. 2FA means a stolen password alone isn't enough, blocking the vast majority of account-takeover attempts. Security experts consider it one of the most effective protections available, and it's free and quick to set up. For important accounts, it's close to essential.

## Card: Key terms
- **Two-factor authentication (2FA)**: a second login step beyond the password.
- **Authenticator app**: an app that generates login codes.
- **SMS code**: a code texted to your phone.
- **Backup codes**: spare codes in case you lose access.

## Card: The two factors
image: knowHaveFactors
The idea is combining something you *know* (your password) with something you *have* (your phone). After entering your password, you confirm with a code from a text or an **authenticator app**, or by approving a prompt. A thief with only your password is stopped at this second gate.

## Card: Which method to use
Common 2FA methods, roughly weakest to strongest: an **SMS code** (good, but phone numbers can be hijacked), an **authenticator app** (stronger, generates codes on your device), and a physical security key (strongest). Any 2FA is far better than none. Also save your **backup codes** somewhere safe in case you lose your phone.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: not enabling 2FA at all, especially on email and banking; never saving **backup codes**, then getting locked out after losing a phone; and — critically — giving a 2FA code to anyone. No legitimate service or support agent will ask you to read out your code; that request is always a scam.

## Card: Quick recap
Two-factor authentication adds a second login step — a code or prompt — so a stolen password alone can't unlock your account. It blocks most takeovers and is free to enable, especially vital for email and banking. Save backup codes, prefer an authenticator app over SMS, and never share a code with anyone.
