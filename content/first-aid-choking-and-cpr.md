## Card: What it is
image: chokingCprHero
**Choking** (a blocked airway) and **cardiac arrest** (the heart stopping) are life-threatening emergencies where quick action matters enormously. This card set builds awareness of the basic responses. Crucially, these are skills best learned hands-on in a certified course — reading about them is a start, not a substitute.

## Card: Why it matters
In these emergencies, minutes count, and a bystander acting can be the difference between life and death. Even basic awareness reduces panic and helps you act or assist. But because correct technique matters, the strongest takeaway is: call emergency services immediately and take a proper first aid course.

## Card: Key terms
- **Choking**: a blocked airway preventing breathing.
- **Back blows**: firm hits between the shoulder blades to clear a blockage.
- **CPR**: cardiopulmonary resuscitation, chest compressions to keep blood flowing.
- **AED**: a defibrillator that can restart a heart, designed for public use.

## Card: Choking basics
If someone is choking but can cough, encourage them to keep coughing. If they can't breathe, cough, or speak, it's serious: call for help and, if trained, give firm **back blows** between the shoulder blades, and abdominal thrusts as taught. For a baby, the technique differs. This is exactly why hands-on training matters.

## Card: CPR and AEDs
image: cprAedHelp
If someone is unresponsive and not breathing normally, call emergency services immediately and start **CPR** if you can — chest compressions keep blood moving to the brain. Dispatchers can talk you through it over the phone. An **AED** (defibrillator) is designed for untrained public use, with spoken instructions — use one if available.

## Card: Common mistakes
The key mistakes: not calling emergency services first; doing nothing out of fear of "getting it wrong" (in cardiac arrest, attempting CPR is far better than no action); using adult techniques on babies; and assuming you can't help. Remember dispatchers guide you, and AEDs instruct you aloud — you're not alone.

## Card: Quick recap
For choking, if they can't breathe or speak, call for help and use back blows/thrusts if trained. For an unresponsive, non-breathing person, call emergency services and start CPR — dispatchers and AEDs guide you. These are awareness notes; take a certified course to learn them properly, because technique truly matters.
