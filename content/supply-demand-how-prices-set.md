## Card: What it is
image: econSupplydemandHowPricesSetHero
The price of something in a free market isn't set arbitrarily — it emerges from supply and demand finding a balance. This balance point is called the **equilibrium price**: where the amount people want to buy matches the amount sellers offer. Understanding it explains how prices "settle" in markets.

## Card: Why it matters
Knowing how prices are determined demystifies why things cost what they do, and why prices change. It reveals that prices aren't usually dictated by one party but emerge from the push and pull of buyers and sellers. This is the mechanism behind market economies and a key to reading economic situations.

## Card: Key terms
- **Equilibrium price**: where supply and demand balance.
- **Surplus**: more supply than demand (price too high).
- **Shortage**: more demand than supply (price too low).
- **Market forces**: the push and pull of supply and demand.

## Card: The balance point
image: econSupplydemandHowPricesSetTheBalancePointHero
At a **price** that's too high, sellers offer lots but few want to buy — a **surplus** piles up, pushing the price down. At a price too low, everyone wants it but little is available — a **shortage**, pushing the price up. The price naturally moves toward the **equilibrium** where the quantity wanted equals the quantity supplied. The market "finds" this balance through these forces.

## Card: Prices as signals
Prices do a remarkable job: they act as *signals*. A high price signals scarcity and tells producers "make more," while drawing fewer buyers. A low price signals abundance, drawing buyers and discouraging production. Through these signals, **market forces** coordinate countless decisions without anyone being "in charge" — a self-organizing system guided by price.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a seller simply "decides" the price freely (they're constrained by what buyers will pay and competitors offer); not realizing surpluses and shortages push prices toward balance; and missing that prices carry information, signaling scarcity or abundance. Price emerges from market forces, not one party's whim.

## Card: Quick recap
Prices in a free market emerge at the equilibrium where supply meets demand. Too high a price causes a surplus (pushing it down); too low causes a shortage (pushing it up). Prices act as signals, coordinating producers and buyers without anyone in charge. Price isn't dictated by one party — it emerges from market forces.
