## Card: What it is
image: doorFixHero
Doors develop small annoyances — a squeaky hinge, a door that sticks or won't latch, a wobbly handle. These are among the most common household irritations and, happily, among the easiest to fix. Most need only a few minutes and a basic tool or two.

## Card: Why it matters
A squeak or stick seems trivial, but it grates daily and can signal wear that worsens over time. Fixing it is quick, free or cheap, and instantly improves how your home feels to use. These small wins also build confidence for bigger DIY jobs.

## Card: Key terms
- **Hinge**: the metal joint a door swings on.
- **Lubricant**: oil or spray that reduces squeak and friction.
- **Strike plate**: the metal plate the latch clicks into.
- **Sticking**: when a door rubs its frame instead of swinging freely.

## Card: Silencing a squeak
A squeaky **hinge** is almost always friction in the metal joint. Apply a little **lubricant** to the hinge pin and swing the door back and forth to work it in. A drop of household oil usually does it. Wipe away excess so it doesn't drip. This is often a literal one-minute fix.

## Card: Curing a sticking door
image: hingeLatchAlignment
**Sticking** means the door rubs the frame. First, tighten the **hinge** screws — loose hinges let the door sag and catch. If it still rubs, find the shiny worn spot where it touches, and sand that small area lightly. Often a sagging door just needs its top hinge screws tightened to lift it back into place.

## Card: Common mistakes
A frequent mistake is over-oiling, leaving drips that attract dust and stain the floor. Another is sanding a sticking door heavily before checking the simple cause — loose hinge screws — and removing far too much. With a loose handle, people force it rather than simply tightening the small screws holding it on.

## Card: Quick recap
Most door problems are quick fixes: oil a squeaky hinge sparingly, tighten loose hinge screws to cure sagging and sticking, and only sand the small worn spot if needed. Tighten handle screws rather than forcing. Start with the simplest cause first — usually a loose screw or a drop of oil.
