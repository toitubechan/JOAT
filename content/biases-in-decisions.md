## Card: What it is
image: decisionBiasesHero
Biases especially distort our **decisions** — about money, risk, and choices. Several well-known biases push us toward irrational decisions in predictable ways. Understanding these helps you make better choices, particularly in high-stakes areas like spending, investing, and judging risk.

## Card: Why it matters
Decision biases cost real money and lead to regret. They make us hold losing investments, throw good money after bad, and misjudge risks. Recognizing them when facing a decision is one of the most practically valuable applications of understanding biases. Better decisions compound over a lifetime.

## Card: Key terms
- **Loss aversion**: feeling losses more strongly than equivalent gains.
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: continuing because of past investment.
- **Overconfidence bias**: overestimating our own judgment.
- **Framing effect**: being swayed by how a choice is presented.

## Card: Loss aversion and sunk costs
image: lossSunkCostHero
**Loss aversion**: we feel the pain of a loss more strongly than the pleasure of an equal gain, which makes us overly cautious or unwilling to cut losses. Related is the **sunk cost fallacy**: continuing something (a project, a film, an investment) *because* we've already invested time or money, even when stopping is wiser. The past spending is gone — but it irrationally pulls us to keep going.

## Card: Overconfidence and framing
**Overconfidence bias**: most people overestimate their own knowledge, skill, and judgment (most drivers think they're above average — impossible). This leads to underestimating risks and overcommitting. The **framing effect**: the *same* choice feels different depending on how it's presented ("90% survival" vs. "10% mortality" sway us differently). How options are framed influences us, even when the facts are identical.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common bias-driven decision mistakes: refusing to sell a losing investment (loss aversion); finishing something bad because you've "come this far" (sunk cost); overestimating your own judgment and underrating risk (overconfidence); and being swayed by wording rather than facts (framing). Awareness when deciding — especially "is a sunk cost or framing trapping me?" — helps a lot.

## Card: Quick recap
Biases distort decisions: loss aversion makes us fear losses more than we value gains; the sunk cost fallacy keeps us going due to past investment; overconfidence makes us overrate our judgment and underrate risk; and framing sways us by how choices are worded. Spotting these when deciding — especially in money and risk — leads to clearer, better choices.
