## Card: What it is
image: fastenersHero
**Fasteners** are the bits that hold things together — mostly **screws** and **nails** — and **screwdrivers** are how you drive screws in and out. They come in different types and sizes, and matching them correctly is the secret to fixings that hold and screws that don't get mangled.

## Card: Why it matters
The wrong screwdriver slips, strips the screw head, and ruins both the screw and your patience. Knowing the common types and how to match them makes every screwing job smoother. It's a small bit of knowledge that prevents one of DIY's most common frustrations.

## Card: Key terms
- **Screw**: a fastener with a thread, driven by turning.
- **Nail**: a fastener driven straight in with a hammer.
- **Flat-head**: a screw with a single straight slot.
- **Cross-head (Phillips)**: a screw with a plus-shaped slot.

## Card: Screws vs. nails
**Screws** grip with their thread, hold strongly, and can be removed cleanly — ideal where you want a firm, reversible fixing. **Nails** are driven straight in with a hammer; they're faster but hold by friction and are harder to remove neatly. For most repairs and assembly, screws are the more forgiving choice.

## Card: Matching driver to screw
image: driverMatch
The two everyday screw heads are **flat-head** (single slot) and **cross-head** (plus shape, often called Phillips). Use the matching driver, and the right *size* too — a driver that's too small slips and strips the head. "Righty-tighty, lefty-loosey": turn clockwise to drive in, counterclockwise to remove.

## Card: Common mistakes
The classic mistake is using a too-small or wrong-type screwdriver, stripping the screw head into a useless smooth crater. Another is forcing a screw into hard material without a pilot hole, which can split wood. And many strip screws by pressing too lightly — firm downward pressure keeps the driver seated in the head.

## Card: Quick recap
Screws hold firmly and remove cleanly; nails are fast but cruder. Match your screwdriver to the screw's type (flat or cross-head) and size, press down firmly, and turn clockwise to tighten. The right driver, well seated, prevents stripped heads — the most common screwing frustration.
