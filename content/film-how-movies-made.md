## Card: What it is
image: filmHowMoviesMadeHero
**Making a movie** is a long, collaborative process that goes through distinct stages — from writing and planning, to filming, to assembling the final film. It involves many people and far more than just "shooting." Understanding the overall process demystifies how films come to be and reveals filmmaking as a structured craft, not just pointing a camera at actors.

## Card: Why it matters
Movies are central to culture, but how they're made is a mystery to most viewers. Understanding the process deepens appreciation of films, reveals the enormous collaborative effort behind them, and is the foundation for anyone wanting to make video or film themselves. It turns watching a movie into understanding the craft and work behind it.

## Card: Key terms
- **Pre-production**: planning before filming.
- **Production**: the actual filming.
- **Post-production**: editing and finishing after filming.
- **Collaboration**: the many roles involved in filmmaking.

## Card: The three main stages
image: filmHowMoviesMadeTheThreeMainStagesHero
Filmmaking has three broad stages. **Pre-production** — all the planning *before* filming: writing the script, planning shots, casting, finding locations, budgeting, and organizing. **Production** — the actual filming (often the shortest stage in time, despite being what people picture). **Post-production** — everything *after* filming: editing the footage, adding sound and music, visual effects, and polishing into the final film. Each stage is essential, and a huge amount of work happens before and after the actual shooting.

## Card: A massive collaboration
A key realization: filmmaking is intensely **collaborative**. Beyond the director and actors, films involve writers, producers, cinematographers, editors, sound designers, composers, set and costume teams, visual effects artists, and many more — sometimes hundreds of people. Each contributes specialized work. The finished film is the combined effort of all these roles across all three stages. Understanding this reveals why making even a modest film is a significant, organized undertaking, not a one-person job.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking filmmaking is mostly just the filming (pre- and post-production are huge); imagining the director does everything (it's a vast collaboration of specialized roles); underrating how much planning and editing matter; and not realizing how long and structured the process is. Making a movie is a multi-stage, deeply collaborative craft — far more than pointing a camera at actors.

## Card: Quick recap
Making a movie goes through three main stages: pre-production (planning — script, casting, locations, budgeting), production (the actual filming, often the shortest), and post-production (editing, sound, effects, and finishing). It's an intensely collaborative effort involving many specialized roles — writers, cinematographers, editors, sound designers, and more. A huge amount of work happens before and after shooting. Filmmaking is a structured, collaborative craft.
