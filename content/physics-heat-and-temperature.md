## Card: What it is
image: heatTemperatureHero
**Heat** is a form of energy — specifically the energy of jiggling, moving particles — and **temperature** measures how fast those particles are moving on average. "Hot" means particles moving fast; "cold" means moving slowly. This view explains everyday experiences with warmth, cooking, and weather.

## Card: Why it matters
Heat and temperature affect daily life constantly — cooking, heating homes, the weather, why a metal spoon feels colder than a wooden one. Understanding what heat actually *is* (moving particles) demystifies all of this and corrects common confusions, like the difference between heat and temperature, which aren't the same thing.

## Card: Key terms
- **Heat**: energy transferred due to temperature difference.
- **Temperature**: a measure of average particle motion.
- **Conduction, convection, radiation**: the three ways heat travels.
- **Thermal equilibrium**: when temperatures even out.

## Card: Hot means fast-moving particles
Everything is made of particles in constant motion. **Temperature** measures how fast they jiggle on average — fast means hot, slow means cold. **Heat** is energy flowing from hotter to colder objects, until they reach the same temperature (**thermal equilibrium**). This is why a hot drink cools and a cold one warms — heat always flows from hot to cold, never the reverse on its own.

## Card: How heat travels
image: heatTransferWays
Heat moves three ways: **conduction** (through direct contact, like a spoon heating in soup), **convection** (through moving fluids, like hot air rising), and **radiation** (through space as waves, like the Sun's warmth or a fire's glow). These explain everyday things — why metal feels cold (it conducts heat away from your hand fast), why ovens heat air, and why you feel the Sun's warmth across empty space.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: confusing heat and temperature (a spark is high-temperature but carries little heat; a warm bath is lower-temperature but holds far more heat energy); thinking cold "flows" into things (heat flows out instead); and believing metal is "colder" than wood when both are room temperature — metal just conducts heat from your hand faster, so it *feels* colder.

## Card: Quick recap
Heat is the energy of moving particles; temperature measures their average speed — fast is hot, slow is cold. Heat flows from hot to cold until temperatures even out. It travels by conduction, convection, and radiation. Heat and temperature aren't the same, and metal feels cold because it conducts heat from your hand quickly.
