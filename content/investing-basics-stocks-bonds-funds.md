## Card: What it is
image: financeInvestingStocksBondsFundsHero
Most investing comes down to a few basic building blocks. **Stocks** are tiny slices of ownership in a company. **Bonds** are loans you make to a government or company. **Funds** bundle many of these together in one purchase. Knowing these three covers most of what beginners encounter.

## Card: Why it matters
Investing jargon can feel like a foreign language, which scares people off. But almost every product is built from these basic pieces. Once you understand stocks, bonds, and funds, the fancier terms mostly become combinations of them — and you can judge what you're actually buying.

## Card: Key terms
- **Stock (share)**: a small piece of ownership in a company.
- **Bond**: a loan to a government or company, repaid with interest.
- **Fund**: a basket of many investments bought together.
- **Index fund**: a fund that simply tracks a whole market.

## Card: Stocks and bonds
image: financeInvestingStocksBondsFundsStocksAndBondsHero
A **stock** makes you a part-owner of a company; if it grows, your slice can rise in value (and some pay **dividends**, a share of profits). Stocks can swing a lot. A **bond** is the opposite feel: you lend money and get fixed interest, then your money back. Bonds are generally steadier but grow more slowly.

## Card: Funds and index funds
Buying single stocks is risky — one company can fail. A **fund** spreads your money across many investments at once, so no single failure sinks you. An **index fund** is a popular low-cost type that simply tracks an entire market (like "the 500 biggest companies"), giving instant broad spread without you picking winners.

## Card: Common mistakes
A frequent beginner mistake is betting big on a single "hot" stock, ignoring that one company can collapse. Another is not noticing **fees** — some funds quietly charge high yearly costs that eat returns. And many confuse a fund's past performance with a guarantee; past results never promise future ones.

## Card: Quick recap
Stocks are ownership slices (higher growth, more swings); bonds are loans (steadier, slower); funds bundle many together to spread risk, with index funds offering cheap, broad exposure. Watch fees, avoid betting everything on one stock, and remember past performance isn't a promise.
