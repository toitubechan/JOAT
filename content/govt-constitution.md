## Card: What it is
image: govtConstitutionHero
A **constitution** is the fundamental set of rules and principles by which a country is governed — defining how government is structured, what powers it has, and often the rights of citizens. It's like the supreme rulebook that other laws must follow. Understanding what a constitution does reveals the foundation on which a country's whole system of government rests. This is a neutral, educational overview.

## Card: Why it matters
A constitution shapes everything about how a country is governed — the structure of power, the limits on government, and citizens' rights. Understanding its role helps you grasp how a country's system works, why constitutions are so important, and how they protect (or fail to protect) freedoms. It's foundational to understanding government and rights.

## Card: Key terms
- **Constitution**: a country's fundamental rules for government.
- **Supreme law**: the highest law, which others must follow.
- **Rights**: freedoms a constitution may protect.
- **Limits on power**: constraints a constitution places on government.

## Card: The supreme rulebook
image: govtConstitutionTheSupremeRulebookHero
A **constitution** sets out the basic framework of government: how it's structured (e.g., the branches and their powers), how leaders are chosen, and how decisions are made. Crucially, it's usually the **supreme law** — the highest rules, which all other laws and government actions must follow. If an ordinary law conflicts with the constitution, the constitution wins. It's the foundation everything else is built on, giving stability and a shared framework.

## Card: Protecting rights and limiting power
Beyond structure, many constitutions also define **rights** — freedoms guaranteed to citizens (like free speech or fair treatment) that the government must respect — and place **limits on power**, constraining what government can do. This is a key protective function: by enshrining rights and limits in the supreme law, a constitution aims to prevent government from overstepping or abusing power. How well this works in practice varies, but it's a central purpose of having a constitution.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a constitution is just one more law (it's usually the *supreme* law others must follow); not knowing it both structures government *and* often protects rights and limits power; assuming all countries have the same kind (they vary, and some are written, some unwritten); and underrating its protective role. A constitution is the foundational rulebook defining and limiting how a country is governed.

## Card: Quick recap
A constitution is a country's fundamental set of rules for government — defining how power is structured and chosen, usually as the supreme law that all other laws must follow. Many also protect citizens' rights and place limits on government power, aiming to prevent abuse. It's the foundation a country's whole system rests on. Understanding it reveals how government is structured and how freedoms are (meant to be) protected.
