## Card: What it is
image: medicineHeartDiseaseHero
**Heart disease** broadly refers to conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels — and it's one of the leading causes of death worldwide. A common form involves the gradual narrowing of arteries that supply the heart. Understanding how it develops reveals why prevention matters. (General educational overview, not medical advice.)

## Card: Why it matters
Heart disease is a top cause of death globally, yet much of the risk is influenced by factors people can understand and partly manage. Understanding how it develops demystifies the condition, explains why lifestyle and check-ups matter, and supports general health awareness. For personal risk and advice, a doctor is the right source.

## Card: Key terms
- **Arteries**: vessels carrying blood from the heart.
- **Plaque**: fatty buildup that narrows arteries.
- **Risk factors**: things raising the chance of heart disease.
- **Prevention**: reducing risk through habits and monitoring.

## Card: How it develops
image: medicineHeartDiseaseHowItDevelopsHero
A common path: over years, fatty deposits (**plaque**) can build up inside the **arteries** that supply the heart, gradually narrowing them. This restricts blood flow. If an artery becomes badly blocked, the part of the heart it feeds can be starved of oxygen — which is what causes a heart attack. This buildup typically develops slowly and silently over a long time, often without obvious symptoms early on.

## Card: Risk factors and prevention
Heart disease has well-known **risk factors**, some you can't change (age, genetics) and many you can influence: high blood pressure, smoking, poor diet, inactivity, excess weight, and high cholesterol. Because much risk is modifiable, **prevention** — through healthy habits and regular check-ups to catch issues like high blood pressure early — can substantially lower the chances. It's not guaranteed, but the risk is meaningfully reducible.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking heart disease strikes suddenly out of nowhere (it usually develops slowly over years); assuming it's purely genetic with nothing you can do (many risk factors are modifiable); ignoring "silent" risks like high blood pressure (often symptomless but important); and expecting prevention to be a guarantee (it reduces risk, not eliminates it). Awareness supports prevention.

## Card: Quick recap
Heart disease, a leading global killer, often develops as fatty plaque slowly builds up in the heart's arteries over years, narrowing them and restricting blood flow — a severe blockage can cause a heart attack. Many risk factors are modifiable (blood pressure, smoking, diet, activity), so prevention through healthy habits and check-ups can substantially reduce risk. For personal advice, see a doctor.
