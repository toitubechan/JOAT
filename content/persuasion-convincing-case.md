## Card: What it is
image: convincingCaseHero
Making a **convincing case** is the craft of putting your argument together so it persuades — combining logic, credibility, and emotion in the right balance. A good case isn't just true; it's presented in a way that moves people. This is how you turn a sound point into one that actually changes minds.

## Card: Why it matters
Being right isn't enough — plenty of correct arguments fail to persuade because they're presented poorly. Knowing how to build a compelling case means your genuinely good ideas get the hearing they deserve. It's the difference between being right and being *convincing*, which is what actually moves people to act.

## Card: Key terms
- **Logic (logos)**: reasoning and evidence.
- **Credibility (ethos)**: trust in the messenger.
- **Emotion (pathos)**: how the case makes people feel.
- **Storytelling**: using narrative to make a case vivid.

## Card: The three appeals
image: threeAppealsHero
A classic framework: persuasion combines **logic** (sound reasoning and evidence), **credibility** (you being trustworthy and knowledgeable), and **emotion** (how the case makes people feel). All three matter. Pure logic often fails to move people; pure emotion feels manipulative; you need the blend. People are convinced by good reasons *and* by how those reasons feel.

## Card: Make it concrete and vivid
Abstract arguments don't stick — concrete ones do. Use specific examples, real numbers, and especially **storytelling**: a relatable story or vivid example makes a case land far harder than dry assertion. "Costs have risen" is forgettable; a specific story of someone affected is memorable and moving. Make your case tangible, not theoretical.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: relying on logic alone and ignoring emotion (or vice versa); neglecting your own credibility; being abstract instead of concrete; overwhelming people with too many points (a few strong ones beat many weak); and not addressing the obvious counterargument, which leaves a hole. Balance the appeals, stay concrete, and keep it focused.

## Card: Quick recap
A convincing case blends logic (reasoning and evidence), credibility (being trustworthy), and emotion (how it feels) — all three, not just one. Make it concrete and vivid with examples and stories rather than abstractions. Keep it focused on a few strong points, and address the obvious counterargument. Being right isn't enough; be convincing.
