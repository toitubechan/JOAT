## Card: What it is
image: histIndustrialLegacyTodayHero
The Industrial Revolution's **legacy** is all around us — our economy, technology, cities, work patterns, and even today's environmental challenges trace back to it. Understanding this legacy connects a historical turning point directly to the world you live in now, and to debates about the future.

## Card: Why it matters
The Industrial Revolution isn't just history — it set in motion forces still shaping our lives and our biggest challenges, including climate change. Understanding its ongoing legacy helps you see how the past created the present, and informs current debates about technology, work, growth, and sustainability. It links then to now and to what comes next.

## Card: Key terms
- **Legacy**: lasting effects continuing into the present.
- **Consumer society**: an economy built on mass-produced goods.
- **Fossil fuels**: coal, oil, gas — central to industrialization.
- **Sustainability**: meeting needs without harming the future.

## Card: The world it built
image: histIndustrialLegacyTodayTheWorldItBuiltHero
Much of modern life is the Industrial Revolution's **legacy**: a **consumer society** of abundant, affordable mass-produced goods; urban living and commuting to workplaces; rapid, continuous technological progress; and a global economy of trade and production. The patterns of work, consumption, and city life we treat as normal were largely set in motion by industrialization. We still live in the world it created.

## Card: The environmental legacy
A crucial part of the legacy is environmental. Industrialization was powered by **fossil fuels** (coal, then oil and gas), and the burning of these fuels — vastly expanded since the Industrial Revolution — is the main driver of climate change today. So one of our era's greatest challenges traces directly back to this turning point. **Sustainability** debates are, in part, about addressing the long tail of industrialization's reliance on fossil fuels.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: treating the Industrial Revolution as distant history with no present relevance (its legacy is everywhere); not connecting it to modern environmental challenges like climate change; assuming our consumer, urban, fossil-fueled way of life is "natural" rather than a recent legacy; and ignoring how it shapes current debates about growth and sustainability. The past is very present.

## Card: Quick recap
The Industrial Revolution's legacy surrounds us: our consumer society, urban life, continuous technological progress, and global economy all trace to it. So does a major modern challenge — its reliance on fossil fuels is the main driver of today's climate change. Understanding this legacy connects the past to the present and to debates about sustainability and the future.
