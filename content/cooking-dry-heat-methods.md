## Card: What it is
image: dryHeatHero
**Dry-heat** methods cook food with hot air, hot fat, or direct heat — no water involved. They include **roasting**, **frying**, **sautéing**, and **grilling**. Their signature is **browning**: the rich, savory flavor and golden color that develops when food gets hot enough on the outside.

## Card: Why it matters
Browning is where much of cooking's deep flavor comes from — a seared steak, roasted vegetables, crispy fried food. Understanding dry-heat methods lets you create that flavor reliably instead of by accident. They're behind most of the dishes people find genuinely delicious.

## Card: Key terms
- **Roasting**: cooking with dry heat in an oven.
- **Frying / sautéing**: cooking in hot fat in a pan.
- **Grilling**: cooking with direct high heat from below or above.
- **Browning (Maillard reaction)**: the flavor-building reaction when food gets hot.

## Card: The magic of browning
image: browningMagic
When food gets hot and dry enough, the **Maillard reaction** kicks in — proteins and sugars transform into hundreds of new flavor compounds, giving that irresistible savory taste and brown color. This is why a seared steak beats a boiled one. The key requirement: high enough heat and a dry surface, since water blocks browning.

## Card: Roasting, frying, grilling
**Roasting** surrounds food with dry oven heat — great for vegetables and larger cuts of meat. **Frying/sautéing** uses hot fat in a pan for quick, flavorful cooking. **Grilling** applies intense direct heat for char and smokiness. All three rely on getting hot enough to brown, so patience and proper heat matter.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: pans or ovens not hot enough, so food steams and goes grey instead of browning; wet food surfaces (pat meat dry first!); overcrowding, which traps steam; and flipping or stirring too often, never letting a crust form. Browning needs heat, dryness, space, and a little patience.

## Card: Quick recap
Dry-heat methods — roasting, frying, grilling — cook with hot air, fat, or direct heat, and their gift is browning: the Maillard reaction that builds deep savory flavor. Get the heat high enough, keep the surface dry, don't overcrowd, and let a crust form. That's the route to genuinely tasty food.
