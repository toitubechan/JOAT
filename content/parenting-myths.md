## Card: What it is
image: parentingMythsHero
**Parenting myths** are widely believed but inaccurate ideas about raising children — passed around as fact but not supported by good evidence or child development understanding. They can cause unnecessary worry, guilt, or poor practices. Understanding that many common "parenting truths" are myths helps caregivers parent more confidently and sensibly. This is general educational knowledge, not professional advice.

## Card: Why it matters
Parents are surrounded by advice, much of it conflicting or simply wrong. Believing parenting myths causes needless guilt, anxiety, and sometimes unhelpful practices. Understanding that many widespread beliefs are myths — and how to think critically about parenting advice — helps caregivers feel more confident, worry less, and make better decisions. It's freeing to know you needn't believe everything you're told.

## Card: Key terms
- **Parenting myth**: a widely believed but inaccurate parenting idea.
- **Conflicting advice**: the flood of contradictory parenting guidance.
- **Evidence vs. folklore**: well-supported knowledge vs. passed-down beliefs.
- **Critical thinking**: evaluating parenting advice sensibly.

## Card: Myths cause needless worry
image: parentingMythsMythsCauseNeedlessWorryHero
Many **parenting myths** circulate as fact — rigid claims about what you "must" or "must never" do, often causing guilt and anxiety when reality is more flexible. Examples of the *type*: overly rigid rules presented as absolute, claims that minor choices will "ruin" a child, or one-size-fits-all prescriptions. The reality is usually more forgiving: children are resilient, there's rarely one "right" way, and good parenting comes in many forms. Recognizing myths frees caregivers from needless worry over things that don't actually matter much.

## Card: Thinking critically about advice
The antidote to myths is **critical thinking** about parenting advice. Parents face a flood of **conflicting advice**, so it helps to distinguish **evidence vs. folklore** — well-supported knowledge from passed-down beliefs or someone's strong opinion. Ask: is this actually supported, or just commonly repeated? Be wary of rigid, guilt-inducing absolutes and one-size-fits-all claims. Trust reputable sources, your knowledge of your own child, and sensible judgment over folklore and pressure. Critical thinking turns the overwhelming flood of advice into something manageable.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: believing parenting myths because they're widely repeated (popularity isn't evidence); feeling guilt and anxiety over myths that don't reflect reality; not distinguishing evidence from folklore; and following rigid one-size-fits-all rules. Think critically about parenting advice, distinguish evidence from folklore, and remember children are resilient and good parenting comes in many forms — freeing you from needless worry.

## Card: Quick recap
Parenting myths are widely believed but inaccurate ideas about raising children, often causing needless guilt and anxiety with rigid absolutes when reality is more forgiving (children are resilient; there's rarely one "right" way). The antidote is critical thinking: distinguish evidence from folklore, ask whether advice is actually supported or just commonly repeated, and be wary of guilt-inducing absolutes. This frees caregivers to parent more confidently and worry less.
