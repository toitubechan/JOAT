## Card: What it is
image: writingFormatsHero
**Writing for different formats** means adapting your approach to the medium — a novel, short story, screenplay, blog post, or social media post each have different demands. Good writing isn't one-size-fits-all; what works in one format may not in another. Understanding how formats differ helps you write effectively for whatever you're creating.

## Card: Why it matters
Writers often apply the same approach everywhere and wonder why it doesn't land. Each format has its own conventions, constraints, and reader expectations. Understanding these helps you write effectively across mediums — whether crafting a story, a script, an article, or a post — rather than misapplying techniques from one to another. It makes your writing fit its purpose and audience.

## Card: Key terms
- **Format**: the medium or form of writing (novel, script, post, etc.).
- **Conventions**: the expected norms of a format.
- **Constraints**: limits a format imposes (length, structure).
- **Audience and purpose**: who you're writing for and why.

## Card: Different formats, different demands
image: formatDemandsHero
Each **format** has distinct demands. A novel allows depth, interiority, and length; a short story demands compression and a tight focus; a screenplay is mostly action and dialogue (showing, not interior thoughts, since it's meant to be seen); a blog post or article prioritizes clarity and value for readers skimming online; social media demands brevity and immediate hooks. The same idea must be shaped differently for each — the **constraints** and **conventions** of the format guide how you write.

## Card: Match audience and purpose
Beyond conventions, always consider **audience and purpose**. Who's reading, in what context, and what do they need? Online readers skim and want value fast; a literary reader may savor language; a script reader needs it visual and performable. Adapting tone, length, structure, and style to fit the format's audience and purpose is what makes writing effective. The core craft (good sentences, clear thinking) carries across, but how you apply it should flex to the format.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: writing every format the same way (ignoring their different demands); applying novel-style interiority to a screenplay (which must be visual); ignoring online readers' need for clarity and brevity; and disregarding each format's conventions and audience. Adapt your approach to the format's constraints, conventions, audience, and purpose — while the core craft of good writing carries across all of them.

## Card: Quick recap
Writing for different formats means adapting to the medium: novels allow depth and length, short stories demand compression, screenplays are visual (action and dialogue), articles prioritize clarity for skimming readers, and social media demands brevity. Each has its own conventions, constraints, and audience. Match your tone, length, and style to the format's purpose and readers. Core craft carries across, but how you apply it should flex.
