## Card: What it is
image: philosophyTrolleyProblemHero
The **trolley problem** is a famous ethical thought experiment: a runaway trolley will kill five people, but you could divert it to a track where it kills only one. Do you act? It's the most famous of many **moral dilemmas** — situations where every option has a serious downside. These dilemmas reveal deep tensions in how we think about right and wrong.

## Card: Why it matters
Moral dilemmas like the trolley problem aren't just puzzles — they expose the conflicts between different ethical principles and force us to examine our moral reasoning. They also have real-world relevance (e.g., how should a self-driving car be programmed to act in an unavoidable accident?). Understanding them deepens your grasp of ethics and reveals why hard moral choices are genuinely hard.

## Card: Key terms
- **Trolley problem**: a dilemma of sacrificing one to save five.
- **Moral dilemma**: a choice where every option has a serious cost.
- **Action vs. inaction**: the moral weight of doing versus not doing.
- **Conflicting principles**: ethical rules that pull in different directions.

## Card: The dilemma and its twist
image: philosophyTrolleyProblemTheDilemmaAndItsTwistHero
In the basic **trolley problem**, most people say they'd divert the trolley to save five at the cost of one — judging by consequences (five lives over one). But variations change our intuitions: if saving the five required *physically pushing* one person to their death, most people refuse — even though the numbers are identical. This reveals that our morality isn't purely about outcomes; how directly we cause harm, and **action vs. inaction**, deeply affect our moral intuitions.

## Card: Why dilemmas matter
The trolley problem works because it pits **conflicting principles** against each other: "minimize deaths" (consequences) versus "don't actively kill an innocent person" (a moral rule). Real **moral dilemmas** do the same — there's no option without a serious cost, and different ethical lenses give different answers. This is why such dilemmas have no clean solution, and why they're so revealing: they show that our moral reasoning is complex, and good principles can genuinely conflict.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking the trolley problem has one "correct" answer (it reveals conflicting intuitions, not a solution); dismissing it as unrealistic (its point is to expose how we reason, and it has real applications like self-driving cars); assuming morality is purely about outcomes (the variations show otherwise); and finding dilemmas frustrating rather than illuminating. They reveal the genuine complexity of ethics.

## Card: Quick recap
The trolley problem — divert a trolley to kill one instead of five? — is the most famous moral dilemma, where every choice has a serious cost. Variations (like physically pushing someone) flip our intuitions, revealing that morality isn't purely about outcomes — how directly we cause harm matters too. Such dilemmas pit conflicting ethical principles against each other, exposing why hard moral choices are genuinely hard, with real relevance like self-driving cars.
