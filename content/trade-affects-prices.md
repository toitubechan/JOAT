## Card: What it is
image: econTradeAffectsPricesHero
Global trade directly affects the **prices** and variety of goods you buy. By letting countries source goods from wherever they're made cheapest or best, trade generally lowers prices and widens choice — but it also links your local prices to events around the world. Understanding this connects global trade to your shopping.

## Card: Why it matters
Trade is a big reason many goods are affordable and varied. Understanding how it shapes prices explains why things cost what they do, why global events (a distant disruption) can raise your local prices, and the trade-offs in debates about trade. It links the world economy directly to your wallet.

## Card: Key terms
- **Global supply chain**: the worldwide network producing a good.
- **Competition from imports**: foreign goods competing on price.
- **Disruption**: an event interrupting trade or supply.
- **Cost and variety**: trade's main effects on what you buy.

## Card: Trade lowers prices and widens choice
image: econTradeAffectsPricesTradeLowersPricesAndWidensHero
Trade generally pushes prices *down* and **variety** up. By sourcing from wherever production is cheapest and importing goods that compete with local ones (**competition from imports**), trade gives consumers more choice at lower cost. The wide range of affordable goods in a typical shop — from many countries — is largely thanks to global trade and the worldwide **supply chains** behind them.

## Card: But it links you to the world
The flip side: trade connects your local prices to global events. Because goods travel through **global supply chains**, a **disruption** far away — a shipping problem, a crop failure, a conflict, an energy shock — can raise prices in your local shop. This interconnection means global trade brings cheaper goods *and* exposure to distant shocks. It's a trade-off, not a free lunch.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: not connecting global trade to local prices and choice; being surprised when a distant event raises local prices (supply chains link the world); assuming trade only lowers prices with no downsides (it also brings exposure to global shocks); and overlooking trade's role in the affordability and variety we take for granted.

## Card: Quick recap
Global trade generally lowers prices and widens choice by sourcing goods from wherever they're cheapest or best, with import competition keeping prices down. But it links your local prices to global events — a distant disruption can raise prices via worldwide supply chains. Trade brings affordability and variety, alongside exposure to global shocks.
