## Card: What it is
image: speakingMistakesHero
Most public speaking goes wrong in a handful of predictable ways. This card set collects the common mistakes — from slide overload to running over time — so you can avoid them and give a noticeably better talk. Sidestepping these is often easier than perfecting your delivery.

## Card: Why it matters
A single avoidable mistake — a cluttered slide, going way over time, an unclear point — can undermine an otherwise good talk. Knowing the pitfalls lets you prevent them in advance. Since most are easy to avoid once named, this is a quick route to better presentations.

## Card: Key terms
- **Slide overload**: cramming too much onto slides.
- **Reading slides**: reciting text instead of speaking to the audience.
- **Going over time**: exceeding your allotted slot.
- **Death by PowerPoint**: dull, text-heavy slide presentations.

## Card: Slide and content mistakes
image: slideContentMistakesHero
The classic: **slide overload** and **reading slides** — packing slides with text, then reading it aloud (often called "death by PowerPoint"). Slides should support you with visuals or a few words, not be your script. Other content errors: trying to cover too much, no clear main message, and burying the point. Less, focused, beats more, cluttered.

## Card: Delivery and timing mistakes
Common delivery and timing failures: **going over time** (which frustrates audiences and organizers — respect the clock); speaking too fast from nerves; no eye contact (reading notes or slides the whole time); and a flat, energy-less delivery. Practicing your talk against the clock and away from your notes prevents most of these.

## Card: Common mistakes
To gather them: text-heavy slides and reading them aloud; covering too much with no clear message; going over time; rushing from nerves; no eye contact; and not practicing beforehand. Nearly all are fixed by simpler slides, a focused message, rehearsing against the clock, and connecting with the audience instead of your notes.

## Card: Quick recap
Common speaking mistakes include text-heavy slides you read aloud ("death by PowerPoint"), covering too much with no clear message, going over time, rushing, and avoiding eye contact. The fixes: simple supporting slides, one focused message, rehearse against the clock, and speak to the audience, not your notes.
