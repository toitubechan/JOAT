## Card: What it is
image: healthyRelationshipsHero
**Healthy relationships** are those built on mutual respect, trust, good communication, and support — where both people feel valued and free to be themselves. Understanding what characterizes a healthy relationship (versus an unhealthy one) helps you build and recognize good relationships of all kinds. This is general educational knowledge about relationships and wellbeing, not professional advice.

## Card: Why it matters
Relationships are central to human happiness and wellbeing, yet what makes them healthy is rarely taught. Understanding the foundations of healthy relationships helps you build better ones, recognize when something's wrong, and have realistic, constructive expectations. It applies to romantic relationships, friendships, and family alike — making it broadly valuable for a good life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Healthy relationship**: one built on respect, trust, and support.
- **Mutual respect**: valuing each other as equals.
- **Interdependence**: connection without losing yourself.
- **Reciprocity**: both people giving and supporting, not one-sided.

## Card: The foundations
Healthy relationships rest on a few foundations: **mutual respect** (valuing each other as equals, respecting boundaries and differences), trust, honest communication, and genuine support for each other. Crucially, there's **reciprocity** — both people give, support, and invest, rather than one person doing all the giving. In a healthy relationship, both people feel safe, valued, heard, and free to be themselves. These foundations apply across romantic, family, and friend relationships alike.

## Card: Connection without losing yourself
image: connectionSelfHero
A healthy relationship involves **interdependence** — genuine closeness and mutual support — *without* either person losing their independence or identity. Healthy partners or friends support each other while still being whole individuals with their own lives, interests, and boundaries. This balance distinguishes healthy closeness from unhealthy patterns (like total dependence, control, or losing yourself in another). Good relationships make both people *more* themselves, not less — connected yet still individually whole.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking healthy relationships have no conflict (healthy ones handle conflict respectfully, not avoid it entirely); believing closeness means losing your independence (healthy relationships balance connection with individuality); tolerating one-sided relationships lacking reciprocity; and confusing intensity or control with love. Healthy relationships are built on mutual respect, trust, communication, support, reciprocity, and connection without losing yourself.

## Card: Quick recap
Healthy relationships are built on mutual respect, trust, honest communication, and genuine support, with reciprocity (both people giving, not one-sided). Both people feel safe, valued, and free to be themselves. They involve interdependence — real closeness *without* losing your independence or identity — so good relationships make you more yourself, not less. These foundations apply to romantic, family, and friend relationships alike.
