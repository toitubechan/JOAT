## Card: What it is
image: howworksEnginesHero
An **engine** converts energy — usually from burning fuel — into mechanical motion. The most common type, the internal combustion engine, powers most traditional cars by burning fuel in small controlled explosions. Understanding the basic idea reveals how stored chemical energy becomes the turning force that moves machines.

## Card: Why it matters
Engines power cars, generators, machinery, and much of the modern world. Understanding the basic principle — turning fuel's energy into motion through controlled combustion — demystifies a core technology and helps you understand vehicles, the shift to electric motors, and energy conversion in general. It's foundational mechanical knowledge.

## Card: Key terms
- **Engine**: a machine converting energy into motion.
- **Internal combustion**: burning fuel inside the engine.
- **Piston**: a part pushed by the explosion, creating motion.
- **Energy conversion**: changing one form of energy to another.

## Card: Controlled explosions
image: howworksEnginesControlledExplosionsHero
The classic **internal combustion** engine works through a repeating cycle: fuel mixed with air is ignited in a small chamber, and the controlled explosion pushes a **piston**. The piston's up-and-down motion is converted (via a crankshaft) into the rotating motion that turns wheels or machinery. This happens thousands of times a minute across multiple chambers, producing smooth, continuous power. It's essentially a series of tiny, harnessed explosions.

## Card: Energy conversion at the core
At heart, an engine is an **energy conversion** machine: it turns the *chemical* energy stored in fuel into *mechanical* energy (motion), with heat as a byproduct (which is why engines get hot and need cooling). This is why engines need fuel and produce exhaust and heat. Electric motors do a similar job — converting electrical energy into motion — but more simply and cleanly, which is part of why electric vehicles are rising.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking an engine is one solid moving part (it's a cycle of combustion driving pistons, converted to rotation); not realizing it works by controlled explosions; confusing engines (burning fuel) with electric motors (using electricity); and not seeing it as energy conversion (chemical to mechanical, with heat lost). Engines turn fuel's stored energy into motion through repeated controlled combustion.

## Card: Quick recap
An engine converts energy into motion. The internal combustion engine burns fuel in small controlled explosions that push pistons, whose motion is converted into the rotation that drives wheels or machinery — happening thousands of times a minute. At its core it's energy conversion: chemical energy (fuel) into mechanical energy (motion), with heat as a byproduct. Electric motors do this more simply with electricity.
