## Card: What it is
image: exerciseMistakesHero
Beginners and returners tend to trip over the same exercise mistakes — ones that cause injury, stall progress, or kill motivation. This card set gathers the most common so you can avoid learning them the hard way. Sidestepping these is half the battle.

## Card: Why it matters
A single injury or burnout episode can set you back weeks and crush your enthusiasm. Most of these mistakes are easy to avoid once you know them. Recognizing the traps protects your body, your progress, and your willingness to keep going — which is what ultimately matters.

## Card: Key terms
- **Overtraining**: too much exercise without enough recovery.
- **Plateau**: when progress stalls and needs a change.
- **Form**: correct technique for an exercise.
- **Consistency**: regular activity over time.

## Card: Doing too much, resting too little
The classic mistake is **overtraining** — exercising hard daily with no recovery, leading to exhaustion, injury, and burnout. More isn't always better; the body adapts during rest. Pushing through real pain (not normal effort) is how injuries happen. Recovery and listening to your body are part of training, not weakness.

## Card: Neglecting form and balance
image: restFormBalance
Poor **form** is a top cause of injury and wasted effort — technique beats heavy weight or speed. Many also train unevenly: only the muscles they can see, or only cardio. And hitting a **plateau** (progress stalling) makes some quit, when it usually just signals it's time to vary the routine or increase the challenge.

## Card: Common mistakes
To recap the traps: overtraining without rest; ignoring warm-ups; sacrificing form for ego; doing only exercise you're already good at; expecting fast results and quitting when they're slow; and the biggest of all — inconsistency, doing nothing between bursts of enthusiasm. **Consistency** quietly beats every other factor.

## Card: Quick recap
Avoid the common exercise traps: overtraining without recovery, poor form, unbalanced training, and quitting at a plateau or when results feel slow. Most of all, beat inconsistency — regular moderate effort outperforms occasional intense bursts. Protect your body and your motivation, and progress takes care of itself.
