## Card: What it is
image: cvPurposeHero
A **CV** (curriculum vitae) or **résumé** is a short document summarizing your skills, experience, and qualifications for a job. Its real job isn't to tell your whole life story — it's a marketing document with one goal: to get you an *interview*. Understanding that purpose changes how you write it.

## Card: Why it matters
The CV is usually the first impression an employer gets, and it decides whether you make the shortlist. A good one opens doors; a poor one ends your chances before you've said a word. Since the same person can succeed or fail purely on CV quality, learning to write one well genuinely matters.

## Card: Key terms
- **CV / résumé**: a summary document of your professional background.
- **Recruiter**: the person who screens applications.
- **Shortlist**: the candidates selected for interview.
- **Relevance**: how well your CV matches the specific job.

## Card: Its one job: get the interview
image: interviewDoorHero
A CV's purpose is **not** to get you the job — it's to get you the *interview*. That's a crucial reframe: every line should earn its place by making you a more compelling candidate for *this* role. You're not documenting everything you've ever done; you're persuading a busy reader that you're worth meeting.

## Card: Written for a busy reader
**Recruiters** often skim each CV for mere seconds before deciding. So a CV must be clear, scannable, and quick to grasp — the most relevant, impressive information up top, easy to find. Dense, cluttered, or unfocused CVs get put down. Write for someone with little time and a big pile of applications.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: treating the CV as a complete life history rather than a targeted pitch; making it dense and hard to skim; burying the most relevant points; and using one generic CV for every job. Remember the goal (get the interview) and the reader (busy, skimming), and most CV problems resolve themselves.

## Card: Quick recap
A CV is a marketing document with one job: to get you an interview, not the job itself. It's read by busy recruiters who skim in seconds, so it must be clear, scannable, and lead with relevant, impressive information. Write a targeted pitch for each role, not a generic life history.
