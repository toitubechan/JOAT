## Card: What it is
image: philosophyStoicismHero
**Stoicism** is an ancient Greek and Roman philosophy offering practical wisdom for living well, especially handling adversity. Its core idea: focus on what you can control, accept what you can't, and cultivate inner calm and virtue. Far from dusty history, Stoicism has had a huge modern revival as a practical guide to resilience and peace of mind.

## Card: Why it matters
Stoicism offers genuinely useful, time-tested tools for dealing with stress, setbacks, and life's ups and downs. Its ideas overlap with modern psychology and resonate with many people today. Understanding it gives you practical wisdom for staying calm and resilient — a philosophy you can actually *use* in daily life, not just contemplate.

## Card: Key terms
- **Stoicism**: an ancient philosophy of practical wisdom and resilience.
- **Dichotomy of control**: distinguishing what you can and can't control.
- **Virtue**: living with wisdom, courage, justice, and self-control.
- **Equanimity**: calm, steady composure regardless of circumstances.

## Card: Focus on what you control
image: philosophyStoicismFocusOnWhatYouControlHero
The central Stoic idea is the **dichotomy of control**: some things are within your control (your judgments, choices, actions, responses) and many are not (other people, events, outcomes, the past). Stoicism teaches focusing your energy on what you *can* control and accepting what you can't — rather than being distressed by things beyond your power. This simple shift is remarkably powerful for reducing anxiety and frustration. Much of our suffering comes from fighting what we can't change.

## Card: Virtue and equanimity
Stoicism also emphasizes **virtue** — living with wisdom, courage, justice, and self-control — as the true source of a good life, more than wealth or pleasure. And it cultivates **equanimity**: staying calm and composed regardless of circumstances, not by suppressing emotion but by reframing how we view events. Stoics practice seeing setbacks as opportunities and external things as neither good nor bad in themselves. The result is a steady, resilient mind.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking Stoicism means being emotionless or suppressing feelings (it's about managing reactions and finding calm, not numbness); seeing it as passive resignation (it's active — focus effort where you can act); imagining it's just dry ancient history (it's a practical, modern-relevant guide); and confusing "stoic" (the everyday sense of unemotional) with the rich philosophy. Stoicism is active, practical wisdom for resilience.

## Card: Quick recap
Stoicism is an ancient philosophy of practical wisdom, now widely revived. Its core: focus on what you control (your choices and responses), accept what you can't (events, others, outcomes), and cultivate virtue and equanimity (calm composure). This reduces anxiety and builds resilience. It's not emotionless suppression or passive resignation, but active, practical wisdom for living well and handling adversity — usable in daily life.
