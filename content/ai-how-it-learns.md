## Card: What it is
image: machineLearningHero
Most modern AI works through **machine learning** — instead of being programmed with exact rules, it *learns* from examples. Show it thousands of labeled photos of cats, and it figures out the patterns of "cat" on its own. This learning-from-data approach is what makes today's AI so powerful and flexible.

## Card: Why it matters
Understanding how AI learns explains its strengths and weaknesses — why it's great at some things, why it makes odd mistakes, and why its quality depends entirely on its training data. This insight helps you predict where AI will be reliable and where it'll likely stumble.

## Card: Key terms
- **Machine learning**: AI that learns patterns from examples.
- **Training data**: the examples an AI learns from.
- **Training**: the process of learning from that data.
- **Bias**: skewed results from skewed training data.

## Card: Learning from examples
image: trainingExamples
In traditional software, a human writes every rule. In **machine learning**, you instead feed the system lots of **training data** and let it discover the patterns itself. After enough examples, it can handle new cases it hasn't seen. This is why AI can do fuzzy tasks — like recognizing speech — that are nearly impossible to write exact rules for.

## Card: Garbage in, garbage out
Because AI learns from data, its quality depends on that data. Biased, incomplete, or outdated **training data** produces biased or wrong results — a principle called "garbage in, garbage out." If the examples over-represent one group or contain errors, the AI inherits those flaws. This is why **bias** in AI is a real and important concern.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking AI "knows" facts like a database, when it learned statistical patterns; assuming it's neutral and objective, when it reflects its training data including human biases; and expecting it to handle things very different from what it was trained on, where it often fails or makes things up.

## Card: Quick recap
Machine learning lets AI learn patterns from example data rather than fixed rules, enabling fuzzy tasks like recognizing images or speech. But its quality mirrors its training data — biased or poor data means biased or poor results. AI reflects what it learned from, so it's neither neutral nor all-knowing.
