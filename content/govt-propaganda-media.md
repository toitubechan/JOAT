## Card: What it is
image: govtPropagandaMediaHero
**Propaganda** is information — often biased, misleading, or emotionally charged — spread to shape opinions or promote a cause. The **media** (news and information sources) hugely influence what people know and believe. Understanding how information can be used to persuade or mislead is vital for thinking independently. This is a neutral overview of media literacy, not a partisan take.

## Card: Why it matters
We're flooded with information from media and online, some honest, some biased or manipulative. Being able to think critically about it — spotting propaganda, recognizing bias, and seeking reliable sources — is essential for forming your own informed views and resisting manipulation. Media literacy is one of the most important civic skills in the modern information age.

## Card: Key terms
- **Propaganda**: biased information spread to shape opinion.
- **Bias**: a slant or one-sided perspective in information.
- **Media literacy**: the skill of critically evaluating information.
- **Reliable sources**: information sources with good standards and accuracy.

## Card: How propaganda and bias work
image: govtPropagandaMediaHowPropagandaAndBiasWorkHero
**Propaganda** shapes opinion through techniques like emotional appeals, one-sided framing, repetition, fear, half-truths, and presenting opinion as fact. Even ordinary media can carry **bias** — slanting coverage through what they emphasize, omit, or how they frame things, sometimes unintentionally. The point isn't that all media is propaganda, but that information is rarely perfectly neutral, so it pays to be aware of *how* it might be shaping your views.

## Card: Thinking critically — media literacy
**Media literacy** is the defense: critically evaluating the information you consume. Useful habits: check the **source** and whether it's reliable; notice emotional manipulation and one-sided framing; seek multiple and diverse sources rather than relying on one; distinguish fact from opinion; and be extra skeptical of content designed to provoke strong emotions. The goal is to form your *own* informed views from good information, rather than being passively shaped by biased or manipulative content.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming all media is either fully trustworthy or all propaganda (the reality is more nuanced — quality and bias vary); not checking sources; relying on a single source or only ones that confirm your views; being swayed by emotionally manipulative content; and confusing opinion with fact. Media literacy — critically evaluating information and seeking reliable, diverse sources — is the key defense.

## Card: Quick recap
Propaganda is biased information spread to shape opinion, using techniques like emotional appeals, one-sided framing, and half-truths; even ordinary media can carry bias. The defense is media literacy: check sources and their reliability, notice manipulation and framing, seek multiple diverse sources, and separate fact from opinion. The goal is forming your own informed views from good information, not being passively shaped — a vital modern civic skill.
