## Card: What it is
image: growthHabitsBreakingBadOnesHero
Breaking a bad habit means weakening an automatic behavior you'd rather not do — like mindless snacking, doom-scrolling, or nail-biting. The trick is working *with* the habit loop rather than fighting it head-on. You rarely erase a habit; more often, you replace or disrupt it.

## Card: Why it matters
Bad habits quietly undermine health, time, money, and goals, and willpower alone usually fails against them. Understanding how to disrupt the habit loop gives you real leverage. Replacing even one stubborn bad habit can have an outsized positive effect on your life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Disrupting the cue**: removing or avoiding the trigger.
- **Habit substitution**: swapping the routine for a better one.
- **Friction**: making a bad habit harder to do.
- **Trigger**: the cue that sets off the habit.

## Card: Make it harder, remove the cue
image: growthHabitsBreakingBadOnesMakeItHarderRemoveTheCueHero
Two powerful moves. **Disrupt the cue**: identify the **trigger** and remove it — if you scroll because the phone's beside you, leave it in another room. And add **friction**: make the bad habit annoying to do (log out of apps, don't keep junk food in the house). Bad habits thrive on being easy; making them hard weakens them.

## Card: Substitute, don't just suppress
Pure willpower to "just stop" often fails because the underlying **craving** remains. Instead, use **habit substitution**: keep the same cue and reward, but swap the **routine** for a better one. Crave a break and reach for a cigarette? Take a short walk instead. You satisfy the craving in a healthier way, rather than fighting it.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: relying only on willpower to suppress the habit (the craving wins); not identifying the cue, so you can't remove it; keeping temptations easily within reach; and trying to drop several bad habits at once. Also: harsh self-blame after a slip, which adds stress and fuels the very habit you're fighting.

## Card: Quick recap
Break bad habits by working with the loop: disrupt or remove the cue, add friction to make the habit harder, and substitute a better routine that satisfies the same craving rather than fighting it with willpower alone. Identify the trigger, make temptation inconvenient, and forgive slips instead of spiraling.
