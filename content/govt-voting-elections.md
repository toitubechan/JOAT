## Card: What it is
image: govtVotingElectionsHero
**Elections** are the process by which people choose their representatives and leaders by **voting**. They're the core mechanism of democracy — how citizens exercise their say in who holds power. Understanding how voting and elections work, and why they matter, is essential civic knowledge. This is a neutral, non-partisan overview.

## Card: Why it matters
Voting is how citizens influence their government and society — a fundamental right and responsibility in a democracy. Understanding elections helps you participate effectively, value your vote, and recognize what makes elections fair or unfair. Whatever your political views, understanding the *process* of choosing leaders is foundational civic knowledge.

## Card: Key terms
- **Election**: a process of choosing leaders by voting.
- **Vote**: a citizen's choice in an election.
- **Free and fair**: elections that are genuine, honest, and open.
- **Turnout**: the share of eligible people who vote.

## Card: How elections work
image: govtVotingElectionsHowElectionsWorkHero
In an **election**, eligible citizens cast **votes** to choose between candidates or options. The candidate or party with the most support (by whatever rules that system uses) wins. Different systems count votes differently, but the shared idea is that the outcome reflects the people's choices. Regular elections let citizens not only choose leaders but also remove them — a key way democracy holds power accountable. Your vote is your formal say in this.

## Card: What makes elections legitimate
For elections to genuinely reflect the people's will, they must be **free and fair**: everyone eligible can vote, votes are counted honestly, candidates can campaign openly, and the process isn't rigged or coerced. Free and fair elections are what separate genuine democracy from a sham. Also relevant is **turnout** — when more eligible people vote, the result better reflects the whole population. Fairness and participation both matter for elections to mean something.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking one vote doesn't matter (collectively votes decide outcomes, and close results show every vote can count); assuming all elections are automatically fair (free and fair conditions are essential and not guaranteed everywhere); not understanding that elections also let citizens *remove* leaders; and disengaging. Voting is a fundamental way citizens influence power; fair elections and participation make it meaningful.

## Card: Quick recap
Elections are how people choose (and can remove) their leaders by voting — the core mechanism of democracy and citizens' formal say in power. For elections to genuinely reflect the people's will, they must be free and fair: open to all eligible voters, honestly counted, and uncoerced. Turnout matters too. Understanding the process — and that your vote is your say — is foundational civic knowledge, whatever your views.
