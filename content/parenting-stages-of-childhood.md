## Card: What it is
image: parentingStagesOfChildhoodHero
**Stages of childhood** are the broad phases children pass through as they grow — infancy, early childhood, middle childhood, and adolescence — each with distinctive characteristics, needs, and developmental tasks. Understanding these stages helps caregivers know what to expect and how to support children appropriately at each point. This is general educational knowledge, not professional advice.

## Card: Why it matters
Each stage of childhood has different needs, abilities, and challenges, so understanding them helps caregivers respond appropriately — rather than expecting a toddler to behave like an older child, or treating a teenager like a young one. It sets realistic expectations and reduces frustration. Knowing the broad stages is a practical foundation for supporting children well as they grow.

## Card: Key terms
- **Infancy**: roughly the first year or two (babies).
- **Early childhood**: the preschool/toddler years.
- **Middle childhood**: the school-age years.
- **Adolescence**: the teenage years toward adulthood.

## Card: The broad stages
image: parentingStagesOfChildhoodTheBroadStagesHero
Childhood is often divided into broad stages. **Infancy** (roughly the first year or two) — rapid physical growth and total dependence, building basic trust and bonds. **Early childhood** (toddler/preschool years) — explosive language, growing independence, play, and learning the world. **Middle childhood** (school-age years) — developing skills, friendships, learning, and a sense of competence. **Adolescence** (the teenage years) — major physical and emotional changes, growing independence, and forming identity. Each stage brings distinctive developments and needs.

## Card: Matching support to the stage
The practical value is matching your expectations and support to the child's **stage**. Each stage has its own developmental tasks and challenges — a toddler exploring and testing limits, a school-age child building competence and friendships, a teen seeking independence and identity. Understanding the stage helps caregivers respond appropriately (what's normal, what the child needs) rather than misreading stage-appropriate behavior as a problem. Remember stages are broad guides with normal individual variation — not rigid timetables.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: expecting children to behave beyond their stage (e.g., a toddler to have an older child's self-control); treating all ages the same; misreading normal stage-appropriate behavior (like teenage independence-seeking) as misbehavior; and treating stages as rigid timetables rather than broad guides. Understanding the stages — and matching support to each — helps caregivers respond appropriately, with realistic expectations.

## Card: Quick recap
Childhood passes through broad stages — infancy (rapid growth, dependence, bonding), early childhood (language, independence, play), middle childhood (skills, friendships, competence), and adolescence (big changes, independence, identity) — each with distinctive needs and developmental tasks. Matching your expectations and support to the child's stage helps you respond appropriately rather than misreading normal behavior. Stages are broad guides with individual variation, not rigid timetables.
