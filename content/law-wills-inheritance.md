## Card: What it is
image: lawWillsInheritanceHero
A **will** is a legal document stating how you want your possessions and money (your **estate**) distributed after you die. **Inheritance** is the passing of those assets to others. Understanding the basics helps you plan ahead and grasp what happens to an estate. This is general educational knowledge; inheritance law varies by country and can be complex, so for actual planning, consult a qualified professional.

## Card: Why it matters
Everyone will eventually leave something behind, and what happens to it matters greatly to loved ones. Understanding wills and inheritance helps you plan ahead, avoid leaving a mess or disputes, and grasp a topic many avoid thinking about. Having a basic understanding (and, in time, a will) can spare your loved ones stress and conflict at a difficult time.

## Card: Key terms
- **Will**: a legal document directing how your estate is distributed.
- **Estate**: everything you own that you leave behind.
- **Beneficiary**: someone who receives part of an estate.
- **Intestate**: dying without a valid will.

## Card: What a will does
image: lawWillsInheritanceWhatAWillDoesHero
A **will** lets you decide who receives your **estate** (your money, property, and possessions) after you die — naming your **beneficiaries**. It can also handle other wishes, like guardianship of children, depending on the system. A valid will gives you control and clarity over what happens, rather than leaving it to default rules. It's a way of ensuring your wishes are followed and reducing confusion and conflict for those you leave behind.

## Card: Dying without a will
If someone dies **intestate** (without a valid will), the law decides how their estate is distributed according to default rules — which may *not* match what they would have wanted, and can cause delays, disputes, and stress for loved ones. This is why making a will is widely recommended, even though many people put it off. Having one means *you* decide, rather than leaving it to default legal rules and potential family conflict.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking wills are only for the wealthy or elderly (anyone with possessions or dependents can benefit); putting off making one indefinitely (dying without one leaves it to default rules); assuming your wishes will "obviously" be followed without a will (they may not be); and not keeping a will updated as life changes. For real planning, inheritance law varies and is complex, so consult a qualified professional.

## Card: Quick recap
A will is a legal document directing how your estate (money, property, possessions) is distributed after you die, naming beneficiaries and sometimes handling other wishes. Dying without one (intestate) means the law decides by default rules — possibly not as you'd want, and often causing stress and disputes. Making a will gives you control and spares loved ones difficulty. For actual planning, consult a qualified professional, as laws vary.
