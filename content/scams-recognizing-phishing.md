## Card: What it is
image: phishingSignsHero
**Phishing messages** — fake emails, texts, or DMs — share recognizable warning signs once you know what to look for. Learning to spot these signs lets you catch a scam in seconds, before you click or reply. It's a practical skill that works across email, text, and social media alike.

## Card: Why it matters
Phishing is the entry point for most online fraud and account theft. A single careless click can hand over a password or install malicious software. Being able to scan a message for red flags is your front-line defense — and it takes only a moment once the signs are familiar.

## Card: Key terms
- **Sender address**: the real email or number a message came from.
- **Link / URL**: where a link actually leads (not what it says).
- **Urgency**: pressure to act immediately.
- **Generic greeting**: vague openings like "Dear Customer".

## Card: Check the sender and links
image: senderLinkCheck
Two of the best checks. First, the **sender address**: scammers use addresses that look almost right but aren't (like `service@paypa1.com`). Second, **links**: hover over (or long-press) a link to see where it *really* goes before clicking — the visible text can hide a fake destination. A mismatch is a giant red flag.

## Card: Other warning signs
Watch for: **urgency** ("act now or your account closes!"); a **generic greeting** instead of your name; requests for passwords, codes, or payment details (legitimate firms don't ask this way); spelling and odd phrasing; and unexpected attachments. Any one warrants suspicion; several together almost certainly mean phishing.

## Card: Common mistakes
The big mistakes: clicking links in a message instead of going to the site directly; trusting the *display name* without checking the real address; reacting to urgency without pausing; and assuming a message is genuine because it has a real logo. Slowing down and checking is what defeats nearly all phishing.

## Card: Quick recap
Spot phishing by checking the sender's real address and where links actually lead, and by watching for urgency, generic greetings, requests for sensitive details, and odd spelling. When unsure, don't click — go to the official site or app directly. A few seconds of checking stops most attacks cold.
