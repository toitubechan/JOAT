## Card: What it is
image: econTradeImportsAndExportsHero
**Imports** are goods and services a country buys from abroad; **exports** are what it sells to other countries. The balance between them — and the related idea of a **trade balance** — is a basic part of how national economies connect. Understanding these terms decodes much trade-related news.

## Card: Why it matters
Imports and exports appear constantly in economic news and political debate, often with confusing claims about "trade deficits." Understanding what these terms really mean — and what they do and don't signify — helps you read trade news critically rather than absorbing misleading framing.

## Card: Key terms
- **Import**: a good or service bought from abroad.
- **Export**: a good or service sold to another country.
- **Trade balance**: exports minus imports.
- **Trade deficit/surplus**: importing more than exporting / vice versa.

## Card: Imports and exports
image: econTradeImportsAndExportsImportsAndExportsHero
**Imports** flow *into* a country (you import a foreign-made phone); **exports** flow *out* (a country sells its goods abroad). Most countries do both extensively. Imports give people access to goods their country doesn't produce well or cheaply; exports earn the country income. The two flows together weave each economy into the global web of trade.

## Card: The trade balance
The **trade balance** is exports minus imports. A **trade surplus** means exporting more than importing; a **trade deficit** means importing more. A deficit is often framed as "bad," but it's not simply so — it can reflect a wealthy country buying lots of goods, and it's balanced by other financial flows. A trade deficit isn't inherently "losing"; it's more nuanced than headlines suggest.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking a trade deficit always means a country is "losing" or doing badly (it's more nuanced); confusing imports with something negative (they give consumers access to goods); assuming exports are always "good" and imports "bad" (both serve purposes); and not realizing trade balances connect to broader financial flows. The simple "deficit = bad" framing is misleading.

## Card: Quick recap
Imports are what a country buys from abroad; exports are what it sells. The trade balance is exports minus imports — a surplus or deficit. A trade deficit isn't simply "bad"; it can reflect a wealthy country buying lots and is offset by other flows. Both imports and exports serve purposes, so beware the misleading "deficit = losing" framing.
