## Card: What it is
image: sustainCircularEconomyHero
The **circular economy** is an alternative to the traditional "take, make, throw away" model. Instead, it aims to keep resources in use as long as possible — through reusing, repairing, refurbishing, and recycling — so waste is designed out of the system. It's a big-picture idea about how the whole economy could work more sustainably.

## Card: Why it matters
Our current economy is mostly **linear** — we extract resources, make products, use them briefly, and discard them. This wastes resources and creates pollution. The circular economy offers a more sustainable model, and understanding it helps you see your own habits (reuse, repair) as part of a bigger systemic shift. It reframes sustainability as smart design, not just individual sacrifice.

## Card: Key terms
- **Linear economy**: take, make, use, throw away.
- **Circular economy**: keep resources in use, design out waste.
- **Repair and refurbish**: extending product life.
- **Closed loop**: materials cycled back into new products.

## Card: Linear vs. circular
image: sustainCircularEconomyLinearVsCircularHero
The **linear economy** runs one way: extract resources → make products → use → discard. The **circular economy** loops instead: products are designed to be durable, repairable, and recyclable, so materials cycle back into use rather than becoming waste. Picture a circle, not a straight line ending in a bin. The goal is a **closed loop** where today's products become tomorrow's resources.

## Card: How it works in practice
A circular economy involves: designing products to last and be repaired; **repairing and refurbishing** instead of replacing; reusing and sharing; and recycling materials back into new products. It also includes business models like renting, leasing, and product-as-a-service. As an individual, choosing durable goods, repairing, reusing, and buying secondhand all support circular principles in your own life.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking recycling alone makes an economy circular (reuse and repair rank higher, and designing out waste matters most); seeing it as only a business concept with no personal relevance (your reuse and repair habits are part of it); and assuming the linear model is the only way. The circular economy reimagines waste as a design flaw to eliminate.

## Card: Quick recap
The circular economy replaces the linear "take, make, throw away" model with one that keeps resources in use — through durable design, repair, reuse, and recycling — so waste is designed out. It loops materials back into use rather than discarding them. Your own habits of repairing, reusing, and buying durable goods support this bigger systemic shift toward sustainability.
