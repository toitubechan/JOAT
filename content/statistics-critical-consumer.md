## Card: What it is
image: dataStatisticsCriticalConsumerHero
Being a **critical consumer of statistics** means having a mental checklist of questions to ask whenever you encounter a number or study. It pulls together the whole topic into a practical habit — so you can quickly judge whether a statistic is trustworthy or being used to mislead.

## Card: Why it matters
You're bombarded with statistics daily, and you can't fact-check everything. A few good habitual questions let you quickly filter trustworthy figures from dubious ones. This protects you from manipulation, misinformation, and bad decisions. It turns statistical knowledge into an everyday, automatic defense.

## Card: Key terms
- **Source**: who produced the statistic, and their motives.
- **Sample size**: how many cases the figure is based on.
- **Context**: what the number should be compared to.
- **Healthy skepticism**: questioning without cynicism.

## Card: The key questions to ask
image: dataStatisticsCriticalConsumerTheKeyQuestionsToAskHero
When you meet a statistic, run through a quick checklist. **Who's the source**, and do they have a motive? **How big was the sample**, and was it representative? **Compared to what** — what's the context or base? **Which average** is it (mean, median)? Is it **correlation being sold as causation**? And **what's not being shown**? These few questions catch most misleading statistics.

## Card: Skeptical, not cynical
The goal is **healthy skepticism**, not cynicism. Cynicism dismisses all statistics ("you can prove anything with numbers"), which is just as foolish as believing everything. Good statistics genuinely inform us. The skill is to *question* figures — checking source, sample, and context — while still valuing solid evidence. Aim to judge each statistic on its merits, neither gullible nor dismissive.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: accepting statistics because they sound authoritative; the opposite, dismissing all statistics cynically; not checking the source's motives; ignoring sample size and context; and not pausing to question at all. The fix is the habit: a quick mental checklist of source, sample, context, and what's missing — skeptical but not cynical.

## Card: Quick recap
Be a critical consumer of statistics with a habitual checklist: who's the source (and their motive)? How big and representative was the sample? Compared to what? Which average? Correlation or causation? What's not shown? Aim for healthy skepticism — questioning figures while still valuing good evidence — neither gullible nor cynical. It's an everyday defense against being misled.
