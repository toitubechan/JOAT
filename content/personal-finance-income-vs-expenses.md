## Card: What it is
image: financePersonalIncomeVsExpensesHero
Every month, money flows two ways. **Income** is what comes in — your salary, wages, or any money you receive. **Expenses** are what goes out — rent, food, transport, subscriptions. The relationship between these two flows is the foundation of your whole financial life.

## Card: Why it matters
You can't manage what you don't measure. When you know exactly how much comes in and goes out, you can spot leaks, plan ahead, and stop wondering where your money went. Most money stress comes not from low income, but from not knowing the numbers.

## Card: Key terms
- **Cash flow**: the movement of money in and out over time.
- **Fixed expenses**: costs that stay the same, like rent.
- **Variable expenses**: costs that change, like groceries or fun.
- **Surplus**: money left over after expenses.

## Card: The gap is everything
image: financePersonalIncomeVsExpensesTheGapIsEverythingHero
Subtract your expenses from your income. A positive result is a **surplus** — money you can save or invest. A negative result is a **deficit**, meaning you're spending more than you make and likely going into debt. The whole game is keeping that gap positive, month after month.

## Card: Fixed vs. variable
**Fixed expenses** are predictable and hard to change quickly — rent, loan payments, insurance. **Variable expenses** flex with your choices — eating out, shopping, entertainment. When you need to free up money fast, variable expenses are usually where you have the most control.

## Card: Common mistakes
People often track big purchases but ignore small, repeated ones — a daily coffee, several subscriptions, frequent deliveries. These "tiny" costs quietly add up to a large monthly drain. The second trap is counting income before taxes and deductions, which makes you feel richer than you really are.

## Card: Quick recap
Income comes in, expenses go out, and the gap between them shapes your finances. Aim for a surplus, watch the small repeated costs, and know the difference between fixed and variable spending. Track the real numbers — that's the first step to control.
