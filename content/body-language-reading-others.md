## Card: What it is
image: readingBodyLanguageHero
**Reading body language** is noticing others' non-verbal signals to better understand how they're feeling or reacting. Done sensibly — in clusters and context — it helps you pick up on comfort, discomfort, interest, or disengagement that words alone might hide. It's a useful skill for any interaction.

## Card: Why it matters
People don't always say what they feel, but their bodies often hint at it. Noticing these cues helps you respond better — easing someone's discomfort, noticing when you've lost their interest, or sensing tension. It makes you more perceptive and socially attuned, improving how you handle conversations and relationships.

## Card: Key terms
- **Open vs. closed**: relaxed/receptive vs. guarded posture.
- **Engagement cues**: signals of interest like leaning in.
- **Discomfort signals**: signs someone is uneasy.
- **Baseline**: a person's normal behavior, for comparison.

## Card: Open vs. closed signals
image: openClosedSignalsHero
A useful broad distinction: **open** body language (relaxed posture, uncrossed arms, facing you, good eye contact) generally suggests comfort and receptiveness, while **closed** signals (crossed arms, turning away, minimal eye contact) can suggest discomfort or guardedness. Remember to read these as clusters and in context, not as single guaranteed meanings.

## Card: Engagement and baselines
Watch for **engagement cues** — leaning in, nodding, eye contact, responsive expressions — versus disengagement (checking the time, looking away, fidgeting). Crucially, compare against a person's **baseline**: their normal behavior. A *change* from someone's usual manner is more telling than any single gesture, because it signals something shifted in the moment.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: treating one signal as a definitive read; ignoring context and culture (gestures and norms vary); not knowing the person's baseline, so you misread their normal behavior; and over-analyzing, becoming a suspicious "body language detective." Use it as gentle insight, not mind-reading — clues to check, not conclusions to trust blindly.

## Card: Quick recap
Reading body language means noticing others' non-verbal cues — open vs. closed posture, engagement vs. disengagement — to sense how they feel. Compare against their baseline (normal behavior); a change is most telling. Always read clusters in context, mind cultural differences, and treat it as gentle insight, not certain mind-reading.
