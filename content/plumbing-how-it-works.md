## Card: What it is
image: plumbingSystemHero
Home **plumbing** is two systems working together: one brings clean water *in* under pressure, and one carries waste water *out* using gravity. Once you see it as "fresh water in, dirty water out," the maze of pipes behind your walls suddenly makes sense.

## Card: Why it matters
Understanding the basic layout helps you spot problems, know what's normal, and act fast in an emergency — like a burst pipe. You don't need to be a plumber, but knowing how water flows turns a panic into a manageable situation and helps you describe issues clearly when you do call one.

## Card: Key terms
- **Supply line**: pipes bringing pressurized clean water in.
- **Drain line**: pipes carrying waste water out by gravity.
- **Shut-off valve**: a tap that stops water flow.
- **Trap**: the curved pipe under a drain that blocks smells.

## Card: Water coming in
The **supply line** delivers clean water under **pressure**, which is why it sprays out when you open a tap. It splits to feed every fixture — sinks, showers, toilets — and often a heater for hot water. Because it's pressurized, a break in a supply pipe sprays water actively, making fast shut-off important.

## Card: Water going out
image: supplyDrainFlow
The **drain line** has no pressure — it relies on **gravity**, so pipes slope downward to carry waste away. Each drain has a curved **trap** (the U-bend) that holds a little water to block sewer smells from rising. Drain problems are usually blockages, since there's no pressure pushing things through.

## Card: Common mistakes
A common confusion is not knowing the difference between supply (pressurized) and drain (gravity) problems — they behave and are fixed differently. Another is not knowing where the main **shut-off valve** is until water is already spraying. And many pour grease or wipes down drains, causing the blockages that gravity can't clear.

## Card: Quick recap
Plumbing is clean water in under pressure, waste water out by gravity. Supply leaks spray; drain problems clog. Each drain has a trap that blocks smells. Know where your shut-off valves are before you need them. This simple mental model makes plumbing far less mysterious.
