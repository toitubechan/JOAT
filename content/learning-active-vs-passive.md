## Card: What it is
image: growthLearningActiveVsPassiveHero
There are two broad ways to study: **passive** (rereading, highlighting, watching) and **active** (testing yourself, explaining, applying). They feel similar in effort but differ enormously in results. Active learning is far more effective — knowing the difference is one of the biggest study upgrades there is.

## Card: Why it matters
Most people default to passive methods because they're comfortable and feel productive. But they waste time, producing weak, fading memory. Switching to active methods means you learn more in less time. This single shift can transform how effectively you study, work, or pick up any new skill.

## Card: Key terms
- **Passive learning**: absorbing material without much effort (rereading).
- **Active learning**: engaging effortfully (self-testing, explaining).
- **Retrieval practice**: recalling information from memory.
- **The illusion of competence**: feeling you know more than you do.

## Card: The trap of passive learning
image: growthLearningActiveVsPassiveTheTrapOfPassiveLearningHero
**Passive learning** — rereading notes, highlighting, re-watching — feels productive and easy, which is exactly the trap. It creates an **illusion of competence**: the material feels familiar, so you think you know it, but you can't actually recall it under pressure. Comfort is not the same as learning, and passive study mostly delivers comfort.

## Card: The power of active learning
**Active learning** forces your brain to *do* something with the material: **retrieval practice** (testing yourself, recalling without looking), explaining it in your own words, teaching it to someone, or applying it to problems. This effort is harder and feels less smooth — but it builds far stronger, longer-lasting memory. Struggle a little, learn a lot.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: defaulting to highlighting and rereading because they're easy; mistaking the comfortable familiarity of passive review for real knowledge; avoiding self-testing because it reveals what you *don't* know (which is exactly its value); and judging study by hours spent rather than by active engagement.

## Card: Quick recap
Passive learning (rereading, highlighting) feels productive but creates only an illusion of competence. Active learning — self-testing, explaining, applying — is harder but builds real, lasting knowledge. Favor retrieval practice over rereading. The shift from passive to active is one of the most powerful study upgrades you can make.
