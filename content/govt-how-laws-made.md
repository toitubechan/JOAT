## Card: What it is
image: govtHowLawsMadeHero
**Laws** are the rules a society must follow, and they're created through a deliberate process — typically proposed, debated, voted on, and then enforced. The exact process varies by country, but the general idea is similar. Understanding how laws are made demystifies a core function of government and shows how rules that affect everyone come to be. This is a neutral, educational overview.

## Card: Why it matters
Laws govern nearly everything in society, and understanding how they're made helps you grasp government, the news, and how change happens. It reveals that laws aren't arbitrary or permanent — they're created, debated, and can be changed through a process, often one citizens can influence. It's foundational civic knowledge.

## Card: Key terms
- **Law**: a rule everyone in a society must follow.
- **Bill**: a proposed law before it's passed.
- **Legislature**: the body that debates and votes on laws.
- **Amendment / repeal**: changing or removing existing laws.

## Card: From proposal to law
image: govtHowLawsMadeFromProposalToLawHero
The general process: a proposed law (a **bill**) is introduced, then debated and often revised by the **legislature** (the law-making body, like a parliament or congress). It's voted on, and if it passes (often through several stages, sometimes more than one chamber), it may need final approval before becoming an enforceable **law**. The specifics differ by country, but the pattern — propose, debate, vote, enact — is widely shared. This deliberation is meant to scrutinize laws before they bind everyone.

## Card: Laws can change
A key point: laws aren't fixed forever. Through the same kind of process, existing laws can be **amended** (changed) or **repealed** (removed) as society's needs and views evolve. This is why laws differ across countries and change over time. In many systems, citizens can influence law-making — by voting for representatives, contacting them, or participating in public debate. Laws reflect a society's choices, made through a process, and they can be reshaped.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking laws are arbitrary or handed down with no process (they're proposed, debated, and voted on); assuming laws are permanent (they can be amended or repealed); imagining citizens have no influence (in many systems they can, through voting and participation); and assuming the process is identical everywhere (it varies by country). Laws are created and changed through a deliberate, often influenceable process.

## Card: Quick recap
Laws are society's binding rules, created through a process: a bill is proposed, debated and revised by the legislature, voted on, and enacted into law (specifics vary by country). Laws aren't permanent — they can be amended or repealed as society evolves, and citizens can often influence the process through voting and participation. Understanding this shows how the rules affecting everyone come to be, and can be changed.
