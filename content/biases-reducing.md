## Card: What it is
image: reducingBiasesHero
You can't eliminate biases — they're built into how minds work — but you *can* reduce their influence with deliberate strategies. **Reducing bias** is about practical habits that help you think more clearly and decide better, working *around* your biases rather than pretending you don't have them.

## Card: Why it matters
Since biases are unavoidable, the practical question is how to limit their damage. A few strategies meaningfully improve your thinking and decisions over a lifetime. This is the payoff of learning about biases: not just knowing they exist, but actually thinking and choosing more wisely because of that knowledge.

## Card: Key terms
- **Slow thinking**: pausing to reason deliberately.
- **Devil's advocate**: deliberately arguing against your own view.
- **Seeking disconfirmation**: looking for evidence you're wrong.
- **Outside perspective**: getting others' views to check your own.

## Card: Slow down and question yourself
Biases thrive on fast, automatic thinking, so for important decisions, **slow down** and reason deliberately. Actively question your first instinct. A powerful habit is **seeking disconfirmation** — deliberately looking for evidence that you might be *wrong*, which counters confirmation bias. Playing **devil's advocate** against your own view forces you to consider what you'd otherwise ignore.

## Card: Get outside perspectives
image: outsidePerspectiveHero
Because of the bias blind spot, we can't easily see our own biases — so **outside perspectives** help. Ask others (especially those who think differently) to challenge your view. Seek diverse information sources rather than ones that just confirm you. And on decisions, ask "what would I advise a friend here?" — distance reduces the biases that cloud our own choices.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common mistakes: believing awareness alone removes bias (it helps, but strategies are needed); only seeking confirming information; relying on fast gut reactions for important decisions; and never inviting challenge to your views. The fix is humility plus active habits — slow down, seek disconfirmation, and get outside perspectives, rather than trusting you're simply unbiased.

## Card: Quick recap
You can't eliminate biases, but you can reduce their influence: slow down for important decisions, actively seek evidence you might be wrong (countering confirmation bias), play devil's advocate, and get outside perspectives to overcome your blind spot. Awareness plus these deliberate habits — not the false belief you're unbiased — is how you think more clearly.
