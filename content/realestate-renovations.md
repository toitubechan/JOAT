## Card: What it is
image: realestateRenovationsHero
**Renovating to add value** means improving a property to increase its worth — but not all renovations are equal. Some add more value than they cost; others cost more than they return. Understanding which improvements genuinely add value (and which don't) helps you renovate wisely rather than wasting money. This is general educational knowledge, not professional advice.

## Card: Why it matters
People often assume any renovation adds value, then are surprised when an expensive project doesn't pay off when selling. Understanding which improvements genuinely increase value — and which are mainly for personal enjoyment — helps you spend renovation money wisely, whether improving a home to sell or just deciding what's worth doing. It can save (or make) significant money.

## Card: Key terms
- **Adding value**: improvements that increase a property's worth.
- **Return on investment**: whether an improvement adds more value than it cost.
- **Over-improving**: spending beyond what the property/area supports.
- **Cosmetic vs. structural**: surface updates vs. deeper changes.

## Card: Not all renovations add value
image: realestateRenovationsNotAllRenovationsAddValueHero
A key principle: a renovation only **adds value** if it increases the property's worth by more than it cost (a positive **return on investment**). Many improvements *don't* pay off financially — some are purely for personal enjoyment, and some even cost more than they add. Generally, sensible **cosmetic** updates (fresh paint, fixing what's broken, modernizing kitchens and bathrooms tastefully) and adding usable space tend to add value efficiently, while highly personalized or over-the-top projects often don't return their cost.

## Card: Avoid over-improving
A common trap is **over-improving** — spending more on a property than the area or market will reward. There's often a ceiling to what properties in a given area sell for, so a luxury renovation in a modest neighborhood may not recoup its cost. The smart approach: improve in line with what the property and area support, focus on changes that genuinely add value, and distinguish improvements made for resale value from those made purely for your own enjoyment (both are fine, but know which is which).

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: assuming any renovation adds value (many don't pay off); over-improving beyond what the area supports (you won't recoup it); confusing personal-enjoyment projects with value-adding ones; and over-personalizing in ways future buyers won't value. Renovate wisely: focus on improvements with a positive return, don't over-improve for the area, and be clear whether a project is for value or personal enjoyment.

## Card: Quick recap
Renovating adds value only if it increases the property's worth by more than it cost — and many renovations don't pay off financially. Sensible cosmetic updates and adding usable space tend to add value efficiently, while over-personalized or excessive projects often don't. Beware over-improving beyond what the area supports. Renovate wisely: focus on positive-return improvements, and distinguish value-adding projects from those for personal enjoyment.
