## Card: What it is
image: spaceExplorationHero
**Space exploration** is how humanity studies and ventures into space — using telescopes to observe from afar, and rockets, satellites, probes, and crewed missions to reach beyond Earth. It's how we've learned almost everything we know about the cosmos, and it touches daily life more than people realize.

## Card: Why it matters
Space exploration has expanded human knowledge enormously and produced technology we use daily. Understanding how we explore demystifies space missions in the news and reveals that "space stuff" isn't remote — satellites power GPS, weather forecasts, and communication. It connects the cosmos to your everyday life.

## Card: Key terms
- **Telescope**: an instrument for observing distant objects.
- **Satellite**: an object placed in orbit around Earth.
- **Probe**: an uncrewed spacecraft sent to explore.
- **Orbit**: a stable path around a larger body.

## Card: Observing from afar
Most of what we know comes from **telescopes**, which gather light (and other signals) from distant objects. Telescopes on the ground and in space let us study planets, stars, and galaxies without leaving home. They're essentially time machines too — since light takes so long to arrive, powerful telescopes see distant objects as they were long ago.

## Card: Reaching into space
image: rocketOrbitHero
To physically reach space, rockets must go fast enough to enter **orbit** — a stable path where a craft keeps "falling around" Earth. We send **satellites** (for communication, GPS, weather, observation), **probes** (uncrewed craft exploring other planets), and occasionally crewed missions. Much space technology quietly powers daily life: your phone's maps, weather forecasts, and TV signals often rely on satellites.

## Card: Common mistakes
Common misunderstandings: thinking space exploration is irrelevant to daily life (satellites underpin GPS, weather, and communication); imagining we've visited far-off planets in person (humans have only reached the Moon; probes do the distant exploring); not realizing telescopes show the past; and thinking orbit means "escaping gravity" (it's actually continuously falling around Earth).

## Card: Quick recap
We explore space by observing with telescopes (which gather distant light, showing the past) and by reaching it with rockets, satellites, probes, and crewed missions. Orbit is continuously falling around Earth. Space technology quietly powers daily life — GPS, weather, communication — so exploration is far more relevant than it seems.
